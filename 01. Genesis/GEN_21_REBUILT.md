## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 21 is the chapter of fulfillment. Every previous chapter in the Abraham narrative has been building toward this one sentence: "And the LORD visited Sarah as he had said, and the LORD did unto Sarah as he had spoken." The precise double-echo — "as he had said... as he had spoken" — is the narrator's declaration that God's word arrived exactly. Isaac was born to a hundred-year-old father and a ninety-year-old mother, at the set time (moed) God had appointed, fulfilling the promises of Genesis 17:21 and 18:14. Abraham circumcised him on the eighth day as commanded. Sarah's response became one of the most joyful declarations in Scripture: "God hath made me to laugh; so that all that hear will laugh with me." The name Isaac — Laughter — had been spoken by God before the child existed; now the child existed and his name was the testimony: God turned the incredulous laughter of Genesis 17-18 into the jubilant laughter of Genesis 21.

The feast at Isaac's weaning was the occasion for the expulsion crisis. Sarah saw Ishmael — the son of Hagar the Egyptian — mocking (metsachaq — playing-with-mockery, the same tsachaq root as Isaac's name). She demanded Abraham cast out the bondwoman and her son: "the son of this bondwoman shall not be heir with my son, even with Isaac." Abraham was deeply grieved — this was his son too. God resolved the grief by confirming Sarah's word as covenant truth: "in Isaac shall thy seed be called" — and by separately promising that Ishmael also would become a nation. Both promises were given in the same divine word; both would be kept.

Abraham rose early, gave Hagar bread and a skin of water, and sent her into the wilderness of Beersheba. When the water was spent, Hagar placed the boy — described here as "the child" and "the lad" — under a shrub, walked a bowshot away, and wept: "Let me not see the death of the child." Then God heard the boy's voice, and the angel of God called to Hagar from heaven: "What aileth thee, Hagar? Fear not; for God hath heard the voice of the lad where he is." The divine reassurance was accompanied by the divine action: "God opened her eyes, and she saw a well of water." The well was already there. The miracle was the opened eyes. Ishmael grew in the wilderness of Paran, became an archer, and his mother took him a wife from Egypt.

The chapter closes with two covenant transactions at Beersheba. First, Abimelech and his general Phichol sought a mutual covenant with Abraham: "God is with thee in all that thou doest." Abraham agreed, reproved Abimelech about a well his servants had seized, accepted Abimelech's claimed ignorance, and established the well's ownership by setting apart seven ewe lambs as a public witness. The place was called Beersheba — Beer (well) Sheva (seven / oath) — encoding both meanings in the name. Abraham planted a tamarisk tree there and called on the name of the LORD — El Olam — the Everlasting God. The first use of this divine name in Scripture came at the end of the chapter of fulfillment: the God who kept the promise is the God of all ages.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "The LORD Visited Sarah as He Had Said" — Precision Fulfillment at the Moed

**Genesis 21:1-2** — "And the LORD visited Sarah as he had said, and the LORD did unto Sarah as he had spoken. For Sarah conceived, and bare Abraham a son in his old age, at the set time (moed) of which God had spoken to him"

- **The double echo:** v.1 uses two parallel phrases — "as he had said" and "as he had spoken" — to anchor the fulfillment explicitly to the prior word; this is the narrator making sure the reader registers the connection; the fulfillment is not incidental to the promise, it is the point; God's word is not illustrated by events — events are the arrival of God's word
- **The promises being fulfilled simultaneously:**
  - Genesis 17:19 — "Sarah thy wife shall bear thee a son indeed; and thou shalt call his name Isaac: and I will establish my covenant with him for an everlasting covenant, and with his seed after him"
  - Genesis 17:21 — "But my covenant will I establish with Isaac, which Sarah shall bear unto thee at this set time (bammoedim) in the next year"
  - Genesis 18:10 — "I will certainly return unto thee according to the time of life (kaet chayah); and, lo, Sarah thy wife shall have a son"
  - Genesis 18:14 — "At the time appointed (lammoedim) I will return unto thee, according to the time of life, and Sarah shall have a son"
  - All four prior statements are simultaneously fulfilled in Genesis 21:1-2; the text registers this with its deliberate "as he had said... as he had spoken"
- **Romans 9:9** — "For this is the word of promise, At this time will I come, and Sarah shall have a son" — Paul quotes the Genesis 18:14 / 21:1 fulfillment as the foundational example of how promise-word works; the promise is not a general statement of intention but a specific word with a delivery time; the delivery came; that is the proof-text of promise-theology
- **Galatians 4:4** — "But when the fulness of the time (to plērōma tou chronou) was come, God sent forth his Son, made of a woman, made under the law" — the Incarnation operates on the same "at the appointed time" principle as Isaac's birth; both were impossible births that arrived at God's calendar-point; both were preceded by long waiting that human impatience interpreted as delay; both arrived exactly as spoken; the moed of Isaac's birth is the Old Testament pattern for the plērōma-time of Christ's birth
- **The word "visited" (paqad — פָּקַד):** Genesis 21:1 — "the LORD visited (wayyifqod) Sarah" — paqad = to visit, to attend to, to act upon with purposeful attention; it carries the connotation of sovereign oversight turned to active engagement; when God "visits" in Scripture He moves; He does not merely observe:
  - Exodus 3:16 — "I have surely visited (paqod paqadti) you, and seen that which is done to you in Egypt" — the Exodus was preceded by divine paqad
  - Ruth 1:6 — "she had heard in the country of Moab how that the LORD had visited (paqad) his people in giving them bread" — the end of famine was paqad
  - Luke 1:68 — Zechariah's Benedictus: "Blessed be the Lord God of Israel; for he hath visited (epeskepsato) and redeemed his people" — the Incarnation as the ultimate divine paqad
  - The word "visited" in Genesis 21:1 is covenant-action language; God visited Sarah means God turned His full sovereign attention to the exact need the promise had created and acted on it precisely

### The Eighth Day Circumcision — New Creation Sign

**Genesis 21:4** — "And Abraham circumcised his son Isaac being eight days old, as God had commanded him"

- **Eight as the number of new creation:** Seven is the number of completion — God rested on the seventh day, completing creation (Genesis 2:2); eight is the day after completion, the beginning of a new cycle, the number of new creation and resurrection; every male child circumcised on the eighth day was enrolled in the covenant on the first day of the new-creation week, a physical declaration that the covenant child belonged to a new order, not merely to the biological order of the first seven
- **The eighth day throughout Scripture:**
  - Genesis 17:12 — the command: "he that is eight days old shall be circumcised among you, every man child in your generations" — the eighth day is the covenant's prescribed entry point
  - Genesis 21:4 — Isaac circumcised on the eighth day "as God had commanded him" — the obedience is precise; the day is not approximate
  - Leviticus 9:1 — "And it came to pass on the eighth day, that Moses called Aaron and his sons" — Aaron's priestly ministry began on the eighth day; new covenant-service begins on the new-creation day
  - Leviticus 12:3 — "And in the eighth day the flesh of his foreskin shall be circumcised" — the Law codified the Genesis 17 command
  - John 20:1 — "The first day of the week" (the eighth day from the Sabbath) — Christ rose on the eighth day; the resurrection is the new creation; the circumcision enacted what the resurrection revealed: entry into a new order beyond the completed old one
  - John 20:26 — "And after eight days again his disciples were within" — Thomas's encounter; the second post-resurrection appearance also on the eighth day; the new creation's week is made up of eighth days
- **Colossians 2:11-12** — "In whom also ye are circumcised with the circumcision made without hands, in putting off the body of the sins of the flesh by the circumcision of Christ: Buried with him in baptism, wherein also ye are risen with him through the faith of the operation of God" — the spiritual circumcision of new birth is the antitype of the eighth-day physical circumcision; entrance into Christ is entrance into the new creation; the eighth-day sign pointed to the resurrection-life that the Spirit performs in the new birth

### "God Hath Made Me to Laugh" — The Three-Phase Arc of Tsachaq

**Genesis 21:6** — "And Sarah said, God hath made me to laugh (tsachaq), so that all that hear will laugh with me"

- **Phase 1 — Abraham's astonished laughter (Genesis 17:17):** "Then Abraham fell upon his face, and laughed, and said in his heart, Shall a child be born unto him that is an hundred years old? and shall Sarah, that is ninety years old, bear?" — the laughter of overwhelmed astonishment; Abraham fell on his face first (covenant reverence), then laughed inwardly; Paul describes his ultimate condition as "fully persuaded" (Romans 4:21), suggesting the laughter was the beginning of wonder, not settled unbelief; no rebuke was recorded
- **Phase 2 — Sarah's doubtful laughter (Genesis 18:12-15):** "Therefore Sarah laughed within herself, saying, After I am waxed old shall I have pleasure, my lord being old also?" — the laughter of biological calculation displacing faith; hidden, internal, fear-driven; God heard it, asked about it, and when Sarah denied it said plainly "Nay; but thou didst laugh" — the only recorded divine rebuke connected to tsachaq
- **Phase 3 — Joy-laughter at fulfillment (Genesis 21:6):** "God HATH MADE ME to laugh" — the passive form is crucial; Sarah is not claiming she laughed; she is declaring that God produced the laughter; the source shifted from Sarah's doubting self to God's fulfilling act; the laughter that was previously a symptom of unbelief became the testimony of faithfulness
- **Ishmael mocking (Genesis 21:9) — Phase 4 — Mocking laughter (metsachaq):** The piel intensive form of the same root; mocking-play; the son whose name was NOT laughter was using the laughter-word to mock the son whose name WAS laughter; the name Isaac itself became the instrument of Ishmael's mockery; the irony is precise — the one who inherited the name "laughter" was mocked with laughter by the one who did not receive the promise
- **Psalm 126:1-2** — "When the LORD turned again the captivity of Zion, we were like them that dream. Then was our mouth filled with laughter (sechok), and our tongue with singing" — the national form of Sarah's personal tsachaq-arc; Israel's restoration from captivity produced the same laughter-at-fulfillment that Sarah experienced; the pattern is permanent: God's people weep at the impossibility and laugh at the fulfillment; **Psalm 30:11** — "Thou hast turned for me my mourning into dancing: thou hast put off my sackcloth, and girded me with gladness"
- *Interpretation:* The name Isaac is a three-chapter theological statement compressed into a single word. Abraham's falling-and-laughing was astonished wonder at the impossible. Sarah's internal laugh was unbelief confronted by truth. Sarah's declaration at Isaac's birth was faith confirmed by fulfillment. The same sound — tsachaq — carried all three states. God named the child after the starting-point of the journey (astonishment at impossibility) to declare that He transforms every form of that laughter — wonder, doubt, and fear — into the final form: joy at promises kept.

### "Cast Out the Bondwoman and Her Son" — The Covenant Narrowing

**Genesis 21:10-13** — "Cast out this bondwoman and her son: for the son of this bondwoman shall not be heir with my son, even with Isaac... in Isaac shall thy seed be called... also of the son of the bondwoman will I make a nation, because he is thy seed"

- **Paul's Spirit-given interpretation in Galatians 4:21-31:** This is one of the most explicitly interpreted Old Testament passages in the New Testament; Paul under inspiration identifies the typological meaning:
  - Hagar = Mount Sinai = the covenant that produces bondage; she "gendereth to bondage" (Galatians 4:24); the covenant of law-keeping that, when pursued as the basis of righteousness, enslaves rather than frees
  - Sarah = Jerusalem which is above = the covenant of promise; free; the mother of all who are born of the Spirit
  - Ishmael = those born after the flesh = those who pursue righteousness by natural human effort, by the works of the law
  - Isaac = those born after the Spirit = those who are "children of promise" (Galatians 4:28); born not of human striving but of divine intervention
  - Galatians 4:29 — "But as then he that was born after the flesh persecuted him that was born after the Spirit, even so it is now" — the Genesis 21:9 mocking is the historical prototype of every subsequent persecution of promise-born believers by flesh-born religion; the religious persecution of Christians by law-keeping religionists follows Ishmael's pattern
  - Galatians 4:30 — "Nevertheless what saith the scripture? Cast out the bondwoman and her son: for the son of the bondwoman shall not be heir with the son of the freewoman" — Paul quotes Genesis 21:10 directly as authoritative Scripture governing the church's situation; the Galatian church was to "cast out" the Judaizing teaching (the Hagar-covenant) as incompatible with the promise-covenant (the Sarah-covenant)
- **Romans 9:6-8** — "They are not all Israel, which are of Israel: Neither, because they are the seed of Abraham, are they all children: but, In Isaac shall thy seed be called. That is, They which are the children of the flesh, these are not the children of God: but the children of the promise are counted for the seed" — Paul's second use of Genesis 21:12; the "in Isaac shall thy seed be called" establishes that covenant lineage is not biological but promise-based; the children of the promise are counted as the seed, not all biological descendants
- **The dual divine response in vv.12-13:** God confirmed Sarah's demand ("in Isaac shall thy seed be called") AND separately promised Ishmael's future ("also of the son of the bondwoman will I make a nation, because he is thy seed"); both were real promises; both were kept; the distinction is: covenant-election through Isaac (the redemptive line), common grace and national blessing for Ishmael (twelve princes, Genesis 17:20; 25:12-18); God did not abandon Ishmael — He simply distinguished between the two types of promise; this distinction is the foundation of the theological category of election: God is free to fulfill general blessing and specific covenant purposes through different instruments
- **The expulsion as typological necessity:** The two sons could not co-inherit because the two covenants are mutually exclusive as foundations for righteousness; law-works and promise-faith cannot be co-heirs; Galatians 5:4 — "Christ is become of no effect unto you, whosoever of you are justified by the law; ye are fallen from grace" — the co-inheritance is impossible in the same way that justification by law and justification by faith are mutually exclusive; the expulsion enacted the theological reality

### Hagar and Ishmael in the Wilderness — God Hears Outside the Covenant

**Genesis 21:14-19** — "She departed, and wandered in the wilderness of Beersheba... And God heard the voice of the lad... God opened her eyes, and she saw a well of water"

- **The crisis:** v.15 — "the water was spent in the bottle"; v.15 — "she cast the child under one of the shrubs" — the word "cast" (vatashlek) is strong; she abandoned him under the shrub; she could not bear to watch him die; v.16 — "Let me not see the death of the child" — she walked a bowshot away and wept; the mother and the dying child, separated by the distance of a bowshot, both weeping (v.16 Hagar; v.17 "the voice of the lad" implies his crying)
- **"God heard the voice of the lad where he is":** v.17 — this phrase contains a profound theological statement; "where he is" = God heard Ishmael in the specific place he was in, not in the place he should have been or the place someone else was; God's hearing is location-specific, condition-specific, person-specific; Ishmael was not in the covenant; he was in the wilderness under a shrub; God heard him there; **Psalm 139:7-10** — "Whither shall I go from thy spirit? or whither shall I flee from thy presence? If I ascend up into heaven, thou art there: if I make my bed in hell, behold, thou art there" — there is no location outside the reach of divine hearing; "where he is" is every location
- **The distinction between hearing and electing:** God heard Ishmael and preserved him; God did not establish His redemptive covenant through Ishmael; both are true simultaneously; the same God who narrowed the covenant line through Isaac also heard and responded to the cry of the one outside the covenant line; **Matthew 5:45** — "He maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust" — God's care extends beyond covenant boundaries; common grace (care, preservation, blessing) is not the same as covenant grace (redemption, election, the Messianic line); distinguishing them prevents both the error of thinking God has abandoned all non-covenant people and the error of thinking universal care equals universal redemption
- **"God opened her eyes, and she saw a well of water":** v.19 — the well was already present; the opened eyes were the miracle; the provision had been ordained before the crisis arrived; the desert geography already contained the water; Hagar was dying of thirst within sight of water she could not see; this is one of Scripture's most concentrated images of how divine provision works: not the creation of new resources in response to crisis but the opening of eyes to see resources that were already placed
  - **2 Kings 6:15-17** — Elisha's servant saw only the Syrian army; Elisha prayed: "LORD, I pray thee, open his eyes, that he may see"; the LORD opened the servant's eyes and he saw the mountain full of horses and chariots of fire; the angelic host was already there before the eyes were opened; the need was not more horses but open eyes
  - **Luke 24:31** — the Emmaus disciples' eyes were opened and they recognized Christ; He had been present throughout the journey; the provision of His presence was not new — the opened eyes were
  - **John 4:14; 7:38-39** — the well of water springing up, the rivers of living water — Christ's language for the Spirit draws on the wilderness-well imagery; the Spirit is already given in Christ; the miracle of conversion is the opening of spiritually blind eyes to see what is already there

### El Olam — The Everlasting God — First Use

**Genesis 21:33** — "And Abraham planted a grove (tamarisk tree) in Beersheba, and called there on the name of the LORD, the everlasting God (El Olam)"

- **El Olam** = God of eternity / Everlasting God / God of the ages — El (God, the strong one) + Olam (age, eternity, forever, beyond the horizon of time); olam can mean the indefinite past ("from everlasting"), the indefinite future ("forever"), or both simultaneously ("from everlasting to everlasting"); the God who spans all time in both directions
- **Why Abraham gave God this name at this moment:** The chapter of fulfillment produced the name of the eternal fulfiller; Abraham had watched God keep a promise declared decades earlier; he had seen the appointed time arrive exactly; he had been preserved, corrected, enriched, and made a father of nations; at Beersheba — the place of the oath — he planted a tree that would outlive him and called on the name of the God who would outlive the tree; El Olam was the theological conclusion drawn from observed covenant faithfulness across time
- **Isaiah 40:28** — "Hast thou not known? hast thou not heard, that the everlasting God (Elohim Olam), the LORD, the Creator of the ends of the earth, fainteth not, neither is weary? there is no searching of his understanding" — Isaiah's great comfort chapter (40) uses El Olam as the basis for the assurance that God will renew the strength of those who wait on Him (v.31); the God who does not grow weary is the same El Olam Abraham named at Beersheba; this is why He kept His promise to Sarah — He never ran out of capacity
- **Psalm 90:2** — "Before the mountains were brought forth, or ever thou hadst formed the earth and the world, even from everlasting (me-olam) to everlasting (ad-olam), thou art God" — Moses's great meditation on divine eternity; the human lifespan of seventy or eighty years (v.10) is set against the olam-to-olam nature of God; Abraham's planting of a long-lived tree was an act of worship of this olam God — a human reaching toward a timescale beyond human life in honor of the God who inhabits all timescales
- **Romans 16:26** — "the everlasting God (tou aiōniou theou)" — Paul closes the letter to the Romans with this title; the same El Olam, now revealed in the gospel of Christ, is the God through whose prophetic writings and commandment the faith is made known to all nations; the first use of El Olam in Genesis 21:33 is the seed of Paul's doxological El Olam in Romans 16:26
- **Isaiah 26:4** — "Trust ye in the LORD for ever: for in the LORD JEHOVAH is everlasting strength (tsur olamim — the Rock of ages)" — the everlasting strength of El Olam is the specific resource that makes trust in Him permanent and warranted; Abraham planted a tree in trust that the El Olam would outlast the tree and the planter and the land's current occupants; he was right

### Abimelech's Testimony — "God Is with Thee in All That Thou Doest"

**Genesis 21:22** — "God is with thee in all that thou doest"

- **A pagan king's theological observation:** Abimelech had seen Abraham in Egypt (Genesis 12 — he had heard the stories), in Gerar (Genesis 20 — directly experienced the protection of Abraham's God), and now sought a formal covenant because he recognized that Abraham's God was actively engaged with Abraham in everything; this is not a confession of faith but a geopolitical observation translated into theological language; the visible hand of God on Abraham's life was so evident that the pagan saw it
- **The principle:** God's blessing on His covenant people is observable to the world around them; this is not a guarantee of material prosperity theology but a statement about the identifiable divine signature on covenant lives; **Matthew 5:16** — "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven" — the observable blessing principle; Abimelech saw enough of God's working in Abraham to fear Abraham's enemies; the New Testament command is that the light be visible enough for the world to see the Father
- **The same testimony in other covenant contexts:**
  - Genesis 26:28 — Abimelech to Isaac: "We saw certainly that the LORD was with thee" — exact pattern repeated in the next generation
  - Genesis 39:3 — "And his master saw that the LORD was with him, and that the LORD made all that he did to prosper in his hand" — Potiphar observing the divine hand on Joseph
  - Acts 4:13 — "they took knowledge of them, that they had been with Jesus" — the disciples; the observable mark of having been with Christ; the same pattern across both testaments

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### TSACHAQ — LAUGHTER (Hebrew: צָחַק)

**Genesis 21:6** — "God hath made me to laugh (tsachaq)"

- **Root:** Tsachaq = to laugh, to mock, to play, to rejoice — the verb from which Isaac's name (Yitschaq) derives; the name Isaac means "he laughs" or "laughter"; the name encodes the entire emotional arc of the promise
- **The three registers of tsachaq in the Abraham narrative:**
  1. **Wonder-laughter (qal — simple form):** Genesis 17:17 — Abraham "fell upon his face, and laughed" — the posture (prostration) combined with the laughter suggests overwhelmed astonishment rather than mockery or unbelief; Paul's description of Abraham in Romans 4:18-21 as one who was ultimately "fully persuaded" makes this the starting point of wonder rather than the settled point of disbelief
  2. **Doubt-laughter (qal):** Genesis 18:12 — Sarah "laughed within herself" — internal, fear-driven, biological-arithmetic laughter; the content ("after I am waxed old shall I have pleasure?") reveals calculated doubt; God heard it, asked about it, corrected the denial; this is the only form of tsachaq that received divine rebuke in the narrative
  3. **Joy-laughter (causative sense implied):** Genesis 21:6 — "God HATH MADE ME to laugh" — the passive/causative force; Sarah is not the agent of the laughter; God is; the previous laughter arose from her own doubting heart; this laughter was produced by God through fulfilled promise; the source determines the quality
- **The mocking form (piel — intensive):** Genesis 21:9 — Ishmael "mocking" (metsachaq) — the intensive form of the same verb; to play-mock, to laugh-at; the piel often carries the mocking or sensual dimension of tsachaq; Ishmael used the laughter-word against the laughter-child; the son whose name was not laughter mocked the son whose name was laughter; the irony deepens when Galatians 4:29 interprets this as "he that was born after the flesh persecuted him that was born after the Spirit"
- **The national form:** Psalm 126:1-2 — "When the LORD turned again the captivity of Zion, we were like them that dream. Then was our mouth filled with laughter (sechok), and our tongue with singing: then said they among the heathen, The LORD hath done great things for them" — the laughter of Israel's restoration from Babylon mirrors Sarah's personal tsachaq-arc; impossibility → waiting → fulfillment → irrepressible laughter; the nations observed it; God's impossible-made-possible consistently produces laughter that testifies beyond the community of faith
- **The theological arc of Isaac's name:** God named the child after the starting-point emotion (wonder-and-doubt laughter) to declare that He would transform every form of that response — the overwhelmed wonder, the doubting calculation, and the mocking opposition — into the final form: "God hath made me to laugh." Every variety of laughter in the promise-story ended in the same place: testimony. The child's name was the promise of the transformation.

### MOED — APPOINTED TIME (Hebrew: מוֹעֵד)

**Genesis 21:2** — "at the set time (lammoedim) of which God had spoken to him"

- **Root:** From yaad (to appoint, to designate, to fix a meeting time); the moed is the fixed point on God's calendar, the specific intersection of divine intention and temporal reality
- **The moed as sacred-feast vocabulary:** Leviticus 23:2 — "These are the feasts (moedim) of the LORD, even holy convocations, which ye shall proclaim in their seasons" — every sacred feast is a moed; the Passover, Pentecost, Tabernacles — all are moedim; Isaac's birth was announced for a moed (Genesis 17:21 — bammoedim) with the same precision that God announced the feasts; the birth of the covenant child was on the divine feast-calendar level of precision
- **The Passover moed:** Exodus 12:6 — the lamb slain at twilight on the fourteenth of Nisan; Exodus 23:15 — "Thou shalt keep the feast of unleavened bread... in the time appointed (moed) of the month Abib" — to the day, to the month; God's calendar does not shift
- **Daniel's prophetic moedim:** Daniel 8:19 — "the time appointed (moed)" — end-time events are also on the moed calendar; the same divine precision that gave Isaac to Sarah at the moed will fulfill every prophetic declaration at its moed
- **Galatians 4:4** — "when the fulness of the time (to plērōma tou chronou) was come" — the Incarnation was God's ultimate moed; every preparation (the Law, the prophets, the Greco-Roman world's preparation for a universal message, the Pax Romana, the Greek language's spread) converged at the plērōma-moed when God sent His Son; Isaac's birth at the moed was the pattern-event for the Incarnation at the fulness of time
- **Habakkuk 2:3** — "For the vision is yet for an appointed time (moed); but at the end it shall speak, and not lie: though it tarry, wait for it; because it will surely come, it will not tarry" — the command to wait for the moed is the pastoral application of Genesis 21:2; what looks like tarrying to the one inside the waiting is moed-precision from the Author's perspective; Abraham and Sarah waited twenty-five years for the moed of Isaac's birth; the waiting was the appointed path to the appointed time
- *Interpretation:* The moed principle is one of Scripture's most consistently comforting theological structures. God does not issue promises with approximate delivery windows. He issues them with fixed calendar points that will not be adjusted by human unbelief, enemy opposition, or biological impossibility. Isaac was born at the moed. Christ came at the fulness of the moed. The New Jerusalem will descend at the moed. Every covenant promise has its moed, and every moed will arrive exactly as spoken.

### YARESH — HEIR / INHERITANCE (Hebrew: יָרַשׁ)

**Genesis 21:10** — "the son of this bondwoman shall not be heir (yaresh) with my son, even with Isaac"

- **Yaresh** = to possess, to inherit, to take possession of, to dispossess — from a root meaning to take as one's own what belongs to you by right or by grant; it encompasses both the taking of what one is entitled to and the dispossessing of what others held; in the covenant context it is consistently used for the possession of the Promised Land and the inheritance of covenant blessing
- **The heir-question through Genesis:**
  - Genesis 15:2-4 — "I go childless, and the steward of my house is this Eliezer of Damascus... one born in my house is mine heir (yoresh)" → "This shall not be thine heir; but he that shall come forth out of thine own bowels shall be thine heir (yayrash)" — the first heir-declaration; God designated a biological son as the heir, not a servant
  - Genesis 21:10 — Sarah's demand; the son of the bondwoman "shall not be heir (lo yirsah)" with Isaac; the negative form excludes Ishmael from covenant inheritance
  - Genesis 21:12 — God's confirmation: "in Isaac shall thy seed be called" — the positive counterpart to v.10's negative; the covenant-heir is designated, the non-heir is excluded
- **The heir-question resolved in Christ:**
  - Galatians 4:7 — "Wherefore thou art no more a servant, but a son; and if a son, then an heir (klēronomos) of God through Christ" — adoption into sonship creates heirship; the Greek klēronomos (heir) is the New Testament form of Hebrew yaresh; Christ the Son is the heir (Hebrews 1:2 — "whom he hath appointed heir of all things"); those in Christ are joint-heirs (Romans 8:17)
  - Romans 8:17 — "And if children, then heirs (klēronomoi); heirs of God, and joint-heirs (sygklēronomoi) with Christ" — the New Testament expansion of the heir-concept; the inheritance is not Canaan but "all things" (Romans 8:32); not the land between the rivers but the new creation
  - Galatians 3:29 — "And if ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed, and heirs (klēronomoi) according to the promise" — being in Christ = being in Isaac = being the heir; the yaresh question of Genesis is resolved in the klēronomos-reality of being in Christ
- **The heir and the servant contrast:**
  - John 8:35 — "And the servant abideth not in the house for ever: but the Son abideth ever" — Christ directly applied the Ishmael/Isaac principle; the servant (ebed/doulos) has no permanent claim; the son (ben/huios) is the permanent heir; the contrast is the Hagar/Sarah covenant contrast applied to the nature of the relationship with God
  - Galatians 4:1-2 — "Now I say, That the heir (klēronomos), as long as he is a child, differeth nothing from a servant, though he be lord of all" — Paul used the heir/servant tension to describe the Mosaic era as a period of minority; the law was a guardian until the moed of Christ; at the moed of Christ the heir came of age and entered into the full inheritance

### TWO SEEDS — PROMISE-BORN AND FLESH-BORN (Hebrew: zera — זֶרַע)

**Genesis 21:12-13** — "in Isaac shall thy seed (zera) be called... also of the son of the bondwoman will I make a nation, because he is thy seed (zara'acha)"

- **Zera** = seed, offspring, descendant — the biological and covenant-continuity word; used for both the physical seed (offspring) and the specific covenant seed (the Messianic line); the dual use is the source of the interpretive challenge: which sense of "seed" applies in which promise?
- **The two-seeds distinction in the covenant:**
  - Genesis 3:15 — "I will put enmity between thee and the woman, and between thy seed (zaracha) and her seed (zarah)" — the first two-seeds distinction; the serpent's seed and the woman's seed; the conflict established in Eden is the pattern that reappears in Isaac/Ishmael, Jacob/Esau, and ultimately Christ/Satan
  - Genesis 21:12 — "in Isaac shall thy seed (zera) be called" — the covenant-seed narrowed to the promise-born line
  - Genesis 21:13 — "also of the son of the bondwoman will I make a nation, because he is thy seed (zaracha)" — the blessing of the natural-born seed; real, significant, historical; not the covenant-redemptive seed but genuinely Abraham's biological offspring with genuine divine blessing
- **Paul's singular/plural analysis of "seed":** Galatians 3:16 — "Now to Abraham and his seed were the promises made. He saith not, And to seeds, as of many; but as of one, And to thy seed, which is Christ" — Paul's grammatical argument; the singular "seed" in the promises points ultimately to one person — Christ; Galatians 3:29 — "if ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed" — being in Christ = being the singular seed; the many become one in the One
- **Romans 9:6-13 — the two-seeds principle extended:** "They are not all Israel, which are of Israel... the children of the flesh, these are not the children of God: but the children of the promise are counted for the seed. For this is the word of promise... At this time will I come, and Sarah shall have a son. And not only this; but when Rebecca also had conceived by one, even by our father Isaac; (For the children being not yet born, neither having done any good or evil, that the purpose of God according to election might stand, not of works, but of him that calleth)..." — Paul applied the two-seeds principle across two generations (Isaac/Ishmael and Jacob/Esau) to establish the doctrine of election; the common element is that God's choice was prior to any human merit; the promise-child was born by divine intervention, not natural sequence

### BEERSHEBA (Hebrew: בְּאֵר שֶׁבַע)

**Genesis 21:31** — "Wherefore he called that place Beersheba (Be'er Sheva); because there they sware both of them"

- **The dual etymology — two overlapping meanings both present in the narrative:**
  1. **Be'er** (well) + **Sheva** (seven) = "Well of Seven" — the seven ewe lambs (v.28-30) that Abraham set apart as a witness; the number seven embedded in the name as the permanent record of the covenant-transaction
  2. **Be'er** (well) + **Shava** (to swear, to take an oath — from the same root as sheva/seven, since oaths were sealed by seven-fold declarations or seven witnesses) = "Well of the Oath" — the covenant oath between Abraham and Abimelech; both meanings are present simultaneously because sheva (seven) and shava (swear/oath) share the same consonantal root (sh-v-'; שְׁבַע); ancient Hebrew names were frequently chosen for this kind of dual resonance
- **Beersheba through Scripture:**
  - Genesis 21:31-33 — founding by Abraham; covenant with Abimelech; Abraham plants tamarisk and calls on El Olam
  - Genesis 22:19 — Abraham returned to Beersheba after Moriah; covenant ground restored after the ultimate test
  - Genesis 26:23-33 — Isaac re-digs the well and re-names it Beersheba; the covenant inheritance of the name passes to the son
  - Genesis 28:10 — Jacob set out from Beersheba toward Haran; the southern covenant landmark is the departure point for the journey to the north
  - Genesis 46:1-5 — Jacob offered sacrifices at Beersheba on the way to Egypt; God spoke to him there: "I am God, the God of thy father: fear not to go down into Egypt"
  - 1 Samuel 3:20 — "all Israel from Dan even to Beersheba" — Beersheba became the southernmost definition of the Promised Land's extent; the boundary established by Abraham's covenant became the permanent geographical definition of the nation
  - Amos 5:5 — God warned Israel against going to Beersheba for idolatrous worship; the covenant place had become a site of false religion; the place Abraham had named for El Olam was being misused
- *Interpretation:* Beersheba was simultaneously the well, the number, and the oath. Abraham dug the well, counted out the seven lambs, and swore the covenant — and the name of the place encoded all three permanently. Every time a biblical character "dwelt in Beersheba" or "went from Dan to Beersheba" they were standing in the place Abraham had named for the covenant transaction with a pagan king. The place where El Olam was first named became the southern boundary of everything God had promised.

### EL OLAM — THE EVERLASTING GOD (Hebrew: אֵל עוֹלָם)

**Genesis 21:33** — "And Abraham... called there on the name of the LORD, the everlasting God (El Olam)"

- **First occurrence:** This is the first time El Olam appears in Scripture; the name was Abraham's own declaration arising from the conclusion of the Isaac birth narrative; God did not give Himself this name in this passage — Abraham called on God by this name; it was Abraham's theological summary of observed divine faithfulness
- **Olam** = age, eternity, perpetuity — from a root meaning "the horizon" or "what is hidden in the distance"; the time that stretches beyond the horizon of human sight in both directions; me-olam ad-olam (from everlasting to everlasting); Psalm 90:2 — "Before the mountains were brought forth, or ever thou hadst formed the earth and the world, even from everlasting (me-olam) to everlasting (ad-olam), thou art God" — the olam of El Olam spans the entire temporal order in both directions
- **El** = God, the strong one — the same first element as El Shaddai (Genesis 17:1), El Elyon (Genesis 14:18), El Roi (Genesis 16:13); each El-name adds a specific attribute; El Olam adds the temporal-eternal dimension
- **Why this name at Beersheba after Isaac's birth and Ishmael's preservation:** Abraham had watched God fulfill a promise 25 years in the making. He had seen God keep covenant with a pagan king (Abimelech). He had seen his son Ishmael preserved in the wilderness by a God who heard a cry "where he is." He had planted a tree that would outlive him. At this convergence of faithful events — at the covenant landmark he himself had established — he called on the God who was active in all of it by the name that encompassed all of it: the God of all ages, the God beyond the horizon of any single lifetime, the God whose faithfulness is not limited to the years Abraham could observe
- **The name's development through Scripture:**
  - Genesis 21:33 — first use; Abraham's personal theological declaration
  - Isaiah 40:28 — "Hast thou not known? hast thou not heard, that the everlasting God (Elohim Olam), the LORD, the Creator of the ends of the earth, fainteth not, neither is weary?" — the comfort of El Olam to the exilic community; the basis for strength renewal in Isaiah 40:31
  - Isaiah 26:4 — "Trust ye in the LORD for ever: for in the LORD JEHOVAH is everlasting strength (tsur olamim — the Rock of ages)"
  - Romans 16:26 — "the everlasting God (tou aiōniou theou)" — Paul's final doxological use; the God of Genesis 21:33 disclosed in the gospel of Christ to all nations
  - Revelation 4:8 — "Holy, holy, holy, Lord God Almighty, which was, and is, and is to come" — the doxological form of El Olam in Revelation; "which was, and is, and is to come" is the temporal-in-all-directions declaration that El Olam encodes

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

**Genesis 21:2-4** — Born at the exact appointed time; named by God before birth; circumcised on the eighth day

**The type-antitype structure:**

| Isaac (Genesis 21) | Christ (New Testament) |
|---|---|
| Born at the exact moed — "at the set time of which God had spoken" | Born at the fulness of time — "when the fulness of the time was come" — Galatians 4:4 |
| Named by God before conception — "thou shalt call his name Isaac" — Genesis 17:19 | Named by the angel before conception — "thou shalt call his name JESUS" — Luke 1:31 |
| Born of a dead womb — impossible birth | Born of a virgin — impossible birth |
| Circumcised on the eighth day | Circumcised on the eighth day — Luke 2:21 |
| Son of promise, not of works | "Promise... fulfilled unto us their children, in that he hath raised up Jesus again" — Acts 13:32-33 |
| His birth was the result of God's "visiting" Sarah | God "visited and redeemed his people" in Christ — Luke 1:68 |
| Carried wood to the place of sacrifice — Genesis 22:6 | Carried His cross — John 19:17 |

**Genesis 21:10-12** — The expulsion and covenant narrowing

**Paul's interpretation in Galatians 4:21-31:**

| Hagar / Ishmael | Sarah / Isaac |
|---|---|
| Bondwoman (slave) | Freewoman |
| Born after the flesh — natural generation | Born after the Spirit — supernatural promise |
| Corresponds to Mount Sinai — law covenant | Corresponds to Jerusalem above — promise covenant |
| "Gendereth to bondage" — Galatians 4:24 | "Jerusalem which is above is free, which is the mother of us all" — Galatians 4:26 |
| Persecutes the son of promise | The persecuted heir |
| "Cast out" — Galatians 4:30 (quoting Genesis 21:10) | "Shall not be heir with the son of the freewoman" |

| Cannot co-inherit | Inherits alone through promise |

- Galatians 5:4 — "Christ is become of no effect unto you, whosoever of you are justified by the law; ye are fallen from grace" — the theological impossibility of co-inheriting is enacted in Genesis 21:10 and defined in Galatians 5:4; the two covenants cannot be mixed as foundations for justification

**Genesis 21:19** — "God opened her eyes, and she saw a well of water"

- The well was already present before Hagar's eyes were opened; the miracle was not creation of water but revelation of water already provided
- **John 4:10, 14** — "If thou knewest the gift of God... he would have given thee living water... the water that I shall give him shall be in him a well of water springing up into everlasting life" — Christ offered what Hagar found: a well of water in a wilderness; the spiritual well is already given in Christ; the eyes must be opened to receive it
- **John 7:37-39** — "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink. He that believeth on me, as the scripture hath said, out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water. (But this spake he of the Spirit...)" — the Spirit is the living water; already given; already present in Christ; the reception is the opened eye of faith
- **Isaiah 43:19-20** — "I will make a way in the wilderness, and rivers in the desert... to give drink to my people, my chosen" — Isaiah's wilderness-water promise; the covenant people in the wilderness of exile would receive what Hagar received; the God who showed Hagar the well shows His people the rivers

### "God Was with the Lad" — Common Grace Sustaining the Non-Covenant Line

**Genesis 21:20** — "And God was with the lad; and he grew, and dwelt in the wilderness, and became an archer"

- God's presence with Ishmael (not the covenant presence of election but the presence of care and enabling) produced a fully-formed historical people; the twelve princes of Genesis 25:12-18 were the fulfillment of Genesis 17:20 and 21:18; the nations that trace through Ishmael are real nations sustained by real divine common grace
- **Matthew 5:45** — "He maketh his sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust" — the God who was with the lad Ishmael sends His sun on all; common grace is real grace; it is not redemptive grace; both are genuine divine activities toward different ends
- **Acts 17:26-27** — "And hath made of one blood all nations of men for to dwell on all the face of the earth, and hath determined the times before appointed, and the bounds of their habitation; That they should seek the Lord" — God determined the habitations of all nations, including the nations that trace through Ishmael; His sovereign hand is over all national histories, even those outside the covenant line

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "In Isaac Shall Thy Seed Be Called" — All Physical Descendants of Abraham Are Covenant Heirs

**The Misuse:** Genesis 21:12 — "in Isaac shall thy seed be called" — is read as meaning that all physical descendants of Isaac (the Jewish people) are automatically heirs of the covenant promises; biological descent from Abraham through Isaac guarantees covenant standing.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Romans 9:6-8** — "For they are not all Israel, which are of Israel: Neither, because they are the seed of Abraham, are they all children: but, In Isaac shall thy seed be called. That is, They which are the children of the flesh, these are not the children of God: but the children of the promise are counted for the seed" — Paul's direct use of Genesis 21:12 is the canonical interpretation; "in Isaac shall thy seed be called" is Paul's proof that biological descent from Abraham does not constitute covenant heirs; the children of the flesh (all biological Abrahamites including Ishmael) are distinguished from "the children of the promise" who alone are counted for the seed
- **Romans 9:9-13** — Paul immediately extends the principle to Jacob/Esau (both sons of Isaac) to show that even being physically descended from Isaac is insufficient; God's election was prior to birth and prior to any works; the covenant narrowing continues beyond the Isaac generation
- **Galatians 3:29** — "And if ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise" — the positive statement: being in Christ constitutes being Abraham's seed and an heir of the promise; this opens the promise to Gentiles who trust Christ and demonstrates that the "seed" category is promise-based, not biology-based
- **John 8:39-40** — Christ to those who claimed "Abraham is our father": "If ye were Abraham's children, ye would do the works of Abraham. But now ye seek to kill me, a man that hath told you the truth... this did not Abraham" — Jesus Himself distinguished between biological descent from Abraham and being "Abraham's children" in the covenant sense; the distinguishing mark was doing the works of Abraham (faith, Hebrews 11:8-19), not biological origin
- *Correction from Scripture:* "In Isaac shall thy seed be called" narrows the covenant line, it does not automatically include all biological descendants of that line. Paul's inspired interpretation in Romans 9:6-8 is authoritative: the children of the promise (those born by divine supernatural initiative, not human biology alone) are the true seed. In the New Covenant this means those in Christ — Jew or Gentile — are Abraham's heirs by promise.

### "Cast Out the Bondwoman" — Christians Should Reject the Old Testament Law Entirely

**The Misuse:** Galatians 4:30's quotation of Genesis 21:10 — "Cast out the bondwoman and her son" — is used to argue that the entire Old Testament (including the Law, the Mosaic commands, and the Hebrew Scriptures) should be "cast out" by New Covenant believers; the Hagar-covenant is equated with the entire Old Testament.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Paul's specific argument in Galatians 4:** The "Hagar covenant" Paul identified was not the entire Old Testament but specifically the covenant of law-keeping as a BASIS FOR JUSTIFICATION; the Judaizing teachers in Galatia were teaching that circumcision and law-observance were necessary for salvation (Galatians 2:16; 5:4); Paul's "cast out the bondwoman" was directed at that specific false gospel, not at the Hebrew Scriptures as a whole
- **Romans 3:31** — "Do we then make void the law through faith? God forbid: yea, we establish the law" — Paul, who wrote Galatians, also wrote Romans 3:31; faith does not abolish the law; it establishes the law's proper function (as a mirror revealing sin — Romans 3:20 and as the pattern that Christ fulfilled — Matthew 5:17)
- **Matthew 5:17-18** — "Think not that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets: I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil. For verily I say unto you, Till heaven and earth pass, one jot or one tittle shall in no wise pass from the law, till all be fulfilled" — Christ's own declaration; the law is not abolished; it is fulfilled; the "Hagar" being cast out is the use of the law as a righteousness-earning mechanism, not the law as God's revealed moral character
- **2 Timothy 3:16-17** — "All scripture is given by inspiration of God, and is profitable for doctrine, for reproof, for correction, for instruction in righteousness" — Paul wrote this about the Old Testament Scriptures (the New Testament was still being written); the Hebrew Scriptures are God-breathed and profitable; they are not the "bondwoman" to be cast out
- *Correction from Scripture:* Galatians 4:30's "cast out the bondwoman" is directed at the specific error of seeking justification through law-works as a supplement to or substitute for faith in Christ. Paul's Hagar-covenant is not the Hebrew Scriptures but the religious system that turned the law from a pedagogue pointing to Christ (Galatians 3:24) into the basis of righteousness. The Old Testament is foundational Scripture; the Hagar-covenant is the misuse of that Scripture.

### "God Heard the Voice of the Lad" — God Hears Everyone Therefore All Are Saved

**The Misuse:** Genesis 21:17's "God heard the voice of the lad where he is" is used to argue that God's hearing of Ishmael's cry demonstrates universal salvation or that the distinction between covenant and non-covenant is irrelevant; God hears everyone therefore everyone is equally in His care and eternally secure.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The distinction between hearing and electing is explicit in the same context:** v.12 — "in Isaac shall thy seed be called" establishes that the covenant narrowed through Isaac, not Ishmael; v.17 — "God heard the voice of the lad" demonstrates God's care for Ishmael; both verses are true simultaneously and they represent different categories of divine action: covenant election and covenant common grace
- **God's hearing of a cry is not equivalent to covenant inclusion:** Psalm 145:9 — "The LORD is good to all: and his tender mercies are over all his works" — God's general goodness and His specific covenant grace are both real and distinguishable; the same God who hears the cry of all (Psalm 145:9) also distinguishes between those in His redemptive covenant and those outside it (Romans 9:6-8)
- **Acts 17:30** — "the times of this ignorance God winked at; but now commandeth all men every where to repent" — God's patience and care for all nations does not remove the universal command to repent; the universal hearing does not cancel the universal accountability
- **Romans 2:11-16** — God's impartial hearing and judging of all men confirms that all are accountable, not that all are saved; hearing and responding to a cry of distress is an act of mercy; it does not constitute redemptive election
- *Correction from Scripture:* God's hearing of Ishmael's cry in the wilderness is an act of divine mercy and common grace — real, genuine, and significant. It is not equivalent to covenant election, the forgiveness of sins, or eternal salvation. The Bible consistently distinguishes between God's care for all creation (including those outside the covenant) and His redemptive covenant with those who are in Christ. Genesis 21:17 demonstrates the former; Romans 8:29-30 defines the latter.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Laughter Arc — From Doubt to Joy to Mockery to Testimony

**Genesis 17:17 → 18:12-15 → 21:6 → 21:9 → Psalm 126:2**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 17:17 — Abraham's astonished wonder-laughter; fell on his face before it; no rebuke
- Genesis 18:12 — Sarah's internal doubt-laughter; biological calculation displacing faith; gently rebuked; denial corrected
- Genesis 21:6 — Sarah's joy-laughter at fulfillment; "God hath MADE me to laugh" — passive, God-produced
- Genesis 21:9 — Ishmael's mocking-laughter (metsachaq); the flesh using the laughter-name against the laughter-child
- Psalm 126:1-2 — national restoration produces national laughter; "our mouth filled with laughter"
- Luke 6:21 — "Blessed are ye that weep now: for ye shall laugh" — Christ's beatitude; the weeping that precedes the laughter-of-fulfillment; Sarah's arc made universal principle
- Revelation 21:4 — "God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain" — the final form of the laughter-arc; every Isaac-pattern of impossible-promise-to-fulfilled-joy reaches its permanent expression

### The Moed — God's Calendar of Fulfillment

**Genesis 21:2 — "at the set time (moed) of which God had spoken"**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 17:21 / 18:14 — Isaac's birth announced for a moed
- Genesis 21:2 — Isaac born at the moed exactly
- Exodus 12:6 / 23:15 — Passover lamb slain at the moed; the sacred feasts are all moedim
- Daniel 8:19; 11:27, 29, 35 — end-time events each at their moed
- Galatians 4:4 — the Incarnation at the fulness of time; the ultimate moed
- Acts 1:7 — the Parousia at the Father's appointed time (kairos)
- Revelation 11:18 — "the time of the dead, that they should be judged" — judgment at the moed
- The entire biblical meta-narrative is a series of moedim, each arriving exactly as spoken

---

### Opening Eyes to See — Provision Already Present

**Genesis 21:19 — "God opened her eyes, and she saw a well of water"**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 21:19 — Hagar's eyes opened to the well in the wilderness
- Numbers 22:31 — "Then the LORD opened the eyes of Balaam, and he saw the angel of the LORD standing in the way" — Balaam's eyes opened to what was already present and blocking his path
- 2 Kings 6:17 — "And Elisha prayed, and said, LORD, I pray thee, open his eyes, that he may see. And the LORD opened the eyes of the young man; and he saw: and, behold, the mountain was full of horses and chariots of fire" — the angelic host was already there; the eyes were opened to see it
- Luke 24:31 — the Emmaus disciples: "their eyes were opened, and they knew him" — Christ had been present throughout the journey; the opening of eyes revealed the presence
- Acts 9:8; 9:17-18 — Paul's eyes closed by the encounter with Christ and then opened again; the opening of eyes to divine reality is the turning point
- Ephesians 1:18 — "The eyes of your understanding being enlightened; that ye may know what is the hope of his calling" — Paul's prayer; the provision of hope, inheritance, and power is already present in Christ; the prayer is for opened eyes to see it
- Revelation 3:18 — "anoint thine eyes with eyesalve, that thou mayest see" — Christ's counsel to Laodicea; the problem was not absence of provision but inability to see it

### El Olam and the Covenant Names of God — Cumulative Self-Disclosure

**Genesis 21:33 — "El Olam" — the Everlasting God — first use**

**Thread of the El-names in the Abraham narrative:**
- Genesis 14:18-20 — El Elyon (God Most High) — revealed through Melchizedek at the victory celebration; God as sovereign over all nations
- Genesis 16:13 — El Roi (the God Who Sees) — revealed to Hagar in her first wilderness crisis; God as the intimate observer of the unseen
- Genesis 17:1 — El Shaddai (God Almighty / All-Sufficient) — the God of impossible conception; sufficient where human resources end
- Genesis 21:33 — El Olam (the Everlasting God) — the God of all ages; sufficient not just for one impossible moment but for all time
- Exodus 3:14 — I AM THAT I AM (YHWH) — the full self-disclosure at the burning bush; the God who IS encompasses all the El-names
- Isaiah 9:6 — Wonderful, Counsellor, The mighty God, The everlasting Father, The Prince of Peace — the Messianic name that incorporates El Olam ("everlasting Father") in the Incarnation
- Revelation 1:8 — "I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty" — the final form; the Pantokrator who was, is, and is to come is the El Olam revealed completely in Christ

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Recorded Exact Fulfillment of a Time-Specific Covenant Promise

**Genesis 21:1-2** — "as he had said... as he had spoken... at the set time of which God had spoken to him"
- The first time a promise announced with a specific time-marker was fulfilled and the text explicitly connected the fulfillment to the prior announcement
- First mention establishes: God's promises are timed events; the fulfillment-to-promise connection is documented by the narrator; the theological principle — God's word arrives at the exact appointment, not before and not after — is demonstrated before being applied to every subsequent promise-fulfillment in Scripture

### First Use of El Olam as a Divine Name

**Genesis 21:33** — "and called there on the name of the LORD, the everlasting God (El Olam)"
- The first and only use of El Olam in the patriarchal narrative; a name arising from Abraham's personal theological reflection on observed covenant faithfulness
- First mention establishes: the God who keeps promises across twenty-five years of human waiting is the Everlasting God; the name encompasses the full temporal span of every unfulfilled promise; Isaiah 40:28 and Romans 16:26 develop what Genesis 21:33 first names

### First Record of Joy-Laughter at Covenant Fulfillment

**Genesis 21:6** — "God hath made me to laugh, so that all that hear will laugh with me"
- The first time laughter arising from covenant fulfillment was recorded as testimony
- First mention establishes: the God who keeps impossible promises produces a specific quality of joy — the laughter that cannot be suppressed and invites others into its testimony; Psalm 126:2 and Luke 6:21 develop this as the pattern for all eschatological reversal-joy

### First Use of "God Was with the Lad" — Divine Presence with the Non-Covenant Line

**Genesis 21:20** — "And God was with the lad"
- The first time Scripture explicitly stated God's presence with someone outside the designated covenant line
- First mention establishes: common grace (God's enabling, sustaining, blessing presence) extends beyond the boundaries of redemptive covenant; God was with Ishmael in the wilderness while the covenant was narrowing through Isaac; both realities coexist; Genesis 26:3, 28 and Genesis 39:2-3 repeat "God was with" for Isaac and Joseph — but the first use is with Ishmael, establishing that the phrase is not exclusively reserved for covenant persons

### First Planting of a Tree as an Act of Covenant Faith in Future Possession

**Genesis 21:33** — "And Abraham planted a grove (tamarisk tree) in Beersheba"
- The first time a patriarch planted a tree in the Promised Land as a deliberate act of long-term faith
- First mention establishes: faith in promised possession acts for a future beyond the planter's lifetime; the tamarisk is slow-growing and long-lived; Abraham planted what he would not see mature because the El Olam would outlast the tree; the planting was faith in the covenant, not in the planter's lifespan

## NAME MEANINGS

| Name | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Isaac (Yitschaq) | He laughs / Laughter | The only person in Scripture whose name was spoken by God to his parents before conception (Genesis 17:19); the name encoded the entire emotional arc of the promise from astonished doubt to fulfilled joy; every time his name was spoken for the rest of Scripture it proclaimed: God turned the impossible into testimony |
| Ishmael (Yishma'el) | God hears / God will hear | The name given in Genesis 16:11 ("the LORD hath heard thy affliction") was enacted again in Genesis 21:17 ("God hath heard the voice of the lad where he is"); his name was a twice-fulfilled prophecy; the God who named him "God hears" demonstrated the name's meaning twice — in the wilderness of Shur and in the wilderness of Beersheba |
| Hagar | Stranger / Flight / Sojourner | Her name described her status (a foreigner, a servant) and her experience (twice she fled to the wilderness); the stranger became the mother of nations; Genesis 16:13 and 21:19 show God attending to the stranger's need precisely because He is El Roi and El Shaddai |
| Beersheba (Be'er Sheva) | Well of Seven / Well of the Oath | The dual etymology (sheva = seven; shava = to swear) encoding both the number of the witness-lambs and the covenant oath into the place-name; became the southernmost geographic boundary of the Promised Land; "from Dan to Beersheba" = the full extent of Israel's covenant territory |
| El Olam | Everlasting God / God of the Ages | Abraham's own declaration; the first use in Scripture; arising from the convergence of Isaac's birth, Ishmael's preservation, and the Beersheba covenant; the theological conclusion drawn from observed faithfulness across decades; developed in Isaiah 40:28, Romans 16:26, and implicitly in Revelation 1:8 |
| Paran | Place of Caverns / Abounding in Foliage | The wilderness region where Ishmael settled and grew; later the staging ground for Israel's journey in Numbers 10:12; the wilderness that Ishmael turned into a homeland is the same wilderness where Moses would later govern a nation — the geography of the non-covenant became the geography of the covenant journey |
| Phichol | Mouth of All / Strong | The title of Abimelech's military commander; his presence with Abimelech in Genesis 21:22 and Genesis 26:26 suggests he was either very long-lived or "Phichol" was a title like "Abimelech" rather than a personal name; his presence at the covenant marked the formal military-political character of the agreement |
