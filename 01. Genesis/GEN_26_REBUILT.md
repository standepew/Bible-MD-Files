## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 26 is the only chapter in Genesis dedicated exclusively to Isaac. It is a chapter of repetitions — the famine, the "she is my sister" deception, the Abimelech encounter, the wells, the Beersheba covenant — all of which echo Abraham's story. The repetitions are not careless; they are theologically structural. Isaac walked in his father's footsteps in both obedience and failure, and the covenant that came to him came because of who his father was and what his father had done.

A famine struck the land — described as distinct from "the first famine that was in the days of Abraham." Isaac moved toward Gerar and toward Egypt. The LORD appeared and explicitly redirected him: "Go not down into Egypt; dwell in the land which I shall tell thee of." God then gave Isaac the fullest direct statement of the Abrahamic covenant he would receive — the land, the seed multiplied as the stars of heaven, all nations blessed through his seed — and grounded every element of this promise in a single reason: "because that Abraham obeyed my voice, and kept my charge, my commandments, my statutes, and my laws." The obedience of the father was the stated basis for the son's inheritance.

Isaac settled in Gerar. Fearing for his life, he called Rebekah his sister — the same deception his father had used twice, with a worse version: at least Abraham's claim had a partial truth (Sarah was his half-sister); Isaac's claim was a pure lie. Abimelech discovered the truth when he looked out his window and saw Isaac and Rebekah together in a way that made their relationship unmistakable. He rebuked Isaac justly: "What is this thou hast done unto us? one of the people might lightly have lien with thy wife, and thou shouldest have brought guiltiness upon us." He issued a protective decree.

The LORD then blessed Isaac with a hundredfold harvest in famine. He became so great that the Philistines envied him, stopped up all the wells Abraham's servants had dug, and Abimelech asked him to leave: "Go from us; for thou art much mightier than we." Isaac moved to the valley of Gerar and began re-digging Abraham's stopped wells — restoring the names his father had given them. The first new well was contested (Esek — strife). The second was contested (Sitnah — opposition; the same root as satan). At the third there was no striving; he named it Rehoboth: "For now the LORD hath made room for us, and we shall be fruitful in the land."

He went to Beersheba. That same night the LORD appeared: "I am the God of Abraham thy father: fear not, for I am with thee, and will bless thee, and multiply thy seed for my servant Abraham's sake." Isaac built an altar, called on the name of the LORD, and pitched his tent. His servants found water. Then Abimelech came from Gerar — with Phichol his army commander and Ahuzzath his friend — to make a covenant. Isaac confronted them directly: "Wherefore come ye to me, seeing ye hate me, and have sent me away from you?" Their answer was the testimony the entire chapter had been building toward: "We saw certainly that the LORD was with thee." They made a feast, swore an oath, and departed in peace. Isaac's servants reported water found at the new well; he named it Shebah — oath — and the city was called Beersheba.

The chapter ends with a jarring note: Esau at forty married two Hittite women — Judith daughter of Beeri and Bashemath daughter of Elon — "which were a grief of mind unto Isaac and to Rebekah." The man who sold his birthright now violated the covenant marriage standard, doing the very thing Abraham had sworn Isaac would never do.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Go Not Down Into Egypt" — Staying in the Land as the Test of Faith

**Genesis 26:2** — "And the LORD appeared unto him, and said, Go not down into Egypt; dwell in the land which I shall tell thee of"

- **Egypt as the recurring alternative to trusting God in the covenant land:**
  - Genesis 12:10 — "And there was a famine in the land: and Abram went down into Egypt to sojourn there; for the famine was grievous in the land" — Abraham's default when the land failed to provide; the Egypt-trip produced Hagar, which produced Ishmael, which produced the conflict of the two seeds; Egypt's "provision" came with consequences that ran for generations
  - Genesis 26:2 — Isaac begins moving toward Egypt; God stopped him explicitly; this was the test: would Isaac repeat his father's choice, or would he trust God in the land? God's instruction was specific — not just "don't go to Egypt" but "dwell in the land which I shall tell thee of"; the staying was itself an act of faith, not a passive default
  - Numbers 14:3-4 — Israel in the wilderness: "And wherefore hath the LORD brought us unto this land, to fall by the sword... were it not better for us to return into Egypt? And they said one to another, Let us make a captain, and let us return into Egypt" — the Egypt-instinct of fallen faith; when the land of promise seems dangerous or scarce, the heart returns to the place of old provision rather than trusting the God of new promise
  - Isaiah 31:1-3 — "Woe to them that go down to Egypt for help; and stay on horses, and trust in chariots... But they look not unto the Holy One of Israel, neither seek the LORD... Now the Egyptians are men, and not God; and their horses flesh, and not spirit" — the theological definition of what Egypt represents in every generation: the alternative of human strength, human provision, human security, chosen over the word of God; "they look not unto the Holy One of Israel, neither seek the LORD" is the spiritual diagnosis; to go to Egypt is to stop seeking God
  - Hosea 11:1-2 — "When Israel was a child, then I loved him, and called my son out of Egypt. As they called them, so they went from them" — Egypt is where God's people go into bondage before being called out; the calling out of Egypt is the redemption pattern (Matthew 2:15 applied Hosea 11:1 to Christ's return from Egypt in the infancy narrative — the Son of God recapitulated the exodus-pattern)
- **The theology of "staying in the land" as faith:** The command to Isaac was not just geographic — it was theological; staying in a famine-stricken land on the word of God is an act of faith that going to Egypt in a famine-stricken land could never be; the test was whether Isaac would obey the specific word given ("dwell in the land which I shall tell thee of") or would make a reasonable-looking human calculation; Isaac passed the test; he stayed; and the LORD blessed him a hundredfold
- **Philippians 4:11-12** — "I have learned, in whatsoever state I am, therewith to be content. I know both how to be abased, and I know how to abound" — contentment in the land of present assignment, however difficult, rather than seeking the more comfortable alternative, is the New Testament form of Isaac's obedience at Gerar

### The Covenant Reconfirmed — "Because Abraham Obeyed My Voice"

**Genesis 26:3-5** — "Sojourn in this land, and I will be with thee, and will bless thee; for unto thee, and unto thy seed, I will give all these countries, and I will perform the oath which I sware unto Abraham thy father; And I will make thy seed to multiply as the stars of heaven, and will give unto thy seed all these countries; and in thy seed shall all the nations of the earth be blessed; Because that Abraham obeyed my voice, and kept my charge, my commandments, my statutes, and my laws"

- **The three covenant elements are reproduced exactly:**
  1. The land — "unto thee, and unto thy seed, I will give all these countries"
  2. The seed — "I will make thy seed to multiply as the stars of heaven"
  3. The nations — "in thy seed shall all the nations of the earth be blessed"
  - These are the same three elements first given in Genesis 12:1-3, sworn by oath in Genesis 22:16-18, and now confirmed to Isaac; the covenant was not renegotiated — it was performed (the Hebrew: "I will perform the oath" — haqimothi = I will cause to stand, I will establish, I will fulfill)
- **"Because Abraham obeyed my voice":** This is the only place in Genesis where the reason for the covenant's continuation is stated in terms of the previous generation's obedience; God did not give the covenant to Isaac because Isaac had earned it — Isaac had not yet done anything; the grounding was in Abraham's covenant faithfulness; this established the principle that a parent's covenant obedience creates a heritage for the children — not automatic salvation, but a covenant environment, a covering, a set of promises that the next generation inherits and into which they are born
- **The vocabulary of Abraham's obedience is comprehensive:** v.5 — "obeyed my voice... kept my charge... my commandments... my statutes... and my laws" — five terms stacked: voice (qol), charge (mishmereth), commandments (mitsvoth), statutes (chuqqoth), laws (torot — plural of torah); this is the language of complete covenant obedience; the five terms together describe the totality of God's revealed will; Abraham's keeping of what God had not yet formally codified at Sinai established that the heart of the law was not the Mosaic form but the covenant reality that preceded it
- **Galatians 3:16-17** — "Now to Abraham and his seed were the promises made. He saith not, And to seeds, as of many; but as of one, And to thy seed, which is Christ. And this I say, that the covenant, that was confirmed before of God in Christ, can not be disannulled by the law, which was four hundred and thirty years after" — Paul's theological use of the Genesis covenant-to-Isaac structure: the covenant was confirmed across generations and was unbreakable; it was confirmed "before of God in Christ" — the covenant always had Christ as its ultimate ground and goal; the reconfirmation to Isaac pointed forward to the one Seed in whom all the promises stood
- **The theological principle of covenant inheritance:** Deuteronomy 7:9 — "Know therefore that the LORD thy God, he is God, the faithful God, which keepeth covenant and mercy with them that love him and keep his commandments to a thousand generations" — a thousand generations; the faithfulness of one covenant person does not expire at their death; it creates a covenant lineage that God honors through subsequent generations; this is not works-salvation transferred to descendants but the consistent biblical testimony that God's covenant faithfulness responds to and builds upon covenant faithfulness in the family line

### "She Is My Sister" — The Father's Sin Repeated Worse

**Genesis 26:7** — "And the men of the place asked him of his wife; and he said, She is my sister: for he feared to say, She is my wife; lest, said he, the men of the place should kill me for Rebekah; because she was fair to look upon"

- **The progressive worsening of the pattern:**
  - Genesis 12:11-13 — Abraham to Sarai: "Say, I pray thee, thou art my sister: that it may be well with me for thy sake" — there was a partial truth: Sarai was his half-sister (Genesis 20:12); the deception was built on a genuine kinship relation
  - Genesis 20:2 — Abraham again called Sarah his sister before Abimelech; same location, same king, same deception; at least the partial truth remained
  - Genesis 26:7 — Isaac called Rebekah his sister; Rebekah was not his sister in any sense — she was his cousin (Genesis 24:29, 47); the deception had no partial-truth basis at all; the lie was complete
  - The escalation is significant: the sin worsened with each generation and each repetition; the covenant son was not morally superior to his covenant father; the same fear, the same failure, the same location (Gerar), the same Abimelech
- **Abimelech's discovery — the sporting:** v.8 — "Abimelech king of the Philistines looked out at a window, and saw, and, behold, Isaac was sporting with Rebekah his wife" — the Hebrew metsacheq = laughing, playing, caressing; the root of Isaac's own name (Yitschaq — he laughs); the play on words is intentional: Isaac (the one who laughs) was found laughing/playing in a way that made the marriage unmistakable; the very nature encoded in his name revealed the truth
- **Abimelech's rebuke — the pagan king as the moral voice:** v.9-10 — "And Abimelech called Isaac, and said, Behold, of a surety she is thy wife: and how saidst thou, She is my sister?... What is this thou hast done unto us? one of the people might lightly have lien with thy wife, and thou shouldest have brought guiltiness upon us" — Abimelech's rebuke is the text's moral verdict; the narrator did not editorially condemn Isaac — he let Abimelech do it; the pagan king's ethical clarity contrasted with the covenant patriarch's fear-driven deception; the Scripture recorded the rebuke without softening it
- **The pattern in Scripture — pagan rebukes of covenant people:**
  - Pharaoh rebuked Abraham (Genesis 12:18-19): "Why didst thou not tell me that she was thy wife? Why saidst thou, She is my sister? so I might have taken her to me to wife: now therefore behold thy wife, take her, and go thy way"
  - Abimelech rebuked Abraham (Genesis 20:9-10): "What hast thou done unto us? and what have I offended thee, that thou hast brought on me and on my kingdom a great sin?"
  - Abimelech rebuked Isaac (Genesis 26:9-10): "What is this thou hast done unto us?"
  - In each case, the pagan ruler's rebuke stood as the narrative's ethical judgment; God uses the outsider to confront the insider; the covenant person was not protected from just rebuke for covenant failures
- **Exodus 20:16** — "Thou shalt not bear false witness against thy neighbour" — the principle that makes the deception a sin; **Proverbs 12:22** — "Lying lips are abomination to the LORD: but they that deal truly are his delight" — the divine assessment of deceptive speech; Isaac's sin was not endorsed by the narrative's recording of it; the text shows consequences nearly visiting Abimelech's household and the just rebuke that followed

### The Hundredfold Harvest in Famine — Covenant Fruitfulness in Impossible Conditions

**Genesis 26:12-14** — "Then Isaac sowed in that land, and received in the same year an hundredfold: and the LORD blessed him. And the man waxed great, and went forward, and grew until he became very great: For he had possession of flocks, and possession of herds, and great store of servants: and the Philistines envied him"

- **The extraordinary nature of the hundredfold:** A normal good harvest in the ancient Near East was a sevenfold to tenfold return on seed planted; twentyfold was exceptional; a hundredfold in a year of famine was supernatural; it was not the product of Isaac's superior farming technique — it was the LORD blessing him in a context where natural conditions would have made such a return impossible
- **The threefold escalation of language:** v.13 — "the man waxed great (gadal), and went forward (halok), and grew until he became very great (gadol me'od)" — the Hebrew progression: great... going forward and growing... very great; the language described an ongoing, compounding increase that was visible and continuous; his greatness was not sudden but accumulating; it was conspicuous over time
- **Matthew 13:8, 23** — "Other fell into good ground, and brought forth fruit, some an hundredfold, some sixtyfold, some thirtyfold... He that received seed into the good ground is he that heareth the word, and understandeth it; which also beareth fruit, and bringeth forth, some an hundredfold, some sixty, some thirty" — Jesus in the parable of the sower used the hundredfold as the maximum measure of fruitfulness in the best possible soil; the connection between Isaac's hundred-fold in famine and the parable's hundredfold is deliberate: the seed of the Word planted in the good ground of faith produces covenant fruitfulness regardless of the external conditions of the world; the famine was the world's condition; the hundredfold was God's condition imposed on God's man
- **The envy of the Philistines as the consequence:** v.14 — "the Philistines envied him" — the Hebrew qana = to be jealous, to envy, to be intensely desirous of what another has; the very blessing that God gave became the source of the world's hostility; this is the consistent biblical pattern: when God visibly blesses His covenant people, the world around them responds with envy, which produces persecution; the blessing was not given secretly or privately but accumulated publicly until it was unmistakable; and the unmistakable blessing produced unmistakable opposition
- **John 15:18-19** — "If the world hate you, ye know that it hated me before it hated you. If ye were of the world, the world would love his own: but because ye are not of the world, but I have chosen you out of the world, therefore the world hateth you" — Christ's explanation of the Isaac-pattern; the world does not hate what is part of it; it hates what is visibly other, visibly blessed in a way that the world cannot produce or explain; Isaac's hundredfold in famine made him "not of the world" in the only way the Philistines could perceive, and they responded accordingly

### The Three Wells — Esek, Sitnah, Rehoboth

**Genesis 26:20-22** — the progression from contested wells to the uncontested well of enlargement

- **Esek — the first well:** v.20 — "And the herdmen of Gerar did strive with Isaac's herdmen, saying, The water is ours: and he called the name of the well Esek (strife); because they strove with him" — Esek = contention, striving; from asaq (to press upon, to oppress, to quarrel); the first response to the covenant person recovering the covenant inheritance was direct physical strife; the world contended for the well
- **Sitnah — the second well:** v.21 — "And they digged another well, and strove for that also: and he called the name of it Sitnah" — Sitnah = opposition, enmity, accusation; the root is the same as satan (the adversary, the accuser); the hostility of the second well was not merely natural contention but adversarial, accusing opposition — the spiritual dimension of the conflict was encoded in the name; Isaac did not fight for the well; he named it truthfully and moved
- **The pattern of naming without fighting:** Isaac named both contested wells for what they were (Esek = strife; Sitnah = satanic opposition) but did not fight to retain them; he moved; this was not passivity but strategic covenant-walking; the wells that were contested were let go; the energy was redirected to digging again; the approach was: name the opposition truthfully, do not give it your life, keep moving in the direction God has set
- **Rehoboth — the third well:** v.22 — "And he removed from thence, and digged another well; and for that they strove not: and he called the name of it Rehoboth; and he said, For now the LORD hath made room for us, and we shall be fruitful in the land" — Rehoboth = wide places, room; the same root as rechov (broad place, plaza, open space); from rachav (to be wide, to be spacious, to enlarge); Isaac's declaration was theological: "the LORD hath made room" — not "we found a better location" or "they gave up"; the enlargement was attributed entirely to God
- **The three-stage spiritual map:**
  - Esek (strife) = natural opposition from the world system; the world contests what belongs to the covenant person
  - Sitnah (satanic opposition) = spiritual-level adversarial accusation; the opposition escalates beyond natural contention to spiritually charged enmity
  - Rehoboth (God making room) = the breakthrough that comes not from warfare but from persistent obedience and movement; God provides the enlargement after and through the opposition
- **Psalm 31:8** — "Thou hast not shut me up into the hand of the enemy: thou hast set my feet in a large room (merchav)" — David's celebration of what Isaac experienced at Rehoboth; the same word (rachav-root) for the large room/wide place God provides; **Psalm 118:5** — "I called upon the LORD in distress: the LORD answered me, and set me in a large place (merchav)" — the same structure: distress → call → divine enlargement; **2 Samuel 22:20** — "He brought me forth also into a large place (merchav); he delivered me, because he delighted in me" — the pattern of Rehoboth is the pattern of deliverance throughout the Psalms: God sets His people in wide open places after the strife and opposition
- **Isaiah 54:2-3** — "Enlarge the place of thy tent, and let them stretch forth the curtains of thine habitations: spare not, lengthen thy cords, and strengthen thy stakes; For thou shalt break forth on the right hand and on the left; and thy seed shall inherit the Gentiles, and make the desolate cities to be inhabited" — the Rehoboth-principle applied eschatologically: after the opposition (the barrenness and desolation of v.1 — "sing, O barren"), God commands enlargement, and the enlargement exceeds the original boundaries; the Rehoboth of Isaac was a type of the eschatological enlargement of the covenant people

### God Appears at Beersheba — "Fear Not, I Am with Thee"

**Genesis 26:24** — "And the LORD appeared unto him the same night, and said, I am the God of Abraham thy father: fear not, for I am with thee, and will bless thee, and multiply thy seed for my servant Abraham's sake"

- **The timing — "the same night":** The divine appearance came on the night of Isaac's arrival at Beersheba — immediately after the strife of the wells, immediately after the expulsion from Gerar; God did not wait for Isaac to build the altar first; He appeared before Isaac had done anything at Beersheba; the appearance was God's initiative, not the reward for Isaac's worship
- **"I am the God of Abraham thy father":** The self-identification was relational and genealogical — God anchored His appearance in the covenant relationship established with the previous generation; He was not introducing Himself to Isaac as a new deity but confirming the continuity of the covenant God: the same God who appeared to Abraham was now appearing to Isaac; the covenant was generationally continuous
- **"Fear not" — the recurring covenant word at moments of pressure:**
  - Genesis 15:1 — "Fear not, Abram: I am thy shield" — after the battle of the kings; at the point of vulnerability; the first "fear not" to a patriarch
  - Genesis 21:17 — "Fear not; for God hath heard the voice of the lad" — to Hagar; at the point of death in the wilderness; the "fear not" crossed covenant boundaries
  - Genesis 26:24 — "Fear not, for I am with thee" — to Isaac; after expulsion and contested wells
  - Genesis 46:3 — "Fear not to go down into Egypt; for I will there make of thee a great nation" — to Jacob; at the point of the great departure
  - Isaiah 41:10 — "Fear thou not; for I am with thee: be not dismayed; for I am thy God: I will strengthen thee; yea, I will help thee; yea, I will uphold thee with the right hand of my righteousness" — the Isaian development of the "fear not" pattern; every element of Genesis 26:24 was expanded by Isaiah: "I am with thee" → "I am thy God... I will strengthen thee... I will help thee... I will uphold thee"
  - Isaiah 43:1 — "Fear not: for I have redeemed thee, I have called thee by thy name; thou art mine" — the deepest form of the "fear not": the ground is not merely presence but redemption and naming; the covenant person belongs to God; this makes fear irrational because the one who fears belongs to the one who removes all grounds for fear
  - 2 Timothy 1:7 — "For God hath not given us the spirit of fear; but of power, and of love, and of a sound mind" — the New Testament theological declaration; the spirit of fear is not from God; the "fear not" of Genesis 26:24 was the patriarchal form of what Paul stated as a settled theological truth about the Spirit's character
- **Isaac's response — the altar and the calling on the name:** v.25 — "And he builded an altar there, and called upon the name of the LORD, and pitched his tent there: and there Isaac's servants digged a well" — the fourfold response: altar (worship), calling on the name (prayer/proclamation), tent (dwelling in the land as a sojourner), well (digging for covenant life); these four elements were the marks of the covenant patriarch's presence in the land; where the patriarch built the altar, there was covenant ground; where he pitched his tent, there was covenant sojourn; where he dug the well, there was covenant provision

### "We Saw Certainly That the LORD Was with Thee" — Enemies Acknowledging Covenant Blessing

**Genesis 26:28** — "And they said, We saw certainly that the LORD was with thee"

- **The testimony of the adversary:** Abimelech came with his army commander (Phichol) and friend (Ahuzzath) — a diplomatic and military delegation — to make a covenant with Isaac because of what they had observed; their confession was the climax of the entire chapter's narrative: "We saw certainly (ra'o ra'inu — we saw, seeing — the emphatic repetition of the verb) that the LORD was with thee"
- **The pattern of adversaries acknowledging covenant blessing:**
  - Genesis 21:22 — Abimelech to Abraham: "God is with thee in all that thou doest" — the same pattern a generation earlier; the same Abimelech (or his dynastic predecessor/title) making the same acknowledgment to the covenant patriarch
  - Numbers 22:6 — Balak to Balaam: "I know that he whom thou blessest is blessed, and he whom thou cursest is cursed" — Israel's enemies acknowledged the covenant blessing before trying to exploit it
  - Joshua 2:9-11 — Rahab to the spies: "I know that the LORD hath given you the land... for the LORD your God, he is God in heaven above, and in earth beneath" — the Canaanite woman's confession; adversaries of the covenant people saw the LORD's presence as clearly as the covenant people themselves
  - Acts 5:11-13 — after the judgment of Ananias and Sapphira: "And great fear came upon all the church, and upon as many as heard these things... and of the rest durst no man join himself to them: but the people magnified them" — the same dynamic: those outside the covenant community saw clearly that God was present in His covenant people in a way that commanded both fear and respect
- **The theological point:** Isaac did not need to argue that the LORD was with him; the accumulated evidence of the hundredfold harvest, the growing wealth, and the inability of the Philistines to permanently stop his wells had made the LORD's presence undeniable to those who had been his adversaries; the LORD's blessing was its own testimony; **Matthew 5:16** — "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven" — the New Testament form of the pattern; when the covenant life is lived fully, even adversaries "see certainly that the LORD is with thee"

### Esau's Hittite Wives — The Covenant Marriage Standard Violated

**Genesis 26:34-35** — "And Esau was forty years old when he took to wife Judith the daughter of Beeri the Hittite, and Bashemath the daughter of Elon the Hittite: Which were a grief of mind unto Isaac and to Rebekah"

- **The grief was covenant grief, not merely cultural displeasure:** The Hebrew marat ruach = literally "bitterness of spirit" — not mild disappointment but deep spiritual anguish; the same phrase (marat) was used of intense grief throughout the Old Testament; the grief of Isaac and Rebekah was not about cultural incompatibility or parental preference — it was about what Abraham had explicitly sworn and what the covenant required
- **Abraham's oath had been specific:** Genesis 24:3 — "thou shalt not take a wife unto my son of the daughters of the Canaanites, among whom I dwell" — the Hittites were Canaanites; Esau violated the explicit covenant marriage standard that Abraham had consecrated by oath; the man who sold his birthright for pottage (Genesis 25:33) now confirmed the pattern by treating the covenant marriage standard with the same contempt
- **The escalating pattern of Esau's contempt for covenant:**
  - Genesis 25:33-34 — Esau sold the birthright for pottage — the covenant inheritance despised for immediate appetite
  - Genesis 26:34-35 — Esau took Hittite wives — the covenant marriage standard despised for immediate desire
  - Genesis 28:8-9 — Esau, seeing that his Canaanite wives displeased his father, took Ishmael's daughter as a third wife — the attempted correction went to Ishmael (the excluded covenant line) rather than to Laban (the included covenant line); even his correction was outside the covenant path
  - Each successive choice moved Esau further from the covenant and demonstrated that his contempt was not one impulsive moment but a consistent character orientation: he could not perceive or value what was sacred
- **The generational consequence:** Genesis 27:46 — "And Rebekah said to Isaac, I am weary of my life because of the daughters of Heth: if Jacob take a wife of the daughters of Heth, such as these which are of the daughters of the land, what good shall my life do me?" — Rebekah's anguish over Esau's choices became the stated reason for sending Jacob to Laban; the grief of Genesis 26:35 was the immediate cause of the covenant marriage-mission of Genesis 28; Esau's violation of the standard prompted Isaac to make the explicit command to Jacob (Genesis 28:1-2)
- **2 Corinthians 6:14** — "Be ye not unequally yoked together with unbelievers: for what fellowship hath righteousness with unrighteousness? and what communion hath light with darkness?" — the New Testament principle that carries the Genesis 26:34-35 pattern forward; the grief of Isaac and Rebekah over Esau's Hittite marriages was the patriarchal form of what Paul commanded; covenant marriages require covenant compatibility; the unequal yoke produces grief

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### REHOBOTH — WIDE PLACES / GOD MAKING ROOM (Hebrew: רְחֹבוֹת)

**Genesis 26:22** — "he called the name of it Rehoboth; and he said, For now the LORD hath made room for us, and we shall be fruitful in the land"

- **Root:** Rehoboth from rachav = to be wide, to be spacious, to be enlarged; the noun form rechov = a broad open place, a plaza; Rehoboth = wide places, broad places, places of spaciousness
- **The three-word Hebrew statement of enlargement:** hirchhiv YHWH lanu — "the LORD has made wide/spacious for us"; the subject is YHWH, the verb is the causative form of rachav (He caused wideness), the object is lanu (for us); the enlargement was not self-produced; Isaac did not strategize his way to Rehoboth; he moved repeatedly in patient obedience and arrived at the place where God had already made room
- **Rehoboth in the rest of Scripture:**
  - Genesis 36:37 — "And Saul of Rehoboth by the river reigned in his stead" — a king of Edom (Esau's line) came from Rehoboth; even the place of Isaac's enlargement produced a dynasty among the Edomites; the name spread
  - Genesis 10:11 — "Rehoboth-Ir" (Rehoboth-City) is mentioned in the table of nations in connection with Nimrod's building program; the wide-city name appears in the earliest anti-covenant empire
  - 1 Chronicles 4:28 — Rehoboth appears in the territory of Simeon; the covenant community eventually settled in a place bearing the enlargement-name
- **The Psalm thread of divine enlargement:**
  - Psalm 18:19 — "He brought me forth also into a large place (merchav); he delivered me, because he delighted in me" — the enlargement tied to delight, not merit; Psalm 31:8 — "thou hast set my feet in a large room (merchav)"; Psalm 118:5 — "the LORD answered me, and set me in a large place (merchav)"; all three use the rachav-root; all three describe the divine provision of open space after the experience of being hemmed in
  - The pattern: pressed in → call to God → divine enlargement; Esek → Sitnah → Rehoboth is the narrative version of the Psalm pattern
- **Isaiah 54:2-3** — "Enlarge (harchivi) the place of thy tent, and let them stretch forth the curtains of thine habitations: spare not, lengthen thy cords, and strengthen thy stakes; For thou shalt break forth on the right hand and on the left; and thy seed shall inherit the Gentiles" — the same harchiv/rachav root; Isaiah commanded the covenant community to anticipate Rehoboth-scale enlargement in the eschatological age; the Rehoboth of Genesis 26 was the type of the eschatological breaking forth that Isaiah promised the post-exilic community
- **The typological significance:** Isaac's third well became the city of his dwelling, the place of the altar, the place of the Beersheba covenant with Abimelech; the place God made room was not a temporary stopping point but a permanent inheritance; the Rehoboth that God makes for His covenant people is always the place of fruitfulness — "we shall be fruitful in the land"

### SITNAH — OPPOSITION / ACCUSATION (Hebrew: שִׂטְנָה)

**Genesis 26:21** — "and they digged another well, and strove for that also: and he called the name of it Sitnah"

- **Root:** Sitnah from satan = to be an adversary, to accuse, to oppose; the root verb satan means to obstruct, to oppose, to act as an adversary; sitnah (the noun form) = accusation, opposition, enmity; the related noun satan = the adversary, the accuser
- **The connection to Satan:** The name Isaac gave to the second contested well encoded the spiritual reality of the opposition he was facing; the second level of resistance was not merely natural contention (Esek = strife between herdsmen) but adversarial, accusing enmity — the spiritual character of the opposition was satanic in its nature; Isaac's naming of the well was a theological interpretation of the conflict, not merely a geographic label
- **Sitnah in later Scripture:**
  - Zechariah 3:1 — "And he shewed me Joshua the high priest standing before the angel of the LORD, and Satan standing at his right hand to resist (lesitno) him" — the verb is the same root; Satan standing to sitnah = to obstruct, to accuse; the spiritual opposition Joshua the high priest faced was the same root-word as Isaac's contested well
  - Job 1:6-7 — the satan (the adversary) in the divine council; the accuser who contested Job's covenant faithfulness; the same spirit of sitnah that contested Isaac's well
  - Revelation 12:10 — "the accuser of our brethren is cast down, which accused them before our God day and night" — the ultimate form of sitnah; the accuser's work is continuous accusation; the second well's name encoded the spiritual reality that would eventually be cast down at Christ's resurrection
- **The pastoral application:** Isaac named the second well Sitnah not to dwell on it but to identify it accurately and move on; the acknowledgment of satanic opposition is not defeat — it is honest spiritual discernment; the response to Sitnah was not warfare over that well but movement to the next; the Christian answer to satanic-level opposition is not always frontal confrontation but patient, obedient movement in the direction God sets, trusting that Rehoboth lies ahead

### QANA — ENVIED (Hebrew: קָנָא)

**Genesis 26:14** — "and the Philistines envied him"

- **Root:** Qana = to be jealous, to be envious, to be intensely aroused by what another possesses; the same root produces both the positive form (God is a qanna = jealous God — Exodus 20:5; the jealousy of protective covenant love) and the negative form (sinful envy — resentment of another's blessing)
- **The distinction between divine jealousy and human envy:** God's qanna is the jealousy of the covenant Lord who will not share His covenant people with other gods — it is the jealousy of exclusive love and holy ownership; the Philistines' qana of Isaac was the envy of those who wanted what God had given to another without relationship to the God who gave it; the same word describes opposite moral realities: divine covenant-love protecting what is His vs. human sin resenting what belongs to another
- **Envy in Scripture as the root of persecution:**
  - Genesis 37:11 — "And his brethren envied him (vaye'qan'u bo)" — Joseph's brothers envied him after the dreams; the envy preceded the plan to kill him; Acts 7:9 — "And the patriarchs, moved with envy (zēlōsantes), sold Joseph into Egypt" — the NT quotation confirmed envy as the cause
  - Matthew 27:18 / Mark 15:10 — "For he knew that for envy (phthonon) they had delivered him" — Pilate's perception that the chief priests had delivered Christ to him out of envy; the Philistines' envy of Isaac's hundredfold harvest was the same root as the envy that drove the crucifixion; the world's envy of covenant blessing is the spiritual mechanism behind all persecution of the covenant people
  - Acts 13:45 — "But when the Jews saw the multitudes, they were filled with envy (zēlou), and spake against those things which were spoken by Paul, contradicting and blaspheming" — the same pattern in the New Testament missionary narrative; visible fruitfulness in the covenant community produced envy in those outside it, which produced opposition
- **Proverbs 14:30** — "A sound heart is the life of the flesh: but envy the rottenness of the bones" — the internal cost of envy to the envious; the Philistines' envy of Isaac destroyed their own wells (stopping them up — v.15) while doing nothing to diminish Isaac's blessing; envy is self-destructive; the act of stopping the wells was an expression of envy that produced no benefit for the Philistines while it generated significant additional work for Isaac

### MARAT RUACH — BITTERNESS OF SPIRIT / GRIEF OF MIND (Hebrew: מֹרַת רוּחַ)

**Genesis 26:35** — "which were a grief of mind unto Isaac and to Rebekah"

- **The Hebrew:** marat ruach = literally "bitterness of spirit"; marat = bitterness, that which is bitter; ruach = spirit, breath, the inner person; together: bitterness/grief at the level of the spirit, not merely external emotion
- **The same phrase in intense grief contexts:**
  - 1 Samuel 1:10 — "And she was in bitterness of soul (marat nefesh), and prayed unto the LORD, and wept sore" — Hannah's anguish over her barrenness; a different but related phrase (marat of the nefesh/soul rather than the ruach/spirit); the same quality of deep interior grief; Hannah's marat was the crucible of the prayer that brought Samuel; Isaac and Rebekah's marat was the crucible of the decision that sent Jacob to Laban
  - Proverbs 17:25 — "A foolish son is a grief to his father, and bitterness (maret) to her that bare him" — the Proverbs expression of what Genesis 26:35 described; a son's covenant-contemptuous choices produce bitterness of spirit in covenant parents
- **Why the grief was spiritual, not merely social:** Isaac and Rebekah were not grieving because Hittite women were culturally different; they were grieving because Abraham had made explicit covenantal provision to prevent exactly this; the sent servant, the specific oath, the entire Genesis 24 mission — all had been to ensure the covenant wife came from the covenant line; Esau's casual disregard of that entire sacred structure, his double choice of Hittite women, was an act of contempt toward the most carefully laid covenantal preparation in the family's history; the grief was proportionate to the sacred significance of what had been violated

### HALOK VA-GADOL — "WENT FORWARD AND GREW" (Hebrew: הָלוֹךְ וְגָדֵל)

**Genesis 26:13** — "and the man waxed great, and went forward (halok), and grew (gadel) until he became very great"

- **The grammatical form:** halok va-gadol is an infinitive absolute construction used as an adverb — it intensified the action; literally: "going and growing" — the continuous, progressive, uninterrupted nature of the increase; the construction described a trajectory, not a moment; Isaac was not momentarily great; his greatness was a direction
- **The same grammatical pattern of continuous intensification:**
  - Genesis 8:5 — "the waters decreased continually (halok va-chasor = going and decreasing)" — the same construction describing the progressive decrease of the floodwaters
  - 2 Samuel 3:1 — "Now there was long war between the house of Saul and the house of David: but David waxed stronger and stronger (halok va-chazaq), and the house of Saul waxed weaker and weaker (halok va-dalal)" — the same construction applied to the political ascension of David; progressive, continuous, unidirectional increase
  - The construction captures the idea of movement that has direction and cannot be reversed; Isaac's blessing was not a one-time event but a trajectory established by God's covenant; his greatness "went and grew" — directional, progressive, covenant-grounded

**Genesis 26:20-22 — Esek, Sitnah, Rehoboth**

| Well | Name Meaning | Spiritual Dimension | Resolution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Esek | Strife / Contention | Natural opposition: the world contests what belongs to the covenant person | Isaac named it and moved on |
| Sitnah | Satanic Opposition / Accusation | Spiritual-level adversarial enmity (same root as satan / the accuser) | Isaac named it and moved on |

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "She Is My Sister" — The Bible Endorses Lying for Self-Preservation

**The Misuse:** The patriarchs' repeated deception (Abraham twice, Isaac once) about their wives is cited to argue that the Bible endorses pragmatic deception for self-preservation, or that there is a category of "protective lying" that Scripture permits.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The text records, condemns, and moves on — never endorses:** In every instance of the patriarch's deception about his wife, the narrative shows: (1) the deception did not protect — it endangered; (2) a pagan ruler rebuked the patriarch morally; (3) God intervened to protect the patriarch's wife in spite of the deception, not because of it; the protection came from God's grace, not from the patriarch's strategy
- **Abimelech's rebuke is the text's verdict:** v.10 — "What is this thou hast done unto us? one of the people might lightly have lien with thy wife, and thou shouldest have brought guiltiness upon us" — the moral responsibility for potential sin was laid at Isaac's feet by the king; the pagan king behaved with greater ethical clarity than the covenant patriarch; the text never offered a counter-rebuke or justified Isaac's choice
- **The escalation pattern indicts rather than justifies:** Isaac's lie was worse than Abraham's (Rebekah was genuinely not his sister; Sarah was Abraham's half-sister); the worsening of the pattern across generations was the narrator's way of recording the problem's depth; if the deception were endorsed, the escalation would be expected to diminish, not increase
- **Exodus 20:16 / Proverbs 12:22:** The commandment against false witness and the explicit statement that "lying lips are abomination to the LORD" leave no room for an endorsed category of self-preservation deception; Rahab's lie (Joshua 2:4-6) is sometimes cited as a parallel — the midwives' deception in Exodus 1:15-21 and Rahab's lie in Joshua 2 are distinct cases the church has debated; neither establishes a general principle that the patriarchs' deceptions about their wives were approved
- **The theological point the text makes:** The covenant blessing survived Isaac's failure — not because the deception was right but because God's covenant faithfulness operated in spite of the patriarch's weakness; Romans 3:3-4 — "shall their unbelief make the faith of God without effect? God forbid... let God be true, but every man a liar" — the faithfulness of God to the covenant was not dependent on the moral perfection of the covenant carrier
- *Correction:* The "she is my sister" passages in Genesis record the patriarchs' fear-driven failures. The text's own embedded rebuke (from a pagan king), the escalating pattern, and the consistent biblical prohibitions on deception establish that these episodes were recorded as warnings and as evidence of God's grace, not as endorsed models of self-protective behavior.

### "The LORD Blessed Him" — Prosperity Proves God's Approval

**The Misuse:** Genesis 26:12-14 — the hundredfold harvest and Isaac's growing wealth — is cited to argue that material prosperity is always the direct sign of God's covenant approval, and conversely that poverty or hardship indicates spiritual failure or divine disapproval.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The hundredfold was explicitly covenantal and contextual:** The blessing of Genesis 26:12 came directly after God's appearance and covenant reconfirmation (vv.2-5); it was not a general promise that all wealthy people are blessed of God but the specific fulfillment of the covenant promise ("I will bless thee" — v.3) in the life of the specific covenant son who had just been obedient in staying in the land; the prosperity was the covenant sign in a specific covenant context
- **Scripture contains abundant counter-evidence:** Job was stripped of his wealth while God praised him as "perfect and upright" (Job 1:8); the Psalms frequently describe the righteous poor and the wicked wealthy: Psalm 73:3-12 — "I was envious at the foolish, when I saw the prosperity of the wicked... They are not in trouble as other men; neither are they plagued like other men"; James 2:5 — "Hath not God chosen the poor of this world rich in faith, and heirs of the kingdom which he hath promised to them that love him?"
- **Christ's explicit rejection of the prosperity-approval equation:** John 9:2-3 — "And his disciples asked him, saying, Master, who did sin, this man, or his parents, that he was born blind? Jesus answered, Neither hath this man sinned, nor his parents: but that the works of God should be made manifest in him" — the disciples assumed a direct suffering→sin connection; Christ rejected it; the same logic in reverse (prosperity→approval) is equally rejected
- **The specific purpose of Isaac's prosperity was testimony, not entitlement:** The hundredfold harvest produced the testimony "We saw certainly that the LORD was with thee" (v.28); the blessing served the witness function of making the covenant God's presence visible to the Philistines; it was missional, not merely personal; wealth given as testimony is not the same thing as wealth as the universal signal of divine approval
- *Correction:* Genesis 26:12-14 records the specific covenant blessing of the specific covenant son in the specific context of covenant obedience. It does not establish a universal principle that God always materially prospers the faithful or that material prosperity always indicates divine approval. The Bible's consistent testimony includes prosperous wicked, suffering righteous, and the explicit teaching of Christ that suffering is not always sin-caused and that the poor can be rich in faith.

### "Esek, Sitnah, Rehoboth" — God Always Removes Opposition If You Are Patient Enough

**The Misuse:** The three-well pattern is used to argue that every situation of opposition or difficulty will inevitably be resolved if the believer is patient — that God always provides a Rehoboth after every Esek and Sitnah; or conversely, that any ongoing difficulty proves the believer has not yet found the right well (the right church, the right opportunity, the right city).

**What Scripture says:**

- **The pattern of Esek/Sitnah/Rehoboth is a real and recurring biblical pattern — but it is not a mechanical formula:** The three-well sequence described Isaac's specific journey in a specific time; it became a pattern woven into the Psalms (Psalm 31:8; 118:5) and prophets (Isaiah 54:2) as a recurring theological reality; God does bring His people through opposition to enlargement — but the timing, form, and path are sovereign, not mechanical
- **Paul's "thorn in the flesh" was not removed after patient waiting:** 2 Corinthians 12:7-9 — "there was given to me a thorn in the flesh, the messenger of Satan to buffet me... For this thing I besought the Lord thrice, that it might depart from me. And he said unto me, My grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness" — Paul experienced sustained opposition (sitnah-level: "messenger of Satan") that was not removed but redeemed; God's response was not Rehoboth-through-relocation but sufficiency-in-the-opposition; both patterns are real; neither cancels the other
- **Hebrews 11:35-38** — "Others were tortured, not accepting deliverance; that they might obtain a better resurrection... of whom the world was not worthy: they wandered in deserts, and in mountains, and in dens and caves of the earth" — the faith-hall of Hebrews 11 contained people who did not find their Rehoboth in this life; some received great deliverances; others died in faith without receiving the earthly promise; both were commended
- **The promise is Rehoboth — but the road through Esek and Sitnah is real and may be long:** The pattern is not "eventually you will find the right opportunity"; it is "the LORD makes room in His time and His way for those who keep moving in obedience"; the formula-izing of the pattern removes the sovereign timing and substitutes a mechanistic expectation that can produce either presumption (assuming Rehoboth is always imminent) or condemnation (assuming continued difficulty means spiritual failure)
- *Correction:* The Esek/Sitnah/Rehoboth pattern is a genuine biblical map of covenant life under opposition — and is a real pattern. It does not operate as a mechanical formula guaranteeing a specific earthly resolution within a specific timeframe. God does make room; He does bring His people through; but the path includes real and sustained Sitnah-level opposition that may not be removed in this life (Hebrews 11). The counsel from the three wells is: name the opposition accurately, keep moving in the direction of obedience, and trust that the LORD who makes room will do so in His time.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Well — Covenant Life Contested and Recovered

**Genesis 26:15-22 — stopped wells, re-dug wells, the three named wells**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 21:25-30 — Abraham and Abimelech covenant over the well at Beersheba; Abraham dug the wells; the covenant was sealed over the water-source
- Genesis 26:15 — "the Philistines had stopped them, and filled them with earth" — the inheritance stopped up after the patriarch's death; the world's response to covenant prosperity is to bury the source
- Genesis 26:18-22 — Isaac re-dug every well; he called them by the same names his father had given them; the restoration of names was the restoration of identity — these were not new wells but the same wells recovered
- John 4:10-14 — "Whosoever drinketh of the water that I shall give him shall never thirst; but the water that I shall give him shall be in him a well of water springing up into everlasting life" — the living water Christ offered at Jacob's well (another contested, covenant-significant well) was the ultimate fulfillment of everything the re-dug wells represented; the world stops up every source of covenant life; Christ is the well that cannot be stopped
- John 7:37-39 — "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink... (this spake he of the Spirit)" — the Spirit as the living well within the believer; the Rehoboth that cannot be contested; the place inside the covenant person where the LORD has made room permanently
- Revelation 22:17 — "And whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely" — the final well; the covenant water freely available; all opposition permanently ended; no more Esek, no more Sitnah

### "Fear Not" — The Covenant Reassurance Thread

**Genesis 26:24 — "fear not, for I am with thee"**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 15:1 — "Fear not, Abram: I am thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward" — first "fear not" to a patriarch; at the point of vulnerability after battle; the shield metaphor
- Genesis 21:17 — "Fear not; for God hath heard the voice of the lad" — to Hagar; the "fear not" crossing covenant boundaries to the excluded one
- Genesis 26:24 — "Fear not, for I am with thee" — to Isaac; at night; after expulsion and contested wells; the "I am with thee" form
- Genesis 46:3 — "Fear not to go down into Egypt; for I will there make of thee a great nation" — to Jacob; fear about the departure from the land; the "I will make you great there" form
- Isaiah 41:10 — "Fear thou not; for I am with thee: be not dismayed; for I am thy God" — the Isaian development; every element of Genesis 26:24 expanded
- Isaiah 43:1 — "Fear not: for I have redeemed thee, I have called thee by thy name; thou art mine" — the deepest form: redemption and naming as the ground of fearlessness
- Matthew 28:20 — "lo, I am with you alway, even unto the end of the world" — the risen Christ's form of "fear not, for I am with thee"; the same "I am with thee" of Genesis 26:24 extended to every believer through the end of the age
- Revelation 1:17 — "And when I saw him, I fell at his feet as dead. And he laid his right hand upon me, saying unto me, Fear not; I am the first and the last" — the final "fear not" of Scripture; the risen and glorified Christ to John; the same pattern from Genesis 15 to Revelation 1: "Fear not" + who God is + what God does

### The Father's Obedience as the Ground of the Son's Inheritance — The Thread of Covenant Continuity

**Genesis 26:5 — "because Abraham obeyed my voice"**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 26:5 — God gave the covenant to Isaac "because Abraham obeyed my voice" — the father's obedience grounded the son's inheritance
- Deuteronomy 7:9 — "the LORD thy God... keepeth covenant and mercy with them that love him and keep his commandments to a thousand generations" — the generational reach of covenant faithfulness; a thousand generations
- Psalm 103:17-18 — "But the mercy of the LORD is from everlasting to everlasting upon them that fear him, and his righteousness unto children's children; To such as keep his covenant, and to those that remember his commandments to do them" — the covenant mercy reaching to children's children of those who keep the covenant

- Acts 2:39 — "For the promise is unto you, and to your children, and to all that are afar off, even as many as the Lord our God shall call" — Peter's Pentecost declaration; the covenant promise extending to children; the generational principle of Acts 2:39 is the New Testament form of Genesis 26:5

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time a Covenant Son Was Explicitly Forbidden to Go to Egypt

**Genesis 26:2** — "Go not down into Egypt"
- Abraham had gone to Egypt without prohibition (Genesis 12:10); no explicit command was given to Abraham against Egypt; Isaac received the first explicit prohibition
- First mention establishes: the covenant son's stay in the land of promise is a matter of explicit divine instruction, not merely wise advice; the land of promise is not to be abandoned in famine; the prohibition to Isaac established a covenant principle that later prophets would develop (Isaiah 31:1) — Egypt as the alternative to trusting God is always forbidden to the covenant people

### First Time the Covenant Was Explicitly Grounded in the Previous Generation's Obedience

**Genesis 26:5** — "because Abraham obeyed my voice"
- In earlier restatements of the covenant (Genesis 12, 15, 17, 22), the grounding was in God's own sovereign initiative and oath; here, for the first time, God explicitly stated that the continuation of the covenant to the next generation was because of the previous generation's faithfulness
- First mention establishes: covenant faithfulness in the parent generation has real generational consequences for the children; this is not works-based salvation for the next generation but the covenant structure God established in which obedience creates a heritage; the principle of the covenant blessing passing through the obedient family line was established explicitly for the first time here

### First Time a Patriarch Built an Altar and Called on the LORD's Name at Beersheba

**Genesis 26:25** — "he builded an altar there, and called upon the name of the LORD"
- Abraham had built altars at Shechem, between Bethel and Ai, Mamre, and Moriah; but Beersheba's altar was first built by Isaac; Abraham had planted a tree at Beersheba (Genesis 21:33) and called on the name there; Isaac's altar was the formal cultic establishment of Beersheba as covenant ground
- First mention establishes: each generation of the covenant family is called to consecrate their specific dwelling place through worship; the altar and the calling on the name are the marks of covenant presence wherever the patriarch sojourns; Isaac consecrated Beersheba with the same acts (altar + calling on the name) that his father had used to consecrate other sites

### First Time the Specific Phrase "Blessed of the LORD" Was Spoken by a Non-Covenant Person About a Covenant Person

**Genesis 26:29** — "thou art now the blessed of the LORD"
- Abimelech, the Philistine king, spoke this declaration; the phrase "blessed of the LORD" spoken by the adversary was the culmination of the chapter's testimony
- First mention establishes: the accumulated evidence of covenant blessing becomes undeniable even to adversaries; the blessing is self-testifying; no argument was required; the Philistines' own observation drove them to make the theological statement that Isaac himself could have made; the LORD's blessing on His covenant people eventually compels acknowledgment even from those who would prefer otherwise

### First Time a Covenant Wife Was Called a "Grief of Mind" in the Context of an Ungodly Marriage

**Genesis 26:35** — "which were a grief of mind unto Isaac and to Rebekah"
- Esau's Hittite marriages produced the first recorded instance in the covenant family of the specific covenant grief caused by a covenant member choosing outside the covenant marriage standard
- First mention establishes: the violation of the covenant marriage standard produces a specific kind of grief — bitterness of spirit (marat ruach) — in the covenant household; this grief is the spiritual pain appropriate to a spiritual violation; the first mention of this grief in the covenant family context became the stated cause of the action that sent Jacob on the Genesis 28 mission

## NAME MEANINGS

| Name | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Esek | Contention / Strife | The name Isaac gave to the first contested well; natural opposition named accurately; from asaq (to press, to oppress, to quarrel) |
| Sitnah | Opposition / Enmity / Accusation | The name of the second contested well; from the same root as satan (the adversary, the accuser); the escalation to adversarial spiritual-level opposition encoded in the name |
| Rehoboth | Wide Places / Room | From rachav (to be wide, spacious, enlarged); "the LORD hath made room for us"; the city of the covenant breakthrough; the name of divine enlargement after opposition |
| Shebah | Oath / Seven | Isaac's name for the new well at Beersheba; renaming the covenant place in his own generation; oath and seven intertwined — the covenant oath and the completeness-number; Beersheba = Well of the Oath / Well of Seven |
| Abimelech | Father of the King / My Father Is King | The dynastic title of the Philistine king; may be a throne-name rather than a personal name, explaining why both Abraham and Isaac encountered an "Abimelech" in the same region |
| Phichol | Mouth of All | The commander of Abimelech's army; also appearing in Abraham's day (Genesis 21:22); likely a dynastic title for the army commander |
| Ahuzzath | Possession / He Who Holds | One of Abimelech's friends who accompanied him to the covenant-making with Isaac |
| Judith (Yehudit) | Praised / The One Who Praises | The Hittite wife of Esau; her name carried covenant resonance (from yadah — to praise, the root of Judah) but her people were Canaanites; the contrast between the covenant-name meaning and the Canaanite identity was its own irony |
| Bashemath | Fragrant / Sweet-Smelling | The second Hittite wife of Esau; the name of pleasant fragrance for the woman who brought bitterness of spirit (marat ruach) to Isaac and Rebekah |
