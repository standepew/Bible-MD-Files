## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 35 was the chapter of covenant completion. Between the altar at Shechem (Genesis 33:20) and this chapter lay the darkness of Genesis 34 — violation, deception, massacre, the land doubly defiled. God's response was not condemnation but a call: "Arise, go up to Beth-el." The covenant God did not abandon the defiled household; He commanded it back to the covenant place.

Jacob's first act was the purging. He commanded the household to put away the strange gods, purify themselves, and change their garments. Everything that belonged to the surrounding culture — the teraphim Rachel had stolen from Laban (Genesis 31:19), the earrings associated with pagan practice — was surrendered. Jacob buried them under the oak at Shechem, the same location where Joshua would later issue the identical command to all Israel: "Put away the gods which your fathers served" (Joshua 24:14). The command and the location were the same centuries apart; God had embedded the pattern in the place.

They journeyed toward Bethel. The terror of God fell on every city around them and none pursued — the divine protection that Exodus 23:27 promised in embryonic form, operative here before Sinai, before Moses, because the covenant was already in force. Deborah, Rebekah's nurse, died beneath Bethel and was buried at the oak they called Allon-bachuth — the oak of weeping. The mourning for this woman, barely named in the story but beloved enough to name a tree for her grief, was the human sorrow that accompanied the return to the covenant place.

At Bethel God appeared again, blessed Jacob, and confirmed the name Israel — not as a fresh gift but as a divine ratification: what had happened at Peniel was real, was permanent, and was being sealed publicly at the covenant location. God spoke as El Shaddai — the patriarchal covenant name of Genesis 17:1, the name Exodus 6:3 would later identify as the name by which God was known to the patriarchs. The promises given to Abraham (Genesis 12:1-3; 17:1-8) and Isaac (Genesis 26:2-5) were now explicitly reaffirmed to Jacob/Israel: be fruitful and multiply, a nation and a company of nations, kings from his loins, the land to him and to his seed. God went up from him. Jacob set a pillar, poured out the first drink offering recorded in Scripture, poured oil — and named the place Bethel. The Genesis 28:21 vow was fully, completely, publicly closed.

They journeyed from Bethel. Near Ephrath — which was Bethlehem — Rachel travailed in hard labor. The midwife: "Fear not; thou shalt have this son also." As Rachel's soul was departing she named him Benoni — son of my sorrow. His father called him Benjamin — son of the right hand. The dying mother named the child with her grief; the living father renamed him with honor. Jacob set a pillar over her grave at the road to Bethlehem.

Israel journeyed on and spread his tent beyond the tower of Edar. Reuben lay with Bilhah his father's concubine — and Israel heard it. The text said no more at this point; the verdict waited for Genesis 49:3-4. The twelve sons were then formally listed — all twelve, all complete, the nation of Israel present in seed form for the first time.

Jacob came to his father Isaac at Mamre/Hebron. Isaac died at 180 years, old and full of days, gathered to his people. His sons Esau and Jacob buried him — the same quiet reunion over a father's grave that Ishmael and Isaac had enacted over Abraham's in Genesis 25:9. The second patriarch was laid to rest. The third remained.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Put Away the Strange Gods" — The Covenant Purging as Prerequisite to Renewal

**Genesis 35:2-4** — "Put away the strange gods that are among you, and be clean, and change your garments... And they gave unto Jacob all the strange gods which were in their hand, and all their earrings which were in their ears; and Jacob hid them under the oak which was by Shechem"

- **Rachel's stolen teraphim and their final disposition:** Genesis 31:19 — "And Rachel had stolen the images (teraphim) that were her father's" — the idols that had been hidden under the camel's saddle (Genesis 31:34), that Jacob's unknowing curse had hovered over (Genesis 31:32), that had not been dealt with at the Shechem altar (Genesis 33:20), were now surrendered; the covenant household had carried the teraphim through the Peniel wrestling, the Esau reunion, and the Shechem altar without purging them; God's call to Bethel was simultaneously the call to complete what had been left undone
- **The earrings and their association with pagan practice:** Exodus 32:2-3 — "And Aaron said unto them, Break off the golden earrings, which are in the ears of your wives, of your sons, and of your daughters, and bring them unto me. And all the people brake off the golden earrings which were in their ears, and brought them unto Aaron" — Aaron used the people's earrings to make the golden calf; the earrings of Genesis 35:4 were buried alongside the strange gods, not merely laid aside; the text treated them as part of the same category; the physical ornament associated with the cultic practice was surrendered with the cultic objects; the purging was comprehensive
- **The identical command at the identical location — Joshua at Shechem:** Joshua 24:14-15, 23 — "Now therefore fear the LORD, and serve him in sincerity and in truth: and put away the gods which your fathers served on the other side of the flood, and in Egypt; and serve ye the LORD... put away, said he, the strange gods which are among you, and incline your heart unto the LORD God of Israel" — Joshua issued the identical command ("put away the strange gods") at Shechem — the same place Jacob had buried the gods under the oak; the covenant-renewal ceremony of Joshua 24 was enacted at the exact location of Jacob's earlier purging; the same ground, the same command, four centuries apart; Shechem was the place of covenant decisions — for good and for ill (Genesis 34 was also at Shechem)
- **The consistent biblical pattern — purging before renewal:**
  - 1 Samuel 7:3-4 — Samuel to Israel: "if ye do return unto the LORD with all your hearts, then put away the strange gods and Ashtaroth from among you, and prepare your hearts unto the LORD, and serve him only: and he will deliver you out of the hand of the Philistines. Then the children of Israel did put away Baalim and Ashtaroth, and served the LORD only" — purging followed by deliverance; the pattern of Genesis 35 enacted at the national level
  - 2 Kings 23:4-24 — Josiah's comprehensive purging before the covenant renewal of 2 Kings 23:1-3: "the king commanded... to bring forth out of the temple of the LORD all the vessels that were made for Baal, and for the grove, and for all the host of heaven... the idolatrous priests... the teraphim, and the idols" — the most comprehensive purging in Israel's history, before the most solemn covenant renewal; the pattern of Jacob's household burying the gods before approaching Bethel was the pattern of Josiah clearing the temple before reading the book of the covenant
  - Acts 19:18-19 — Ephesian believers: "Many of them also which used curious arts brought their books together, and burned them before all men: and they counted the price of them, and found it fifty thousand pieces of silver" — the New Covenant pattern; the books of magic arts burned in public; the cost was enormous; the purging was genuine; the same principle as Jacob's household surrendering and burying the strange gods: you cannot carry what belongs to the old allegiance into the presence of the covenant God
- **"Be clean and change your garments" — the ritual of preparation:** The three commands — put away the gods, be clean, change garments — formed the complete preparation sequence; outward (garments), inward (be clean), relational (put away the gods); the changing of garments was not merely hygiene but the ancient ritual of entering a new status or a sacred occasion; Exodus 19:10 — "Go unto the people, and sanctify them to day and to morrow, and let them wash their clothes" — God commanded the same preparation of Israel before the Sinai theophany; the pattern of Jacob's household at Shechem was the prototype of Sinai's preparation; the covenant God required preparation for His presence

### "The Terror of God Was Upon the Cities" — Divine Protection of the Covenant Journey

**Genesis 35:5** — "And they journeyed: and the terror of God was upon the cities that were round about them, and they did not pursue after the sons of Jacob"

- **The theological context of the terror:** After the Shechem massacre (Genesis 34:25-29), Jacob had feared that the surrounding Canaanites and Perizzites would gather against his small household and destroy them (Genesis 34:30). The fear was reasonable. The Shechemites had been wealthy and numerous; other cities would have known what Jacob's sons had done. God's response to the threat was not to make Jacob invisible but to make Him terrible: the terror (chittath — dread, dismay) of God fell on every city around them
- **The divine terror as covenant protection — the pattern:**
  - Exodus 23:27-28 — God's promise to Moses: "I will send my fear before thee, and will destroy all the people to whom thou shalt come, and I will make all thine enemies turn their backs unto thee. And I will send hornets before thee, which shall drive out the Hivite, the Canaanite, and the Hittite, from before thee" — the exact pattern of Genesis 35:5 articulated as a covenant promise; God's fear going before Israel to clear the path; what was enacted at Shechem's gates was a proto-fulfillment of the Exodus promise
  - Deuteronomy 2:25 — "This day will I begin to put the dread of thee and the fear of thee upon the nations that are under the whole heaven, who shall hear report of thee, and shall tremble, and be in anguish because of thee" — the fear of Israel upon the nations as God's instrument of advance; the same chittath
  - Joshua 2:9-11 — Rahab to the spies: "I know that the LORD hath given you the land; and that your terror is fallen upon us, and that all the inhabitants of the land faint because of you... for the LORD your God, he is God in heaven above, and in earth beneath" — Rahab was reporting what had been true at every covenant advance: the terror of God fell on those in Israel's path; and the report of it produced the only righteous response (faith and allegiance) in Rahab, and paralysis and destruction in those who did not respond in faith
  - Joshua 5:1 — "It came to pass, when all the kings of the Amorites which were on the side of Jordan westward... heard that the LORD had dried up the waters of Jordan from before the children of Israel, until we were passed over, that their heart melted, neither was there spirit in them any more" — the heart-melting as the divine terror's most concentrated expression; Canaan's kings were undone by report alone; no weapon was needed; the same God who caused the surrounding cities not to pursue Jacob's limping household caused the hearts of Canaan's kings to melt forty years before Joshua arrived
- **The relationship between the purging and the protection:** The sequence in Genesis 35 was not coincidental: (1) purge the household of idols (vv.2-4); (2) journey — and the terror of God goes before you (v.5); the protection followed the purging; the covenant God did not go before a household that still carried its idols; the condition for the chittath was the cleansing of the household; the principle was stated formally in Deuteronomy 7:25-26 — "The graven images of their gods shall ye burn with fire... neither shalt thou bring an abomination into thine house... thou shalt utterly detest it... for it is a cursed thing" — the removal of the idol was the prerequisite for covenant protection; Jacob's household demonstrated the principle before Deuteronomy articulated it

### God Appears Again at Bethel — The Name Confirmed and the Covenant Reaffirmed

**Genesis 35:9-13** — "And God appeared unto Jacob again, when he came out of Padanaram, and blessed him. And God said unto him, Thy name is Jacob: thy name shall not be called any more Jacob, but Israel shall be thy name... I am God Almighty: be fruitful and multiply..."

- **The double confirmation of the name — Peniel and Bethel:** Genesis 32:28 — God gave the name Israel at Peniel, in the wrestling at the ford of Jabbok; Genesis 35:10 — God confirmed it again at Bethel: "thy name shall not be called any more Jacob, but Israel shall be thy name"; the double confirmation was theologically significant; what was given in the night of crisis at the ford was sealed in the daylight of covenant worship at the altar; the name was not merely a nocturnal gift — it was the covenant reality of the man's identity, confirmed publicly at the house of God
- **El Shaddai — the patriarchal covenant name:**
  - Genesis 17:1 — the first use: "I am the Almighty God (El Shaddai); walk before me, and be thou perfect" — to Abraham at the covenant of circumcision; the name introduced at the moment of the covenant's comprehensive formalization
  - Genesis 28:3 — Isaac's blessing to Jacob before his departure: "And God Almighty (El Shaddai) bless thee, and make thee fruitful and multiply thee, that thou mayest be a multitude of people" — Isaac used the name in the blessing; now at Bethel, God used the name in the fulfillment of that blessing; Isaac's blessing and God's self-declaration were the same name
  - Exodus 6:2-3 — "And God spake unto Moses, and said unto him, I am the LORD: And I appeared unto Abraham, unto Isaac, and unto Jacob, by the name of God Almighty (El Shaddai), but by my name JEHOVAH was I not known to them" — the divine commentary: El Shaddai was the patriarchal covenant name; the fuller revelation of the personal name Yahweh/Jehovah was reserved for the Mosaic era; Jacob at Bethel was in the El Shaddai era of revelation; every time God appeared to the patriarchs and spoke covenant promises, He appeared as El Shaddai
  - The meaning of Shaddai: the root was debated — mountain (shad = breast/mountain, with the idea of nurture and strength), almighty (shadday from shadad = to overpower), or self-sufficient (connecting to Akkadian šadû = mountain); the biblical usage established the meaning: El Shaddai was the God of sufficient power for every covenant promise; no promise He made exceeded His capacity to fulfill; the name was used at moments when the promise exceeded human possibility (Abraham and Sarah's ages, Genesis 17; Jacob's need for protection and multiplication, Genesis 35)
  - Revelation 1:8; 4:8 — "Lord God Almighty (Pantokrator — the all-powerful one)" — the New Testament carried the El Shaddai title into its Greek equivalent; John's Revelation used it at the moments of divine majesty and universal sovereignty; what the patriarchs encountered as El Shaddai, John saw enthroned as Pantokrator
- **The covenant promises reaffirmed — the threefold structure:**
  - "Be fruitful and multiply" — the creation mandate of Genesis 1:28, restated after the flood to Noah (Genesis 9:1), given as the specific patriarchal promise in Genesis 17:6 and 28:3, now reaffirmed directly by God to Jacob/Israel; the continuity was unbroken: the original creation purpose of filling the earth with the image of God was being channeled through the covenant seed
  - "A nation and a company of nations shall be of thee, and kings shall come out of thy loins" — the national promise and the royal promise; the twelve sons who were listed in vv.22-26 were the structural beginning of "a nation"; the "company of nations" anticipated the full scope of the Abrahamic promise (Genesis 12:3 — "in thee shall all families of the earth be blessed"); the "kings from thy loins" anticipated the Davidic line and ultimately the Messianic king; Matthew 1:1 — "Jesus Christ, the son of David, the son of Abraham" — the king promised to Jacob's loins at Bethel was born at Bethlehem, seven miles from where Rachel died on the journey away from Bethel
  - "And the land which I gave Abraham and Isaac, to thee I will give it, and to thy seed after thee will I give the land" — the land promise, tracing explicitly from Abraham through Isaac to Jacob/Israel and to his seed; the "seed" (zera) was the covenant word that Paul identified in Galatians 3:16 as ultimately singular: "He saith not, And to seeds, as of many; but as of one, And to thy seed, which is Christ" — the land promise given to Jacob at Bethel had a singular ultimate fulfillment in the one Seed who would inherit all things (Hebrews 1:2)

### The First Drink Offering in Scripture — Jacob at Bethel

**Genesis 35:14** — "And Jacob set up a pillar in the place where he talked with him, even a pillar of stone: and he poured a drink offering thereon, and he poured oil thereon"

- **The first drink offering recorded in Scripture:** The nesek (drink offering) appeared here for the first time in the biblical narrative; its formal institution would come later in the Mosaic law (Numbers 15:5-7; 28:7-14), but Jacob poured it out spontaneously as an act of worship at Bethel — the natural instinct of a worshipper exceeded the formal legislation that would follow; the Law later codified what the covenant person already knew: that worship involved complete pouring out
- **The Mosaic drink offering — its structure:**
  - Numbers 15:5 — "And the fourth part of an hin of wine for a drink offering shalt thou prepare with the burnt offering or sacrifice, for one lamb" — the drink offering was poured out accompanying the burnt offering; it was added to the sacrifice, not substituted for it; the wine was poured out completely — nothing was retained; the total giving was the point
  - Numbers 28:7 — "And the drink offering thereof shall be the fourth part of an hin for the one lamb: in the holy place shalt thou cause the strong wine to be poured unto the LORD for a drink offering" — "poured unto the LORD" — the drink offering was entirely given; it was not eaten or partially returned; it was the sacrifice that had no remainder; the worshipper poured it out and received nothing material back
  - Leviticus 23:13 — the drink offering with the wave sheaf at firstfruits — connected to the harvest celebration and the offering of the first and best; the drink offering was always associated with the highest moments of covenant worship
- **The theological meaning of the poured-out drink offering:**
  - Philippians 2:17 — Paul: "Yea, and if I be offered upon the sacrifice and service of your faith, I am glad, and rejoice with you all" — the word "offered" was spendomai = to be poured out as a drink offering; Paul used the drink offering image for his own life given in service; the total pouring-out, nothing retained, was the image of complete sacrifice of self for the covenant community
  - 2 Timothy 4:6 — "For I am now ready to be offered (spendomai), and the time of my departure is at hand" — Paul used the same word as his death approached; he was being poured out as a drink offering; the image Jacob originated at Bethel became Paul's image for martyrdom
  - The theological arc: Jacob poured out the first drink offering at the stone pillar of Bethel — the house of God, the place where heaven and earth met; centuries later, Christ's blood was poured out at the cross; Paul poured out his life as a drink offering in service; the first nesek at Bethel was the first instance of the total-giving worship that found its ultimate expression in the Lamb whose blood was the drink offering that needed no repetition

### Rachel's Death — Benoni, Benjamin, and the Bethlehem Connection

**Genesis 35:16-20** — Rachel travailing, dying, naming, Jacob renaming, buried at Ephrath/Bethlehem

- **The shadow of Genesis 30:24 reaching forward:** Genesis 30:24 — when Joseph was born: "And she called his name Joseph; and said, The LORD shall add to me another son" — Rachel's prayer at Joseph's birth was for another son; she received it; the son cost her her life; the prayer was answered and the answer was the door to her death; this was not punishment but the covenant's hard mathematics: the desire of her heart was given, and the price of its fulfillment was her departure
- **Jacob's unknowing curse and its proximity:** Genesis 31:32 — "With whomsoever thou findest thy gods, let him not live" — Jacob had pronounced the curse over the teraphim-stealer before Laban's search; Rachel was the teraphim-stealer; the text in Genesis 35 drew no explicit causal line; but the narrator who recorded both the curse and the death left them in proximity for the reader; the teraphim that was not dealt with at the Shechem altar (Genesis 33:20) was surrendered at the purging of Genesis 35:4 — and between the concealment and the surrender, Rachel died; the careful reader noted that the household carried the hidden idols through the covenant journey until the command to Bethel forced their surrender; and Rachel's death came after the surrender, on the road from Bethel
- **Benoni and Benjamin — the dying name and the living name:**
  - Benoni = ben (son) + oni (my sorrow, my affliction, my grief) — the name a woman gave from the threshold of death; it was the truest name of the moment: this son cost everything she had
  - Benjamin = ben (son) + yamin (right hand) — the name a father gave from the other side of grief; "son of the right hand" was the name of honor and power; Psalm 110:1 — "Sit thou at my right hand, until I make thine enemies thy footstool" — the right hand was the place of highest honor and authority; the king was seated at the right hand; the most favored son sat there; Jacob refused to let the grief-name define the child
  - The pattern of the two names: the mother's name expressed the cost of the moment; the father's name expressed the purpose beyond the moment; both were true simultaneously; Benoni was the reality from below; Benjamin was the reality from above; the same child, seen from grief and seen from grace
  - Acts 2:33 — "Therefore being by the right hand of God exalted, and having received of the Father the promise of the Holy Ghost, he hath shed forth this, which ye now see and hear" — Christ exalted to the right hand of the Father; the "right hand" language that Benjamin's name encoded in Genesis 35 traced through Psalm 110:1 to the ascension and exaltation of Christ; what Jacob declared over his last-born at the road to Bethlehem became the ultimate location of the risen Son
- **Jeremiah 31:15 and Matthew 2:17-18 — Rachel weeping at Bethlehem:**
  - Jeremiah 31:15 — "A voice was heard in Ramah, lamentation, and bitter weeping; Rachel weeping for her children refused to be comforted for her children, because they were not" — Jeremiah used Rachel as the prophetic voice of maternal grief; she wept from her grave at Bethlehem (Ramah was near Bethlehem) for the covenant children taken into exile; she was the mother who had died giving birth near Bethlehem; her grave was the place from which the voice of grief arose
  - Matthew 2:17-18 — Herod's slaughter of the infant boys in Bethlehem: "Then was fulfilled that which was spoken by Jeremy the prophet, saying, In Rama was there a voice heard, lamentation, and weeping, and great mourning, Rachel weeping for her children, and would not be comforted, because they are not" — the children killed in Bethlehem at Herod's command woke Rachel's voice again; she had died near Bethlehem giving birth to Benjamin; she wept for Israel's children taken to Babylon from near the same place; now she wept again for the infant boys killed in Bethlehem at the birth of the one who was the ultimate Benjamin — the son of the right hand — whose birth the magi sought and whom Herod's massacre tried to prevent
  - The theological thread: Rachel died near Bethlehem, the place of Benjamin's birth; Jeremiah invoked her grief for the exile; Matthew invoked her grief for Herod's massacre; the place of the dying mother's travail became the place of repeated grief over covenant children; and the child whose birth caused all of this — the Christ born in Bethlehem — was the one who bore all of Rachel's grief and all the grief of every desolate mother: Isaiah 53:4 — "Surely he hath borne our griefs, and carried our sorrows"

### Reuben's Sin — Defiling the Father's Bed and the Firstborn Pattern

**Genesis 35:22** — "Reuben went and lay with Bilhah his father's concubine: and Israel heard it"

- **The political meaning of lying with the father's concubine:** In the ancient world, inheriting or taking a father's women was a public assertion of succession — it declared the transfer of household authority from the father to the usurper:
  - 2 Samuel 16:21-22 — Ahithophel's advice to Absalom: "Go in unto thy father's concubines... and all Israel shall hear that thou art abhorred of thy father: then shall the hands of all that are with thee be strong" — the act was explicitly political; going in to the father's concubines was the declaration "I have displaced my father"; Absalom did it openly, on the roof, before all Israel; it was the act of a man claiming the throne
  - 1 Kings 2:22 — Solomon's response to Adonijah's request for Abishag the Shunammite (David's nurse): "And why dost thou ask Abishag the Shunammite for Adonijah? ask for him the kingdom also" — Solomon read the request for the king's woman as a bid for the throne; the connection between the king's women and the king's authority was immediate and absolute; Adonijah was executed for the request
  - Reuben's act with Bilhah was in this category: as the firstborn, he may have been asserting the preeminence he felt was already his by right, or acting out the territorial instinct of a son who expected to inherit his father's household including his women; whatever the motivation, the act was a power-assertion that violated his father's authority and bed
- **"Israel heard it" — the weight of the silence:** The text said nothing more at this point; no immediate rebuke, no recorded response from Jacob; just "Israel heard it" — and then the listing of the twelve sons; the silence was not absence of judgment but deferral of judgment; Genesis 49:3-4 provided what Genesis 35:22 withheld: "Reuben, thou art my firstborn, my might, and the beginning of my strength, the excellency of dignity, and the excellency of power: Unstable as water, thou shalt not excel; because thou wentest up to thy father's bed; then defiledst thou it: he went up to my couch" — the verdict waited twenty years for the appointed moment; the deathbed was the patriarch's court; the sin committed in Genesis 35 was named and judged in Genesis 49
- **The birthright's transfer — 1 Chronicles 5:1-2:** "Now the sons of Reuben the firstborn of Israel, (for he was the firstborn; but, forasmuch as he defiled his father's bed, his birthright was given unto the sons of Joseph the son of Israel: and the genealogy is not to be reckoned after the birthright. For Judah prevailed above his brethren, and of him came the chief ruler; but the birthright was Joseph's)" — the explicit statement of what Genesis 35:22 and Genesis 49:3-4 established: the birthright (the double portion) went to Joseph's sons (Ephraim and Manasseh received their own tribal allotments), and the leadership (the ruler's staff) went to Judah; Reuben's sin cost him both dimensions of the firstborn's inheritance
- **The firstborn pattern through the patriarchal narrative — the divine reversal:** The consistent pattern from Genesis 1 onward was that the natural firstborn was displaced by the divinely chosen:
  - Cain (firstborn of Adam) — his offering rejected; Abel's accepted; Genesis 4
  - Ishmael (firstborn in Abraham's household) — sent away; Isaac was the covenant son; Genesis 21
  - Esau (firstborn of Isaac) — birthright and blessing to Jacob; Genesis 25-27
  - Reuben (firstborn of Jacob) — birthright and leadership displaced; Genesis 35:22 / Genesis 49:3-4 / 1 Chronicles 5:1-2
  - The pattern's fulfillment: Colossians 1:15 — "the firstborn of every creature" / Colossians 1:18 — "the firstborn from the dead" / Romans 8:29 — "the firstborn among many brethren" — Christ was the one Firstborn who did not fail; He was the true Firstborn in whom all the displaced natural firstborns found their resolution; what Cain, Ishmael, Esau, and Reuben could not hold — the firstborn's covenant position — Christ held as the eternal and unforfeitable Firstborn

### "Gathered to His People" — Isaac's Death and the Covenant Beyond Death

**Genesis 35:28-29** — "Isaac gave up the ghost, and died, and was gathered unto his people, being old and full of days: and his sons Esau and Jacob buried him"

- **"Gathered to his people" — the covenant death language:** The phrase ne'esaf el ammav = gathered to his people; it appeared at each patriarch's death and at each significant covenant servant's death:
  - Genesis 25:8 — Abraham: "was gathered to his people"
  - Genesis 35:29 — Isaac: "was gathered unto his people"
  - Genesis 49:33 — Jacob: "was gathered unto his people"
  - Numbers 20:24 — Aaron: "gathered unto his people"
  - Numbers 27:13 — Moses: "thou also shalt be gathered unto thy people"
  - Deuteronomy 32:50 — Moses on Nebo: "be gathered unto thy people; as Aaron thy brother died in mount Hor, and was gathered unto his people"
  - The phrase was distinct from the mere fact of death; it was not synonymous with "died" — it was added after "died" as a separate statement; the people to whom the patriarch was gathered were the people who had gone before him in covenant faith; the phrase implied an ongoing community of the covenant dead into which the newly dead were received
- **Jesus's use of this language to prove resurrection:** Matthew 22:31-32 — "But as touching the resurrection of the dead, have ye not read that which was spoken unto you by God, saying, I am the God of Abraham, and the God of Isaac, and the God of Jacob? God is not the God of the dead, but of the living" — Jesus used the present tense of God's covenant identification with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob as the proof of resurrection; the patriarchs were gathered to their people — they were alive in God; He was still their God; the covenant was not dissolved by death; the "gathered to his people" language was the seed of the resurrection hope that Jesus made explicit
- **"Being old and full of days" — the Hebrew sheva:** The phrase sava yamim = satisfied/full of days; the same expression used of Abraham (Genesis 25:8 — "full of years") and Job (42:17 — "Job died, being old and full of days"); it was not merely a statement of age but of completion; a life that had received its full measure; the blessing of old age in the covenant was not prolonged life for its own sake but life that arrived at its appointed end complete; Isaac at 180 was "full of days" — not diminished, not cut short, not tragically incomplete; he had received what was given and he arrived at the end with fullness
- **Esau and Jacob together — the quiet reunion at the grave:** Genesis 25:9 — "And his sons Isaac and Ishmael buried him (Abraham) in the cave of Machpelah" — the pattern was set: the two estranged sons burying the father together; estrangement that had divided them in life was set aside at the grave; Genesis 35:29 repeated the pattern exactly: "his sons Esau and Jacob buried him" — the brothers who had been separated for twenty years by theft and threat, who had reunited with weeping at the Jabbok road (Genesis 33:4), now stood together over their father's grave; no editorial comment; no record of what was said; just the two names together over the grave at Hebron where Abraham lay; the covenant and the non-covenant son doing the one thing that death required of them — honoring the man who had fathered them both

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### EL SHADDAI — GOD ALMIGHTY (Hebrew: אֵל שַׁדַּי)

**Genesis 35:11** — "And God said unto him, I am God Almighty (El Shaddai): be fruitful and multiply"

- **Root:** El = the strong one, the powerful one (the general Semitic word for god/God); Shaddai = the root debated; three main proposals: (1) from shadad = to be powerful, to overpower — El Shaddai = the God who overpowers, the all-sufficient power; (2) from shad = breast — El Shaddai = the God who nourishes, the nursing/sustaining God (emphasizing tender sufficiency); (3) from the Akkadian šadû = mountain — El Shaddai = God of the mountains, the enthroned God; the biblical usage consistently emphasized sufficiency and power for covenant fulfillment, suggesting the shadad/overpowering root was primary
- **Every El Shaddai appearance in the patriarchal narrative:**
  - Genesis 17:1 — to Abraham: "I am the Almighty God; walk before me, and be thou perfect" — at the covenant of circumcision; the most comprehensive covenant formalization with Abraham
  - Genesis 28:3 — Isaac's blessing to Jacob: "And God Almighty bless thee, and make thee fruitful" — before Jacob's departure for Padanaram; the father invoking El Shaddai over the son
  - Genesis 35:11 — God directly to Jacob at Bethel: "I am God Almighty: be fruitful and multiply" — the covenant confirmation on the return; the same promises Isaac invoked, now given directly by El Shaddai Himself
  - Genesis 43:14 — Jacob to his sons going to Egypt: "And God Almighty give you mercy before the man" — Jacob invoking El Shaddai when he sent Benjamin into Egypt's power; the name of sovereign sufficiency called upon in the moment of greatest vulnerability
  - Genesis 48:3 — Jacob to Joseph: "God Almighty appeared unto me at Luz in the land of Canaan, and blessed me" — Jacob's deathbed recall of the Bethel appearance; the Bethel El Shaddai appearance was the anchor of Jacob's covenant memory
  - Genesis 49:25 — Jacob's blessing over Joseph: "Even by the God of thy father, who shall help thee; and by the Almighty (Shaddai), who shall bless thee" — the name given to the most beloved son; the Shaddai blessing as the highest blessing
- **Exodus 6:2-3 — the divine commentary:** "And I appeared unto Abraham, unto Isaac, and unto Jacob, by the name of God Almighty (El Shaddai), but by my name JEHOVAH was I not known to them" — God's own identification of El Shaddai as the patriarchal name; the fuller revelation of YHWH/Jehovah (the personal, self-existent name) was reserved for Moses; the patriarchs knew God as El Shaddai — the God whose power was sufficient for every promise; the Mosaic generation would know Him as YHWH — the God who existed in and of Himself and whose name was the guarantee of His word
- **El Shaddai in the New Testament:** Revelation 1:8 — "I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty (Pantokrator)" — the Greek Pantokrator (all-sovereign, all-holding) was the Septuagint's translation of El Shaddai; the name Jacob heard at Bethel was the name John heard from the throne; El Shaddai and Pantokrator were the same divine reality at two ends of the canon

### NESEK — DRINK OFFERING (Hebrew: נֶסֶךְ)

**Genesis 35:14** — "he poured a drink offering (nesek) thereon, and he poured oil thereon"

- **Root:** Nasak = to pour out, to pour as a libation; nesek = that which is poured out; the word described both the act (pouring) and the substance (the poured offering — wine or liquid); the nesek was the offering that had no remainder; it was entirely given
- **The first nesek in Scripture:** Genesis 35:14 was its first recorded instance; Jacob at Bethel poured it spontaneously, without a Mosaic law requiring it; the instinct of worship preceded the legislation of worship; what Jacob did at Bethel with wine and oil was later codified in Numbers 15 and 28; the covenant worshipper's natural overflow anticipated the Law's structure
- **The nesek in the Mosaic system:**
  - Numbers 15:5 — a quarter hin of wine with the burnt offering of a lamb; the drink offering was always given in addition to the burnt offering, not instead of it; it was the overflow of the primary sacrifice
  - Numbers 28:7 — "in the holy place shalt thou cause the strong wine to be poured unto the LORD for a drink offering" — poured in the holy place; the holiest substance in the holiest location; the total giving in the most sacred space
  - Ecclesiasticus (Sirach) 50:15 (for context) — the high priest pouring out the drink offering before the altar; the Levitical drink offering as the completion of the sacrificial act
- **The drink offering as the image of complete self-giving:**
  - Philippians 2:17 — spendomai: "Yea, and if I be offered upon the sacrifice and service of your faith, I am glad, and rejoice with you all" — Paul using the drink offering image for his own expenditure of life in apostolic service; the nesek as the metaphor for the life that poured everything out and kept nothing back
  - 2 Timothy 4:6 — "For I am now ready to be offered (spendomai), and the time of my departure is at hand" — Paul's final use of the drink offering image for his own martyrdom; to be poured out as a nesek was to be completely given, nothing retained, everything spent for the covenant purpose
  - The ultimate drink offering: the blood of Christ poured out at the cross was the divine nesek; what Jacob enacted in type at Bethel with wine and oil, Christ enacted in ultimate reality with His own blood; Isaiah 53:12 — "he hath poured out his soul unto death" — the verb shaphak (poured out) was the pouring of the ultimate drink offering; the priest who was also the sacrifice, poured out completely before the Father

### BENONI / BENJAMIN — SON OF MY SORROW / SON OF THE RIGHT HAND (Hebrew: בֶּן-אוֹנִי / בִּנְיָמִין)

**Genesis 35:18** — "she called his name Benoni: but his father called him Benjamin"

- **Benoni:** Ben (son) + oni (my strength/sorrow/trouble); the root aven/on in the sorrow sense was the word for grief, affliction, iniquity (the same root gave aven = worthlessness/vanity); Rachel named the child from the threshold of death with the truest name of the moment; the son cost her everything; she named him with the cost
- **Benjamin:** Ben (son) + yamin (right hand, south — in Hebrew, the south was associated with the right hand because of east-facing orientation); the right hand was the place of honor, strength, and authority in the ancient world and throughout Scripture; Jacob refused the grief-name and gave the honor-name
- **The right hand in Scripture — Benjamin's name and its trajectory:**
  - Psalm 110:1 — "The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at my right hand, until I make thine enemies thy footstool" — the Messianic throne at the right hand; Benjamin's name was "son of the right hand"; the tribe of Benjamin produced Saul (Israel's first king) and Paul (the apostle to the Gentiles); but the right hand ultimately belonged to the Son of David, the Christ
  - Acts 2:33 — "Therefore being by the right hand of God exalted, and having received of the Father the promise of the Holy Ghost, he hath shed forth this, which ye now see and hear" — Christ's exaltation to the right hand; the fulfillment of Psalm 110:1; the ultimate Benjamin — the Son who was born through death's door into the place of supreme honor
  - Romans 11:1 — Paul: "For I also am an Israelite, of the seed of Abraham, of the tribe of Benjamin" — Paul came from the tribe born in Rachel's dying; the man who called himself the chief of sinners (1 Timothy 1:15) came from the tribe born in sorrow; his name in Hebrew (Saul) was the name of the tribe's most famous king; God took the son born in the worst moment and made the tribe that carried both kings and apostles
- **The dying-mother / living-father name pattern:** The pattern of two names — one given by the dying/grief-stricken, one given by the living/faith-filled — appeared again at the cross: John 19:26-27 — Jesus gave His mother to John; the one who was dying named the new relationship; but the risen Christ's exaltation to the right hand was the living Father's renaming of the event — not "the death" but "the resurrection"; not Benoni but Benjamin

### CHITTATH ELOHIM — THE TERROR OF GOD (Hebrew: חִתַּת אֱלֹהִים)

**Genesis 35:5** — "the terror of God (chittath elohim) was upon the cities that were round about them"

- **Root:** Chathath = to be shattered, to be dismayed, to be broken in spirit; chittath = that which shatters or dismays; chittath elohim = the dismay/terror that comes from God; it was not merely fear of Jacob's sons but a God-induced paralysis of will; the surrounding cities could not pursue even if they had wanted to
- **The chittath thread through Scripture:**
  - Genesis 35:5 — the first use; the divine terror protecting the covenant household on the road to Bethel
  - Exodus 23:27 — God's promise: "I will send my fear (eithi) before thee" — the promise of the divine-sent terror as the instrument of advance
  - Deuteronomy 2:25 — "the dread (pachad) of thee and the fear of thee upon the nations" — the fullness of the divine-terror promise articulated before the conquest
  - Joshua 2:9 — Rahab: "your terror (eitimachem) is fallen upon us" — the report of the chittath at work; every city Rahab described was already broken in spirit before Israel arrived
  - Deuteronomy 32:25 — "from without the sword shall bereave, and from the chambers terror (emah)" — the terror working in both directions — the sword outside, the fear within; the comprehensive dismay
  - Isaiah 8:13 — "Sanctify the LORD of hosts himself; and let him be your fear, and let him be your dread" — the covenant people's own relationship to the divine terror; the chittath that fell on Israel's enemies was the same quality as the fear of God that was Israel's wisdom; Proverbs 1:7 — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom"

### ALLON-BACHUTH — OAK OF WEEPING (Hebrew: אַלּוֹן בָּכוּת)

**Genesis 35:8** — "Deborah Rebekah's nurse died, and she was buried beneath Beth-el under an oak: and the name of it was called Allon-bachuth"

- **Root:** Allon = oak, a large tree (from ul or ayil = strength; the oak as the tree of strength); bachuth = weeping (from bakah = to weep, to cry); the oak named for the weeping that occurred at the death of this woman — a nurse, barely mentioned, whose death produced enough grief to name a tree
- **Who was Deborah:** She was the nurse of Rebekah — Jacob's mother; she had apparently come with Jacob from Padanaram or had been with the household; she was old, certainly older than Jacob himself; she had nursed the woman who sent Jacob away; she was the living link to Rebekah who had died in Jacob's absence (Genesis 27:46 and the narrative implied Rebekah's death); the grief at Deborah's death may have been both for her and for the mother she represented — the mourning for Rebekah that had never been mourned because Jacob was not there
- **The oak as the tree of covenant moments in Genesis:**
  - Genesis 12:6 — Abraham at the oak/terebinth of Moreh at Shechem: the first land-promise
  - Genesis 13:18 — Abraham at the oaks of Mamre at Hebron: his covenant dwelling place
  - Genesis 14:13 — Abraham dwelt by the oaks of Mamre
  - Genesis 18:1 — the LORD appeared to Abraham by the oaks of Mamre
  - Genesis 35:4 — the idols buried under the oak at Shechem
  - Genesis 35:8 — Deborah buried under the oak at Bethel — named Allon-bachuth
  - The oak was the covenant tree; significant events happened at oaks; the oaks marked covenant locations and covenant moments; the oak of weeping was the specific tree that named the grief that accompanied covenant return — you cannot come back to the house of God without also passing through the oak of weeping

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### The Earrings Were Buried with the Idols — Therefore All Jewelry Is Forbidden

**The Misuse:** Genesis 35:4 — the earrings were buried alongside the strange gods — is used to argue that all personal adornment, specifically earrings, was prohibited as idolatrous; that the text equated wearing jewelry with idol worship.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The text specified that these particular earrings were surrendered alongside the strange gods — not that all earrings were idolatrous:** The earrings of v.4 were those in the hands and ears of the household members who had come out of Padanaram with their teraphim; they were surrendered as part of the household purging of everything associated with the pagan worship and culture of Laban's household; the text did not state that earrings were inherently idolatrous
- **The Exodus 32:2-3 connection established the cultural context:** Aaron used the specific earrings of the Israelites to make the golden calf; in the culture of Padanaram and Egypt, earrings were frequently votive objects — dedicated to specific deities, received at cultic ceremonies, or worn as protective amulets for specific gods; the earrings of Genesis 35:4 were surrendered because of this cultural association, not because earrings as a form of adornment were forbidden
- **The Bible's own record of jewelry without condemnation:** Genesis 24:22 — Abraham's servant gave Rebekah a golden earring and bracelets: objects of honor in the covenant household; Ezekiel 16:11-12 — God's description of His covenant with Israel used jewelry as the image of His blessing: "I decked thee also with ornaments, and I put bracelets upon thy hands, and a chain on thy neck. And I put a jewel on thy forehead, and earrings in thine ears"; Isaiah 61:10 — "as a bride adorneth herself with her jewels" — adornment as an image of covenant joy; the New Testament caution (1 Timothy 2:9; 1 Peter 3:3) was against costly display as the primary source of beauty, not against jewelry itself
- *Correction:* Genesis 35:4 recorded the surrender of earrings that were culturally and personally associated with the pagan worship of Laban's household — part of the comprehensive purging before approaching Bethel. The text did not prohibit earrings universally. The broader biblical record included honorable uses of jewelry as covenant gifts (Genesis 24:22), divine blessings (Ezekiel 16:11-12), and images of covenant joy (Isaiah 61:10). The earrings buried at Shechem were the earrings of the idol-culture; the text did not extend this to all ornamentation.

### "Israel Heard It" — Jacob Did Nothing and Therefore Condoned Reuben's Sin

**The Misuse:** Genesis 35:22 — "Israel heard it" — is read as Jacob's passive acceptance of Reuben's sin; that his silence was consent; that the text recorded no rebuke and therefore Jacob had no moral response.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The text recorded hearing, not responding — deferral, not acceptance:** "Israel heard it" and then moved immediately to the listing of the twelve sons; the narrative moved on; but the deferred-verdict pattern was by now established in the Jacob narrative; Genesis 34:30's pragmatic rebuke was followed by Genesis 49:5-7's full moral verdict; the same structure operated here; Genesis 49:3-4 provided what Genesis 35:22 withheld
- **Genesis 49:3-4 — the full verdict, twenty years later:** "Reuben, thou art my firstborn, my might, and the beginning of my strength, the excellency of dignity, and the excellency of power: Unstable as water, thou shalt not excel; because thou wentest up to thy father's bed; then defiledst thou it: he went up to my couch" — the deathbed blessing was the patriarch's appointed court; Jacob had heard the sin in Genesis 35; he delivered the verdict in Genesis 49; the silence of Genesis 35:22 was not moral emptiness but the reserve of the patriarch who would speak at the proper time
- **1 Chronicles 5:1-2 confirmed the consequences were real and permanent:** "forasmuch as he defiled his father's bed, his birthright was given unto the sons of Joseph" — the consequence was enacted; the birthright was transferred; the silence of Genesis 35 did not prevent the judgment of Genesis 49 from producing real tribal consequences; the hearing was the beginning of the reckoning, not the end of it
- *Correction:* "Israel heard it" was the recording of the patriarch's knowledge, not his consent. The biblical pattern of the deferred verdict — established in Genesis 34:30 → 49:5-7 for Simeon and Levi — operated identically here: Genesis 35:22 (the hearing) → Genesis 49:3-4 (the verdict). The silence was the patriarch's reserve. The judgment was real, permanent, and enacted.

### Benoni Was the True Name — Jacob Renamed Him to Avoid Grief

**The Misuse:** Genesis 35:18 — Jacob renamed Benoni as Benjamin — is read as a denial of grief, a refusal to acknowledge the reality of Rachel's death; that Benoni was the honest name and Benjamin was Jacob's avoidance of the pain.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Two names were not the denial of grief but its transformation:** Benoni was the name from below — the name of the moment of death; it was fully true as Rachel named it; her soul was departing; the cost was absolute; the grief was real; Jacob did not erase the cost by giving a different name; the text preserved both names; the Bible honored Rachel's name by recording it; the grief was not denied
- **Benjamin was the name from above — the name of purpose and honor:** The right hand in Scripture was not an avoidance of death but the destination beyond it; Psalm 110:1 — "Sit thou at my right hand" — the right hand was where the exalted, the honored, the victorious were placed; Jacob gave the child the name of the destination that death was the door to; this was not avoidance but resurrection-faith; the same faith that said "though he slay me, yet will I trust in him" (Job 13:15) named the child of death as the son of the right hand
- **The pattern in Scripture of the renaming as elevation, not denial:** Genesis 32:28 — God renamed Jacob "Israel" — not to deny his Jacob-nature (the text continued to call him Jacob throughout) but to name the covenant reality that had been born through the wrestling; the new name did not erase the old; both were true; Benoni and Benjamin were both real; the grief was real; the honor was also real; both names were preserved in the text because both were true
- **Christ and the pattern:** The cross was not renamed away from being a death; it was always both Golgotha (the place of the skull — Benoni) and the right hand of the Father (Benjamin — Acts 2:33); the death was not denied; the honor was declared as the destination beyond it
- *Correction:* Jacob's renaming of Benoni as Benjamin was not denial of Rachel's grief but the declaration of the child's covenant identity beyond the death that produced him. Both names were true. Benoni named the cost; Benjamin named the destination. The pattern was the same as every covenant renaming in Genesis: not erasure but elevation. The grief was honored by being recorded; the honor was given by the living father looking past the death to the right hand where the son would be known.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Purging Before Entering — The Prerequisites Thread

**Genesis 35:2-4 — put away the strange gods, be clean, change garments**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 35:2-4 — Jacob's household purging before Bethel; the three-part preparation: put away foreign gods, be clean, change garments; the comprehensive preparation for the covenant place
- Exodus 19:10-11 — Sinai preparation: "Go unto the people, and sanctify them to day and to morrow, and let them wash their clothes: And be ready against the third day" — the same three elements: moral preparation, physical washing, time-bounded readiness; the covenant God required prepared approach
- Joshua 3:5 — Joshua: "Sanctify yourselves: for to morrow the LORD will do wonders among you" — the preparation for the Jordan crossing; sanctification before the divine act
- 2 Chronicles 29:15-16 — Hezekiah's cleansing of the temple: "And they gathered their brethren, and sanctified themselves, and came, according to the commandment of the king, by the words of the LORD, to cleanse the house of the LORD. And the priests went into the inner part of the house of the LORD, to cleanse it, and brought out all the uncleanness that they found in the temple of the LORD" — the institutional purging before the national covenant renewal
- Revelation 19:7-8 — "the marriage of the Lamb is come, and his wife hath made herself ready. And to her was granted that she should be arrayed in fine linen, clean and white" — the eschatological preparation; the church ready for the Lamb; the fine linen was righteousness; the preparation was the putting away of everything that defiled and the putting on of the clean garments; the echo of Genesis 35:2 at the consummation: "be clean and change your garments" → "arrayed in fine linen, clean and white"
- The theological constant: the covenant God required preparation for His presence; not to earn the access (the access was by grace) but to acknowledge the holiness of the One being approached; the purging was the outward expression of the inward decision to have no other gods; every time the covenant people came to God after a period of compromise, the first act was the purging

### The Pillar — Covenant Memory in Stone

**Genesis 28:18 / Genesis 35:14, 20 — three pillars in the Jacob narrative**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 28:18 — the first pillar: the stone Jacob had slept on at Bethel, set upright and anointed; the memorial of the place where heaven opened; the pillar of the vow
- Genesis 31:45-52 — the pillar with Laban at Galeed/Mizpah; the heap of witness; the boundary stones
- Genesis 35:14 — the second Bethel pillar: set up at the covenant confirmation; drink offering and oil poured; the fulfillment of the Genesis 28 vow sealed in stone
- Genesis 35:20 — the pillar over Rachel's grave: "that is the pillar of Rachel's grave unto this day" — the memorial of grief and love; the pillar not of covenant promise but of covenant love
- Exodus 24:4 — Moses: "Moses wrote all the words of the LORD, and rose up early in the morning, and builded an altar under the hill, and twelve pillars, according to the twelve tribes of Israel" — the covenant ratification at Sinai with twelve pillars; one for each tribe
- Isaiah 19:19 — "In that day shall there be an altar to the LORD in the midst of the land of Egypt, and a pillar (matstsebah) at the border thereof to the LORD" — the eschatological pillar; the covenant memorial in Egypt; the nations' covenant return marked by a stone
- 1 Peter 2:4-5 — "To whom coming, as unto a living stone, disallowed indeed of men, but chosen of God, and precious. Ye also, as lively stones, are built up a spiritual house" — the stone theology of the New Testament: Christ as the living stone; the believers as living stones; what Jacob had set up as memorial pillars, Christ became as the ultimate living memorial; the pillar theology resolved in the living stone that was rejected but became the chief cornerstone

### The Voice at Bethlehem — Rachel's Thread

**Genesis 35:16-20 — Rachel dies near Bethlehem**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 35:16-19 — Rachel dies giving birth to Benjamin near Ephrath/Bethlehem; the first grief at Bethlehem; the pillar set over her grave
- Ruth 4:11 — "like Rachel and as Leah, which two did build the house of Israel... do thou worthily in Ephratah, and be famous in Bethlehem" — Ruth entering the covenant at Bethlehem; Rachel named as the covenant mother; the place of Rachel's death became the place of Ruth's covenant entry and Boaz's marriage — Bethlehem as the place of both covenant grief and covenant harvest
- Micah 5:2 — "But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah, though thou be little among the thousands of Judah, yet out of thee shall he come forth unto me that is to be ruler in Israel; whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting" — the town of Rachel's death named as the birthplace of the Messiah; from the place of the mother's dying, the ultimate Son of the right hand would be born
- Jeremiah 31:15 — Rachel weeping for the children taken to Babylon; her voice from Bethlehem; the grief that would not be comforted
- Matthew 2:1, 16-18 — Christ born in Bethlehem; the infants slaughtered; Rachel's voice for the third time; the town of the dying mother became the town of the dying children; and the child who escaped was the One who would ultimately redeem every Benoni and name them Benjamin

### The Covenant Renewal Pattern — Purge, Journey, Encounter, Declare

**Genesis 35 as the pattern of covenant renewal**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 35:1-4 — God calls Jacob to Bethel; Jacob purges the household; they prepare; they surrender idols and earrings
- Genesis 35:5 — they journey; the divine terror protects
- Genesis 35:9-13 — God appears; confirms the name; speaks El Shaddai; reaffirms the covenant promises
- Genesis 35:14-15 — Jacob sets the pillar; pours the drink offering and oil; names the place
- 1 Samuel 7:3-6 — Samuel's pattern: (1) put away the Baals and Ashtaroth; (2) gather at Mizpah; (3) draw water and pour it out before the LORD (the same pouring-out gesture); (4) fast; (5) God delivers from the Philistines at Ebenezer
- 2 Chronicles 34:1-8 — Josiah's pattern: (1) purge the idols; (2) repair the house of God; (3) discover the book of the covenant; (4) weep before God; (5) God's covenant confirmed through Huldah; (6) the national covenant renewal
- Nehemiah 8-10 — Post-exile pattern: (1) Ezra reads the Law; (2) the people weep; (3) they celebrate Sukkot (the Feast of Booths — sukkot = the same word as Succoth in Genesis 33:17); (4) they confess; (5) they make covenant
- Revelation 2-3 — Christ's pattern for the seven churches: (1) what you have done well; (2) what you must repent of; (3) repent; (4) receive the promise
- The theological constant: covenant renewal always required (1) the purging of what competed with God; (2) the humble journey back to the covenant place; (3) the divine encounter and confirmation; (4) the public declaration by the covenant person

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time God Formally Reaffirmed the Full Covenant Trilogy (Fruitfulness, Nations, Land) to the Third Patriarch in Direct Speech

**Genesis 35:11-12** — El Shaddai's threefold covenant declaration to Jacob
- The first time all three covenant elements were spoken directly by God to Jacob personally (as opposed to through Isaac's blessing or the Bethel dream's summary)
- First mention establishes: the covenant was not automatically transferred genealogically; it was personally confirmed by God to each patriarch in His own time and by His own appearance; Isaac received it in Genesis 26:2-5; Jacob received it here in Genesis 35:11-12; the personal divine confirmation was not a formality but a genuine covenant-of-relationship encounter; the covenant was not merely inherited but personally entered and personally confirmed

### First Recorded Drink Offering in Scripture

**Genesis 35:14** — "he poured a drink offering thereon"
- The nesek appeared here before any Mosaic legislation required it
- First mention establishes: the worship instinct of the covenant person anticipated the Law's codification; the drink offering's meaning was total giving, nothing retained; Jacob's spontaneous nesek at Bethel was the first instance of a worship pattern that would find its formal codification in Numbers 15 and its ultimate expression in Christ's blood poured out; the first nesek at the house of God (Bethel) was the seed of every subsequent pouring-out that the covenant system required

### First Time Two Estranged Brothers Were Recorded Burying a Father Together — Establishing the Pattern

**Genesis 35:29** — "his sons Esau and Jacob buried him" after Genesis 25:9's prior instance
- Actually Genesis 25:9 was first (Ishmael and Isaac burying Abraham); Genesis 35:29 was the second instance and confirmed the pattern
- First mention principle (from Genesis 25:9, confirmed here): at the death of the patriarch, the estranged sons were gathered; the grave was the place of provisional reconciliation; the covenant and non-covenant son buried the father together; the pattern became a theological statement: death was the equalizer that brought even the estranged to the same grave; it was not full reconciliation but a dignified completion; it was honored in Genesis 35:29's repetition of the pattern

### First Time the Full Twelve Tribes Were Listed as a Complete Unit

**Genesis 35:22-26** — the formal listing of all twelve
- The nation of Israel in its complete seed form appeared for the first time
- First mention establishes: the governmental completion of the covenant people required twelve; the twelve was not arbitrary but the number of divine governmental fullness; Revelation 21:12-14 confirmed this by inscribing both the twelve tribes and the twelve apostles on the gates and foundations of the New Jerusalem; the first complete listing in Genesis 35 was the seed of every subsequent twelve in Scripture

## NAME MEANINGS

| Name/Term | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| El Shaddai | God Almighty / God All-Sufficient | El (the strong one) + Shaddai (overpowering power/mountain/sufficient one); the patriarchal covenant name; Exodus 6:3 identified it as the name by which the patriarchs knew God; Revelation 1:8 carried it as Pantokrator |
| Benoni | Son of my sorrow | Ben (son) + oni (my affliction/grief); Rachel's dying name for the child born through her death; the name from below the moment |
| Benjamin | Son of the right hand | Ben (son) + yamin (right hand, south); Jacob's living name for the same child; the name from above the moment; Psalm 110:1 and Acts 2:33 as the ultimate right-hand destination |
| Allon-bachuth | Oak of weeping | Allon (oak — strength) + bachuth (weeping, from bakah); the tree named for the grief over Deborah's death; the covenant is accompanied by weeping; the house of God is reached through the oak of tears |
| Bethel | House of God | Beth (house) + El (God); the place where Jacob had the ladder dream; the covenant location; Genesis 28 and 35 as its two appearances in Jacob's story — the vow and its fulfillment |
| El-bethel | God of the house of God | El (God) + Bethel (house of God); the altar name Jacob gave at the place of God's appearance; naming not the location but the God encountered at the location |
| Ephrath / Bethlehem | Fruitful place / House of Bread | Ephrath = fruitfulness; Bethlehem = house of bread (beth + lechem); the place of Rachel's death was also the place of David's birth and Christ's birth; the place of dying became the place of life-giving |
| Nesek | Drink offering | From nasak (to pour out); the totally-given offering that had no remainder; first appeared at Bethel; codified at Sinai; imaged in Philippians 2:17 and 2 Timothy 4:6; fulfilled in the blood of Christ |
