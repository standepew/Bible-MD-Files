## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 39 was structured around a single repeated declaration that the narrator placed at the chapter's beginning, middle, and end like load-bearing columns: "the LORD was with Joseph." Four times the statement appeared — in v.2, v.3, v.21, and v.23 — and the narrative was built around proving it in circumstances that should have made it implausible. A man sold by his brothers, purchased as a slave in a foreign country, falsely accused by his master's wife, and imprisoned without trial — and in every one of these conditions the text insisted: the LORD was with him.

Joseph arrived in Egypt already purchased by Potiphar, Pharaoh's captain of the guard. The text moved immediately to its theological point: "The LORD was with Joseph, and he was a prosperous man." The prosperity was not delayed or conditional — it was declared at the moment of his most profound degradation. He was a slave. He was in a foreign country. His family believed him dead. And: the LORD was with him. Potiphar saw it — an Egyptian, a pagan official, saw the LORD's presence with the Hebrew slave — and he responded by making Joseph overseer over his house and all that he had. Then came the consequence of the covenant seed being present in a pagan household: "the LORD blessed the Egyptian's house for Joseph's sake." The Abrahamic covenant promise of Genesis 12:3 — "I will bless them that bless thee" — was operating even in Potiphar's household. Potiphar had not intended to bless Abraham's seed; he had bought a slave. But the covenant was not waiting for Potiphar's intention.

The delegation was total: "he left all that he had in Joseph's hand; and he knew not ought he had, save the bread which he did eat." This was the first of three complete delegations in the Joseph narrative — Potiphar to Joseph, then the prison keeper to Joseph (v.22-23), then Pharaoh to Joseph (Genesis 41:40-41). Each was a greater sphere of authority; each was based on the same evidence: the LORD's visible blessing on everything Joseph handled. The pattern was the living proof of Luke 16:10 — "He that is faithful in that which is least is faithful also in much."

Joseph was also described as "a goodly person, and well favoured" — handsome and physically attractive. The description was preparation for what followed. Potiphar's wife saw him and said: "Lie with me." The text gave no further context. No relationship history, no pretense, no gradual approach. The words were direct and the approach was without ceremony. Joseph's response was a structured defense in three movements: first, Potiphar's trust — his master had committed everything to his hand; second, the marriage covenant — "thou art his wife"; third, and most decisively — "how then can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God?" The third ground was the deepest and the most revealing. Joseph's ultimate argument was not about Potiphar, not about consequences, not about reputation. It was about God. The same theological structure David used in Psalm 51:4 — "Against thee, thee only, have I sinned" — was operative in Joseph's refusal: the ultimate audience was God, and the ultimate standard was God's character. Sin was not wrong because it would harm him or offend Potiphar; sin was wrong because it was sin against God.

She pressed him "day by day" (v.10). The temptation was not a single dramatic moment but a persistent, daily test. He hearkened not to her — "to lie by her, or to be with her." He avoided not only the act but the proximity that enabled it. Then came the day when none of the men of the house were within. She caught him by his garment. He fled. He left the garment in her hand and got out — the physical garment as the price of faithful flight; the second of the three garment-moments in Joseph's narrative, each marking a stage of his descent-and-ascent arc. The first coat (Genesis 37:23) was stripped by his brothers at the betrayal; this garment was seized by the false accuser; the fine linen (Genesis 41:42) would be given by Pharaoh at the exaltation.

She used the garment as evidence. She called her household servants and told the story — already shaping it before Potiphar arrived. Then she told it again to Potiphar, with a slight variation that made Joseph sound more aggressive. Potiphar's "wrath was kindled" and he put Joseph in the prison where the king's prisoners were held. But this was the chapter's second great theological point: Potiphar did not execute Joseph. He was the captain of the guard — he had the authority and the expected obligation to execute a slave accused of this offense. The text never said he believed his wife. The prison rather than the grave was a "soft landing" — and it was specifically the king's prison, the place that positioned Joseph for his encounter with Pharaoh's officials in chapter 40.

Then: "But the LORD was with Joseph." The word "but" was the hinge. Despite the false accusation, despite the imprisonment, despite every circumstance that argued for divine abandonment — the LORD was with him, showed him hesed (covenant lovingkindness), gave him favor in the sight of the prison keeper. The keeper committed all the prisoners into Joseph's hand. He did not look to anything that was under Joseph's supervision — because the LORD was with him, and that which he did, the LORD made to prosper (tsalach — to accomplish its purpose, to advance). The word tsalach at the end of the chapter was the same word of Genesis 39:2-3 at its beginning. The chapter that opened with Joseph prospering in Potiphar's house closed with him prospering in the prison — the same prosperity in the highest position available to a slave and in the lowest position available to a prisoner.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "The LORD Was With Joseph" — The Fourfold Declaration and Its Biblical Frame

**Genesis 39:2, 3, 21, 23** — stated four times in one chapter

- **The fourfold repetition as deliberate theological emphasis:** Ancient Hebrew narrative used repetition not for literary padding but as a device of emphasis and proof; to say "the LORD was with Joseph" once was to assert it; to say it four times in twenty-three verses was to insist on it against every circumstance that contested it; the chapter's circumstances were: slave status, foreign country, daily harassment, false accusation, imprisonment without trial; the fourfold declaration was the narrator's counter-argument to every one of those circumstances; the theology was not stated in spite of the circumstances but in direct confrontation with them

- **The pattern of "God is with him" as the divine stamp on a covenant life:**
  - Genesis 21:22 — Abimelech and Phicol to Abraham: "God is with thee in all that thou doest" — a pagan king recognized the divine presence on the covenant man; the same pattern as Potiphar recognizing the LORD's presence with Joseph (Genesis 39:3); in both cases a Gentile ruler made the theological observation
  - Genesis 26:28 — Abimelech and his companions to Isaac: "We saw certainly that the LORD was with thee: and we said, Let there now be an oath betwixt us and thee, and let us make a covenant with thee" — they came to make a covenant with Isaac because they could see the LORD's presence; the visible blessing was the evidence; the same evidence drove Potiphar's trust in Joseph
  - Joshua 1:5, 9 — God to Joshua: "I will be with thee: I will not fail thee, nor forsake thee... Be strong and of a good courage; be not afraid, neither be thou dismayed: for the LORD thy God is with thee whithersoever thou goest" — the divine presence promise given to the covenant leader stepping into the most dangerous assignment; the same promise sustained Joseph in the most dangerous assignment
  - Judges 6:12 — the angel to Gideon in the winepress: "The LORD is with thee, thou mighty man of valour" — spoken to a man hiding from the Midianites in a winepress; God's statement of presence given in the context of weakness and fear; the same pattern as the LORD being with Joseph in the slave-house and the prison
  - Matthew 1:23 — Immanuel: "God with us" — the ultimate expression of the divine-presence theme; the covenant presence that had been with Abraham, Isaac, Joseph, Joshua, and Gideon became incarnate in Jesus Christ; the fourfold "LORD was with Joseph" was the Old Testament anticipation of the definitive divine presence in the Son
  - Matthew 28:20 — Christ's final words: "lo, I am with you alway, even unto the end of the world" — the covenant promise given by Christ to His disciples was in the same register as all previous instances of divine presence; the pattern that ran through Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, and Joshua reached its final form in the abiding presence of Christ with His church

- **The active, fruit-producing nature of the divine presence:**
  - The text was precise: the LORD was with Joseph AND he was a prosperous man (v.2); the LORD was with him AND the LORD made all that he did to prosper (v.3); the LORD was with him AND showed him mercy AND gave him favor (v.21); the LORD was with him AND made it to prosper (v.23); in every case the divine presence was not merely affective but operative — it produced observable, measurable results; Potiphar could see it; the prison keeper could see it; the presence of God was not a private spiritual experience but a publicly visible reality that altered the observable world around the bearer
  - Isaiah 43:2-3 — "When thou passest through the waters, I will be with thee; and through the rivers, they shall not overflow thee; when thou walkest through the fire, thou shalt not be burned; and the flame shall not kindle upon thee. For I am the LORD thy God, the Holy One of Israel, thy Saviour" — the divine presence that preserved through water and fire; Joseph went through the figurative waters of slavery and the fire of false accusation; he was not consumed; Isaiah's promise was the theological articulation of what Genesis 39 demonstrated narratively
  - Acts 7:9-10 — Stephen's sermon identified the pattern explicitly: "And the patriarchs, moved with envy, sold Joseph into Egypt: but God was with him, and delivered him out of all his afflictions, and gave him favour and wisdom in the sight of Pharaoh king of Egypt; and he made him governor over Egypt and all his house" — the Holy Spirit through Stephen compressed the entire Joseph story under the single phrase "God was with him"; the connecting thread through all of Joseph's afflictions and all of his elevations was the identical divine presence

### Joseph's Refusal — The Three Grounds and Their Theological Depth

**Genesis 39:8-9** — "he refused... how then can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God?"

- **The structure of Joseph's refusal — three grounds in ascending theological order:**
  - Ground one (v.8): Potiphar's trust — "my master wotteth not what is with me in the house, and he hath committed all that he hath to my hand" — the covenantal dimension of the servant-master relationship; Potiphar had given Joseph his complete trust; to violate his wife was to violate the most fundamental trust a man could place in another; this was the social and relational ground
  - Ground two (v.9a): The marriage covenant — "there is none greater in this house than I; neither hath he kept back any thing from me but thee, because thou art his wife" — the marriage covenant as the absolute boundary; "thou art his wife" was the four-word statement that made the act categorically impossible; the marriage covenant was inviolable regardless of who held power in the household; this was the covenantal-legal ground
  - Ground three (v.9b): God — "how then can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God?" — the deepest ground; not Potiphar's feelings, not social consequences, not fear of punishment, but the recognition that the act was wickedness before God; the transgression was primarily a transgression against God, not against man; this was the theological ground

- **Psalm 51:4 — David's confession as the theological parallel:** "Against thee, thee only, have I sinned, and done this evil in thy sight: that thou mightest be justified when thou speakest, and be clear when thou judgest" — David after committing the sin Joseph refused committed; David's confession named God as the primary offended party; both Joseph's refusal and David's confession were structured on the same theological principle: the ultimate audience of human moral action was God; the difference was that Joseph held this principle before the act; David recovered it after; both declarations acknowledged that sin's primary dimension was vertical, not horizontal
- **Proverbs 15:3 — the watching God as the ground of integrity:** "The eyes of the LORD are in every place, beholding the evil and the good" — the theological basis for Joseph's refusal; there was no scenario in which God was not watching; "none of the men of the house there within" (v.11) was not a condition in which God's eyes were absent; the integrity that operates when no human observer is present because God's observation is constant was the definition of the character Genesis 39 demonstrated
- **1 Corinthians 6:18-20 — the New Testament command built on this pattern:** "Flee fornication... know ye not that your body is the temple of the Holy Ghost which is in you, which ye have of God, and ye are not your own? For ye are bought with a price: therefore glorify God in your body, and in your spirit, which are God's" — Paul's command was grounded in the same theological logic as Joseph's refusal: the body was God's; the offense of sexual immorality was ultimately against God in whose temple the offense occurred; the command to flee was the command Joseph enacted before Paul stated it; the pattern of Genesis preceded the principle of the epistle
- **Hebrews 13:4 — marriage as the absolute boundary:** "Marriage is honourable in all, and the bed undefiled: but whoremongers and adulterers God will judge" — Joseph's "thou art his wife" was the practical application of this principle; the marriage covenant of another was the absolute boundary; the divine judgment on violation was not in doubt; Joseph's refusal was the living demonstration of the principle Hebrews would articulate

### The Daily Temptation — "She Spake to Joseph Day by Day"

**Genesis 39:10** — "And it came to pass, as she spake to Joseph day by day, that he hearkened not unto her, to lie by her, or to be with her"

- **The persistence of the temptation:** The attack was not a single dramatic moment but an ongoing, sustained campaign; "day by day" (yom yom — literally "day day" as an emphatic idiom for repeated daily activity); this was not one great test followed by victory but a continuous test that required continuous faithfulness; the nature of the temptation was its persistence, not its intensity at any single moment
- **"Hearkened not unto her, to lie by her, or to be with her":** The refusal was comprehensive — not merely to the act (to lie by her) but to the proximity (to be with her); Joseph avoided both the act and the situation that enabled the act; 2 Timothy 2:22 — "Flee also youthful lusts: but follow righteousness" — the flight from temptation was not passive but active; Joseph did not wait until the moment of maximum pressure and then resist; he systematically avoided the conditions that increased the pressure; this was the wisdom of the command in 1 Corinthians 6:18 and 2 Timothy 2:22 — flee the category, not merely the specific instance
- **The pattern of daily temptation in the larger biblical narrative:** The daily nature of this test prepared the reader for the character of Joseph that would be required in his later responsibilities; a man who could not hold his integrity under daily persistent temptation in a household would not be able to administer the integrity required to govern Egypt's grain stores with every nation dependent on his honesty; the small daily faithfulness was the preparation for the great responsibility; Luke 16:10 — "He that is faithful in that which is least is faithful also in much: and he that is unjust in the least is unjust also in much" — the daily faithfulness in the face of Potiphar's wife was the proof of the faithfulness that would manage Egypt's seven years of abundance and seven years of famine

### Fled and Got Out — The Biblical Command Enacted Before It Was Commanded

**Genesis 39:12** — "he left his garment in her hand, and fled, and got him out"

- **The physical reality of the flight:** Joseph did not argue further, did not reason with her, did not attempt a measured response; he left his garment — the physical cost of leaving was the garment she held; better to lose the garment than to lose his integrity; the garment was the price of the faithful flight; and the garment in her hand became the instrument of the false accusation — what was left behind in faithfulness became the evidence manufactured against him; the cost of obedience was real and immediate; the vindication would be distant and delayed
- **1 Corinthians 6:18 — "Flee (pheugo) fornication":** The Greek pheugete (plural imperative) meant "run away, take flight, escape"; it was the same concept as Joseph's physical running from the house; Paul did not say "withstand fornication" or "reason against fornication" or "manage fornication carefully"; the command was to flee, to physically remove oneself from the situation; the New Testament command was the theological articulation of the Genesis 39 pattern — Joseph enacted what Paul would later command
- **2 Timothy 2:22 — "Flee also youthful lusts":** The instruction was categorical; flee lusts as a general category; the specific form (fornication, covetousness, pride) mattered less than the disposition: take flight; Joseph's flight from the physical situation was the Genesis embodiment of this principle
- **The garment left behind as a pattern of faithful cost:** Joseph lost something real — his garment, and immediately thereafter his freedom — in the act of faithful obedience; the cost of faithfulness was not theoretical; Matthew 16:25 — "For whosoever will save his life shall lose it: and whosoever will lose his life for my sake shall find it" — the paradox of gain through loss; what Joseph lost (garment, freedom) became the instrument of what he gained (proximity to Pharaoh's prisoners, and through them, to Pharaoh himself); the loss was the door

### The False Accusation — The Righteous Man Condemned Unjustly

**Genesis 39:13-19** — Potiphar's wife's twice-told accusation

- **The structure of the false accusation — told twice with variation:**
  - First telling (to the household servants, v.14-15): "he came in unto me to lie with me, and I cried with a loud voice: And it came to pass, when he heard that I lifted up my voice and cried, that he left his garment with me, and fled, and got him out" — she shaped the narrative of the other servants before Potiphar arrived; the servants' testimony would be predetermined when Potiphar came home
  - Second telling (to Potiphar, v.17-18): "The Hebrew servant, which thou hast brought unto us, came in unto me to mock me: And it came to pass, as I lifted up my voice and cried, that he left his garment with me, and fled out" — the addition "to mock me" (litsachek bi — to laugh at me, to play with me) introduced an element of contempt and racial insult ("The Hebrew servant, which THOU hast brought unto us") that the servant-audience had not received; the story was adjusted to maximize Potiphar's personal offense; the structure of the false accusation was calculated and careful
  - The garment as the instrument of the accusation: the garment that Joseph had left behind in faithful flight was the physical evidence she held; she had waited with it until Potiphar came home; the evidence that looked irrefutable was produced by the exact act of faithfulness it was used to accuse; the righteous act of fleeing became the evidence of the crime that was never committed
- **The double false testimony as a violation of Mosaic principle:** Deuteronomy 17:6 — "At the mouth of two witnesses, or three witnesses, shall he that is worthy of death be put to death; but at the mouth of one witness he shall not be put to death" — the law required multiple witnesses; Potiphar's wife was telling the same story twice, but she was the only witness to the event; the Mosaic law would have required verification; what condemned Joseph was a single accusation told twice with the garment as exhibit; the injustice was embedded in the procedure
- **The type-of-Christ connection — the righteous man falsely accused:**
  - Matthew 26:59-61 — "Now the chief priests, and elders, and all the council, sought false witness against Jesus, to put him to death; But found none: yea, though many false witnesses came, yet found they none. At the last came two false witnesses, And said, This fellow said, I am able to destroy the temple of God, and to build it in three days" — the Sanhedrin explicitly sought false witness; none was found until two came with a distorted version of something Jesus had actually said (John 2:19); the false accusation against the righteous man was the mechanism by which the predetermined outcome was achieved
  - Acts 6:11-14 — Stephen falsely accused: "We have heard him speak blasphemous words against Moses, and against God... This man ceaseth not to speak blasphemous words against this holy place, and the law" — the pattern ran from Joseph through Christ to Stephen; the righteous man falsely accused by a calculated false report produced before an authority; in all three cases the condemnation was unjust; in all three cases God's sovereign purpose was advanced through the unjust condemnation
  - Revelation 12:10 — "the accuser of our brethren is cast down, which accused them before our God day and night" — the false accusation against the righteous person was identified in Revelation as the device of the enemy; Potiphar's wife was not merely a character in a narrative but an instance of the spirit of false accusation that ran from the garden (where the serpent falsely characterized God to Eve — Genesis 3:4-5) through every unjust condemnation to the final defeat of the accuser

### The Soft Landing — Potiphar Sends Joseph to Prison, Not Death

**Genesis 39:19-20** — "his wrath was kindled. And Joseph's master took him, and put him into the prison"

- **The expected outcome was death:** Potiphar was the captain of the guard — sarsar ha-tabachim, the chief of the executioners/slaughterers; his title was a reference to judicial execution; the crime Joseph was accused of — a slave assaulting the master's wife — warranted immediate execution in the ancient Near East; there was no procedural requirement for a trial; Potiphar could have killed Joseph on the spot; the fact that he did not was the first indication of God's providential hand in the very mechanism of the injustice
- **The text said Potiphar's wrath burned — but did not say he believed his wife:** The distinction was significant; the text never said Potiphar believed the accusation; it said his anger burned; a man who fully believed his slave had tried to assault his wife would have executed him immediately; the choice of imprisonment rather than execution may have indicated a residual doubt; or may have been pure divine providence operating through Potiphar's response; the text preserved the ambiguity while making clear that God's hand was in the outcome
- **The specific prison — the king's prisoners' place:** "the prison, a place where the king's prisoners were bound" (v.20); not a generic dungeon but the facility that held political prisoners — the highest-profile and most strategically significant detainees in Egypt; the false accusation that sent Joseph to this specific prison was the very mechanism that positioned him to meet Pharaoh's chief butler and baker (Genesis 40:1-3), through whom he would reach Pharaoh himself; what appeared to be a catastrophic miscarriage of justice was the precise instrument of Joseph's positioning for the next step in God's plan
- **Psalm 105:17-19 — the divine perspective on the imprisonment:** "He sent a man before them, even Joseph, who was sold for a servant: Whose feet they hurt with fetters: he was laid in iron: Until the time that his word came: the word of the LORD tried him" — the psalmist declared that it was God who sent Joseph ahead; the brothers sold him, but God sent him; Potiphar's wife imprisoned him, but God positioned him; the false accusation was real and the injustice was real; and "the word of the LORD tried him" — Joseph was in the prison until the appointed time, when God's word for him would come

### "But the LORD Was With Joseph" — The Second Declaration in the Second Pit

**Genesis 39:21** — "But the LORD was with Joseph, and shewed him mercy, and gave him favour"

- **The word "but" as the theological hinge:** The verse began with "and" in some translations and "but" (waw-adversative) in others; the adversative conjunction was the hinge between the circumstances (imprisonment, injustice, the apparent abandonment of heaven) and the theological reality (the LORD was present, showing hesed); the sentence structure was: all of that is true AND YET — the LORD was with Joseph; the "but" was not a minimizing of the circumstances but an insistence that God's presence was the larger reality
- **Three gifts of divine presence in the prison:**
  - "Shewed him mercy" (hesed — covenant lovingkindness): the covenant love that God had pledged to Abraham's seed was operative in the Egyptian prison; hesed was not a feeling but a covenantal commitment; Lamentations 3:22-23 — "It is of the LORD's mercies that we are not consumed, because his compassions fail not. They are new every morning: great is thy faithfulness" — the hesed in the prison was the same hesed that Jeremiah would declare after Jerusalem's destruction; the covenant faithfulness of God operated in the most destructive circumstances
  - "Gave him favour in the sight of the keeper" (chen — grace, gracious acceptance): the same word as "Noah found grace (chen) in the eyes of the LORD" (Genesis 6:8); the grace that had preserved Noah through the flood was the grace that operated for Joseph in the prison; God could give a man favor in the eyes of any authority He chose; the prison keeper was as subject to God's chen as Potiphar had been
  - The committed management of the prison to Joseph's hand (v.22): the practical expression of the favor; the prison keeper "committed to Joseph's hand all the prisoners that were in the prison; and whatsoever they did there, he was the doer of it" — the third complete delegation in the Joseph narrative
- **Romans 8:28 — the New Testament theological articulation of what Genesis 39 demonstrated narratively:** "And we know that all things work together for good to them that love God, to them who are the called according to his purpose" — not that all things were good in Joseph's circumstances (the false accusation, the imprisonment, the injustice were not good) but that all things worked together toward a good that God was accomplishing; the prison was not good; the divine purpose that ran through the prison was; the Romans 8:28 principle was Genesis 39 in propositional form

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### TSALACH — TO PROSPER / TO PUSH FORWARD / TO ACCOMPLISH (Hebrew: צָלַח)

**Genesis 39:2** — "he was a prosperous man (tsalach)"
**Genesis 39:3** — "the LORD made all that he did to prosper (tsalach)"
**Genesis 39:23** — "that which he did, the LORD made it to prosper (tsalach)"

- **Root:** Tsalach = to push forward, to break through, to advance, to accomplish one's purpose; not primarily a word about financial wealth or ease but about the effective accomplishment of purpose; a thing that tsalach was a thing that moved forward without being stopped, that reached its intended end
- **The distinction from material prosperity:** The prosperity of Joseph in Genesis 39 was not described in terms of personal financial gain — he was a slave and then a prisoner; he had no personal wealth; what tsalach described was the effectiveness of everything he managed for others; Potiphar's house prospered, not Joseph's personal portfolio; the prison functioned effectively under Joseph's management; the prosperity was the measurable effectiveness of God's blessing on Joseph's work, not Joseph's private acquisition
- **Tsalach in Isaiah 55:11 — the word that connects Joseph's prosperity to the divine word:** "So shall my word be that goeth forth out of my mouth: it shall not return unto me void, but it shall accomplish (tsalach) that which I please, and it shall prosper in the thing whereto I sent it" — the same word used three times in Genesis 39 for Joseph's prosperity was the word Isaiah used for the accomplishment of God's own word; what God sent did not fail to tsalach; what was sent out came back having accomplished its purpose; Joseph, sent by Jacob (Genesis 37:13-14), used by God, tsalach in slavery and imprisonment because the divine purpose was advancing through every circumstance; Joseph himself was "the word of the LORD" sent ahead (Psalm 105:17 — "He sent a man before them, even Joseph")
- **Tsalach in Joshua 1:8 — the prosperity that came from covenant faithfulness to the word of God:** "This book of the law shall not depart out of thy mouth; but thou shalt meditate therein day and night, that thou mayest observe to do according to all that is written therein: for then thou shalt make thy way prosperous (tsalach), and then thou shalt have good success (sakal)" — the tsalach of Genesis 39 was not independent of Joseph's covenant faithfulness; his refusal of Potiphar's wife, his faithful management, his consistent integrity — these were the covenant conditions of the tsalach that God produced through him; the Joshua 1:8 principle was the theological articulation of what Genesis 39 demonstrated: the one who was faithful to the word of God in all circumstances would see the LORD produce tsalach through them

### HESED — COVENANT LOVINGKINDNESS / LOYAL LOVE (Hebrew: חֶסֶד)

**Genesis 39:21** — "But the LORD was with Joseph, and shewed him mercy (hesed)"

- **Root:** Chesed = lovingkindness, steadfast love, loyal love, covenant faithfulness; the word was the Old Testament's primary term for the loyal love that God had pledged to His covenant people; it was not a sentimental feeling but a covenantal commitment — the love that was bound by the covenant and would not withdraw even when circumstances provided every justification for withdrawal
- **Hesed as the covenant word that appears in the lowest place:** The LORD showed Joseph hesed in the prison; not in the palace, not in the season of blessing, but in the dungeon; the hesed was not conditional on Joseph's circumstances being favorable — it was conditional on the covenant God had made with Abraham, which covered Joseph as Abraham's great-grandson; the covenant did not expire at the prison gate
- **The hesed thread in key passages:**
  - Genesis 24:27 — Abraham's servant: "I being in the way, the LORD led me to the house of my master's brethren. Blessed be the LORD God of my master Abraham, who hath not left destitute my master of his mercy (hesed) and his truth" — hesed as the steadfast covenant love that guided providentially; the same hesed guiding the servant to Rebekah was the same hesed operating for Joseph in the prison
  - Exodus 34:6-7 — the LORD's self-declaration to Moses: "The LORD, The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth (hesed and emet), Keeping mercy (hesed) for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin" — hesed as the essential character of God; the God who showed Joseph hesed in the prison was showing His essential nature; the prison was the context; hesed was the constant
  - Psalm 136 — the great hesed psalm: "O give thanks unto the LORD; for he is good: for his mercy (hesed) endureth for ever" — repeated twenty-six times; the hesed that endured forever was the hesed that operated in the Egyptian prison; the refrain of Psalm 136 could have been placed after every verse of Genesis 39 as its commentary
  - Lamentations 3:22-23 — "It is of the LORD's mercies (hesed) that we are not consumed, because his compassions fail not. They are new every morning: great is thy faithfulness" — written after the destruction of Jerusalem; the declaration of hesed in the worst national circumstances; Jeremiah declaring the same theological truth about hesed-in-the-lowest-place that the Genesis 39 narrator declared about Joseph

### CHEN — GRACE / FAVOR / GRACIOUS ACCEPTANCE (Hebrew: חֵן)

**Genesis 39:4** — "Joseph found grace (chen) in his sight"
**Genesis 39:21** — "gave him favour (chen) in the sight of the keeper of the prison"

- **Root:** Chanan = to be gracious, to show favor, to bend down in compassion; chen = the gracious acceptance that one receives from a superior; the grace was not earned by merit — it was given by the superior to the one in the lower position; the Hebrew concept was not a legal or contractual arrangement but a relational acceptance that the superior chose to extend
- **Chen — the same word as the grace Noah found with God:** Genesis 6:8 — "But Noah found grace (chen) in the eyes of the LORD" — the first use of chen in Scripture; the grace that preserved Noah through the flood; the same grace that preserved Joseph through slavery and imprisonment; in both cases chen was the unearned gracious acceptance that the superior extended to the one who had no standing to demand it; Joseph had no standing to demand Potiphar's favor; he had no standing to demand the prison keeper's favor; he received both because the LORD gave him chen
- **Chen in the eyes of pagan authorities as a pattern of covenant blessing:** Genesis 32:5 — Jacob sending messengers to Esau: "that I may find grace (chen) in thy sight"; Genesis 33:8 — Jacob to Esau: "to find grace (chen) in the sight of my lord"; Genesis 50:4 — Joseph to Pharaoh's household after Jacob's death: "if now I have found grace (chen) in your eyes"; throughout Genesis, chen in the eyes of human authorities was the mechanism by which the covenant person navigated a world that could otherwise destroy them; the LORD gave chen, and the human authority responded with favorable treatment; the divine grace produced the human favor
- **Chen from below and chen from above — the double structure of Genesis 39:** Joseph found chen in Potiphar's sight (v.4) — this was God giving him chen in the eyes of a human authority; then in the prison the LORD gave him chen in the sight of the prison keeper (v.21) — this was explicitly God's direct action producing the human response; in both cases the source of the chen was the LORD; the human authorities were the instruments; the divine grace operated through human favor without requiring that the human authorities understood or acknowledged the God who was working through them

### BOR — THE SECOND PIT / PRISON (Hebrew: בּוֹר)

**Genesis 39:20** — "Joseph's master took him, and put him into the prison (bor)"

- **Root:** The same bor as Genesis 37:24 — the pit without water at Dothan; in Genesis 39:20 the word rendered "prison" was the same bor (cistern/pit/dungeon); the narrative was deliberate in its use of vocabulary — Joseph's first descent was into a literal bor without water (Genesis 37:24); his second descent was into the bor of the prison (Genesis 39:20); both were pits, both were figures of death and descent, both were followed by elevation
- **The two bors of Joseph's descent:**
  - First bor (Genesis 37:24) — the pit at Dothan; cast in by his brothers; "there was no water in it"; the figure of Sheol; exited when the Midianites drew him up and sold him to the Ishmaelites
  - Second bor (Genesis 39:20) — the prison in Egypt; cast in by Potiphar on his wife's false testimony; the second figure of death; exited when Pharaoh's butler remembered him (Genesis 41:9-14)
  - The pattern: bor → elevation to Potiphar's house → bor → elevation to Pharaoh's throne; the two descents and two ascents formed the full pattern of Joseph's death-and-resurrection arc; each bor was lower than the last available descent; each elevation was higher than the previous station
- **Psalm 105:18 — the prison described in the prophetic retelling:** "Whose feet they hurt with fetters: he was laid in iron (barzel)" — the psalm added the physical detail of the chains that the Genesis narrative did not explicitly describe; the psalmist's "he was laid in iron" was the prophetic commentary on the second bor; the imprisonment was not comfortable — Joseph's feet were hurt with fetters; the suffering was bodily, not merely positional
- **Zechariah 9:11 — the pit without water as a figure of covenant deliverance:** "As for thee also, by the blood of thy covenant I have sent forth thy prisoners out of the pit wherein is no water (bor ein bo mayim)" — the covenant people delivered from pits by the blood of the covenant; the image of God releasing prisoners from waterless pits was the image that ran from Genesis 37:24 through Genesis 39:20 through Zechariah 9:11; the final release from all pits was through the blood of the covenant — the blood of Christ

### TSADDIQ — RIGHTEOUS / JUST (Hebrew: צַדִּיק, implied in the narrative structure)

**Genesis 39:9** — "how then can I do this great wickedness, and sin against God?" — righteousness expressed through refusal

- **Root:** Tsaddiq = the one who is in right relationship, who acts rightly; the word was not used explicitly in Genesis 39 but the concept structured the entire chapter; Joseph's refusal to sin was the expression of tsaddiq character; his suffering at the hands of the false accuser was the suffering of the tsaddiq unjustly condemned; the chapter's entire theological logic rested on the distinction between the tsaddiq (Joseph) and the wicked (the false accusation)
- **The righteous man who suffers unjustly — a major biblical theological category:**
  - Psalm 34:19 — "Many are the afflictions of the righteous (tsaddiq): but the LORD delivereth him out of them all" — the theological promise that was Genesis 39's narrative demonstration; the afflictions were many and real; the deliverance was certain; Joseph went through the afflictions of Genesis 39 and was delivered in Genesis 41
  - Isaiah 53:11 — "He shall see of the travail of his soul, and shall be satisfied: by his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many; for he shall bear their iniquities" — the righteous Servant who suffered for the iniquities of others; the pattern Joseph embodied was fulfilled ultimately in the Servant Song; Joseph's unjust suffering prepared the world to understand the Servant's unjust suffering
  - Habakkuk 2:4 — "but the just (tsaddiq) shall live by his faith" — the principle that sustained Joseph through the prison; his faithfulness in the pit was the faithfulness of one who lived by trust in God's covenant even when circumstances contradicted it

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "The LORD Was With Joseph" Means God's People Will Always Have Material Prosperity and Pleasant Circumstances

**The Misuse:** Genesis 39:2-3 — "the LORD was with Joseph, and he was a prosperous man" — the divine presence with Joseph and his prosperity are taken as a general promise that God's presence with His people will always produce material blessing, comfortable circumstances, and earthly success. Used in prosperity gospel teaching to argue that suffering and hardship indicate either divine absence or the believer's lack of faith.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The text applied the formula in a slave's house and in a prison:** The "LORD was with Joseph" formula appeared four times in Genesis 39 — twice while he was a slave (vv.2-3), twice while he was unjustly imprisoned (vv.21-23); the formula was equally applied in both conditions; divine presence produced measurable blessing in both the relatively elevated condition (slave overseer) and the degraded condition (imprisoned); the text refused to limit the formula to pleasant circumstances; the divine presence operated in the dungeon as fully as in the house
- **Psalm 105:17-18 — the cost of the divine purpose was real:** "Joseph, who was sold for a servant: Whose feet they hurt with fetters: he was laid in iron" — the psalmist described the bodily suffering; the hurt feet, the iron chains; these were not symbolic; they were concurrent with the divine presence; the "LORD was with him" coexisted with "he was laid in iron"; the formula and the suffering occupied the same temporal space; no verse in the passage said the LORD was with him therefore the chains were light or the feet did not hurt
- **Acts 14:22 — "through much tribulation enter we into the kingdom of God":** The apostolic declaration was that tribulation was the path, not the exception; Joseph's tribulation (pit, slavery, prison, forgotten) was the path through which the LORD's purpose for him advanced; the tribulation was not evidence of the LORD's absence but of the LORD's path
- **Isaiah 43:2 — the fire and the water as the context of the divine presence:** "When thou passest through the waters, I will be with thee; and through the rivers, they shall not overflow thee; when thou walkest through the fire, thou shalt not be burned" — the promise was not "I will prevent the waters and the fire" but "I will be with thee through the waters and the fire"; the divine presence was promised in the hardship, not instead of it; Genesis 39 was the narrative illustration of Isaiah 43:2
- *Correction:* "The LORD was with Joseph" described an active divine presence that produced effective, observable fruitfulness — in a slave house and in a prison equally. The formula was applied in the most adverse circumstances to demonstrate that God's presence was not contingent on pleasant circumstances. The divine presence was the theological constant; the circumstances were the variable. The text deliberately set the divine-presence declarations against conditions of slavery and imprisonment to prove that the formula was not conditioned on ease.

---

### Joseph's Integrity Proves He Was Sinless and Perfect

**The Misuse:** Genesis 39's sustained portrait of Joseph's integrity — daily refusal of temptation, immediate flight, no self-defense in prison — is used to argue that Joseph was without sin or morally perfect, and therefore a perfect type of Christ in a way that requires his sinlessness rather than merely his typological role.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Genesis 37:2 — Joseph brought an evil report (dibbah) about his brothers:** The chapter opened with Joseph bringing a negative report about his brothers to Jacob; the text did not editorially condemn this behavior, but it established that Joseph's character was complex; he was not presented as sinless; the dibbah was the first action attributed to him in the Joseph narrative and it was not portrayed unambiguously as virtuous
- **Genesis 37:9 — the second dream told to his brothers:** The telling of the second dream after the first had already intensified the brothers' hatred; the text said "his father rebuked him" (v.10); the rebuke suggested that even Jacob saw imprudence in Joseph's manner, though he "observed the saying"; the text preserved the complexity without resolving it into a simple verdict
- **Romans 3:23 — "for all have sinned, and come short of the glory of God":** No type of Christ was itself sinless — the type was always a partial representation of the antitype; the typological correspondence was real and significant, but the type was never equal to the thing it signified; Joseph's integrity in Genesis 39 was genuine and impressive and truly typological of Christ's perfect integrity; but the type derived its significance from the antitype's perfection, not from its own; Hebrews 7:26-27 distinguished the sinlessness of Christ from all previous priests and types: "For such an high priest became us, who is holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from sinners, and made higher than the heavens; Who needeth not daily, as those high priests, to offer up sacrifice, first for his own sins, and then for the people's"
- *Correction:* Joseph's integrity in Genesis 39 was genuine, sustained, and typologically significant as a prefiguring of Christ's perfect faithfulness. But the type was not sinless — Joseph was a fallen man whose character was genuinely formed by God's grace and genuinely complex. The typological significance was the pattern: the beloved son, sent by the father, rejected by his brethren, falsely accused and unjustly condemned, raised to the right hand of the supreme authority, the savior of both his people and the nations. The perfection belonged to the antitype; the pattern belonged to the type.

---

### Potiphar's Wife Was the Victim and Joseph Was in a Position of Power

**The Misuse:** Genesis 39 is sometimes read through the lens of power dynamics — Joseph had authority over the household; Potiphar's wife was a woman in a restrictive social structure; therefore Joseph's refusal should be read as a power-based story with Potiphar's wife as a frustrated victim rather than a false accuser.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The text's own moral verdict was unambiguous:** Potiphar's wife initiated the proposition (v.7 — "she said, Lie with me"); the initiation was hers, not Joseph's; the repeated daily pressure was hers (v.10 — "she spake to Joseph day by day"); the physical grabbing was hers (v.12 — "she caught him by his garment"); the false accusation was hers (vv.14-18); the text never presented her as constrained, reluctant, or coerced; it presented her as the agent of repeated, deliberate, escalating pressure; the moral agency in the account belonged to her
- **Joseph's power in the household did not extend to the master's wife:** The text explicitly preserved this boundary: "neither hath he kept back any thing from me but thee, because thou art his wife" (v.9) — Potiphar had deliberately excluded his wife from the authority he gave Joseph; Joseph's authority was over the household's management, not over the persons of Potiphar's family; the marriage covenant made her categorically unavailable regardless of any household authority structure
- **The false accusation was deliberate and calculated:** She pre-briefed the servants (v.14) before Potiphar arrived; she kept the garment as evidence (v.16); she adjusted the story for maximum effect with Potiphar (v.17 — adding "to mock us"); the structure of her accusation was the structure of calculated deliberate false testimony; the text's moral portraiture was not ambiguous
- **Matthew 19:18 and Proverbs 19:5 — false witness as a moral category:** "Thou shalt not bear false witness against thy neighbour"; "A false witness shall not be unpunished, and he that speaketh lies shall not escape" — the Scripture's own moral framework for false accusation was clear; the text never invited the reader to read her accusation as a response to victimization; it invited the reader to identify it as false witness, as Proverbs 19:5 categorized it
- *Correction:* The text's own moral presentation was consistent and unambiguous: Potiphar's wife was the agent of the sexual pressure, the false accusation, and the calculated deployment of false evidence. Joseph's refusal was the righteous response. The typological significance — the righteous man falsely accused, condemned, and then exalted — required the text's own moral framework to be maintained. The text's moral verdict was not a cultural artifact to be revised but the divinely inspired narrative judgment through which the typological meaning was established.

**Genesis 37:24 / 39:20-21 / Psalm 139:7-8 / Isaiah 43:2 / Romans 8:38-39**

- Genesis 37:24 — the pit at Dothan without water; Joseph cast in; brothers sitting eating above; the appearance of total abandonment in a dry pit

- Genesis 41:56-57 — Joseph distributing grain to all nations: "all countries came into Egypt to Joseph for to buy corn; because that the famine was sore in all lands" — the fullest expression of the blessing-overflow; the nations of the earth came to the covenant person for preservation; the Abrahamic promise reaching its Genesis-era fulfillment
- Galatians 3:8-9 — "And the scripture, foreseeing that God would justify the heathen through faith, preached before the gospel unto Abraham, saying, In thee shall all nations be blessed. So then they which be of faith are blessed with faithful Abraham" — Paul identified the Genesis 12:3 promise as the "gospel preached beforehand"; the blessing that flowed through Abraham, through Jacob, through Joseph to the pagan households and nations was the same blessing that would flow through Christ to all who believed; Joseph's overflow to Potiphar and then to the nations was the type of the gospel's overflow to all who came to Christ in their famine

### The Daily Temptation That Built the Throne-Ready Character

**Genesis 39:10 / Luke 16:10 / James 1:2-4 / Hebrews 5:8**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 39:10 — "she spake to Joseph day by day" — the daily persistence of the test; the daily faithfulness of the resistance; not a single dramatic moment but a formation through sustained daily choice
- Luke 16:10 — "He that is faithful in that which is least is faithful also in much: and he that is unjust in the least is unjust also in much" — the principle that the daily faithfulness in Potiphar's house was the preparation for the faithfulness required in Pharaoh's; the daily refusal of temptation was the daily building of the integrity that would manage Egypt's grain stores; no day's choice was isolated; each was the formation of the character that the next responsibility would require
- James 1:2-4 — "My brethren, count it all joy when ye fall into divers temptations; Knowing this, that the trying of your faith worketh patience. But let patience have her perfect work, that ye may be perfect and entire, wanting nothing" — the testing produced the perfected character; Joseph's daily testing by Potiphar's wife was the working of patience in his faith; the character "perfect and entire" was not produced without the testing; the throne-ready administrator of Genesis 41 was the product of the daily-tested man of Genesis 39
- Hebrews 5:8 — "Though he were a Son, yet learned he obedience by the things which he suffered" — applied to Christ; the pattern was the same as Joseph; the suffering was the curriculum; the obedience was the product; what Joseph learned in Potiphar's house and in the prison was the obedience that his supreme responsibility would require; the pattern was the preparation

### The "But" — The Hinge Word of the Bible's Darkest Passages

**Genesis 39:21 — "But the LORD was with Joseph"**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 39:21 — "But the LORD was with Joseph" — against all circumstances of injustice and imprisonment, the hinge word: but; the adversative conjunction that introduced the theological reality against the experiential circumstance
- Psalm 73:26 — "My flesh and my heart faileth: but God is the strength of my heart, and my portion for ever" — Asaph's "but God" after the extended meditation on the prosperity of the wicked (Psalm 73:2-22); the hinge from confusion and despair to the divine reality
- Ephesians 2:4-5 — "But God, who is rich in mercy, for his great love wherewith he loved us, Even when we were dead in sins, hath quickened us together with Christ" — the "but God" of the gospel; dead in sins, and then: but God; the supreme hinge in all of Scripture; what introduced the divine action against all human circumstance
- The "but God" / "but the LORD" pattern ran through Scripture as the consistent marker of divine intervention against impossible circumstances: Genesis 39:21 (prison), Psalm 73:26 (spiritual crisis), Ephesians 2:4 (spiritual death); in every case the "but" introduced the theological reality that overturned the circumstantial verdict; the God of the "but" was the God of Joseph, the God of Asaph, and the God of the apostle

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time the LORD's Presence Was Noted as Visibly Perceptible to a Pagan Observer in a Covenant Person's Life

**Genesis 39:3** — "And his master saw that the LORD was with him"
- The first explicit statement that a pagan authority visually perceived the LORD's presence with a covenant person through observable results
- First mention establishes: the divine presence with the covenant person was not only spiritually real but observationally demonstrable; the fruit of God's presence was visible to those who did not share the covenant; this principle would continue through Acts 2:47 ("and the Lord added to the church daily such as should be saved") and Matthew 5:16 ("Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven"); the church's witness was visible precisely because the LORD's presence with His people produced observable fruit

### First Time the Formula "The LORD Was With [Name]" Was Applied to a Person in Conditions of Slavery and Imprisonment

**Genesis 39:2, 21** — applied in a slave-house and in a prison
- Previous uses of divine presence language (Genesis 21:22; 26:28) were spoken of covenant persons in relatively neutral or favorable circumstances
- First mention in adverse conditions establishes: the "LORD was with" formula was not conditional on favorable circumstances; it could be equally and fully applied to slavery and imprisonment as to free and successful conditions; this established the theological principle that divine presence was not circumstantially contingent; every subsequent instance of divine presence in suffering — Daniel in the lions' den (Daniel 6:22), Paul in prison (Acts 16:25-26), John on Patmos (Revelation 1:9) — built on this first application of the formula in adversity

### First Time the Word Tsalach Was Applied to the Fruit of a Person's Labor Rather Than to a Military Campaign or a Journey

**Genesis 39:2-3, 23** — tsalach applied to Joseph's work and management
- Earlier uses of tsalach in Genesis were about journeys succeeding and missions being accomplished
- First mention in a labor context established: the same divine accomplishment-force that succeeded journeys and missions was also operative in daily work and management; whatever Joseph put his hand to in service of another was made to advance and accomplish its purpose; this was the basis for Colossians 3:23-24 — "whatsoever ye do, do it heartily, as to the Lord, and not unto men; Knowing that of the Lord ye shall receive the reward of the inheritance: for ye serve the Lord Christ" — the sanctification of daily labor as the sphere of divine operation; work done faithfully was work that God could make tsalach

## NAME MEANINGS

| Name/Term | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Potiphar (Pa-di-pa-Ra) | He whom Ra gave | Egyptian: "he who was given by Ra (the sun god)"; an officer of Pharaoh under the sun-worship empire of Egypt; the pagan name of the man whose household God blessed for Joseph's sake; the blessing of the covenant God overrode the domain claimed by the pagan deity whose name the man bore |
| Joseph (Yosef) | He will add / May God add | From yasaf (to add, to increase); the name remained consistent as the person descended and ascended; the adding continued through all conditions — God added blessing to Potiphar's house, God added favor in the prison, God would add authority over Egypt |
| The prison keeper (sar ha-sohar — chief of the round house/prison) | Chief of the guarded place | The title identified the role: sar (chief/captain) of the sohar (the round/enclosed place — the prison); the same structural role as Potiphar (captain of the guard); the parallel was deliberate — Joseph served as the operative overseer under both "captains" through the same evident divine blessing |
