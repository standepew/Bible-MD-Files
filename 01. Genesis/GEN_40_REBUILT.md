## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 40 occupied the lowest point in Joseph's narrative trajectory. He had been sold by his brothers, falsely accused by Potiphar's wife, and imprisoned without trial. He had been in the king's prison long enough that the text no longer counted the days — it said only "a season." Genesis 40 was set entirely within that prison, and its concluding verse — "yet did not the chief butler remember Joseph, but forgat him" — was the most desolate sentence in the chapter. God was not absent from it; He was present as the sovereign author of every detail. But the chapter ended on the note of human forgetfulness, deliberately, as the theological staging for what would follow.

Pharaoh's chief butler and chief baker had offended their lord and been cast into the same prison where Joseph was held — the house of the captain of the guard, the same structure that had held Joseph since Genesis 39:20. The captain of the guard charged Joseph to serve them. Joseph noticed. The text preserved this detail carefully: "In the morning, he looked upon them, and, behold, they were sad." The man in the pit attended to those newly arrived in the pit. The man who had been falsely accused and forgotten asked those who were troubled: "Wherefore look ye so sadly to day?" The capacity to see and ask about another's sadness while in the middle of one's own extended suffering was not incidental — it was the character formation that the prison had been producing.

They told him about the dreams. Joseph's response was immediate and complete: "Do not interpretations belong to God? tell me them, I pray you." He did not hesitate to engage. He did not require credentials or divine confirmation before he asked to hear the dreams. He declared the principle — all true interpretation came from God — and then invited the disclosure. The question was at once a theological declaration, a deflection of personal credit, and an expression of confidence in the God who interpreted: God had given the dreams; God would give the interpretation; Joseph was the vessel, not the source.

The butler's dream was given first. A vine with three branches budded, blossomed, and produced ripe grapes in rapid succession — the language compressed the full agricultural cycle into the dreamscape's moment. The butler pressed the grapes into Pharaoh's cup and gave it into Pharaoh's hand. Joseph interpreted: the three branches were three days; within three days Pharaoh would lift up the butler's head and restore him to his position. Then Joseph made his plea. He did not petition God — he petitioned the man being restored. "Think on me when it shall be well with thee, and shew kindness, I pray thee, unto me, and make mention of me unto Pharaoh." He identified himself: he had been stolen from the land of the Hebrews; he had done nothing to deserve the dungeon. The plea was specific, vulnerable, and based entirely on the word of a man who would shortly be restored to Pharaoh's daily presence.

The baker saw that the interpretation was good and spoke his dream. Three white baskets on his head; the topmost full of baked goods for Pharaoh; birds eating from the basket on his head. Joseph did not soften what he saw. "The three baskets are three days: Yet within three days shall Pharaoh lift up thy head from off thee, and shall hang thee on a tree; and the birds shall eat thy flesh from off thee." The two interpretations used the same phrase — "lift up thine head" — but with opposite valences: for the butler, lifted up to restoration; for the baker, lifted up in execution. The verbal parallel was deliberate; the divergent outcomes were the judicial point.

On Pharaoh's birthday — the third day, exactly as interpreted — Pharaoh made a feast. He lifted up the head of the butler and the baker among his servants. The butler was restored; the baker was hanged. Both exactly as Joseph had interpreted. And then the closing sentence: "Yet did not the chief butler remember Joseph, but forgat him."

The forgetting was not a failure of God's plan — it was the extension of the trial. Psalm 105:18-19 declared: "he was laid in iron: Until the time that his word came: the word of the LORD tried him." The prison was not the end of the story; it was the testing that the word of God was conducting. Two more years would pass (Genesis 41:1) before the butler's memory was triggered — not by conscience but by necessity, when Pharaoh himself had dreams that none in Egypt could interpret. The delay that looked like divine abandonment was the precision of divine timing: Joseph needed to arrive before Pharaoh not when a butler casually remembered a helpful prisoner but when Pharaoh himself was desperate for an interpreter, and when the interpretation would be the occasion for Joseph's elevation to the second throne in all of Egypt.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Do Not Interpretations Belong to God?" — The Consistent Posture of Every True Interpreter

**Genesis 40:8** — "Do not interpretations belong to God? tell me them, I pray you"

- **The immediate deflection of personal glory:** The first thing Joseph said when the opportunity arose to display an extraordinary gift was to deny that the gift was his; the interpretive ability was not Joseph's — it belonged to God; Joseph was not modest about his relationship with God — he was theologically precise; he understood the gift's source and said so; the declaration "interpretations belong to God" was not a performative humility but a doctrinal statement about the nature of prophetic revelation
- **Genesis 41:16 — the pattern held before Pharaoh himself:** "And Joseph answered Pharaoh, saying, It is not in me: God shall give Pharaoh an answer of peace" — the same deflection at the higher court; when Pharaoh said "I have heard say of thee, that thou canst understand a dream" (Genesis 41:15), Joseph's response was: "It is not in me"; the consistent message was that the interpreter had no independent interpretive authority; God was the author of the dream and the interpreter of the dream; the human was the transmitter
- **Daniel 2:27-28 — the identical posture in Babylon:** "Daniel answered in the presence of the king, and said, The secret which the king hath demanded cannot the wise men, the astrologers, the magicians, the soothsayers, shew unto the king; But there is a God in heaven that revealeth secrets, and maketh known to king Nebuchadnezzar what shall be in the latter days" — Daniel's first words before Nebuchadnezzar were to deny the human capacity to reveal secrets and attribute it exclusively to "a God in heaven"; the Babylonian wise men had failed; Daniel succeeded not because he was wiser but because the God of heaven revealed; the pattern was structurally identical to Joseph's in Egypt
- **Daniel 2:30 — Daniel's specific denial of personal credit:** "But as for me, this secret is not revealed to me for any wisdom that I have more than any living, but for their sakes that shall make known the interpretation to the king, and that thou mightest know the thoughts of thy heart" — the revelation was for the benefit of the king and kingdom, not for Daniel's personal advancement; the same logic operated for Joseph: the interpretation was for Pharaoh and Egypt, not for Joseph's personal glory; both servants deflected, attributed to God, and then proceeded to serve
- **2 Peter 1:20-21 — the governing hermeneutical principle stated as doctrine:** "Knowing this first, that no prophecy of the scripture is of any private interpretation. For the prophecy came not in old time by the will of man: but holy men of God spake as they were moved by the Holy Ghost" — the dream and its interpretation were not the dreamer's private property to be unlocked by human wisdom; they were God's communication requiring God's interpretation; the "holy men" who spoke were moved (pheromenoi — carried along, like a ship driven by wind) by the Holy Spirit; Joseph and Daniel were operating in the same category as the prophets Peter described; in all cases the source was God, the vessel was human, and the transmission was the Holy Spirit's work
- **The contrast with the Egyptian and Babylonian alternatives:** In both the Joseph and the Daniel narratives, the divine interpreter succeeded where the court system of professional interpreters failed; Genesis 41:8 — "Pharaoh's spirit was troubled; and he sent and called for all the magicians of Egypt, and all the wise men thereof: and Pharaoh told them his dream; but there was none that could interpret them unto Pharaoh"; Daniel 2:1-12 — the Babylonian wise men unable to tell the dream or its interpretation; in both cases the failure of professional human systems of interpretation preceded the vindication of the God-dependent interpreter; the point was theological: the God who gave the dream was the only one who could open it

### Three Days — The Bible's Own Resurrection Number Established in Pattern

**Genesis 40:12-13** — "The three branches are three days: Yet within three days shall Pharaoh lift up thine head"
**Genesis 40:18-19** — "The three baskets are three days: Yet within three days shall Pharaoh lift up thy head from off thee"

- **Three days as the consistent divine interval in Scripture — the pattern built before the Resurrection made it explicit:**
  - Genesis 22:4 — "Then on the third day Abraham lifted up his eyes, and saw the place afar off" — Isaac, the type of the sacrificed son, was three days on the journey to Moriah; from the moment Abraham received the command to sacrifice Isaac, Isaac was "as good as dead" (Hebrews 11:19 — "accounting that God was able to raise him up, even from the dead; from whence also he received him in a figure"); the third day was the day of the provision of the substitute and the restoration of the son
  - Exodus 19:11, 16 — God descended on Mount Sinai on the third day: "And be ready against the third day: for the third day the LORD will come down in the sight of all the people upon mount Sinai"; the covenant-defining theophany occurred on the third day; divine self-revelation was a third-day event
  - Joshua 1:11 — "Within three days ye shall pass over this Jordan, to go in to possess the land" — the entrance into the promised land on the third day; from the command to prepare to the crossing was three days
  - Esther 4:16 — 5:1 — "fast ye for me, and neither eat nor drink three days, night or day... and so will I go in unto the king... Now it came to pass on the third day, that Esther put on her royal apparel, and stood in the inner court of the king's house" — Esther's three days of fasting followed by the third-day audience with the king; the pattern of crisis-interval-resolution in three days
  - Hosea 6:2 — "After two days will he revive us: in the third day he will raise us up, and we shall live in his sight" — the prophetic statement of the third-day resurrection in the context of national revival; the third day as the day of divine raising-up
  - Jonah 1:17 — "And Jonah was in the belly of the fish three days and three nights" — three days in the death-figure of the great fish; three days in the watery deep; Jonah's prayer from the fish was the prayer from Sheol (Jonah 2:2 — "out of the belly of hell (Sheol) cried I")
  - Matthew 12:40 — Jesus Himself identified Jonah's three days as the type of His own death and resurrection: "For as Jonas was three days and three nights in the whale's belly; so shall the Son of man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth" — the definitive interpretive key from the lips of Christ; every three-day pattern in the Old Testament was a pointer toward this; the three-day interval of Genesis 40 (for the butler's restoration) was part of the same divinely embedded pattern
  - 1 Corinthians 15:4 — "And that he was buried, and that he rose again the third day according to the scriptures" — Paul's "according to the scriptures" indicated that the third-day resurrection was anticipated in the Old Testament; the scriptures Paul had in mind included the Hosea 6:2 pattern, the Jonah pattern, the Isaac pattern, and the accumulated three-day structures of the Old Testament narrative; Genesis 40's butler restored on the third day was part of the scriptural matrix Paul cited

- **The two outcomes on the same third day:** Both the butler and the baker received their verdicts on the same third day; the third day was not inherently a day of salvation — it was the appointed day of the divine reckoning; the butler was restored (life); the baker was executed (death); the identical temporal interval produced opposite outcomes because the verdicts were different; the third day was the day God determined, not the day that determined the outcome; the outcome was determined by the moral and judicial condition of each man; the third day was simply the moment of execution of what had already been decided

### Two Beside Joseph — The Pattern of One Saved and One Condemned

**Genesis 40 — butler restored, baker executed**

- **The structural pattern: two persons beside the central figure, one saved, one condemned:**
  - Genesis 40: the butler and the baker, both beside Joseph in the prison; both receive their verdict in the same moment; one is restored to life; one is put to death
  - This was not an incidental narrative — it was the most compressed form of the final-judgment pattern in the entire book of Genesis; two human beings, presented before the supreme authority, receiving two opposite verdicts, on the same day, through the same mediating interpreter

- **Luke 23:39-43 — the two criminals at the cross:**
  - "And one of the malefactors which were hanged railed on him, saying, If thou be Christ, save thyself and us. But the other answering rebuked him, saying, Dost not thou fear God, seeing thou art in the same condemnation? And we indeed justly; for we receive the due reward of our deeds: but this man hath done nothing amiss. And he said unto Jesus, Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom. And Jesus said unto him, Verily I say unto thee, To day shalt thou be with me in paradise" — two men beside Christ; one condemned and reviling; one condemned and repenting; the repentant one said "Lord, remember me" — the same plea as Joseph's "think on me when it shall be well with thee" (Genesis 40:14); the one who cried "remember me" to Joseph was the one who was restored; the one who cried "Lord, remember me" to Christ was told "today shalt thou be with me in paradise"; the restored butler was the type of the penitent criminal; the executed baker was the type of the unrepentant criminal

- **The language of "lifting up the head" — life versus death:**
  - Genesis 40:13 — "Pharaoh shall lift up thine head, and restore thee unto thy place" — the lifting up of the head as restoration to dignity and life
  - Genesis 40:19 — "Pharaoh shall lift up thy head from off thee, and shall hang thee on a tree" — the same phrase, but "from off thee" indicated removal — decapitation before hanging, or the lifting of the head in execution; the verbal parallel was deliberate and chilling: the same phrase used for life and for death, distinguished by the addition of two words
  - John 3:14-15 — "And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up: That whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have eternal life"; John 12:32-33 — "And I, if I be lifted up from the earth, will draw all men unto me. This he said, signifying what death he should die" — the "lifting up" of Christ on the cross was simultaneously his death and his exaltation; the same act of lifting up that was execution for the baker was, in Christ, the moment of universal drawing; the Genesis 40 "lift up" language carried both its life-pole and its death-pole into the cross narrative

### "Remember Me" — The Plea of the Forgotten Righteous and the God Who Does Not Forget

**Genesis 40:14-15** — "think on me when it shall be well with thee... and bring me out of this house: For indeed I was stolen away out of the land of the Hebrews"

- **Joseph's threefold identification of his innocence:**
  - "I was stolen away out of the land of the Hebrews" — the original crime was against him; he had not emigrated; he had been taken by force; the description "stolen" (gannov gunnavti — literally "I was indeed stolen") was the language of theft applied to a person; he was not a criminal seeking pardon but an innocent man seeking justice
  - "Here also have I done nothing that they should put me into the dungeon" — the second injustice confirmed; he had been put in the dungeon for a crime he did not commit; his statement of innocence was not self-justification but the necessary context for his appeal
  - Both statements together established the case: he was in the wrong country by theft and in the wrong prison by false accusation; his entire condition was the result of other people's crimes against him, not his own

- **Genesis 40:23 — the butler forgot:** "Yet did not the chief butler remember Joseph, but forgat him" — shakach; the word fell at the end of the chapter with finality; the one person Joseph had asked to remember him had not done so; the human intercessor failed; two full years would pass (Genesis 41:1 — "at the end of two full years")

- **Psalm 105:17-20 — God's account of what the forgetting actually was:** "He sent a man before them, even Joseph, who was sold for a servant: Whose feet they hurt with fetters: he was laid in iron: Until the time that his word came: the word of the LORD tried him. The king sent and loosed him" — the psalm interpreted the imprisonment and the forgetting as "the word of the LORD tried him"; the delay was not abandonment but trial; the prison was not God's forgetfulness but God's testing ground; "until the time that his word came" — God's word had a time; the time had not yet come; when it came, it came not through the butler's memory but through the king's own desperation

- **Isaiah 49:14-16 — God's answer to the feeling of being forgotten:** "But Zion said, The LORD hath forsaken me, and my Lord hath forgotten me. Can a woman forget her sucking child, that she should not have compassion on the son of her womb? yea, they may forget, yet will I not forget thee. Behold, I have graven thee upon the palms of my hands; thy walls are continually before me" — the divine answer to the theology of the forgotten; God said the human analogy of a nursing mother forgetting her infant was closer to impossible than any other human experience — "yea, they may forget" acknowledged the extreme rarity and unnaturalness — "yet will I not forget thee"; Joseph was not forgotten by God; he was graven on God's hands; the butler's forgetting was the human failure that God overrode at the appointed time

- **Luke 23:42 — the criminal's plea as the completion of the pattern:** "Lord, remember me when thou comest into thy kingdom" — the same structure as Joseph's plea: I am suffering unjustly (the criminal admitted his own guilt but the unjust aspect was dying next to an innocent man); remember me; I have nowhere else to turn; please bring me out of this condition; the answer to the criminal's "remember me" was immediate and certain: "Today shalt thou be with me in paradise"; the answer to Joseph's "remember me" was delayed two years and delivered through a vastly greater deliverance; both cried from the place of no human resource; both were answered

### Hanged on a Tree — The First Mention and Its Theological Trajectory

**Genesis 40:19** — "shall hang thee on a tree; and the birds shall eat thy flesh from off thee"
**Genesis 40:22** — "But he hanged the chief baker: as Joseph had interpreted to them"

- **First mention of hanging on a tree as a form of execution in Scripture:** The baker was hanged on a tree after death or as the manner of execution; the phrase "hang on a tree" entered the biblical vocabulary here; the specific combination of execution and tree became a theologically significant cluster
- **Deuteronomy 21:22-23 — the Mosaic law's formal treatment:** "And if a man have committed a sin worthy of death, and he be to be put to death, and thou hang him on a tree: His body shall not remain all night upon the tree, but thou shalt in any wise bury him that day; (for he that is hanged is accursed of God;) that thy land be not defiled, which the LORD thy God giveth thee for an inheritance" — the Law formalized the principle: to be hanged on a tree was to be under the curse of God; the hanging on a tree was not merely an execution method but a theological declaration — the one hanging on the tree was publicly declared accursed of God; Deuteronomy codified what Genesis 40 introduced
- **Joshua 8:29; 10:26-27 — Mosaic law applied in the conquest:** The kings of Ai and the five Amorite kings were hanged on trees after death as the declaration of divine curse upon the defeated enemies; the pattern from Genesis 40 formalized in Deuteronomy 21 applied in Joshua's military campaigns
- **Esther 7:9-10 — Haman hanged on the gallows he had prepared for Mordecai:** "Haman is fallen before Mordecai" — the enemy of God's people hanged on the instrument he had prepared for the righteous; the reversal pattern; Genesis 40:22's baker was hanged by Pharaoh's order; Haman was hanged by the king's order on what he had built for another; in both cases the hanging on the tree was the divine curse enacted through royal authority
- **Galatians 3:13 — Paul's definitive interpretation:** "Christ hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree" — Paul quoted Deuteronomy 21:23 directly and applied it to Christ's crucifixion; the cross was the tree; Christ was hanged on the tree; by the definition embedded in Deuteronomy 21:23, He was therefore publicly declared accursed of God; but Paul's point was that He bore that curse not as His own but as a substitution: "being made a curse for us"; the first mention of hanging on a tree in Genesis 40:19-22 seeded the Deuteronomy 21 principle that Paul applied to Christ; the baker's hanging on the tree in Genesis 40 was the narrative first appearance of the theological category that would define the cross

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### MASHQEH — CUPBEARER / BUTLER (Hebrew: מַשְׁקֶה)

**Genesis 40:1** — "the butler of the king of Egypt"
**Genesis 40:21** — "he restored the chief butler unto his butlership again; and he gave the cup into Pharaoh's hand"

- **Root:** Shaqah = to give to drink, to water; mashqeh = the one who gave drink, the cupbearer; the title described the function — the person who managed and served the king's drinking; in the ancient Near East the royal cupbearer was one of the most trusted and influential positions at court; the cupbearer tasted the king's wine before serving it to protect against poisoning; this required unrestricted access to the king's person, daily proximity to the throne, and the king's absolute trust in the cupbearer's loyalty; the position was simultaneously a service role and an intelligence post — the man who stood daily at the king's right hand with the cup was the man who heard everything

- **The cupbearer's strategic significance for Joseph:** The butler's restoration to Pharaoh's presence meant daily access to the king; this was why Joseph asked him to "make mention of me unto Pharaoh" (v.14); no one was better positioned to introduce Joseph's name to the king's ear than the man who stood beside him daily; Joseph's choice of advocate was strategically sound; the tragedy was the forgetting; but when the memory was triggered (Genesis 41:9-13), the butler's position was the mechanism: "Then spake the chief butler unto Pharaoh, saying, I do remember my faults this day" — his daily proximity to Pharaoh meant the introduction of Joseph's name went directly to the right ear at exactly the right moment

- **The cup in biblical symbolism — carried forward from this chapter:**
  - The butler's act — pressing grapes into Pharaoh's cup and delivering it to the king's hand — was the image that seeded the vine-and-cup symbolism throughout the prophets and the Gospels
  - Isaiah 5:1-7 — "the vineyard of the LORD of hosts is the house of Israel, and the men of Judah his pleasant plant" — Israel as the LORD's vineyard; what the vine produced was pressed and given to the divine hand
  - Matthew 26:27-29 — Jesus at the Last Supper: "And he took the cup, and gave thanks, and gave it to them, saying, Drink ye all of it; For this is my blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins" — the pressed grape pressed into the cup of the new covenant; Christ as the one who pressed out the cup and gave it into the disciples' hands; the butler's action in Genesis 40 — pressing the grapes and delivering the cup — was the typological prefiguring of the Servant who pressed out His blood and offered the cup of the covenant
  - John 15:1 — "I am the true vine, and my Father is the husbandman" — the vine of the butler's dream reappeared as Christ's self-declaration; the vine that produced the grapes, pressed into the cup, delivered to the king's hand — the vine was Christ; the grapes were His life pressed out; the cup was the new covenant; the king's hand was the Father's; the entire symbol-chain of Genesis 40:9-11 found its antitype in John 15:1 and Matthew 26:27-29

### SHALOSH — THREE (Hebrew: שָׁלוֹשׁ)

**Genesis 40:10, 12, 16, 18** — "three branches... three days... three baskets... three days"

- **Root:** Shalash = to do a third time, to be three; shalosh = three; in Hebrew the number was grammatically embedded in the text as both the number and the interpretive key — Joseph decoded both dreams by equating three (the number of objects in each dream) with three (the number of days until fulfillment); the interpretive principle was the same for both dreams: the number in the dream was the number of the days

- **Three as the number of divine witness and completion:**
  - Deuteronomy 19:15 — "at the mouth of two witnesses, or at the mouth of three witnesses, shall the matter be established" — three as the number that established a matter legally; the third witness made the testimony legally complete
  - Ecclesiastes 4:12 — "a threefold cord is not quickly broken" — three as the number of completeness and strength
  - The Trinity — Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — the triune nature of God as the foundational expression of the divine "three"; the number three was theologically embedded in the nature of God before it was a numerical quantity

- **Three days as the specific divine interval:**
  - The three days in Genesis 40 were both literal (the butler and baker's verdicts were three days away) and typologically significant (the three-day pattern of death-interval-resurrection)
  - 1 Corinthians 15:4 — "he rose again the third day according to the scriptures" — the "according to the scriptures" confirmed that the Old Testament had established the three-day pattern as a scriptural expectation; Genesis 40's "three branches are three days / three baskets are three days" was part of the accumulated biblical three-day structure that Paul called "according to the scriptures"
  - The three days of Genesis 40 encoded both outcomes — restoration on the third day (the butler) and judgment on the third day (the baker) — because the third day was the divine day of reckoning, and the outcome on that day depended on what had been determined about the person, not on the day itself

### SHAKACH — TO FORGET / TO CEASE TO REMEMBER (Hebrew: שָׁכַח)

**Genesis 40:23** — "Yet did not the chief butler remember Joseph, but forgat him (shakach)"

- **Root:** Shakach = to forget, to neglect, to let slip from memory; the word carried the connotation of neglect as well as involuntary forgetting — the butler did not actively try to forget Joseph; he simply ceased to bring him to mind; the forgetting was passive but no less a failure for being passive; Joseph had asked for kindness (v.14 — "shew kindness, I pray thee") and the kindness was not shown
- **The theological weight of the final verse:** Genesis 40 closed with this single sentence: "Yet did not the chief butler remember Joseph, but forgat him." The placing of this sentence as the chapter's concluding note was deliberate; the chapter that began with Joseph serving two troubled men ended with those men's outcomes decided and Joseph still in prison, still forgotten; the narrative deliberately left the reader in the waiting, in the silence, in the experience of what it felt like for Joseph during those two years (Genesis 41:1)
- **Shakach in Deuteronomy — the warning against forgetting God:** Deuteronomy 4:9 — "Only take heed to thyself, and keep thy soul diligently, lest thou forget (shakach) the things which thine eyes have seen, and lest they depart from thy heart all the days of thy life"; Deuteronomy 6:12 — "Then beware lest thou forget (shakach) the LORD, which brought thee forth out of the land of Egypt, from the house of bondage"; Deuteronomy 8:11, 14, 19 — the triple warning against forgetting God in prosperity; the butler's forgetting of Joseph was the precise pattern the Deuteronomy warnings described: in the moment of restoration and prosperity, when everything was going well ("when it shall be well with thee" — Genesis 40:14 — the exact condition Joseph had anticipated), the beneficiary forgot the benefactor; the Deuteronomy warnings about forgetting God in prosperity were illustrated in miniature by the butler forgetting Joseph in restoration
- **The God who does not shakach:** Psalm 9:18 — "For the needy shall not alway be forgotten (shakach): the expectation of the poor shall not perish for ever" — the divine counterpoint to human forgetting; Isaiah 49:15 — "yea, they may forget (shakach), yet will I not forget thee" — God's self-declaration as the one who did not forget; the butler's shakach was overridden by God's refusal to shakach; the two-year silence was not divine forgetting — it was divine timing holding Joseph until the moment of maximum strategic positioning

### ERABON — THE INTERPRETATION AS A DIVINE GIFT (see also: PESHER — INTERPRETATION, Hebrew: פֵּשֶׁר)

**Genesis 40:8** — "Do not interpretations (pithronim) belong to God?"
**Genesis 40:12** — "This is the interpretation (pithron) of it"

- **Root (pithron):** Pathar = to interpret a dream; pithron = the interpretation, the solution, the unlocking; the word was specific to the unlocking of dreams and visions; it was the dream-vocabulary equivalent of opening a sealed room — the dream was the sealed message, the pithron was its opening
- **Related term — pesher (Daniel's vocabulary):** Daniel used the related Aramaic word pesher for dream interpretation in the Babylonian context; the Joseph-Daniel connection was both terminological and theological; both men were dream-interpreters for foreign kings; both explicitly attributed the interpretive ability to God; both interpreted accurately; both were elevated to the second highest position in the empire as a result; the vocabulary and the career trajectory were structurally parallel — the two great prophetic interpreters of pagan kings' dreams in the Old Testament shared not only the same calling but the same humility about its source
- **"Each man according to the interpretation of his dream" (v.5):** The text's notation that each dream came "according to its own interpretation" (pithron) established that the dream and its meaning were given together as a unit; the interpretation was not imposed from outside by the interpreter's ingenuity but was inherent in the dream itself; Joseph's role was to receive and transmit the meaning that God had built into the dream; this was the narrative demonstration of 2 Peter 1:20-21 — the interpretation was not of private invention but of divine origin

### YOWM — DAY (Hebrew: יוֹם) — applied in the three-day pattern

**Genesis 40:12-13, 18-19, 20** — "three days... the third day"

- **Root:** Yowm = a day, a period of time, a defined interval; the word was used in both its literal sense (a twenty-four hour period) and its extended sense (an era, a season, an appointed time)
- **"On the third day" — yom ha-shlishi as the consistent appointed-day formula:** The pattern of "on the third day" as the moment when God's appointed action occurred ran through the Old Testament as a structural marker; Genesis 40 used it as the interval of the interpretations' fulfillment; the specific phrase "the third day, which was Pharaoh's birthday" (v.20) combined two time-markers — the third day of the divine interval and the specific festival day of the royal calendar — showing that God's appointed moment coincided with the human calendar's occasion; the divine timing did not require a special day — it arrived embedded in an ordinary (for Pharaoh) day of celebration
- **Birthday (yom hullad — the day of being born):** Genesis 40:20 — "Pharaoh's birthday" — the only birthday feast mentioned in the Old Testament; the next is Herod's birthday in Mark 6:21 at which John the Baptist was beheaded; in both cases a royal birthday celebration became the occasion of the execution of a condemned person; the festivity and the judgment occupied the same day; the pattern confirmed that judgment did not announce itself with appropriate solemnity — it arrived within the ordinary patterns of human celebration

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "The Butler Forgot Joseph" Proves God Had Abandoned Him or Made a Mistake in the Plan

**The Misuse:** Genesis 40:23 — the butler's forgetting of Joseph is read as evidence that Joseph's appeal was a mistake, that God's plan had been disrupted, or that the two years of additional prison time represented divine abandonment or punishment of Joseph for attempting to secure his own release through human means.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Psalm 105:17-19 identified the prison and the delay as divinely purposive:** "He sent a man before them, even Joseph... he was laid in iron: Until the time that his word came: the word of the LORD tried him" — the psalmist's commentary on the Joseph narrative identified the entire prison episode, including the two-year extension, as the "word of the LORD" trying (testing, refining) Joseph; the delay was not God's absence or Joseph's failure — it was the divine testing continuing until the "word of the LORD" had accomplished its refining purpose
- **The specific prison placement was strategic, not incidental:** Joseph had been placed in the king's prison (Genesis 39:20) — the facility that held Pharaoh's political prisoners; the butler and the baker were Pharaoh's officers (v.2); their presence in the same facility as Joseph was the mechanism of the connection; Joseph's interpretation of their dreams was the mechanism of the eventual introduction; the butler's forgetting extended the period but did not invalidate the connection; when the moment came, the butler remembered not out of gratitude but out of desperation (Genesis 41:9 — "Then spake the chief butler unto Pharaoh, saying, I do remember my faults this day") — a more powerful introduction than a casual recommendation would have been
- **Genesis 45:5-8 — Joseph's own theological assessment:** "Now therefore be not grieved, nor angry with yourselves, that ye sold me hither: for God did send me before you to preserve life... And God sent me before you to preserve you a posterity in the earth, and to save your lives by a great deliverance. So now it was not you that sent me hither, but God" — Joseph did not interpret his suffering as God's absence or abandonment; he interpreted every element — the pit, the sale, the false accusation, the prison, the forgetting — as God sending him ahead for a purpose; the butler's forgetting was one element of the same divine sending
- *Correction:* The butler's forgetting of Joseph was the extension of God's trial of Joseph through delay, not evidence of divine abandonment or Joseph's strategic error. Psalm 105:17-19 identified the prison period, including its duration, as "the word of the LORD tried him." The delay positioned Joseph to arrive before Pharaoh not as a casually recommended prisoner but at the moment of maximum royal desperation, through the memory of the one person best positioned to introduce him — the daily cupbearer at Pharaoh's table.

### "Interpretations Belong to God" Means Dreams Today Must Be Treated as Authoritative Divine Revelation

**The Misuse:** Genesis 40:8 — "Do not interpretations belong to God?" is used to argue that contemporary dreams should be received and interpreted as authoritative divine messages requiring interpretation and action, equal in authority to Scripture.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The context of Genesis 40 was specific:** The dreams in Genesis 40 were given to specific people in a specific context — the advance of God's covenant purpose through Joseph; they were not general communication but specific instruments of divine providence with exact, verifiable, literal fulfillments within three days; the Butler's dream was fulfilled exactly (v.21); the baker's dream was fulfilled exactly (v.22); the verification was immediate and precise
- **Numbers 12:6 — the conditions of dream-communication:** "If there be a prophet among you, I the LORD will make myself known unto him in a vision, and will speak unto him in a dream" — God spoke to prophets in dreams; the dream-communication was attached to the prophetic office, not to the general believing population; not every dream was divine communication; not every person who dreamed received a divine message
- **Deuteronomy 13:1-5 — the test for the dream-prophet:** "If there arise among you a prophet, or a dreamer of dreams, and giveth thee a sign or a wonder... Thou shalt not hearken unto the words of that prophet, or that dreamer of dreams: for the LORD your God proveth you, to know whether ye love the LORD your God with all your heart" — the warning that false prophets could also produce signs and wonders through dreams; the test was not whether the dream occurred but whether the interpretation led toward the LORD or away from Him
- **Hebrews 1:1-2 — the sufficiency of the Son's revelation:** "God, who at sundry times and in divers manners spake in time past unto the fathers by the prophets, Hath in these last days spoken unto us by his Son" — the progression of revelation; the dream-communication was part of "sundry times and divers manners"; the full and final revelation was in the Son; the canonical Scripture was the deposit of that revelation; Acts 17:11 — the Bereans were commended not for comparing claims to their dreams but for examining the Scriptures daily
- *Correction:* "Interpretations belong to God" was Joseph's theological declaration about the source of prophetic interpretation — that true interpretation was God's to give, not the interpreter's to invent. It established the principle of divine authorship of revelation and its interpretation. It was not a general authorization of every contemporary dream as authoritative divine revelation requiring interpretation. The Bible's own tests for genuine prophetic dreams (Numbers 12:6; Deuteronomy 13; Hebrews 1:1-2) established that canonical Scripture was the normative and sufficient means of God's communication to His people.

### The Three Days Are a Symbolic Number Only — Not a Literal Interval

**The Misuse:** Genesis 40:12-13's "three branches are three days" is used as evidence that biblical "three days" language should always be read symbolically or approximately rather than literally — and this is then applied to argue that Christ's resurrection "on the third day" was approximate and the specific temporal claim in 1 Corinthians 15:4 was flexible rather than precise.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The fulfillment in Genesis 40 was precise and literal:** Genesis 40:20 — "And it came to pass the third day, which was Pharaoh's birthday... And he restored the chief butler unto his butlership... But he hanged the chief baker: as Joseph had interpreted to them" — both fulfillments occurred on the exact third day; the text presented this as a literal three-day interval with literal fulfillments; Joseph's interpretations were validated by their literal precision, not their approximate symbolic resonance; the entire power of the chapter's theological point (the accuracy of God-given interpretation) rested on the literal, precise fulfillment
- **1 Corinthians 15:4 — Paul's "third day according to the scriptures":** "He rose again the third day according to the scriptures" — the Greek "te hemerai te trite" was the specific ordinal; the resurrection was on a specific, identifiable, calendrically precise day; the "according to the scriptures" referred to the accumulated three-day pattern in the Old Testament that had established the third day as the day of divine restoration; the Old Testament pattern was both typologically significant and literally predictive
- **The nature of typology in Scripture:** The butler's three days were literally three days and typologically prefigured the resurrection's three days; the literal and the typological operated simultaneously; the baker's three days were literally three days and typologically prefigured the judgment; the typological meaning did not require the abolition of the literal; both the Joseph narrative and the resurrection were literal historical events whose literalness was the foundation of their typological significance
- *Correction:* The three days of Genesis 40 were literal — fulfilled on the exact third day as the text stated — and typologically significant. The typological significance did not replace the literal precision; it was established through it. The same was true of the resurrection's third day: literally the third day, and therefore typologically the fulfillment of the accumulated Old Testament three-day pattern. The literalness of both events was the basis of their theological weight.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Forgotten Righteous — The Pattern of the Overlooked Servant and God's Perfect Timing

**Genesis 40:23 / Ecclesiastes 9:15 / Psalm 105:17-20 / Isaiah 49:14-16**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 40:23 — Joseph forgotten by the one he had served; the two-year silence; the dungeon after the interpretation
- Ecclesiastes 9:15 — "Now there was found in it a poor wise man, and he by his wisdom delivered the city; yet no man remembered that same poor man" — Qohelet's observation: the poor wise man who saved the city was not remembered; the pattern of the saving wisdom going unrecognized and unremembered was the common experience of the world
- Psalm 105:17-20 — the divine perspective on the "forgetting": it was the word of the LORD trying Joseph; from God's perspective the prison and the delay were not forgetfulness but purposive testing; the "until the time that his word came" established that God had a time; the time was not yet; when it came it came with precision
- Isaiah 49:14-16 — the divine answer to the feeling of being forgotten: "Zion said, The LORD hath forgotten me... Can a woman forget her sucking child?... yet will I not forget thee. Behold, I have graven thee upon the palms of my hands" — the human analogy of the nursing mother was the most extreme case of natural, instinctive, unavoidable remembering; and God said even that extreme case could theoretically fail; but He would not forget; Joseph was graven on God's hands during the two years of the butler's forgetting
- The theological constant: the butler's forgetting, the Qohelet observation, and the Zion lament were all instances of the human experience of being overlooked; God's answer in every case was the precision of His own memory and timing; what humans forgot, God held; what appeared abandoned was being prepared

### The Binary Outcome — Two Persons, Two Verdicts, One Moment

**Genesis 40 / Matthew 7:13-14 / Matthew 25:31-46 / Luke 23:39-43**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 40 — two officers, same prison, same interpreter, same interval; one restored, one executed; the most compressed form of the binary judgment in the Old Testament
- Matthew 7:13-14 — "Enter ye in at the strait gate: for wide is the gate, and broad is the way, that leadeth to destruction, and many there be which go in thereat: Because strait is the gate, and narrow is the way, which leadeth unto life, and few there be that find it" — two ways, two destinations; the binary of life and death built into the structure of the moral universe
- Matthew 25:31-46 — "And before him shall be gathered all nations: and he shall separate them one from another, as a shepherd divideth his sheep from the goats: And he shall set the sheep on his right hand, but the goats on the left" — the final division; same throne, same moment, two verdicts; the sheep and goat image compressed the Genesis 40 butler-baker binary into the eschatological finality
- Luke 23:39-43 — two criminals at the cross; same crucifixion, same Christ, same moment; one to condemnation, one to paradise; the identical circumstance producing opposite outcomes based on the moral condition of the person, not the circumstance
- The theological constant: from Genesis 40 through the Sermon on the Mount through the final judgment, the Bible consistently presented the ultimate reality as a binary — not a spectrum of degrees but two verdicts, two destinations, two outcomes; the butler-baker structure was the narrative seed of every subsequent binary-judgment passage

### The Vine That Produced the Cup — From Butler's Dream to the Last Supper

**Genesis 40:9-11 / Isaiah 5:1-7 / Matthew 21:33-41 / John 15:1-5 / Matthew 26:26-29**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 40:9-11 — vine, three branches, grapes pressed into cup, cup given into king's hand; the complete vine-cup-king sequence in its first compressed form
- Isaiah 5:1-7 — the LORD's vineyard: planted, cultivated, expected to yield good grapes; yielded wild grapes; judgment declared; "the vineyard of the LORD of hosts is the house of Israel" — the vine identified as Israel; the pressing anticipated by the vineyard parable was the pressing of judgment
- Matthew 21:33-41 — the parable of the wicked husbandmen: the vineyard let out to husbandmen who killed the servants and the son; the vineyard taken and given to others; the vine-and-son connected; the son sent to the vineyard was the Son given to the world
- John 15:1 — "I am the true vine, and my Father is the husbandman" — Christ as the true vine, replacing the failed covenant vine of Isaiah 5; the branches that abide in Him bear fruit; those that do not are cut off
- Matthew 26:27-29 — the Last Supper: "he took the cup, and gave thanks, and gave it to them, saying, Drink ye all of it; For this is my blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins" — the vine pressed into the cup; the cup given into the disciples' hands; the king (God the Father) receiving the product of the vine through the Son's offering; the butler's dream sequence — vine pressed into cup given into king's hand — was the miniature of the entire vine-cup theology from Genesis 40 to John 15 to the Last Supper

### The Birds That Ate — From the Baker's Flesh to Revelation's Supper

**Genesis 40:17, 19 / Deuteronomy 28:26 / Ezekiel 39:17-20 / Revelation 19:17-18**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 40:17 — the birds eating from the baker's basket in his dream: the first introduction of birds as the image of judgment on the condemned
- Genesis 40:19 — Joseph's interpretation: "the birds shall eat thy flesh from off thee" — the baker's judgment was executed not merely by hanging but by the birds devouring his exposed body; this was the covenant curse of the unburied dead
- Deuteronomy 28:26 — covenant curse: "And thy carcase shall be meat unto all fowls of the air, and unto the beasts of the earth, and no man shall fray them away" — the curse of the birds eating the flesh was formalized in Deuteronomy as the covenant judgment on Israel's unfaithfulness; what happened to the baker in Genesis 40 was the curse Deuteronomy would later codify
- 1 Samuel 17:44, 46 — Goliath's threat and David's counter-threat: "I will give thy flesh unto the fowls of the air, and to the beasts of the field" — the birds-eating-flesh as the declaration of total defeat and divine judgment
- Ezekiel 39:17-20 — "Speak unto every feathered fowl, and to every beast of the field, Assemble yourselves, and come; gather yourselves on every side to my sacrifice that I do sacrifice for you, even a great sacrifice upon the mountains of Israel, that ye may eat flesh, and drink blood" — the eschatological birds-gathering for judgment; God's great sacrifice on the mountains as the eschatological reckoning
- Revelation 19:17-18 — "And I saw an angel standing in the sun; and he cried with a loud voice, saying to all the fowls that fly in the midst of heaven, Come and gather yourselves together unto the supper of the great God; That ye may eat the flesh of kings, and the flesh of captains, and the flesh of mighty men" — the final eschatological birds-gathering; what began with birds eating from the baker's basket in Genesis 40:17 reached its ultimate expression in the supper of the great God in Revelation 19; the same image, the same theological content — divine judgment executed on the condemned — from the baker in the prison to the kings of the earth at the final reckoning

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time the Principle "Interpretations Belong to God" Was Stated Explicitly

**Genesis 40:8** — "Do not interpretations belong to God?"
- The first explicit doctrinal statement about the source of dream-interpretation in Scripture
- First mention establishes: the interpreter of divine revelation was not the human vessel but the God who gave the revelation; this principle would govern every subsequent biblical instance of prophetic interpretation — Joseph before Pharaoh (Genesis 41:16), Daniel before Nebuchadnezzar (Daniel 2:27-28), Peter on the day of Pentecost (Acts 2:16 — "this is that which was spoken by the prophet Joel"), and the governing hermeneutic of 2 Peter 1:20-21; the first explicit statement of the principle occurred in the context of a slave in a prison serving troubled court officers — not in a temple, not in a palace, not by a great prophet; God placed the governing hermeneutical principle of all prophetic interpretation in the mouth of a forgotten prisoner

### First Birthday Feast in the Old Testament — The Day of Festivity as the Day of Judgment

**Genesis 40:20** — "Pharaoh's birthday... he made a feast unto all his servants"
- The first birthday celebration recorded in the Old Testament
- First mention establishes: the day of the birthday feast was the day when the judicial verdicts were executed; not the day of mourning or the day of deliberate judgment, but the day of the royal celebration; judgment arrived within festivity; the reckoning was not announced by appropriate solemnity but embedded in the ordinary life of the court; the second birthday feast in Scripture (Mark 6:21-28 — Herod's birthday, the beheading of John the Baptist) confirmed the pattern: the birthday of the powerful was the occasion of execution of the righteous or the fulfillment of unjust verdicts; the first mention established that God's appointed times of reckoning did not require the world's acknowledgment to arrive

### First Explicit Statement That God's Word Tried/Tested a Person Through Extended Suffering

**Psalm 105:18-19** — retrospective commentary on Genesis 40's period: "The word of the LORD tried him"
- Though the statement was in Psalm 105 (not Genesis 40 itself), it was the first retrospective divine commentary on what the Genesis 40 prison period meant
- First mention establishes: extended suffering was the instrument of the "word of the LORD" testing a person; the trial was not punitive but refining; the prison was not punishment but preparation; the delay was not abandonment but the extension of the testing period "until the time that his word came"; this principle structured the theology of patient endurance throughout the Old and New Testaments — James 1:2-4 (testing producing patience), Hebrews 12:11 (chastening producing peaceable fruit), Romans 5:3-4 (tribulation producing patience producing experience producing hope)
