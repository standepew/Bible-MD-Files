## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 46 was the chapter of the great descent — the final movement of the patriarchal family out of Canaan and into Egypt. Jacob rose with all he had and came to Beersheba and offered sacrifices to the God of his father Isaac. Beersheba was the threshold — the southernmost point of Canaan, the well of the oath, the place where both Abraham (Genesis 21:33) and Isaac (Genesis 26:23-25) had worshipped and where God had spoken. Jacob did not cross the border without first worshipping at the last altar in the covenant land.

God spoke to Israel in the visions of the night. He called: "Jacob, Jacob" — the double-name call that marked every decisive moment of divine address in Scripture (Abraham: Genesis 22:11; Moses: Exodus 3:4; Samuel: 1 Samuel 3:10; Simon: Luke 22:31; Saul: Acts 9:4). Jacob answered: "Here am I." God identified Himself: "I am God, the God of thy father" — the covenant formula connecting the patriarchal chain. Then came four declarations: (1) Fear not to go down into Egypt; (2) I will there make of thee a great nation; (3) I will go down with thee into Egypt; (4) I will also surely bring thee up again. The four-part covenant guarantee covered the descent, the growth in Egypt, the divine accompaniment, and the future return. The Exodus was promised before the sojourn began. He added: "and Joseph shall put his hand upon thine eyes" — the specific tender promise that Joseph would be present at Jacob's death.

Jacob rose from Beersheba. The sons of Israel carried their father in the wagons Pharaoh had sent. They came with all their livestock and goods — Jacob and all his seed with him. The great genealogical list followed: the sons of Leah (thirty-three), the sons of Zilpah (sixteen), the sons of Rachel (fourteen), the sons of Bilhah (seven) — plus Jacob himself and Joseph's two Egyptian-born sons — seventy souls in all. The seventy were not a round number but a covenant-precise number; Deuteronomy 10:22 recalled it; Exodus 1:5 memorialized it.

Judah was sent ahead to direct the family to Goshen. Joseph made ready his chariot and drove up to Goshen to meet his father. When he came before him he fell on his neck and wept a good while. Jacob said: "Now let me die, since I have seen thy face, because thou art yet alive." The declaration of completion — the man who had said he would go down to the grave mourning (Genesis 37:35) was now ready to die not in grief but in fulfillment.

Joseph instructed his brothers on the protocol before Pharaoh: declare yourselves shepherds so you will be settled in Goshen, "for every shepherd is an abomination unto the Egyptians." The covenant family's separation within Egypt was secured providentially through Egypt's own cultural prejudice. The host nation's rejection of the Hebrews' occupation was God's instrument for keeping Israel distinct and concentrated in Goshen — the separation that would make the later Exodus a clearly defined departure of a clearly defined people.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Jacob, Jacob" — The Double-Name Call at the Threshold of the Great Commission

**Genesis 46:2** — "And God spake unto Israel in the visions of the night, and said, Jacob, Jacob. And he said, Here am I"

- The doubled name was the biblical mark of divine urgency and intimate personal address at a moment of supreme covenant significance; it was never casual but always preceded a commission that would reshape redemptive history
- Genesis 22:11 — "Abraham, Abraham" — at the binding of Isaac; the angel calling Abraham back from the sacrifice at the most critical moment of his life; the doubling arrested the action
- Exodus 3:4 — "Moses, Moses" — at the burning bush; the commissioning of the deliverer of Israel; the initiation of the Exodus
- 1 Samuel 3:10 — "Samuel, Samuel" — the call of the boy-prophet; the inauguration of the prophetic office in Samuel's generation
- Luke 22:31 — "Simon, Simon" — Christ's warning to Peter at the Last Supper; the doubling marking the crisis of Peter's coming denial and the promise of his restoration
- Acts 9:4 — "Saul, Saul" — the conversion of Paul on the Damascus road; the doubling at the moment of the most dramatic commission in the New Testament
- The pattern was consistent: the doubled name marked the threshold of divine commission; "Jacob, Jacob" at Beersheba was the commission to descend — the same divine urgency that called Abraham to the mountain, Moses to the bush, and Saul from the road called Jacob to Egypt

### "I Will Go Down With Thee — And I Will Also Surely Bring Thee Up Again"

**Genesis 46:4** — the double promise: accompaniment and return

- Genesis 15:13-14 — the Abrahamic covenant had already decreed the sojourn and the Exodus: "thy seed shall be a stranger in a land that is not theirs... four hundred years; And also that nation, whom they shall serve, will I judge: and afterward shall they come out with great substance" — the "bring thee up again" of Genesis 46:4 was the personal activation of the national promise of Genesis 15:14; the return was already guaranteed decades before the descent began
- Isaiah 43:2 — "When thou passest through the waters, I will be with thee; and through the rivers, they shall not overflow thee: when thou walkest through the fire, thou shalt not be burned" — the covenant presence through every environment of difficulty; Egypt was the water and the fire; the divine accompaniment was the guarantee that the sojourn would not swallow the covenanted
- Deuteronomy 31:6 — "Be strong and of a good courage, fear not... for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee" — Moses applying the same Genesis 46:4 accompaniment-promise to the nation crossing into Canaan; the same God who promised Jacob "I go down with thee" promised the nation "I go with thee" at every subsequent crossing
- Hebrews 13:5 — "I will never leave thee, nor forsake thee" — the New Covenant's expression of the same accompaniment principle; the God of Genesis 46:4 was the God of Hebrews 13:5; the "never leave thee" was not a new promise but the eternal form of the promise first given in personal terms at Beersheba
- Hosea 11:1 / Matthew 2:15 — "out of Egypt have I called my son" — the calling up that Genesis 46:4 promised was applied by Matthew to Christ's own descent into Egypt and return; Jacob's family went down; Christ went down; Israel was called out (Exodus); Christ was called out (Matthew 2:15); the pattern of descent-and-return was built into redemptive history at Beersheba

### The Seventy — The Covenant Census at the Threshold of the Nation

**Genesis 46:27** — "all the souls of the house of Jacob, which came into Egypt, were threescore and ten"

- Genesis 10 — the Table of Nations listed seventy nations descended from Noah's three sons; the seventy of the nations and the seventy of Jacob's house were parallel constructions; one seventy populated the earth; the other seventy would become the nation through whom all those earth-peoples would eventually be blessed (Genesis 12:3)
- Deuteronomy 10:22 — Moses recalled: "Thy fathers went down into Egypt with threescore and ten persons; and now the LORD thy God hath made thee as the stars of heaven for multitude" — the covenant arithmetic was preserved: seventy in, stars of heaven out; the Abrahamic promise of Genesis 15:5 was fulfilled through the seventy who descended
- Deuteronomy 32:8 — "When the Most High divided to the nations their inheritance, he set the bounds of the people according to the number of the children of Israel" — the nations of Genesis 10 and the sons of Israel of Genesis 46 were linked in God's governance of history; the seventy of the nations and the seventy of Israel were the two counts by which the Most High ordered the earth
- Luke 10:1 — "After these things the Lord appointed other seventy also, and sent them two and two before his face into every city" — Christ's seventy sent into every city paralleled both the seventy nations of Genesis 10 and the seventy souls of Genesis 46; the mission to the nations through the seventy was the covenant pattern extended to the church's universal mission
- Notably: in the list of seventy, Levi's sons included Gershon, Kohath, and Merari (v.11); from Kohath would come Amram; from Amram would come Moses and Aaron; the deliverer of the Exodus was already present in the womb of the genealogy of the descent — Kohath went down among the seventy, and the Exodus was in his lineage

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### MAR'OT HALLAYLAH — VISIONS OF THE NIGHT (Hebrew: מַרְאֹת הַלַּיְלָה)

**Genesis 46:2** — "And God spake unto Israel in the visions of the night"

- **Root:** Mar'ah = vision, appearance; plural mar'ot = multiple vision-images; the medium was not a single dream-image but a series of night-visions — the medium of the prophets
- **The night vision as the channel for threshold communications:** Every time the patriarchal family stood at a covenant threshold, God communicated in a night-vision or dream: Jacob at Bethel (Genesis 28:12 — the ladder); Jacob returning from Laban (Genesis 31:10-13); now at Beersheba before Egypt; the night visions marked the critical transitions; day-time activity could drown the voice of God; the night was the appointed hour for the decisive word
- **Daniel's night visions:** Daniel 7:2 — "Daniel spake and said, I saw in my vision by night" — the same Hebrew construction mar'ah ballylah; Genesis 46's night vision and Daniel 7's night vision were the same divine medium applied to individual commission and to prophetic vision of the future; both were night-visions at the threshold of great and difficult periods

### ALAH — TO BRING UP / TO ASCEND (Hebrew: עָלָה)

**Genesis 46:4** — "I will also surely bring thee up again (ve'anochi a'alcha gam aloh)"

- **Root:** Alah = to go up, to ascend; the infinitive absolute construction "a'alcha aloh" meant certainly, surely, without doubt bring up; the doubled verb form (the infinitive absolute + finite verb) was the Hebrew grammar of absolute certainty — not "I might bring you up" but "I will surely bring you up"
- **The same verb in the Exodus promise:** Exodus 3:8 — "I am come down to deliver them... and to bring them up (le'alotam) out of that land unto a good land" — God's promise to Moses used the same alah; the promise to Jacob in Genesis 46 (I will bring thee up) and the promise to Moses in Exodus 3 (I will bring them up) were the same divine commitment in personal and national scale; the alah of Genesis 46:4 was the seed of the Exodus
- **Alah and resurrection:** The verb to go up / to ascend / to be brought up was consistently the verb of ascent from death and captivity; Ezekiel 37:12-13 — "I will open your graves, and cause you to come up out of your graves (ve'he'eleti)" — the same alah applied to the resurrection of the dry bones; God's promise to bring Jacob up from Egypt was the pattern of God's promise to raise His people from every grave

### SHIVIM — SEVENTY (Hebrew: שִׁבְעִים)

**Genesis 46:27** — "all the souls of the house of Jacob, which came into Egypt, were threescore and ten"

- **Seventy as the number of covenant completeness:** The seventy nations of Genesis 10; the seventy elders of Israel (Exodus 24:1; Numbers 11:16); the seventy years of Babylon's captivity (Jeremiah 25:11-12); the seventy weeks of Daniel (Daniel 9:24); the seventy sent by Christ (Luke 10:1); seventy consistently marked the divinely appointed complete enumeration — not approximate but covenant-precise
- **The genealogy as the first covenant census:** The list of seventy names in Genesis 46:8-27 was the first covenant census — every individual named before they became a nation; every subsequent census (Numbers 1; Numbers 26) echoed this first naming; the God who counted the seventy before the sojourn was the God who would count every redeemed soul at the end: Revelation 7:4 — "And I heard the number of them which were sealed: and there were sealed an hundred and forty and four thousand of all the tribes of the children of Israel"

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Isaac Forbidden to Go to Egypt / Jacob Commanded to Go — Same God, Different Purposes

**Genesis 26:2 / Genesis 46:3-4**

| Person | Command | Reason | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Isaac | "Go not down into Egypt" (Genesis 26:2) | The covenant sojourn was not yet Isaac's appointment; the Philistine famine was for him to endure in Canaan | Isaac stayed; the Abrahamic blessing flowed in the land |
| Jacob | "Fear not to go down into Egypt" (Genesis 46:3) | The appointed time for the covenant descent had come; the seventy must become the nation in Egypt | Jacob descended; seventy became millions |

*The same God gave opposite directives to father and son. The directives were not contradictory but sequential — Isaac was the wrong generation for Egypt; Jacob was the right generation. Ecclesiastes 3:1 — "To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven." The governing principle was God's timing, not a fixed geographical rule.*

---

### The Descent Into Egypt as the Pattern of Christ's Incarnation and Return

**Genesis 46:3-4 / Hosea 11:1 / Matthew 2:14-15**

Matthew 2:14-15 — "And he arose, and took the young child and his mother by night, and departed into Egypt: And was there until the death of Herod: that it might be fulfilled which was spoken of the Lord by the prophet, saying, Out of Egypt have I called my son."

The pattern: Israel descended into Egypt (Genesis 46); God promised to bring them up (Genesis 46:4); the Exodus fulfilled it (Exodus 3:8-10). Christ descended into Egypt (Matthew 2:14); God called Him out (Matthew 2:15). The family's descent-and-return was the historical type that Christ enacted personally. "I will go down with thee and bring thee up again" (Genesis 46:4) was spoken to Jacob; "Out of Egypt have I called my son" (Hosea 11:1; Matthew 2:15) was spoken of Israel and of Christ — the nation's pattern and the Son's pattern were one.

---

### "Now Let Me Die" — The Nunc Dimittis of the Patriarch

**Genesis 46:30 / Luke 2:29-30 / Psalm 27:13-14**

| Person | The Waiting | The Fulfillment | The Declaration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jacob | Twenty-two years without Joseph | Joseph's face and embrace | "Now let me die, since I have seen thy face" |
| Simeon | Lifetime waiting for the consolation of Israel | The infant Christ in his arms | "Lord, now lettest thou thy servant depart in peace" (Luke 2:29) |

*Both declarations were the language of completion, not despair. Both men had held through the long night of unfulfilled longing. Both declared readiness for death not because life was empty but because the purpose of the waiting had been fulfilled. Both were given more years after the declaration — Jacob lived seventeen more years in Egypt (Genesis 47:28); Simeon's subsequent life is not recorded but the declaration was the turning point, not the ending.*

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "Fear Not to Go Down Into Egypt" Means God Always Blesses Relocation to Difficult Places

**The Misuse:** Genesis 46:3 is used to argue that whenever a believer moves to a difficult or spiritually hostile place for provision or opportunity, God's command to Jacob is their authorization.

**What Scripture says:**

- Genesis 26:2 — God explicitly told Isaac NOT to go to Egypt when faced with famine; the command was location and person-specific, not a universal permission for covenant people to relocate to spiritually hostile territory
- The command to Jacob was given with explicit divine speech confirming it was God's will (Genesis 46:2-4); there was no ambiguity; God spoke directly; Jacob did not decide based on pragmatic assessment and then claim divine authorization
- The Mosaic Law later warned extensively about the dangers of Egypt as a symbol of worldly dependence: Deuteronomy 17:16 — "he shall not cause the people to return to Egypt... forasmuch as the LORD hath said unto you, Ye shall henceforth return no more that way"; Deuteronomy 28:68 — Egypt as the ultimate covenant curse
- *Correction:* The command "fear not to go down into Egypt" was a specific, directly-spoken divine commissioning for the specific task of growing the covenant family into a nation in the specific period of famine and Josephine protection. It was not a template permission for the covenant people to relocate to places of worldly abundance. The governing question was always: has God spoken? — not: is there provision there?

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Beersheba — The Covenant Threshold of Every Great Departure

**Genesis 46:1 / Genesis 21:33 / Genesis 26:23-25 / Genesis 28:10**

- Abraham planted a tamarisk at Beersheba and called on the name of the LORD, the Everlasting God (Genesis 21:33) — the first covenant worship at the southern threshold
- Isaac built an altar at Beersheba after God appeared with the "fear not" assurance (Genesis 26:23-25) — the second covenant worship at the threshold; the altar and the "fear not" formula were the same in both Genesis 26 and Genesis 46
- Jacob departed from Beersheba toward Haran (Genesis 28:10) — the first great departure; the Bethel ladder vision came the first night of the journey
- Jacob now offered at Beersheba before Egypt (Genesis 46:1) — the last great departure; the night vision came at this last threshold

The pattern: Beersheba was the place of covenant worship at every patriarchal departure; not a destination but a threshold; the altar of acknowledgment that the journey ahead belonged to God; Psalm 121:8 — "The LORD shall preserve thy going out and thy coming in from this time forth, and even for evermore."

### The Genealogy — The Exodus Was Already in the List

**Genesis 46:11 / Exodus 2:1-2 / Numbers 3:17-19**

In the genealogical register of the seventy descended into Egypt, verse 11 recorded the sons of Levi: Gershon, Kohath, and Merari. From Kohath came Amram. From Amram came Moses and Aaron (Exodus 6:18, 20). The deliverer of the Exodus was already present in the womb of the genealogy of the descent. The man who would stand before Pharaoh and say "Let my people go" was the great-grandson of the man who went down among the seventy. The Exodus was already inside the descent — the return was built into the going down, exactly as God had promised.

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time Judah Was Sent Ahead as the Functional Leader and Pathfinder for the Whole Family

**Genesis 46:28** — "And he sent Judah before him unto Joseph, to direct his face unto Goshen"
- Jacob sent Judah — not Reuben the firstborn, not Benjamin the youngest, not one of the older sons of the concubines — to lead the family's approach to Egypt
- Establishes: the transfer of functional leadership to Judah was confirmed by Jacob's choice before Genesis 49 formally announced it; the scepter had already functionally moved to Judah; Genesis 49:10 — "The sceptre shall not depart from Judah" — was the formal declaration of what Genesis 46:28 had already demonstrated in practice

### First Time the Entire Covenant Family Was Enumerated in a Comprehensive List

**Genesis 46:8-27** — the seventy named
- First covenant census; the complete enumeration of the covenant family at the moment of the great threshold-crossing
- Establishes: the God of the covenant was a God who counted and named His people at every great transition; the census was not bureaucratic but covenantal; the named individual was the base unit of the covenant, not the anonymous mass; Exodus 1:1-5 would repeat the naming principle; Revelation 7:4-8 would close Scripture with the same named-and-numbered covenant community
