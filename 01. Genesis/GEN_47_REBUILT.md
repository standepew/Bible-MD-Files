## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 47 covered the seventeen final years of Jacob in Egypt from the perspective of two tracks: the public track of Joseph's administration of the famine, and the private track of Jacob's life, faith, and preparation for death.

Joseph brought five of his brothers before Pharaoh, and they declared themselves shepherds seeking to sojourn in Goshen. Pharaoh granted it — made them rulers over his livestock. Then Joseph brought in his father Jacob and presented him to Pharaoh. Pharaoh asked: "How old art thou?" Jacob answered: "The days of the years of my pilgrimage are an hundred and thirty years: few and evil have the days of the years of my life been, and have not attained unto the days of the years of the life of my fathers in the days of their pilgrimage." The answer was precise: his years were a pilgrimage (megurim — a sojourning, never settling as a native); they had been few and evil — not the complaint of a bitter man but the honest assessment of a life shaped by flight (from Esau), labor (under Laban), grief (Joseph's apparent death, Dinah's violation, Simeon's detention), and finally reunion; and he had not yet reached the years of Abraham (175) or Isaac (180). Jacob then blessed Pharaoh — and left.

The famine continued to consume Egypt. The people first brought their money; when money failed, they brought their cattle; when the cattle were gone, they offered their land and themselves: "buy us and our land for bread, and we and our land will be servants unto Pharaoh." Joseph bought all the land of Egypt for Pharaoh — except the priests' land, which Pharaoh had given them a portion that sustained them so they did not sell. He moved the people into the cities. Then he gave them seed for the fields and established the statute that would endure: one-fifth of the harvest to Pharaoh, four-fifths to the people for their households and for seed. The people received it: "thou hast saved our lives: let us find grace in the sight of my lord, and we will be Pharaoh's servants." The twenty-percent tithe to Pharaoh became the law of the land of Egypt.

Israel dwelt in Goshen; they had possessions there and grew and multiplied greatly. Jacob lived seventeen years in Egypt — the same number of years Joseph had lived in Jacob's house before being sold (Genesis 37:2 — Joseph was seventeen when he was sold). The echo was precise. Joseph's seventeen years with his father in Canaan and Jacob's seventeen years with his son in Egypt were the narrative's own bracketing of the separation.

When Jacob knew he was about to die, he called Joseph and made him swear: "Put, I pray thee, thy hand under my thigh, and deal kindly and truly with me; bury me not, I pray thee, in Egypt: But I will lie with my fathers, and thou shalt carry me out of Egypt, and bury me in their buryingplace." The hand-under-the-thigh oath was the most solemn form of patriarchal covenant-swearing — used by Abraham when he sent his servant to find a wife for Isaac (Genesis 24:2); now used by Jacob when he charged Joseph to carry his bones back to Canaan. Joseph swore. Jacob bowed himself on the head of his bed — the posture of worship in the approaching presence of death, which Hebrews 11:21 identified as the act of faith: "by faith Jacob, when he was a dying, blessed both the sons of Joseph; and worshipped, leaning upon the top of his staff."

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Jacob Blessed Pharaoh" — The Lesser Is Blessed by the Greater

**Genesis 47:7, 10** — "And Jacob blessed Pharaoh... And Jacob blessed Pharaoh, and went out from before Pharaoh"

- Jacob blessed Pharaoh twice — when he came before him and when he left; the one who had nothing by the world's measure (the sojourner, the shepherd, the father of slavery-sold sons) blessed the one who had everything; the patriarch who had been sustained by the covenant God blessed the king of the world's greatest power
- Hebrews 7:7 — "And without all contradiction the less is blessed of the better" — the Hebrews principle stated in the context of Melchizedek blessing Abraham; the greater blesses the lesser; if Jacob blessed Pharaoh, Jacob was the greater — not in earthly power but in covenant standing; the bearer of the Abrahamic covenant who carried the promises of God in his person was greater than any earthly throne
- Genesis 12:3 — "and in thee shall all families of the earth be blessed" — the Abrahamic blessing that was meant to flow through the covenant line to all nations; Jacob blessing Pharaoh was the first sustained instance of the Abrahamic blessing-to-the-nations being enacted; the family of Abraham had come to Egypt in famine and the patriarch's blessing rested on the king; the Abrahamic promise was not idle — it moved through Jacob's hands even when his hands were empty

### "Few and Evil Have the Days of the Years of My Life Been" — The Honest Pilgrimage

**Genesis 47:9** — "The days of the years of my pilgrimage are an hundred and thirty years: few and evil have the days of the years of my life been"

- The Hebrew word for "pilgrimage" was megurim — from gur, the word for sojourning as a stranger/alien in a land that is not yours; Jacob described his entire life as megurim; he had never been a settled resident anywhere — always a sojourner in Canaan, a sojourner under Laban, now a sojourner in Egypt
- Hebrews 11:13 — "These all died in faith, not having received the promises, but having seen them afar off, and were persuaded of them, and embraced them, and confessed that they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth" — the patriarchs' self-identification as strangers and pilgrims was not a failure of faith but an expression of it; they were looking for the city whose builder and maker was God (Hebrews 11:10); Jacob's "few and evil" was the honest pilgrimage-testimony of a man who had held the promise without fully receiving it
- Psalm 39:12 — "Hear my prayer, O LORD, and give ear unto my cry; hold not thy peace at my tears: for I am a stranger with thee, and a sojourner, as all my fathers were" — the psalmist echoing the patriarchal identity; the megurim of Genesis 47:9 was the lived experience behind the Psalm 39 prayer
- 1 Peter 2:11 — "Dearly beloved, I beseech you as strangers and pilgrims, abstain from fleshly lusts, which war against the soul" — the New Testament application of the patriarchal megurim to the church; all believers are sojourners; Jacob's "the days of my pilgrimage" was the language that Peter applied to the whole covenant people

### The Hand Under the Thigh — The Most Solemn Covenant Oath

**Genesis 47:29** — "put, I pray thee, thy hand under my thigh, and deal kindly and truly with me"

- Genesis 24:2 — Abraham commanded his servant the same oath: "put, I pray thee, thy hand under my thigh" — the only two instances of this form of oath-swearing in the Bible; used by Abraham when commissioning the finding of Isaac's wife and by Jacob when commissioning his own burial in the covenant land; both occasions involved the perpetuation of the covenant beyond the life of the one who swore
- The theological significance of the gesture: the thigh (yarekh) was associated with the place of the covenant sign — circumcision was performed in the region of the thigh; to place the hand under the thigh in swearing was to invoke the covenant of circumcision as the ground of the oath; the oath was sworn on the covenant itself; the most binding oath available to a patriarch
- Hesed ve'emet — "kindly and truly": the phrase Jacob used — "deal kindly and truly with me" — was the same covenantal formula hesed (covenant lovingkindness) and emet (faithfulness/truth) that appeared in Exodus 34:6 as the defining description of God's own character; Jacob asked Joseph to act toward him with the character that God Himself displayed; the covenant-word pair was the highest appeal in patriarchal speech

### Joseph's Administration — The Provider Who Saved Egypt and Set the Terms

**Genesis 47:13-26** — Joseph's management of the famine

- Joseph's administration was remarkably comprehensive: he collected money (vv.14-15), then cattle (vv.16-17), then land and persons (vv.18-21), established the people in the cities (v.21), gave them seed (v.23), and instituted the twenty-percent tax (v.24-26) — the complete reorganization of Egypt's economic structure around the famine
- Proverbs 22:7 — "The rich ruleth over the poor, and the borrower is servant to the lender" — the principle enacted; when the people ran out of money, cattle, and land, they offered themselves as servants; Joseph's redistribution was not rapacious but structured — the people themselves proposed it (v.19) and thanked Joseph for it (v.25); the twenty-percent was not punitive but sustainable
- The priests' exemption (v.22): "Only the land of the priests bought he not; for the priests had a portion assigned them of Pharaoh, and did eat their portion which Pharaoh gave them: wherefore they sold not their lands" — the religious establishment was not subject to the famine-redistribution; the precedent of keeping religious endowments separate from civil acquisition was the first mention of institutional religious protection in the ancient world; this became the pattern for the Levitical portion in Israel (Numbers 18:20-24 — the Levites had no land inheritance because the LORD was their portion)

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### MEGURIM — PILGRIMAGE / SOJOURNING (Hebrew: מְגוּרִים)

**Genesis 47:9** — "The days of the years of my pilgrimage (megurim) are an hundred and thirty years"

- **Root:** Gur = to sojourn, to live as a resident alien, to dwell temporarily in a place where you have no permanent citizenship; megurim = the duration and experience of sojourning; the noun described not a single journey but a life lived as a temporary dweller in lands not permanently one's own
- **The ger (sojourner) as the patriarchal identity:** Abraham: "I am a stranger and a sojourner with you" (Genesis 23:4); Jacob: "the days of my pilgrimage" (Genesis 47:9); the self-description was consistent across the generations; the patriarchs never claimed ownership of Canaan in their lifetimes; they were always sojourners in the land they had been promised
- **The theological reversal:** The world measures a man's years by his permanence, his property, his settled achievements; Jacob measured his years by their quality as megurim — as a stranger's wandering; the sojourner identity was not a failure of the covenant but the evidence of it; Hebrews 11:16 — "But now they desire a better country, that is, an heavenly: wherefore God is not ashamed to be called their God"

### HESED VE'EMET — COVENANT LOVINGKINDNESS AND FAITHFULNESS (Hebrew: חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת)

**Genesis 47:29** — "deal kindly and truly with me (hesed ve'emet)"

- **The covenant word-pair:** Hesed (covenant lovingkindness, loyalty, steadfast love) and emet (truth, faithfulness, reliability) appeared together throughout Scripture as the paired description of God's own character and the standard He required of covenant partners
- **Exodus 34:6-7 — God's self-description:** "The LORD, The LORD God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness (hesed) and truth (emet)" — the first time God declared His own name after the golden calf; hesed and emet were the first qualities He named; Jacob's appeal to Joseph used the same word-pair; he was asking Joseph to act toward him with the character of God
- **Psalm 85:10 — the great meeting:** "Mercy (hesed) and truth (emet) are met together; righteousness and peace have kissed each other" — the psalm of the reunion of the covenant qualities; in the cross, God's hesed (mercy) and emet (truth) met in Christ; the demand of the Law was not surrendered (emet) and the mercy was not withheld (hesed); Jacob's appeal for hesed ve'emet from Joseph was the patriarchal shadow of the divine reconciliation of those two qualities in the atonement

### SHALACHTI — THE TWENTY-PERCENT / THE FIFTH PART (Hebrew: חֹמֶשׁ)

**Genesis 47:24** — "And it shall come to pass in the increase, that ye shall give the fifth part unto Pharaoh"

- **Root:** Chamesh = five; chamishi = fifth; the five-part division: four parts for the people, one part for Pharaoh; the twenty-percent as the covenant rate established in Joseph's administration
- **The tithe and the fifth:** The biblical tithe was ten percent (Leviticus 27:30; Malachi 3:10); Joseph's five-part taxation was twenty percent — twice the tithe; both were fractions of the whole dedicated to an authority above the household; the tithe went to God (through the Levites); Joseph's fifth went to Pharaoh; the principle was the same — a portion of provision acknowledged the source and the governor of that provision
- **The Benjamin echo:** Genesis 43:34 — Benjamin received five portions; Genesis 45:22 — Benjamin received five changes of raiment; Genesis 47:24 — one-fifth to Pharaoh; the number five appeared consistently in the Joseph narrative as the mark of abundant grace set apart

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Joseph the Savior of Egypt — The Nations Say "Thou Hast Saved Our Lives"

**Genesis 47:25 / John 4:42 / Acts 4:12**

The Egyptians said to Joseph: "Thou hast saved our lives." They became his servants voluntarily — not as slaves driven by force but as people who owed their existence to his provision. John 4:42 — "this is indeed the Christ, the Saviour of the world." Acts 4:12 — "Neither is there salvation in any other: for there is none other name under heaven given among men, whereby we must be saved." Joseph as the savior of Egypt who redistributed life to those who came to him was the type of Christ as the Savior of the world who gives eternal life to all who come to Him. Both came to their position through betrayal, suffering, and exaltation. Both opened their storehouse to all who came. Both received voluntary service from those they saved.

### Jacob's Seventeen Years With Joseph in Egypt / Joseph's Seventeen Years With Jacob in Canaan

**Genesis 37:2 / Genesis 47:28**

| Period | Duration | Persons | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| Joseph with Jacob | Seventeen years (Genesis 37:2 — age seventeen when sold) | Joseph with his father | Canaan |
| Jacob with Joseph | Seventeen years (Genesis 47:28) | Jacob with his son | Egypt |

*The seventeen years of Joseph with his father in Canaan were the years of the father's love and the coat of many colors and the dreams. The seventeen years of Jacob with his son in Egypt were the years of the father's completion and the son's provision and the preparation for death. The narrative bracketed the entire separation with the same number. The years of joy before and the years of reunion after were equal in measure. Romans 8:18 — "For I reckon that the sufferings of this present time are not worthy to be compared with the glory which shall be revealed in us."*

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "Few and Evil Have the Days of My Years Been" — Jacob Was Faithless and Bitter

**The Misuse:** Genesis 47:9 is used to argue that Jacob's assessment was the voice of a faithless, ungrateful, self-pitying old man who failed to acknowledge God's goodness.

**What Scripture says:**

- Jacob's assessment was accurate — by any measure his life had been marked by extraordinary grief: the twenty-two-year loss of Joseph, Dinah's violation, the deaths of Rachel and Deborah, the treachery of Laban, the flight from Esau; the days had genuinely been evil in the biblical sense (ra — marked by adversity and hardship); this was not self-pity but accurate accounting
- The same Jacob is in Hebrews 11:21 — "By faith Jacob, when he was a dying, blessed both the sons of Joseph; and worshipped, leaning upon the top of his staff" — God's own assessment of Jacob was that he died in faith; the "few and evil" speech did not disqualify him from the Hall of Faith
- The word "evil" (ra) in the patriarchal vocabulary described not moral wickedness but adversity, hardship, and painful experience — Job used the same vocabulary to describe the "evil" that came upon him (Job 2:11); the days that were marked by ra were the days shaped by painful providence, not the days of a man who had lived sinfully
- *Correction:* "Few and evil" was Jacob's honest, accurate, and theologically appropriate assessment of a life of covenant pilgrimage marked by real suffering. It did not contradict faith — it was the honest statement of faith that did not pretend the suffering had not been real. Hebrews 11:13 confirmed that the patriarchs died in faith while not having received the promises; Jacob's assessment and his faith were simultaneously true.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Seventeen-Year Bracket — Equal Seasons of Love and Reunion

**Genesis 37:2 / Genesis 47:28 / Ecclesiastes 3:1-8**

The narrative's arithmetic was deliberate. Joseph was seventeen when sold; he spent seventeen years with his father before the betrayal. Jacob spent seventeen years with Joseph in Egypt before his death. The years of the father's love before the separation and the years of the son's provision after the reunion were numerically equal. The God who measured the years of Egypt's abundance and famine (seven and seven) measured the years of the patriarchal family's separation and reunion with the same precision. Ecclesiastes 3:1 — "To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven."

### Israel Multiplied in Goshen — The First Verse of the Exodus Story

**Genesis 47:27 / Exodus 1:7 / Genesis 1:28**

- Genesis 47:27 — "And Israel dwelt in the land of Egypt, in the country of Goshen; and they had possessions therein, and grew, and multiplied exceedingly"
- Exodus 1:7 — "And the children of Israel were fruitful, and increased abundantly, and multiplied, and waxed exceeding mighty; and the land was filled with them"
- Both verses used the same Hebrew vocabulary of fruitfulness and multiplication — the Genesis 1:28 mandate ("Be fruitful, and multiply") was being fulfilled in Egypt by the covenant family; the increase that alarmed Pharaoh in Exodus 1 had begun in the protective enclave of Goshen in Genesis 47; the seed of the Exodus was the multiplication that began in the safety of Joseph's provision

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time a Patriarch Was Asked His Age by a Pagan Ruler — And the Age Was a Testimony

**Genesis 47:8-9** — "How old art thou? And Jacob said... the days of the years of my pilgrimage"
- First explicit exchange of patriarchal age-testimony before a Gentile ruler
- Establishes: the believer's age and life-history are a testimony to covenant faithfulness and pilgrimage; Jacob did not answer Pharaoh's question with pride (I am 130 years old, a great man) but with humility (few and evil, megurim); the years were a sojourning, not an achievement; the testimony of honest assessment before the world's power was the model of patriarchal witness

### First Time a Specific Tax Rate Was Instituted as a National Law in Scripture

**Genesis 47:24, 26** — "ye shall give the fifth part unto Pharaoh... Joseph made it a law"
- First recorded national taxation statute in Scripture — twenty percent of increase to the governing authority
- Establishes: the principle of giving a portion of increase to the civil authority as the proper ordering of provision; this became the background for later Mosaic teaching on tithes, firstfruits, and the portion owed to the governing authority (Romans 13:6-7 — "render therefore to all their dues: tribute to whom tribute is due")
