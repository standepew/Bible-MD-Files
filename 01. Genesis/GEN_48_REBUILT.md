## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 48 was the chapter of the crossed hands — one of the most deliberate acts of Jacob's entire life, performed on his deathbed with dim eyes and a dying body, but with full theological intentionality. When Joseph heard his father was ill, he brought his two sons — Manasseh and Ephraim — to receive Jacob's blessing. The scene that followed was the last major act of the patriarchal blessing-transmission and contained within it one of the most structurally significant moments of the book.

Jacob rallied. He gathered his strength and sat up on the bed. He told Joseph what God Almighty (El Shaddai) had said to him at Luz (Bethel) — the covenant appeared to him, said "I will make thee fruitful and multiply thee, and I will make of thee a multitude of people, and I will give this land to thy seed after thee for an everlasting possession." He then made the adoption declaration: Ephraim and Manasseh, your sons born to you in Egypt before I came to you — they are mine; as Reuben and Simeon are mine, they shall be mine. Any children born to Joseph after them would be his own and would be called by their brothers' names. By this act, Joseph's two Egyptian-born sons received full tribal standing in Israel, and Joseph effectively received a double portion — two tribes instead of one. This was the practical implementation of the birthright: Jacob was giving the firstborn's double portion to Joseph by elevating his two sons to tribal status.

Jacob looked up and saw Joseph's sons. He asked who they were (his eyes were dim with age — the same phrase used of Isaac when he blessed Jacob, Genesis 27:1). Joseph identified them. Jacob called them near and embraced and kissed them. He said: "I had not thought to see thy face: and, lo, God hath shewed me also thy seed." Then Joseph arranged them deliberately: Ephraim at Israel's left hand (Jacob's right hand) as the younger, and Manasseh at Israel's right hand (Jacob's left hand) as the firstborn — the proper protocol for the blessing of the firstborn.

Jacob stretched out his right hand — and crossed his arms, laying his right hand on Ephraim (the younger) and his left on Manasseh (the firstborn). He blessed Joseph: "God, before whom my fathers Abraham and Isaac did walk, the God which fed me all my life long unto this day, the Angel which redeemed me from all evil, bless the lads." Then he crossed-named them: "and let my name be named on them, and the name of my fathers Abraham and Isaac; and let them grow into a multitude in the midst of the earth."

Joseph saw the crossed hands and was displeased. He took his father's hand to move it — "Not so, my father: for this is the firstborn; put thy right hand upon his head." Jacob refused. "I know it, my son, I know it: he also shall become a people, and he also shall be great: but truly his younger brother shall be greater than he, and his seed shall become a multitude of nations." Then the concluding blessing: Joseph above his brethren; the angel a redeemer; the promise of the land.

The crossed hands were not accidental. They were deliberate. Jacob had dim eyes — but he crossed his hands "wittingly" (Genesis 48:14 — sikkel, meaning with understanding, with wisdom, intentionally). He was not repeating the mistake of his own father Isaac who had been deceived into blessing Jacob; he was acting with full theological consciousness, overriding the natural order of firstborn priority in accordance with the divine pattern he had seen repeated in his own life and in the lives of his fathers.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh — The Double Portion for Joseph

**Genesis 48:5** — "Ephraim and Manasseh, even as Reuben and Simeon, shall be mine"

- Deuteronomy 21:17 — "But he shall acknowledge the son of the hated for the firstborn, by giving him a double portion of all that he hath" — the law of the firstborn's double portion; Jacob was giving Joseph the firstborn's double portion through the tribal adoption of his two sons; instead of one tribe bearing Joseph's name, two tribes (Ephraim and Manasseh) carried the double portion
- 1 Chronicles 5:1-2 — "Now the sons of Reuben the firstborn of Israel, (for he was the firstborn; but, forasmuch as he defiled his father's bed, his birthright was given unto the sons of Joseph the son of Israel: and the genealogy is not to be reckoned after the birthright. For Judah prevailed above his brethren, and of him came the chief ruler; but the birthright was Joseph's)" — the Chronicles' explicit theological summary: Reuben lost the birthright through sin; it went to Joseph (the double-tribe portion); but the ruler/scepter went to Judah; the two aspects of the firstborn blessing were split
- Genesis 37:3 — Jacob had loved Joseph more than all his children as the son of his old age; the coat of many colors was the visible sign of the firstborn's honor; the adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh was the legal implementation of what the coat had signified

### "The Angel Which Redeemed Me From All Evil" — The First Use of Goel (Redeemer) in Scripture

**Genesis 48:16** — "The Angel which redeemed me (ha-Malakh ha-goel oti) from all evil, bless the lads"

- **The first mention of goel (redeemer) in the Bible:** Jacob's blessing-prayer was the first use of the word goel — the redeemer, the kinsman-redeemer — in all of Scripture; it appeared here before Boaz, before Job, before Isaiah; the first redeemer named in the Bible was the Angel who had kept Jacob through his life
- **The Angel who redeemed:** This was not a created angel but the Angel of the LORD — the same divine messenger who had wrestled with Jacob at Peniel (Genesis 32:28-30 — Jacob said "I have seen God face to face"), who had appeared to Hagar (Genesis 16:7-13), Abraham (Genesis 22:11-12), Moses (Exodus 3:2); the Angel was the pre-incarnate Christ; Jacob's goel was the Son of God acting as the divine kinsman-redeemer
- **Job 19:25 — the same goel:** "For I know that my redeemer (goeli) liveth" — Job's declaration of the living Redeemer; Job used the same word (goel) Jacob first spoke; the kinsman-redeemer who would stand at the last day
- **Isaiah 41:14 — God as Israel's goel:** "Fear not, thou worm Jacob, and ye men of Israel; I will help thee, saith the LORD, and thy redeemer (go'alekh) is the Holy One of Israel" — God identifying Himself as Israel's goel; the same word Jacob used for the Angel in Genesis 48:16; the Angel-goel of Genesis 48 and the Holy One-goel of Isaiah 41 were the same divine person
- **Ruth 3-4 — the goel as the practical kinsman-redeemer:** Boaz as the goel for Ruth and Naomi — the one who paid the redemption price, married the widow, and restored the inheritance; the legal institution of the goel-redeemer was built on the theological reality that Jacob first named in Genesis 48:16

### The Crossed Hands — "He Guided His Hands Wittingly"

**Genesis 48:14** — "And Israel stretched out his right hand, and laid it upon Ephraim's head, who was the younger, and his left hand upon Manasseh's head, guiding his hands wittingly"

- Sikkel (guiding wittingly) = from sakal, to act with insight, prudence, and intentional wisdom; Jacob was not confused or fumbling; he was making a deliberate, theologically informed choice; his eyes were dim but his understanding was clear
- The consistent reversal pattern in Scripture — the younger before the elder as the mark of divine election over natural precedence:
  - Genesis 4 — Abel's offering accepted over Cain's (secondborn over firstborn)
  - Genesis 17-21 — Isaac chosen over Ishmael (second-born over firstborn)
  - Genesis 25-27 — Jacob chosen over Esau (secondborn over firstborn)
  - Genesis 48 — Ephraim before Manasseh (secondborn over firstborn)
  - 1 Samuel 16 — David chosen over seven older brothers
  - Romans 9:10-13 — Paul citing Jacob and Esau: "the elder shall serve the younger"
- Romans 9:11 — "For the children being not yet born, neither having done any good or evil, that the purpose of God according to election might stand, not of works, but of him that calleth" — the divine election of the lesser over the greater was the consistent mark of God's sovereignty operating outside of human natural systems; Jacob's crossed hands enacted what Paul's theology named
- Hebrews 11:21 — "By faith Jacob, when he was a dying, blessed both the sons of Joseph; and worshipped, leaning upon the top of his staff" — the act of crossing his hands was the act of faith the Hebrews writer identified; it was not instinct but faith — faith that God's pattern of election continued in the next generation

### "The God Which Fed Me All My Life Long" — The Pastoral Image of the Provider

**Genesis 48:15** — "the God which fed me all my life long unto this day"

- **The Hebrew ra'ah:** The word "fed" was ra'ah — the shepherd-verb; "the God who shepherded me all my life long"; Jacob's life-review at the moment of blessing his grandsons was the declaration that God had been his shepherd through every season — through the flight from Esau, the years under Laban, the losses, the grief, the Egypt descent
- **Psalm 23:1 — "The LORD is my shepherd":** David's great shepherd-psalm used the same ra'ah; Jacob's "God who shepherded me" (Genesis 48:15) and David's "the LORD is my shepherd" (Psalm 23:1) were the same declaration; the shepherd-God as the one who provided, guided, and protected through every valley
- **John 10:11 — "I am the good shepherd":** Christ's self-identification as the shepherd was the full revelation of what Jacob named in the blessing-prayer; the God who had shepherded Jacob was the same God who would take on flesh as the Good Shepherd who laid down His life for the sheep

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### GOEL — REDEEMER / KINSMAN-REDEEMER (Hebrew: גֹּאֵל)

**Genesis 48:16** — "The Angel which redeemed me (ha-goel oti) from all evil"

- **Root:** Ga'al = to redeem, to reclaim, to act as the kinsman who buys back what was lost; the goel was the next-of-kin who had both the right and the obligation to redeem — to buy back a family member sold into slavery (Leviticus 25:47-49), to redeem forfeited land (Leviticus 25:25), to avenge the blood of a murdered kinsman (Numbers 35:19), to marry the widow of a deceased kinsman to preserve the family line (Ruth 3-4)
- **The first use — the theological foundation of all subsequent uses:** Jacob's goel was the Angel; every subsequent use of goel built on this first declaration; Boaz was the goel for Ruth and Naomi because God was the ultimate goel; Isaiah 41:14 called God Israel's goel because the Angel of Genesis 48:16 was God; Job 19:25's "I know that my redeemer liveth" was the same goel Jacob had named; the entire institution of the kinsman-redeemer in the Law (Leviticus 25; Ruth 3-4) was the legal and social form of what was first named as a divine reality in Jacob's blessing-prayer
- **The goel and atonement:** The goel paid the redemption price; he did not merely advocate — he acted; he put his own resources into the transaction; the Angel who had been Jacob's goel from all evil was the same divine person who would become flesh (John 1:14) to act as the ultimate goel, paying the redemption price for the enslaved and restoring the forfeited inheritance (Ephesians 1:7; 1 Peter 1:18-19)

### SIKKEL — WITTINGLY / WITH INTENTIONAL UNDERSTANDING (Hebrew: שִׂכֵּל)

**Genesis 48:14** — "guiding his hands wittingly (sikkel)"

- **Root:** Sakal = to be prudent, to act with insight, to understand; the intensive hiphil form sikkel = to act with deliberate wisdom; the word was used of prudent and successful action elsewhere: Joshua 1:8 — "that thou mayest observe to do according to all that is written therein: for then thou shalt make thy way prosperous, and then thou shalt have good success (taskil)" — the sakal/sikkel word group described action guided by understanding of God's will, not by human instinct
- **The contrast with Isaac's blindness:** Genesis 27:1 — Isaac's eyes were dim; he was deceived because he could not see; Genesis 48:10 — Jacob's eyes were also dim; but Jacob "guided his hands wittingly"; the same physical limitation produced opposite spiritual results; Isaac's dim eyes were exploited by deception; Jacob's dim eyes were compensated by spiritual insight; the physical blindness and the spiritual clarity were inversely related in both cases

### EL SHADDAI — GOD ALMIGHTY (Hebrew: אֵל שַׁדַּי)

**Genesis 48:3** — "God Almighty appeared unto me at Luz in the land of Canaan"

- **Root:** El = God; Shaddai = variously rendered Almighty, All-Sufficient, the Mountain God, the One of the breast (nurturer); El Shaddai was the name by which God revealed Himself to the patriarchs (Exodus 6:3 — "I appeared unto Abraham, unto Isaac, and unto Jacob, by the name of God Almighty, but by my name JEHOVAH was I not known to them")
- **El Shaddai as the name of covenant sufficiency:** When El Shaddai appeared to Abram (Genesis 17:1), it was to covenant and to change his name; when El Shaddai appeared to Jacob at Bethel (Genesis 35:11), it was to confirm the covenant and rename him Israel; here Jacob cited the El Shaddai appearance as the foundation of his adoption-declaration; the name of covenant sufficiency was the authority behind the tribal adoption; what El Shaddai had promised (a multitude of nations from Jacob) was being implemented through the crossing of hands

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Crossed Hands — The Pattern of the Cross in the Blessing

**Genesis 48:14 / Galatians 3:13-14 / Numbers 21:8-9 / John 3:14**

Jacob's crossed arms — right hand on the younger, left hand on the firstborn — formed the shape of the cross over the two lads. While this is not explicitly stated in the text, the theological pattern was consistent: the blessing crossed the natural order (firstborn over secondborn), and the instrument of the crossing was the deliberate act of the patriarch's hands. The crossing was the mechanism of the reversal — the means by which the grace that was not due by natural right was transferred to the one who received it.

Galatians 3:13 — "Christ hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree" — the cross was the instrument of reversal; on it the natural order of guilt and innocence was crossed; the cursed became the redeemed through the act of the One who took the curse. Jacob's crossed hands were the patriarchal shadow of the mechanism of reversal that the cross enacted.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### The Crossed Hands Prove Jacob Was Confused Because His Eyes Were Dim

**The Misuse:** Genesis 48:17 — Joseph's concern that his father had placed the right hand on the wrong son is used to argue that Jacob had simply confused the two boys because of his failing eyesight.

**What Scripture says:**

- Genesis 48:14 — the text itself explicitly said Jacob "guided his hands wittingly" (sikkel) — with intentional understanding; the narrator did not leave it ambiguous; the crossing was deliberate
- Genesis 48:19 — when Joseph protested, Jacob said "I know it, my son, I know it" — he was fully aware that Manasseh was the firstborn and that he was placing the right hand on Ephraim; the double "I know it" was the text's own emphasis of his complete intentionality
- Hebrews 11:21 — "By faith Jacob, when he was a dying, blessed both the sons of Joseph; and worshipped, leaning upon the top of his staff" — Hebrews identified the act as an act of faith, not a mistake corrected by divine providence; faith implies intentional trust in God's revealed purposes, not accidental fumbling
- The contrast with Isaac: Isaac's dim eyes led to an unintended blessing on the wrong son (Genesis 27) — Jacob knew that story because he had lived it; Jacob's dim eyes produced an intentional blessing on the son God had chosen; the same physical limitation had produced two completely different outcomes because one was deception and the other was faith
- *Correction:* Jacob's crossed hands were a deliberate, faith-filled, theologically conscious act. He knew Manasseh was the firstborn. He chose Ephraim with full understanding. The dim eyes were the context; the faith was the cause; the crossing was the act.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Rachel Mentioned at the Moment of Ephraim-Manasseh Adoption — The Mother Behind the Double Portion

**Genesis 48:7 / Genesis 35:16-20 / Genesis 37:3**

Jacob's rehearsal of Rachel's death in the middle of the adoption declaration was not incidental. Ephraim and Manasseh were Joseph's sons — Rachel's grandsons. The coat of many colors given to Joseph (Genesis 37:3) had been the marker of Rachel's son's favored standing. The adoption of Ephraim and Manasseh gave Rachel's line a double-tribe portion that no other wife's children received. Rachel had died "with but a little way to come unto Ephrath" — she never reached the place of settlement in Canaan. Her grandsons received the double tribal inheritance in the land she never saw. The double portion was the posthumous covenant-honor of Rachel, the beloved wife of Jacob's heart.

### "In Thee Shall Israel Bless" — Ephraim and Manasseh as the Blessing-Names of Israel

**Genesis 48:20 / Numbers 6:24-26**

"God make thee as Ephraim and as Manasseh" became the blessing-formula used by Jewish fathers over their sons to this day. The two names that had meant "God made me forget toil" and "God made me fruitful in affliction" became the blessing-aspiration for every generation — may your children forget the sorrow and be fruitful in the land of their affliction. The names that Joseph had given out of his own experience of redemption became the names that all of Israel would speak as a blessing over the next generation.

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Use of Goel (Redeemer) in All of Scripture

**Genesis 48:16** — "The Angel which redeemed me (ha-goel oti)"
- The first occurrence of the root ga'al (to redeem) in its personal noun form goel (redeemer) in the entire Bible
- Establishes: the Redeemer was divine (the Angel of the LORD, the pre-incarnate Christ); the redemption was from "all evil" (comprehensive); the redeemer was the kinsman who acted on behalf of the one who could not act for himself; every subsequent use of goel — from Boaz (Ruth 3-4) to God as Israel's goel (Isaiah 41:14) to Job's goel (Job 19:25) to Christ as the ultimate goel — was built on this first declaration

### First Time a Patriarch Deliberately Reversed the Birth-Order in a Blessing With Full Knowledge

**Genesis 48:14, 19** — "guiding his hands wittingly... I know it, my son, I know it"
- Isaac had accidentally blessed the wrong son (Genesis 27); Jacob intentionally blessed the lesser son over the greater
- Establishes: the reversal of natural precedence as a conscious theological act, not merely a providential accident; from this point the reversal pattern was no longer only something God did secretly (choosing the womb's second-born before birth) but something the covenant patriarch enacted openly as an act of faith in God's revealed pattern
