## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 50 was the final chapter of the first book of Scripture — and it closed not with arrival but with a coffin in Egypt and an oath over bones, with every covenant promise still outstanding. The ending was deliberate. Genesis opened with creation; it closed with death and the promise of return. The movement of the whole book was toward a home not yet reached.

Joseph fell upon his father's face and wept over him and kissed him. He commanded the physicians to embalm Israel — forty days for the embalming (the appointed time), seventy days of mourning by all Egypt. The whole house of Pharaoh, all the elders, all the servants, all the people wept for Israel. Then Joseph asked Pharaoh's permission to bury his father in the grave he had prepared in Canaan — the oath under the thigh of Genesis 47:29-31 was about to be honored. Pharaoh gave it.

The funeral procession was one of the largest in ancient history: all the servants of Pharaoh, all the elders of his house, all the elders of Egypt, all of Joseph's house, his brothers, his father's house — only the little ones and the flocks were left in Goshen. Chariots and horsemen accompanied them — a military escort of state for the patriarch of the covenant family. When the Canaanites at the threshing floor of Atad saw the great mourning, they named the place Abel-Mizraim — the mourning of Egypt / the meadow of Egypt. Jacob's sons buried him in the cave of Machpelah — the field Abraham had bought from Ephron the Hittite — precisely as Jacob had commanded. Joseph returned to Egypt.

Then the old fear reasserted itself. When Jacob died, the brothers said to one another: "Joseph will peradventure hate us, and will certainly requite us all the evil which we did unto him." They sent a message invoking Jacob's name: "Thy father did command before he died, saying, So shall ye say unto Joseph, Forgive, I pray thee now, the trespass of thy brethren." Joseph wept when the message was brought. His brothers came and fell before him — "Behold, we be thy servants." The dreams were finally complete: all eleven had bowed, again, after Jacob's death, in the fear of the one they had wronged.

Joseph's response was the final and fullest theological statement of Genesis: "Fear not: for am I in the place of God? But as for you, ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good, to bring to pass, as it is this day, to save much people alive." Three elements: the renunciation of personal vengeance ("am I in the place of God?"), the acknowledgment of their evil intent ("ye thought evil"), and the declaration of sovereign divine purpose ("God meant it unto good"). He would nourish them and their children. He comforted them and spoke kindly to them.

Joseph lived a hundred and ten years. He saw Ephraim's children to the third generation; the children of Machir the son of Manasseh were brought up upon his knees. Then the final scene: "I die: and God will surely visit you, and bring you up out of this land unto the land which he sware to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob." The verb "visit" (pakad) was doubled: pakod yifkod — surely visit, certainly visit, God will without doubt visit. Joseph took an oath of the children of Israel: "God will surely visit you, and ye shall carry my bones up from hence."

So Joseph died, being a hundred and ten years old. They embalmed him and put him in a coffin in Egypt. Genesis ended: a coffin in a foreign land. The promise still outstanding. The covenant still waiting. The bones ready to go when God visited.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Ye Thought Evil Against Me; But God Meant It Unto Good" — The Two-Agent Summit

**Genesis 50:20** — the fullest statement of the principle first stated in Genesis 45:8

- **Genesis 45:5-8 compared to Genesis 50:20:**
  - Genesis 45:8 — "not you that sent me hither, but God" — the first statement; made at the moment of revelation; comprehensive but rapid
  - Genesis 50:20 — "ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good" — the final statement; made at the end of Joseph's life; the most precisely balanced; both human intention and divine intention stated side by side with their purposes contrasted
  - The advance from Genesis 45 to Genesis 50 was in the precision: "ye thought evil" (human intention clearly named as evil — ra) and "God meant it (chamav — planned, intended, devised) unto good (tov)" — two intentions, one event, opposite moral qualities, one redemptive outcome
- **Acts 2:23 — the cross as the supreme instance:** "Him, being delivered by the determinate counsel and foreknowledge of God, ye have taken, and by wicked hands have crucified and slain" — the same two-agent structure: "determinate counsel and foreknowledge of God" (God's intention) / "wicked hands" (human evil intention); the crucifixion was the supreme enactment of Genesis 50:20
- **Acts 4:27-28 — the most explicit New Testament parallel:** "For of a truth against thy holy child Jesus, both Herod, and Pontius Pilate, with the Gentiles, and the people of Israel, were gathered together, For to do whatsoever thy hand and thy counsel determined before to be done" — "whatsoever thy hand and thy counsel determined" = "God meant it unto good"; "Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, Israel gathered together" = "ye thought evil against me"; the parallel to Genesis 50:20 was structurally identical
- **Romans 8:28 — the universal extension:** "And we know that all things work together for good to them that love God, to them who are the called according to his purpose" — Paul's generalization of the Genesis 50:20 principle from the Joseph narrative to all of redemptive history; what was true in the specific case of Joseph was true in all cases of the called; the God who meant Joseph's suffering for good was the God who worked all things together for good for His people

### "Am I In the Place of God?" — The Renunciation of Personal Vengeance

**Genesis 50:19** — "Fear not: for am I in the place of God?"

- The question was rhetorical — the answer was no; Joseph was not in the place of God; he did not have the standing to take vengeance because vengeance belonged to the One in whose place he was not
- Deuteronomy 32:35 — "To me belongeth vengeance, and recompence; their foot shall slide in due time" — God's declaration that vengeance was His possession, not a human right; Joseph's "am I in the place of God?" was the practical application of Deuteronomy 32:35 before it was written
- Romans 12:19 — "Dearly beloved, avenge not yourselves, but rather give place unto wrath: for it is written, Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord" — Paul quoting Deuteronomy 32:35 as the governing principle for personal relationships; Joseph was already living the principle of Romans 12:19 before Moses wrote Deuteronomy 32; the patriarchal ethic and the apostolic ethic were the same
- Hebrews 10:30 — "For we know him that hath said, Vengeance belongeth unto me, I will recompense, saith the Lord" — the same principle in Hebrews; "am I in the place of God?" was the question that answered every impulse toward personal retaliation

### "God Will Surely Visit You" — Pakod Yifkod

**Genesis 50:24-25** — "God will surely visit you, and bring you up out of this land unto the land which he sware to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob... God will surely visit you, and ye shall carry my bones up from hence"

- **The doubled infinitive absolute — pakod yifkod:** The most emphatic form of Hebrew assertion; God will certainly, surely, without question, without fail visit; the doubling was the grammar of God's oath-level certainty; no contingency, no qualification, no possibility of failure
- **The same formula in Exodus:** Exodus 3:16 — "I have surely visited (pakod pakadti) you, and seen that which is done to you in Egypt" — God's words to Moses at the burning bush used the same doubled pakad formula; Joseph's dying declaration "God will surely visit you" (pakod yifkod — future) and God's declaration to Moses "I have surely visited you" (pakod pakadti — perfect/completed) were the same promise at two ends; Joseph prophesied it; God confirmed it had happened
- **Hebrews 11:22 — the act of faith that Hebrews identified:** "By faith Joseph, when he died, made mention of the departing of the children of Israel; and gave commandment concerning his bones" — Joseph's oath about his bones was the act of faith that Hebrews identified; not his successful administration of Egypt, not his interpretation of dreams, not his forgiveness of his brothers — but his confidence about the bones; the faith was in the promised return; the bones were the body's bet on the resurrection of the covenant promise

### The Bones of Joseph — Carried Through the Wilderness to Shechem

**Genesis 50:25 / Exodus 13:19 / Joshua 24:32 / Hebrews 11:22**

- Genesis 50:25 — the oath: "ye shall carry my bones up from hence"
- Exodus 13:19 — Moses honored it: "And Moses took the bones of Joseph with him" — in the chaos of the Exodus night, while Pharaoh's army was preparing, Moses took time to locate and carry Joseph's bones; the oath made to a dying man four hundred years earlier was kept to the letter; the bones traveled through the forty years of wilderness wandering
- Joshua 24:32 — the burial: "And the bones of Joseph, which the children of Israel brought up out of Egypt, buried they in Shechem, in a parcel of ground which Jacob bought of the sons of Hamor the father of Shechem" — the bones were buried at Shechem; the same ground Jacob had bought (Genesis 33:18-20); the bones that had gone down into Egypt as a living man and come back as the carried promise were laid to rest in the covenant land
- The theological message: Joseph's bones were the physical embodiment of the promise that God would visit; carrying them through the wilderness was an act of faith that the destination was real; the bones arrived where the promise pointed

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### PAKOD — TO VISIT / TO ATTEND TO / TO APPOINT (Hebrew: פָּקַד)

**Genesis 50:24-25** — "God will surely visit you (pakod yifkod)"

- **Root:** Pakad = to visit, to attend to with purpose, to appoint, to muster, to count, to number; the word carried the meaning of decisive divine attention that resulted in action; when God pakad His people it was never merely a passive observation but always an intervention with purpose — to deliver, to judge, to redeem, or to appoint
- **The doubled infinitive absolute form:** Pakod yifkod = the infinitive absolute + the finite verb of the same root; this was the Hebrew grammar of absolute certainty — "he will surely (without any possibility of failure) visit"; the same doubling was used by God at the burning bush: Exodus 3:16 — "I have surely visited (pakod pakadti) you"; Joseph's prophetic certainty (future) and God's confirmed fulfillment (completed) used the same grammar of absolute certainty
- **Other uses of pakad in Scripture:** Exodus 4:31 — "And the people believed: and when they heard that the LORD had visited (pakad) the children of Israel... they bowed their heads and worshipped" — the news that God had pakad was the news that set the Exodus in motion; Hannah (1 Samuel 2:21) was pakad by the LORD and conceived; Ruth 1:6 — "she had heard in the country of Moab how that the LORD had visited (pakad) his people in giving them bread" — the Exodus provision recalled through the same word; Luke 1:68 — "Blessed be the Lord God of Israel; for he hath visited (epeskepsato — the Greek equivalent) and redeemed his people" — Zechariah's song at John's birth used the same concept for Christ's coming

### CHAMAV — TO DEVISE / TO INTEND / TO PLAN (Hebrew: חָשַׁב — from chashav)

**Genesis 50:20** — "but God meant (chashavah — devised/planned) it unto good"

- **Root:** Chashav = to think, to plan, to devise, to reckon, to account; the same root used for faith reckoned as righteousness (Genesis 15:6 — "and he counted it to him for righteousness"); in Genesis 50:20 God's intention was not reactive but proactive — He had devised the good purpose, not merely accommodated it after the brothers acted; the brothers' evil was real; God's plan was prior and purposive
- **The contrast:** "Ye thought (chashavtem) evil against me; but God thought (chashavah) it unto good" — the same verb for both the brothers' intention and God's intention; they were both chashav; but the object of the brothers' chashav was evil (ra) and the object of God's chashav was good (tov); the same cognitive action (planning, devising, thinking toward an end) was applied by humans toward evil and by God toward good in the same event
- **Isaiah 55:8-9 — "my thoughts are not your thoughts":** "For my thoughts (machshovotai — from chashav) are not your thoughts... For as the heavens are higher than the earth, so are my ways higher than your ways, and my thoughts than your thoughts" — God's chashav (thoughts/plans/designs) operated at a level above human chashav; Genesis 50:20 was the narrative demonstration of what Isaiah 55:8-9 stated propositionally

### NACHAM — TO COMFORT / TO SPEAK TO THE HEART (Hebrew: נִחַם)

**Genesis 50:21** — "And he comforted them, and spake kindly unto them"

- **Root:** Nacham = to comfort, to console, to relieve of grief; "spake kindly" was literally al-libbam = to their hearts, onto their hearts; the comfort was not merely verbal reassurance but the restoration of their confidence through speech directed into the heart
- **Isaiah 40:1-2 — the ultimate comfort:** "Comfort ye, comfort ye my people, saith your God. Speak ye comfortably (al lev — to the heart) to Jerusalem" — Isaiah's great opening of the comfort section used the same phrase: speak to the heart; God's comfort to Israel through Isaiah used the same form as Joseph's comfort to his brothers in Genesis 50:21; Joseph's gentle speech to his grieving brothers was the type of God's comforting speech to the exiled and broken Israel
- **The comfort that followed acknowledgment of sin:** Joseph comforted them after the most explicit statement of God's sovereignty over their evil (v.20); the comfort was not given in denial of the sin but after the sin had been fully named and fully recontextualized; it was the comfort of the gospel — sin acknowledged, grace proclaimed, the frightened conscience addressed with kindness

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### "God Meant It Unto Good" — The Summit of the Joseph Narrative as the Key to the Cross

**Genesis 50:20 / Acts 2:23 / Acts 4:27-28 / Romans 8:28**

| Element | Genesis 50:20 | The Cross (Acts 2:23; 4:27-28) |
|---|---|---|
| The human agents | The brothers — "ye thought evil" | Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, Israel — "wicked hands" |
| The human intention | Evil (ra) | Destruction of the Son |
| God's intention | Good (tov) — "God meant it unto good" | Determined counsel (Acts 2:23) — salvation |
| The outcome | "To save much people alive" | Redemption of all who believe |
| The moral status of each agent | Both real, both responsible | Both real, both responsible |

*Genesis 50:20 was not only the theological summit of Genesis — it was the theological key to the cross. Peter in Acts 2:23, the church in Acts 4:27-28, and Paul in Romans 8:28 all drew on the same two-agent principle that Joseph first articulated at his father's grave. The God who meant Joseph's suffering for good was the same God who meant the cross for the salvation of the world.*

### The Coffin in Egypt — Genesis Ends Looking Forward

**Genesis 50:26 / Hebrews 11:22 / Hebrews 11:39-40 / Romans 8:24-25**

Genesis began with God creating and ended with a coffin in Egypt. The movement of the whole book was not from creation to completion but from creation to promise — and the promise was still outstanding. The coffin was the final image because the coffin was faith's final statement: the bones were ready to go when God visited.

Hebrews 11:39-40 — "And these all, having obtained a good report through faith, received not the promise: God having provided some better thing for us, that they without us should not be made perfect." All the Genesis patriarchs — Abel, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph — received a good report but did not receive the promise. The coffin in Egypt was the most visible emblem of that truth: Joseph died in faith and the promise was still ahead.

The book of Exodus began immediately after: "Now these are the names of the children of Israel, which came into Egypt" — and then the multiplication, and then the new Pharaoh, and then the affliction, and then Moses, and then "I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage" (Exodus 20:2). The coffin in Genesis was the setup for the Exodus. Every bone Joseph left in Egypt was a bet on the promise. God paid it.

---

### The Bones Carried — Faith as a Journey

**Genesis 50:25 / Exodus 13:19 / Joshua 24:32 / Hebrews 11:22**

The oath of the bones was the last act of Joseph's faith in Genesis and the first action of faith that the Exodus generation honored. Moses took the bones in the chaos of departure night. The bones traveled forty years through the wilderness. They arrived at Shechem — the place Jacob had bought from the sons of Hamor (Genesis 33:18-20), the place where Joseph had been sent to check on his brothers before the pit (Genesis 37:12-13), the place where the bones would finally rest. The journey of the bones from Egypt to Shechem was the physical enactment of the faith that Genesis 50:24 had declared: God will surely visit and bring you up out of this land.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "God Meant It Unto Good" Means All Suffering Has an Immediately Discernible Good Purpose

**The Misuse:** Genesis 50:20 is used to argue that every painful experience should have an obvious and observable good outcome, and that the absence of a visible good outcome indicates a lack of faith or a failure of God's promise.

**What Scripture says:**

- Genesis 50:20 was spoken forty years after the sale of Joseph and twenty years after the reunion — Joseph had the perspective of the full narrative completed; he could see the good that had resulted; the statement was retrospective, not prospective; he was not saying "every suffering will produce visible good within a timeframe I can see" but "looking back across the full arc of what happened, God's intention was good"
- Romans 8:28 — "all things work together for good" — not "all things feel good," not "all things produce immediate visible good," but "work together" — the collaboration of all things under God's sovereignty toward good; the working-together might take decades or centuries to become visible
- Hebrews 11:39-40 — the patriarchs "received not the promise" — the good that God intended was not always visible within a single lifetime; the Abraham who died without seeing the nation, the Joseph whose bones traveled forty years through the wilderness before reaching Shechem — neither saw the full good within their lifespan; the good was real but not always immediately discernible
- Job 42:5 — Job's resolution came at the end of the book, not in the middle; he could not see the good while he was in the ash pile; "God meant it unto good" was Job's testimony at the end, not an imposition to be required of him at chapter 3
- *Correction:* "God meant it unto good" was the retrospective declaration of a man who had lived long enough to see the full arc of Providence. It was not a promise that every suffering would produce visible good within the sufferer's timeframe of visibility. Romans 8:28's "all things work together for good" was a promise of ultimate outcome, not a guarantee of immediate discernible purpose.

### "Forgive, I Pray Thee" — The Brothers Were Using Jacob's Name to Manipulate Joseph

**The Misuse:** Genesis 50:16-17 — the brothers sent the message in Jacob's name, and some argue this was a manipulation tactic: Jacob had never actually given this instruction; the brothers fabricated the dying instruction.

**What Scripture says:**

- The text does not say Jacob gave no such instruction; it says the brothers sent the message; the absence of a recording of Jacob giving the instruction did not mean he did not give it — the Bible did not record every conversation; Jacob had specifically charged Joseph in Genesis 47:29 and Genesis 48:21 with the welfare of the family; it was entirely consistent with Jacob's character and concern that he would have spoken to his sons about their relationship with Joseph
- Joseph wept when the message was brought to him (v.17) — not with anger at manipulation but with the grief of compassion; if he had perceived it as manipulation, the response would have been different; Joseph's weeping was the indication of his recognition of the genuine fear behind the message
- The brothers' prostration (v.18 — "fell down before his face; we be thy servants") completed the final bow of the Joseph narrative; the dreams were finally and fully fulfilled in their most complete form; whether the message was precisely Jacob's words or the brothers' genuine expression of what they feared Jacob would have wanted, the outcome was the final completion of the narrative arc
- *Correction:* The text does not provide sufficient evidence to confidently conclude the brothers fabricated the message. The most consistent reading was that the brothers, knowing their father's concern for their welfare and relationship with Joseph, were communicating what Jacob had likely expressed to them. Joseph's response of tears, not confrontation, was the evidence that he received the message as genuine.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Coffin in Egypt — The Perfect Ending of a Book of Promise

**Genesis 50:26 / Hebrews 11:39-40 / Revelation 21:1-5**

Genesis opened: "In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth." Genesis closed: "he was put in a coffin in Egypt." Between creation and the coffin stood every covenant promise, every patriarch's faith, every moment of grace and judgment and testing. And none of the promises had been fully received. Abraham had not seen the nation. Isaac had not seen the Exodus. Jacob had not returned permanently to Canaan. Joseph's bones were in Egypt.

The coffin was the Bible's first declaration that this story was not over. Every coffin in Scripture from Joseph's forward pointed to the same thing: the promises were not exhausted by death. Hebrews 11 preserved the whole row of patriarchal faith and said of them: they received not the promise. Revelation 21:4 — "God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death" — was the answer to every patriarchal coffin.

### The Dreams Fulfilled — Completed at the End

**Genesis 50:18 / Genesis 37:7, 9**

- Genesis 37:7 — the eleven sheaves bowing to Joseph's sheaf — the first dream
- Genesis 37:9 — the sun, moon, and eleven stars bowing — the second dream
- Genesis 42:6 — ten brothers bowed (first partial fulfillment)
- Genesis 43:26 — eleven brothers bowed (second fulfillment, including Benjamin)
- Genesis 44:14 — Judah and brethren fell before Joseph
- Genesis 50:18 — "And his brethren also went and fell down before his face; and they said, Behold, we be thy servants" — the final, complete, voluntary, post-Jacob prostration; the dreams were not fulfilled in a single moment but in a series of increasingly complete bowings; the final bow was in the presence of forgiveness and comfort, not in the presence of fear alone

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time the Word "Coffin" (Aron) Appeared in Scripture

**Genesis 50:26** — "he was put in a coffin (aron) in Egypt"
- The word aron was the same word used for the Ark of the Covenant (Exodus 25:10 — aron ha-berith); the coffin of Joseph and the ark of the covenant were the same Hebrew word; the coffin of the one who had carried the promise in his bones sat in Egypt while the ark that would carry the promise of God's presence was yet to be built
- Establishes: the aron/coffin was the vessel that carried the covenant hope through Egypt — the bones of Joseph in a coffin (aron) in Egypt and the law of God in an ark (aron) in the wilderness were both the material carriers of the covenant promise; the one who carried the promise in death was the precursor to the one who carried the presence in the wilderness

### First Time "God Will Surely Visit" (Pakod Yifkod) Was Used as a Prophetic Declaration About the Exodus

**Genesis 50:24** — "God will surely visit you, and bring you up out of this land"
- Joseph's dying declaration was the first prophetic announcement of the Exodus by name/concept; before Moses, before the burning bush, before the plagues — Joseph declared the Exodus in faith from his deathbed
- Establishes: the Exodus was anticipated by the covenant family from within Egypt; the promise of "bring you up" was the Genesis 46:4 promise ("I will also surely bring thee up again") reaffirmed by Joseph before his death; the faith of the patriarchs and the faith of the Exodus generation was the same faith, connected by the same promise, separated only by the appointed time
