## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses is born to a Levite couple. His mother hides him three months, then places him in an ark of bulrushes on the Nile. Pharaoh's own daughter finds and adopts him — his own mother is hired to nurse him. Moses grows up in Pharaoh's house as an Egyptian prince, yet knows his Hebrew identity. He kills an Egyptian oppressing a Hebrew, flees when the act is known, and goes to Midian. At a well he defends Reuel's daughters and is invited to stay. He marries Zipporah and has a son, Gershom — "a stranger in a strange land." Meanwhile Israel's cry under bondage reaches God, who hears, remembers, and looks upon them. The chapter closes with God fully aware — the redemption is about to begin.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Ark of Bulrushes — Preservation Through Water

**Exodus 2:3** — "she took for him an ark of bulrushes, and daubed it with slime and with pitch"

- **Hebrew word: tebah** — the same word used for Noah's ark (Genesis 6:14). There are only two "arks" (tebah) in the entire Old Testament: Noah's and Moses'. Every other ark (ark of the covenant) uses a different Hebrew word (aron).
- **Genesis 6:14** — "Make thee an ark (tebah) of gopher wood" — daubed with pitch inside and out
- *Interpretation:* The identical word is not coincidence. The tebah always represents preservation through judgment. Noah's tebah carried eight souls through the waters of God's wrath. Moses' tebah carried the deliverer through the waters of Pharaoh's death decree. Both emerge on the other side to a new beginning. Both point to Christ — who passed through death and emerged in resurrection, carrying His people through judgment.
- **1 Peter 3:20-21** — Peter explicitly connects Noah's ark with baptism/salvation: the tebah pattern is the salvation pattern

### Moses' Name — "Drawn Out of Water"

**Exodus 2:10** — "she called his name Moses: and she said, Because I drew him out of the water"

- Hebrew: Mosheh = drawn out / the one who draws out
- **Acts 7:22** — "Moses was learned in all the wisdom of the Egyptians, and was mighty in words and in deeds"
- **Hebrews 11:24-26** — "By faith Moses, when he was come to years, refused to be called the son of Pharaoh's daughter; Esteeming the reproach of Christ greater riches than the treasures in Egypt"
- *Interpretation:* Moses' name encodes his biography and his calling. He was drawn out of water — and he will draw out a nation through water (the Red Sea, Exodus 14). His name is also his office. Christ is the ultimate fulfillment: the One drawn through death's waters draws His people through with Him (1 Corinthians 10:1-2; Romans 6:3-4).

### Moses Hidden Three Months — Faith of His Parents

**Exodus 2:2** — "when she saw him that he was a goodly child, she hid him three months"

- **Hebrews 11:23** — "By faith Moses, when he was born, was hid three months of his parents, because they saw he was a proper child; and they were not afraid of the king's commandment"
- *Interpretation:* The hiding is explicitly called faith in Hebrews 11. Not fear of Pharaoh, but fear of God, governed the parents' choice. The three months of hiding also carries a pattern: the deliverer is hidden, then emerges. Three days/months of hiddenness leading to revelation is a consistent Scripture pattern — Jonah (3 days), Christ (3 days in the tomb). The number 3 as divine witness (2 Corinthians 13:1) is embedded in Moses' preservation.

### Moses' Identity — Refusing Egypt, Choosing Affliction

**Exodus 2:11** — "when Moses was grown, that he went out unto his brethren, and looked on their burdens"

- **Hebrews 11:24-26** — "refused to be called the son of Pharaoh's daughter; Choosing rather to suffer affliction with the people of God, than to enjoy the pleasures of sin for a season; Esteeming the reproach of Christ greater riches than the treasures in Egypt"
- **Acts 7:23-25** — Stephen's account: "it came into his heart to visit his brethren the children of Israel... he supposed his brethren would have understood how that God by his hand would deliver them: but they understood not"
- *Interpretation:* Moses' first attempt at deliverance was premature, self-powered, and rejected. Acts 7:25 reveals Moses thought Israel would understand — they did not. This is the pattern of the rejected deliverer: Moses at 40 (rejected), Moses at 80 (accepted through God's appointment). The rejection of the deliverer by the people he comes to save is the shadow of Christ's first coming (John 1:11).

### "Who Made Thee a Prince and a Judge?" — The Rejected Deliverer

**Exodus 2:14** — "Who made thee a prince and a judge over us?"

- **Acts 7:27, 35** — Stephen quotes this directly and then says: "This Moses whom they refused, saying, Who made thee a ruler and a judge? the same did God send to be a ruler and a deliverer by the hand of the angel which appeared to him in the bush" — Stephen interprets the rejection as the shadow of Christ's rejection
- **John 1:11** — "He came unto his own, and his own received him not"
- **Luke 4:24** — "No prophet is accepted in his own country"
- *Interpretation:* The question "who made thee a prince and a judge?" is not just hostility — it is a biblical prophetic type. Acts 7 explicitly draws the connection to Christ. The deliverer is rejected by those he comes to save; God then commissions him through a supernatural encounter (the burning bush); and he returns with full authority. This is the pattern of both Moses and Christ.

### The Well of Midian — The Deliverer at the Well

**Exodus 2:15-17** — Moses sits by a well, defends women from shepherds

- **Genesis 24:11-14** — Abraham's servant finds Rebekah at a well
- **Genesis 29:10** — Jacob meets Rachel at a well and moves the stone
- **John 4:6-14** — Jesus meets the Samaritan woman at a well and offers living water
- *Interpretation:* The well in Scripture is consistently the site of covenant encounter, betrothal, and revelation. Moses at the well of Midian follows the established pattern: the man of God at the well meets his future wife and begins a new chapter of God's purpose. The pattern culminates in John 4, where Christ at the well offers the water of life itself.

### Gershom — "A Stranger in a Strange Land"

**Exodus 2:22** — "for he said, I have been a stranger in a strange land"

- Hebrew: Gershom = a sojourner there / expelled/stranger
- **Hebrews 11:13** — "These all died in faith... and confessed that they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth"
- **1 Peter 2:11** — "I beseech you as strangers and pilgrims, abstain from fleshly lusts"
- **Philippians 3:20** — "Our conversation (citizenship) is in heaven"
- *Interpretation:* Moses names his son with the confession of his own spiritual condition — stranger in a strange land. The name encodes the theology of the pilgrim: the people of God are always foreigners in the kingdoms of this world. Hebrews 11 takes this confession and applies it to the entire hall of faith. Moses' naming of Gershom is a theological statement, not merely biographical.

### "God Heard... God Remembered... God Looked... God Had Respect" — The Four Covenant Actions

**Exodus 2:24-25** — "God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob. And God looked upon the children of Israel, and God had respect unto them."

- **Genesis 15:13-14** — God's covenant with Abraham: "thy seed shall be a stranger in a land that is not theirs... afterward shall they come out" — God is remembering this specific promise
- **Psalm 105:8** — "He hath remembered his covenant for ever, the word which he commanded to a thousand generations"
- **Luke 1:72-73** — Zechariah's song at the birth of John the Baptist: "To perform the mercy promised to our fathers, and to remember his holy covenant; The oath which he sware to our father Abraham" — the Exodus pattern of covenant remembrance repeats at the dawn of the New Testament
- **Revelation 16:19** — "And great Babylon came in remembrance before God" — God's remembering is always purposeful; He remembers both the cries of the afflicted and the debts of the oppressor
- *Interpretation:* The four verbs — heard, remembered, looked, had respect — are not emotional reactions. They are covenant actions. God had been silent for 400 years (Genesis 15:13 — the sojourn period), but His silence was not absence. When the time was full, God moved. The pattern teaches: God's covenant silences are not abandonments; they are preparations. The cry of the afflicted reaches God and He acts in covenant faithfulness, not merely in sympathy.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### ARK (Hebrew: tebah)
- **Exodus 2:3** — basket/ark of bulrushes
- Only two uses of tebah in all of Scripture: Noah's ark (Genesis 6-9) and Moses' basket here
- **Genesis 6:14** — Noah's tebah: preservation through the waters of judgment
- *Definition here:* A vessel of divine preservation — God-appointed means of carrying His servant through what should have meant death

### GOODLY (Hebrew: tov)
- **Exodus 2:2** — "she saw him that he was a goodly child" — tov = good, beautiful, pleasing
- **Acts 7:20** — "Moses was born, and was exceeding fair (asteios — beautiful before God)"
- **Hebrews 11:23** — "proper child" — the same word
- *Definition here:* Not merely physically attractive — the phrase carries the sense of one marked by God; Hebrews and Acts suggest the parents perceived a divine mark on the child

### REMEMBERED (Hebrew: zakar)
- **Exodus 2:24** — "God remembered his covenant"
- zakar = to call to mind with the intent to act; never passive recollection in the biblical sense
- **Genesis 8:1** — "God remembered Noah, and every living thing" — zakar always precedes active intervention
- *Definition here:* Divine remembering = active, purposeful covenant engagement; when God "remembers," action follows

### STRANGER (Hebrew: ger)
- **Exodus 2:22** — "I have been a stranger (ger) in a strange land"
- ger = a resident alien, one living among a people not his own with limited rights
- **Genesis 15:13** — "thy seed shall be a stranger (ger) in a land that is not theirs"
- **Leviticus 19:34** — "the stranger that dwelleth with you shall be unto you as one born among you, and thou shalt love him as thyself; for ye were strangers in the land of Egypt"
- *Definition here:* A sojourner without full standing in the land; Israel's Exodus experience becomes the basis for the Law's protection of strangers

## THE ACTUAL VERSES — EXODUS 2 (KJV)

**1** And there went a man of the house of Levi, and took to wife a daughter of Levi.
**2** And the woman conceived, and bare a son: and when she saw him that he was a goodly child, she hid him three months.
**3** And when she could not longer hide him, she took for him an ark of bulrushes, and daubed it with slime and with pitch, and put the child therein; and she laid it in the flags by the river's brink.
**4** And his sister stood afar off, to wit what would be done to him.
**5** And the daughter of Pharaoh came down to wash herself at the river; and her maidens walked along by the river's side; and when she saw the ark among the flags, she sent her maid to fetch it.
**6** And when she had opened it, she saw the child: and, behold, the babe wept. And she had compassion on him, and said, This is one of the Hebrews' children.
**7** Then said his sister to Pharaoh's daughter, Shall I go and call to thee a nurse of the Hebrew women, that she may nurse the child for thee?
**8** And Pharaoh's daughter said to her, Go. And the maid went and called the child's mother.
**9** And Pharaoh's daughter said unto her, Take this child away, and nurse it for me, and I will give thee thy wages. And the woman took the child, and nursed it.
**10** And the child grew, and she brought him unto Pharaoh's daughter, and he became her son. And she called his name Moses: and she said, Because I drew him out of the water.
**11** And it came to pass in those days, when Moses was grown, that he went out unto his brethren, and looked on their burdens: and he spied an Egyptian smiting an Hebrew, one of his brethren.
**12** And he looked this way and that way, and when he saw that there was no man, he slew the Egyptian, and hid him in the sand.
**13** And when he went out the second day, behold, two men of the Hebrews strove together: and he said to him that did the wrong, Wherefore smitest thou thy fellow?
**14** And he said, Who made thee a prince and a judge over us? intendest thou to kill me, as thou killedst the Egyptian? And Moses feared, and said, Surely this thing is known.
**15** Now when Pharaoh heard this thing, he sought to slay Moses. But Moses fled from the face of Pharaoh, and dwelt in the land of Midian: and he sat down by a well.
**16** Now the priest of Midian had seven daughters: and they came and drew water, and filled the troughs to water their father's flock.
**17** And the shepherds came and drove them away: but Moses stood up and helped them, and watered their flock.
**18** And when they came to Reuel their father, he said, How is it that ye are come so soon to day?
**19** And they said, An Egyptian delivered us out of the hand of the shepherds, and also drew water enough for us, and watered the flock.
**20** And he said unto his daughters, And where is he? why is it that ye have left the man? call him, that he may eat bread.
**21** And Moses was content to dwell with the man: and he gave Moses Zipporah his daughter.
**22** And she bare him a son, and he called his name Gershom: for he said, I have been a stranger in a strange land.
**23** And it came to pass in process of time, that the king of Egypt died: and the children of Israel sighed by reason of the bondage, and they cried, and their cry came up unto God by reason of the bondage.
**24** And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant with Abraham, with Isaac, and with Jacob.
**25** And God looked upon the children of Israel, and God had respect unto them.

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Moses as Type of Christ — The Rejected and Then Accepted Deliverer

| Moses | Christ |
|-------|--------|
| Hidden at birth from murderous king | Hidden in Egypt from Herod |
| Drawn out of water | Baptized; passed through death |
| Grew up in an alien household | Came to His own, who received Him not |
| Rejected by the people he came to save (Exodus 2:14) | Rejected by Israel at His first coming |
| Fled; prepared in wilderness 40 years | Tempted in the wilderness 40 days |
| Returns with God's full commission | Returns in glory with full authority |
| Acts 7:35 makes this typology explicit | — |

### The Well of Midian — Betrothal Type

- Abraham's servant at the well → Rebekah → covenant marriage (Genesis 24)
- Jacob at the well → Rachel → covenant people multiplied (Genesis 29)
- Moses at the well → Zipporah → the deliverer's household formed
- Jesus at the well → Samaritan woman → living water offered to the nations (John 4)
- *Thread:* The well is the site of covenant encounter. The deliverer at the well meeting his bride is a recurring type of Christ and the church — the Bridegroom offering life to the outcast.

### "God Remembered His Covenant" — The Pattern of Covenant Awakening

- **Genesis 8:1** — God remembered Noah → the flood receded
- **Genesis 19:29** — God remembered Abraham → Lot delivered from Sodom
- **Genesis 30:22** — God remembered Rachel → she conceived
- **Exodus 2:24** — God remembered the Abrahamic covenant → Moses is about to be commissioned
- **Luke 1:72** — God remembered His holy covenant → the forerunner is born
- *Thread:* Divine remembering is always the hinge point before a major redemptive act. The pattern of God's silence followed by covenant remembering followed by action is the rhythm of all redemptive history.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Ark (Tebah) — Preservation Through Judgment Waters

- Genesis 6-9: Noah's tebah — eight souls preserved through global judgment
- Exodus 2:3: Moses' tebah — one deliverer preserved through Pharaoh's decree
- Both emerge to begin a new phase of God's redemptive plan
- Both are sealed with pitch (bitumen) — a sealing that keeps the waters of judgment out
- *Thread:* The tebah is the vessel of salvation. What it carries is what God intends to use for the next chapter of redemption.

### Water — Death Decree Becomes Instrument of Salvation

- Exodus 1:22: Nile = death for Hebrew sons (Pharaoh's decree)
- Exodus 2:3: Nile = life for Moses (God's providence)
- Exodus 14: Sea = death for Egypt, life for Israel
- John 3:5: "Born of water and of the Spirit"
- Revelation 22:1: "A pure river of water of life"
- *Thread:* Throughout Exodus, the same water that the enemy intends for death becomes God's instrument of deliverance. This is the baptism pattern: through the death waters, into new life.
