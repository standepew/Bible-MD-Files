## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses raises three objections to his commissioning: the people won't believe, he is not eloquent, and he pleads for God to send someone else. God gives him three authenticating signs: his rod becomes a serpent and returns to a rod; his hand becomes leprous and is healed; water from the Nile will turn to blood. God answers the eloquence objection by pointing to Himself as the maker of man's mouth — then appoints Aaron as spokesman while Moses provides the words. God reveals that Israel is His firstborn son and warns Pharaoh through Moses: let my firstborn go or I will kill your firstborn. En route to Egypt, God confronts Moses at an inn because his son is uncircumcised. Zipporah performs the circumcision. Aaron is directed to meet Moses; they gather the elders; the people see the signs, believe, and worship.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Rod Becomes a Serpent — Signs to Authenticate the Messenger

**Exodus 4:3-4** — The rod cast down becomes a serpent; Moses takes it by the tail and it becomes a rod

- **Exodus 7:10-12** — At Pharaoh's court, Aaron's rod again becomes a serpent and swallows the magicians' rods — the sign is expanded and proven superior
- **Numbers 21:8-9** — The bronze serpent on a pole: God uses the serpent as an instrument of salvation for those who look at it
- **John 3:14-15** — Jesus: "As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up: That whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have eternal life" — Jesus connects the rod-serpent pattern to His own crucifixion
- **2 Corinthians 5:21** — "He hath made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin" — Christ became what we were (the serpent = sin/curse) to save us
- *Interpretation:* The rod-to-serpent sign is not merely a magic trick — it is a prophetic sign. Moses grasps the serpent by the tail (the dangerous end) and it becomes a rod of authority. Christ "became sin" (the serpent) and by grasping it destroyed its power (Hebrews 2:14). The bronze serpent of Numbers 21 is the link: what was the instrument of death becomes the instrument of healing when lifted up.

---

### The Leprous Hand — Judgment and Healing by the Same Hand

**Exodus 4:6-7** — Hand goes into the bosom: leprous as snow. Hand goes in again: restored as his other flesh.

- **Numbers 12:10** — Miriam struck with leprosy for speaking against Moses — the same judgment sign
- **2 Kings 5:1-14** — Naaman the leper healed by dipping in the Jordan seven times — healing from leprosy is consistently a divine act
- **Luke 17:14** — Jesus heals ten lepers: "Go shew yourselves unto the priests" — only God can cleanse leprosy; Jesus exercising this power was a messianic sign
- **Isaiah 1:18** — "Though your sins be as scarlet, they shall be as white as snow; though they be red like crimson, they shall be as wool" — leprosy (white as snow = judgment) and cleansing/restoration is the pattern of sin and redemption
- *Interpretation:* Leprosy in Scripture is consistently a picture of sin: it is visible, spreading, corrupting, and renders the person unclean and cut off from the community (Leviticus 13-14). The hand going in leprous and coming out clean is a picture of God's power to condemn (make aware of sin) and cleanse (restore). The same hand that brings judgment can bring healing.

### "Who Hath Made Man's Mouth?" — God as the Maker of Every Capacity

**Exodus 4:11** — "Who hath made man's mouth? or who maketh the dumb, or deaf, or the seeing, or the blind? have not I the LORD?"

- **Psalm 94:9** — "He that planted the ear, shall he not hear? he that formed the eye, shall he not see?"
- **Psalm 139:14** — "I am fearfully and wonderfully made: marvellous are thy works"
- **1 Corinthians 1:27** — "God hath chosen the weak things of the world to confound the things which are mighty" — God deliberately uses the inadequate; Moses' slow speech is the instrument God chose
- **2 Corinthians 12:9** — "My strength is made perfect in weakness" — the apostolic principle echoing Exodus 4
- *Interpretation:* God's answer to Moses' "I am slow of speech" is not to argue about Moses' capabilities — it is to assert sovereignty over all human capacities. God made the mouth; He controls what comes out of it. This answer silences the objection theologically: if God made the mouth, He can certainly use the mouth He made, and no human limitation constitutes an excuse for disobedience to divine commission.

### "Israel Is My Son, Even My Firstborn" — The Foundational Declaration

**Exodus 4:22-23** — "Thus saith the LORD, Israel is my son, even my firstborn: And I say unto thee, Let my son go, that he may serve me: and if thou refuse to let him go, behold, I will slay thy son, even thy firstborn."

- **Deuteronomy 14:1** — "Ye are the children of the LORD your God"
- **Hosea 11:1** — "When Israel was a child, then I loved him, and called my son out of Egypt" — Hosea confirms the Exodus 4:22 declaration
- **Matthew 2:15** — Matthew quotes Hosea 11:1 ("Out of Egypt have I called my son") as fulfilled in Christ returning from Egypt after Herod's death — Jesus IS the true Israel; what was said of the nation is fulfilled in the One who embodies the nation
- **Romans 8:29** — Christ is "the firstborn among many brethren" — the believers share in the firstborn status of Christ
- **Colossians 1:15** — Christ is "the firstborn of every creature"
- *Interpretation:* God declaring Israel "my firstborn" is the basis for the coming tenth plague — life for life, firstborn for firstborn. But the statement reaches beyond the Exodus. Hosea 11:1 uses it prophetically; Matthew 2:15 applies it to Christ. Israel is the type; Christ is the antitype. The firstborn-son theology of Exodus 4:22 runs through the entire Scripture to Romans 8 and Colossians 1.

---

### The Circumcision at the Inn — Covenant Sign Cannot Be Bypassed

**Exodus 4:24-26** — The LORD seeks to kill Moses. Zipporah circumcises their son. "So he let him go."

- **Genesis 17:14** — "The uncircumcised man child whose flesh of his foreskin is not circumcised, that soul shall be cut off from his people; he hath broken my covenant" — the covenant requirement; no exception
- **Galatians 5:6** — "In Jesus Christ neither circumcision availeth any thing, nor uncircumcision; but faith which worketh by love" — Paul indicates circumcision is no longer the covenant sign under the New Covenant
- **Colossians 2:11** — "In whom also ye are circumcised with the circumcision made without hands, in putting off the body of the sins of the flesh by the circumcision of Christ" — the New Covenant reality
- *Interpretation:* God was about to use Moses to deliver Israel through the covenant of circumcision — but Moses had not kept the covenant in his own household. God's standards apply to the messenger as well as the message. The principle: God does not bypass His own covenant requirements even for the man He has appointed as deliverer. The circumcision of Moses' son is not incidental — it is the covenant qualification of the household of the deliverer.

---

### "The People Believed... and Bowed Their Heads and Worshipped" — The Response to Authenticated Signs

**Exodus 4:31** — "And the people believed: and when they heard that the LORD had visited the children of Israel, and that he had looked upon their affliction, then they bowed their heads and worshipped."

- **John 20:29-31** — Thomas believes upon seeing; Jesus says "blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed" — faith with signs is the starting point; faith without signs is the maturity
- **Acts 2:43** — "And fear came upon every soul: and many wonders and signs were done by the apostles" — the authenticated messenger pattern repeats in Acts
- **Hebrews 2:4** — "God also bearing them witness, both with signs and wonders, and with divers miracles" — signs authenticate the word
- *Interpretation:* Israel's response — believe, bow, worship — is the correct response to God's authenticated message. The signs serve the word; the word produces faith; faith produces worship. This sequence is the pattern of every genuine revival in Scripture.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### FIRSTBORN (Hebrew: bekor)
- **Exodus 4:22** — "Israel is my son, even my firstborn"
- bekor = the first to open the womb; carries rights of inheritance, honor, and double portion
- **Genesis 25:29-34** — Esau despised his birthright; Jacob valued it
- **Hebrews 12:23** — "The church of the firstborn, which are written in heaven" — believers share in the firstborn status through Christ
- *Definition here:* Israel's firstborn status means: pre-eminent among the nations in God's covenant purpose; possessing the inheritance rights of the covenant. The threat in verse 23 — "I will slay thy son, even thy firstborn" — is the life-for-life covenant principle.

### SIGNS (Hebrew: ot; Greek: semeion)
- **Exodus 4:8-9** — three signs given to Moses
- ot = a distinguishing mark, signal, token, proof
- Used for the rainbow (Genesis 9:12-13), circumcision (Genesis 17:11), Sabbath (Exodus 31:13), and the signs of the prophets
- **John 20:30-31** — "Many other signs truly did Jesus... that ye might believe" — signs always serve faith; they are not ends in themselves
- *Definition here:* A divinely authenticated marker that proves the messenger is from God; signs are given to produce belief in the word, not to replace the word

### HARDEN (Hebrew: chazaq / kashah)
- **Exodus 4:21** — "I will harden his heart" — chazaq = to strengthen, make firm
- Two Hebrew words alternate through the plague narrative: chazaq (God hardens) and kashah (self-hardening/stubborn)
- *Definition here:* Pharaoh's hardening is both his own choice (self-hardening, chapters 7-9) and God's judicial confirmation of that choice (God hardens, chapters 9-14) — **Romans 9:17-18** explains: "For this same purpose have I raised thee up, that I might shew my power in thee" — God's hardening is judicial, not arbitrary; He hardens the one who has already hardened himself

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### Exodus 4:11 — "God Made the Dumb and the Blind" Used to Claim God Authors All Disability

**Verse 11** — "And the LORD said unto him, Who hath made man's mouth? or who maketh the dumb, or deaf, or the seeing, or the blind? have not I the LORD?"

**How it is misused:** This verse is sometimes extracted to argue that God directly and specifically designs each individual disability for that individual's life, or conversely that to seek medical healing is to resist what God has "made." It is also flattened to suggest God is the direct efficient cause of all suffering.

**What the text actually says:** The verse is God's answer to Moses' claim that he cannot speak well — the context is specifically about divine sovereignty over human capacity and commissioning, not a general theology of suffering:
- **Verse 10** — "And Moses said unto the LORD, O my Lord, I am not eloquent, neither heretofore, nor since thou hast spoken unto thy servant: but I am slow of speech, and of a slow tongue" — Moses is making an excuse for why he cannot fulfill a divine commission
- **Verse 12** — "Now therefore go, and I will be with thy mouth, and teach thee what thou shalt say" — the application of verse 11 is not "your disability is God's design for your suffering" but "the God who made your mouth will be with it; your limitation is not an obstacle to my commission"
- **Psalm 94:9** — "He that planted the ear, shall he not hear? he that formed the eye, shall he not see?" — the same rhetorical form; the point is God's sovereignty and competence, not a doctrine of individual disability causation

*Correction:* Exodus 4:11 is God's assertion of His sovereignty over all human faculties as the answer to an excuse for disobedience. The verse does not teach a systematic doctrine of divine causation of every disability. Its context is missional: the God who created the mouth can work through any mouth He chooses, including an imperfect one. Moses' limitation becomes irrelevant once God declares He will be with the mouth.

---

### Exodus 4:21 — "God Hardened Pharaoh's Heart — Therefore Pharaoh Had No Choice" (The Fatalism Misread)

**Verse 21** — "And the LORD said unto Moses, When thou goest to return into Egypt, see that thou do all those wonders before Pharaoh, which I have put in thine hand: but I will harden his heart, that he shall not let the people go."

**How it is misused:** This verse is used to argue that Pharaoh was a puppet with no genuine choice, that his damnation was predetermined without any moral agency on his part, and therefore that the entire plague narrative is a demonstration of arbitrary divine sovereignty rather than responsive divine justice.

**What the text actually says:** The full plague narrative shows Pharaoh repeatedly hardening his own heart before God hardens it — the sequence is deliberate:
- **Exodus 8:15** — "But when Pharaoh saw that there was respite, he hardened his heart, and hearkened not unto them; as the LORD had said" — Pharaoh's own act; no divine intervention described
- **Exodus 8:32** — "And Pharaoh hardened his heart at this time also, neither would he let the people go" — again, Pharaoh's own act
- **Exodus 9:34-35** — "He sinned yet more, and hardened his heart, he and his servants" — his choice, his servants' choice
- **Exodus 9:12** — Only at plague 6 (boils) does God harden Pharaoh's heart — after Pharaoh had already hardened his own heart repeatedly
- **Romans 9:17-18** — "For this same purpose have I raised thee up, that I might shew my power in thee" — Paul uses this as an example of divine sovereignty working through established human choices, not creating them from nothing

*Correction:* God's hardening of Pharaoh is judicial confirmation of a direction Pharaoh had already and repeatedly chosen. The sequence in Exodus 7-14 is: Pharaoh hardens himself (chapters 7-9), then God hardens him (chapters 9-14). Romans 1:24-28 follows the same pattern: "God gave them up" — divine judicial confirmation of what the people had already chosen. Pharaoh is not a marionette; he is a man whose persistent choices are confirmed and used by God to fulfill His declared purpose.
