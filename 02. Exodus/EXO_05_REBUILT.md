## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses and Aaron confront Pharaoh: "Thus saith the LORD God of Israel, Let my people go." Pharaoh's response: "Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice? I know not the LORD." He accuses Israel of idleness and immediately increases their burden — they must make the same quota of bricks but gather their own straw. Israel's officers are beaten. They confront Moses and Aaron: "You have made us abhorred in the eyes of Pharaoh." Moses cries to God: "Lord, why hast thou so evil entreated this people? why is it that thou hast sent me? For since I came to Pharaoh to speak in thy name, he hath done evil to this people; neither hast thou delivered thy people at all." The chapter closes without resolution — in the darkest point before the dawn. This is the pattern: obedience to God's call often produces an immediate worsening of the situation before deliverance comes.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Who Is the LORD, That I Should Obey?" — The Defiance of the Proud

**Exodus 5:2** — "Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice to let Israel go? I know not the LORD"

- **Psalm 2:1-4** — "Why do the heathen rage... The kings of the earth set themselves, and the rulers take counsel together, against the LORD... He that sitteth in the heavens shall laugh"
- **Proverbs 21:30** — "There is no wisdom nor understanding nor counsel against the LORD"
- **Daniel 4:35** — Nebuchadnezzar (after judgment): "He doeth according to his will in the army of heaven, and among the inhabitants of the earth: and none can stay his hand, or say unto him, What doest thou?"
- **Romans 9:17** — "For this same purpose have I raised thee up, that I might shew my power in thee, and that my name might be declared throughout all the earth" — God's answer to "Who is the LORD?" is the entire Exodus narrative; the plagues answer Pharaoh's question definitively
- *Interpretation:* "Who is the LORD?" is not merely ignorance — it is defiance. Pharaoh knows there is a God of Israel; he is asserting he does not recognize that God's authority over him. This is the spirit of every ungodly ruler in Scripture. God's answer is always demonstration rather than argument — He does not debate His own existence or authority; He acts, and the answer is written in the plagues and the parted sea.

### Increased Burdens After Obedience — The Pattern of Worse-Before-Better

**Exodus 5:6-9** — No more straw; same quota; the oppression intensifies immediately after Moses' obedience

- **Genesis 50:20** — "Ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good" — the principle that apparent reversals are part of God's purpose
- **John 11:4** — "This sickness is not unto death, but for the glory of God" — Jesus allows Lazarus to die before raising him; the delay is purposeful
- **John 16:20** — "Ye shall weep and lament, but the world shall rejoice: and ye shall be sorrowful, but your sorrow shall be turned into joy" — the pattern of sorrow before joy is built into the gospel itself
- **Romans 5:3-4** — "Tribulation worketh patience; and patience, experience; and experience, hope" — the pressure is producing something
- **James 1:2-4** — "Count it all joy when ye fall into divers temptations; Knowing this, that the trying of your faith worketh patience"
- *Interpretation:* The intensification of Israel's suffering after Moses' first obedience is one of Scripture's most important patterns. Obedience to God's call does not guarantee immediate improvement — it often produces immediate worsening. This is the darkness before the dawn pattern. The purpose: God's power will be demonstrated against a background of human impossibility (Exodus 6:1). If Pharaoh had immediately relented, the Exodus would have been a negotiated political settlement, not a mighty act of God. The intensification is necessary for the demonstration.

### "The LORD Look Upon You, and Judge" — Turning Against the Messenger

**Exodus 5:21** — "The LORD look upon you, and judge; because ye have made our savour to be abhorred in the eyes of Pharaoh"

- **Numbers 14:2-4** — Israel murmurs against Moses in the wilderness: "Would God that we had died in the land of Egypt!" — the pattern of blaming the deliverer when circumstances worsen
- **Acts 7:27, 35, 39** — Stephen's summary: Israel rejected Moses, turned back to Egypt in their hearts
- **John 6:66** — "From that time many of his disciples went back, and walked no more with him" — the pattern of abandoning the deliverer when the way becomes hard
- **Isaiah 53:3** — "He is despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief" — the rejected deliverer pattern culminates in Christ
- *Interpretation:* The officers' accusation against Moses is the first of many instances of Israel rejecting the deliverer when his message produces suffering rather than immediate comfort. This pattern is the shadow of Christ's rejection — the One who came to deliver is accused of making things worse. Acts 7 reads Exodus 5 as the prototype of Israel's perpetual rejection of God's sent messengers.

---

### Moses' Prayer: "Why Hast Thou So Evil Entreated This People?" — Honest Prayer in Scripture

**Exodus 5:22-23** — Moses returns to the LORD and presses his complaint directly

- **Psalm 22:1** — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — David (and prophetically Christ) praying with raw honesty
- **Jeremiah 20:7** — "O LORD, thou hast deceived me, and I was deceived" — Jeremiah's brutally honest prayer
- **Habakkuk 1:2-3** — "O LORD, how long shall I cry, and thou wilt not hear?"
- **Lamentations 3:8** — "Also when I cry and shout, he shutteth out my prayer"
- **Matthew 27:46** — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" — Christ on the cross
- *Interpretation:* Moses' prayer in Exodus 5:22-23 is not faithlessness — it is the honest prayer of a man who has obeyed God and encountered apparent contradiction. Scripture consistently validates this kind of prayer. The Psalms, Jeremiah, Habakkuk, Job, and Christ Himself all model honest, pressing prayer in the face of apparent divine silence or reversal. God does not rebuke Moses for this prayer; He answers it with the fullest revelation of His name and covenant in Exodus 6.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### KNOW (Hebrew: yada)
- **Exodus 5:2** — "I know not the LORD" — Pharaoh's declaration
- yada = to know through experience, relationship, acknowledgment
- This is not intellectual ignorance — Pharaoh is refusing covenant relationship and acknowledgment
- **Genesis 4:1** — "Adam knew Eve his wife" — yada at its most intimate
- **Jeremiah 9:24** — "Let him that glorieth glory in this, that he understandeth and knoweth me" — yada as covenant intimacy with God
- *Definition here:* Pharaoh is not saying he has never heard of the God of Israel — he is asserting he refuses to acknowledge God's sovereign claim over him. It is covenant rejection, not mere ignorance.

### IDLE (Hebrew: rapheh)
- **Exodus 5:8, 17** — "they be idle" — rapheh = slack, limp, without strength
- Pharaoh weaponizes the concept of productivity to deny Israel's right to worship
- **Joshua 1:9** — "Be strong and of a good courage; be not afraid, neither be thou dismayed" — the opposite of rapheh
- *Definition here:* Pharaoh's use of "idle" is a political accusation — characterizing the desire to worship as laziness to justify increased oppression. The principle: ungodly authority always frames obedience to God as irresponsibility or laziness.

### ABHORRED (Hebrew: baash)
- **Exodus 5:21** — "ye have made our savour to be abhorred" — baash = to stink, to be loathsome
- The word is deliberately vivid: Israel's reputation has been made to stink before Pharaoh
- **1 Samuel 13:4** — "And all Israel heard say that Saul had smitten a garrison of the Philistines, and that Israel also was had in abomination with the Philistines" — same dynamic: Israel becoming odious to the ruling power
- *Definition here:* To be made odious, despised, a stench — the officers' accusation that Moses has destroyed Israel's relationship with Pharaoh by the confrontation

## THE ACTUAL VERSES — EXODUS 5 (KJV)

**1** And afterward Moses and Aaron went in, and told Pharaoh, Thus saith the LORD God of Israel, Let my people go, that they may hold a feast unto me in the wilderness.
**2** And Pharaoh said, Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice to let Israel go? I know not the LORD, neither will I let Israel go.
**3** And they said, The God of the Hebrews hath met with us: let us go, we pray thee, three days' journey into the desert, and sacrifice unto the LORD our God; lest he fall upon us with pestilence, or with the sword.
**4** And the king of Egypt said unto them, Wherefore do ye, Moses and Aaron, let the people from their works? get you unto your burdens.
**5** And Pharaoh said, Behold, the people of the land now are many, and ye make them rest from their burdens.
**6** And Pharaoh commanded the same day the taskmasters of the people, and their officers, saying,
**7** Ye shall no more give the people straw to make brick, as heretofore: let them go and gather straw for themselves.
**8** And the tale of the bricks, which they did make heretofore, ye shall lay upon them; ye shall not diminish ought thereof: for they be idle; therefore they cry, saying, Let us go and sacrifice to our God.
**9** Let there more work be laid upon the men, that they may labour therein; and let them not regard vain words.
**10** And the taskmasters of the people went out, and their officers, and they spake to the people, saying, Thus saith Pharaoh, I will not give you straw.
**11** Go ye, get you straw where ye can find it: yet not ought of your work shall be diminished.
**12** So the people were scattered abroad throughout all the land of Egypt to gather stubble instead of straw.
**13** And the taskmasters hasted them, saying, Fulfil your works, your daily tasks, as when there was straw.
**14** And the officers of the children of Israel, which Pharaoh's taskmasters had set over them, were beaten, and demanded, Wherefore have ye not fulfilled your task in making brick both yesterday and to day, as heretofore?
**15** Then the officers of the children of Israel came and cried unto Pharaoh, saying, Wherefore dealest thou thus with thy servants?
**16** There is no straw given unto thy servants, and they say to us, Make brick: and, behold, thy servants are beaten; but the fault is in thine own people.
**17** But he said, Ye are idle, ye are idle: therefore ye say, Let us go and do sacrifice to the LORD.
**18** Go therefore now, and work; for there shall no straw be given you, yet shall ye deliver the tale of bricks.
**19** And the officers of the children of Israel did see that they were in evil case, after it was said, Ye shall not minish ought from your bricks of your daily task.
**20** And they met Moses and Aaron, who stood in the way, as they came forth from Pharaoh:
**21** And they said unto them, The LORD look upon you, and judge; because ye have made our savour to be abhorred in the eyes of Pharaoh, and in the eyes of his servants, to put a sword in their hand to slay us.
**22** And Moses returned unto the LORD, and said, Lord, wherefore hast thou so evil entreated this people? why is it that thou hast sent me?
**23** For since I came to Pharaoh to speak in thy name, he hath done evil to this people; neither hast thou delivered thy people at all.

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Pattern of Worse Before Better — The Cross Pattern

- Exodus 5: Obedience → immediate intensification of suffering → apparent failure → God answers with greater revelation (Exodus 6)
- **John 12:24** — "Except a corn of wheat fall into the ground and die, it abideth alone: but if it die, it bringeth forth much fruit" — the death before the harvest pattern
- **Luke 24:21** — disciples on the road to Emmaus: "we had trusted that it had been he which should have redeemed Israel" — the disciples had given up; the cross looked like defeat before the resurrection proved otherwise
- *Type:* Exodus 5 is the cross-pattern written into Israel's history: the moment of greatest apparent failure is the moment immediately preceding God's greatest act. The disciples on Easter Saturday are in the same position as Israel after Exodus 5 — the deliverance looks like it has failed. Both situations are resolved by resurrection/redemption that makes the darkest moment into the turning point.

### The Rejected Deliverer — Israel Turns on Moses

- Moses comes to deliver → people initially believe (Exodus 4:31) → things get worse → people reject and accuse Moses
- This is the pattern of every prophet: welcomed in hope, rejected when the message costs something
- **Acts 7:27** — "Who made thee a ruler and a judge over us?" — the people's rejection of Moses
- **Acts 7:52** — "Which of the prophets have not your fathers persecuted?" — the pattern extends to all the prophets
- **John 6:66** — "From that time many of his disciples went back" — Exodus 5's pattern in the ministry of Christ

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Bricks Without Straw — Impossible Standards as Oppression Tool

- The demand for bricks without straw is not merely about production — it is a tool of demoralization
- When the ruling power demands the impossible (same output, fewer resources), it crushes the spirit and eliminates any space for religious practice
- **Daniel 6:7** — the decree against prayer: same tactic — use law to make faithfulness impossible
- **Revelation 13:17** — the mark of the beast: no buying or selling without compliance — the same tactic at the end of the age; economic pressure used to force abandonment of God
- *Thread:* Bricks without straw is the prototype of every system that uses impossible requirements to suppress the worship of God. Exodus 5 establishes the pattern that Revelation 13 brings to its ultimate expression.
