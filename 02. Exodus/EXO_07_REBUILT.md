## CHAPTER SUMMARY

God commissions Moses and Aaron with clarified roles: Moses is as a god to Pharaoh; Aaron is his prophet. God declares His full purpose: multiply signs and wonders, Pharaoh will not listen, God will bring Israel out "by great judgments," and Egypt will know He is the LORD. Moses is 80, Aaron 83, when they stand before Pharaoh. Aaron's rod becomes a serpent; Pharaoh's magicians replicate this — but Aaron's serpent swallows theirs. Pharaoh's heart is hardened. The first plague: the Nile and all waters of Egypt are turned to blood. Fish die; the river stinks; Egyptians cannot drink. The magicians replicate this. Pharaoh turns away, heart hardened. The Egyptians dig for water. Seven days pass.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "I Have Made Thee a God to Pharaoh" — The Prophet-Pattern

**Exodus 7:1** — "See, I have made thee a god to Pharaoh: and Aaron thy brother shall be thy prophet"

- **Exodus 4:16** — "He shall be thy spokesman unto the people: and he shall be to thee instead of a mouth, and thou shalt be to him instead of God" — the Moses/Aaron relationship established earlier; now applied to the Pharaoh encounter
- **John 10:34-35** — Jesus quotes Psalm 82:6 ("I said, ye are gods") in response to accusations of blasphemy: "If he called them gods, unto whom the word of God came..." — those who bear God's word and authority to others are described with god-language
- **2 Corinthians 5:20** — "Now then we are ambassadors for Christ, as though God did beseech you by us" — the New Testament equivalent; the messenger bears the authority of the One who sent him
- *Interpretation:* "Made thee a god to Pharaoh" does not mean Moses is divine — it means Moses stands in God's stead before Pharaoh with God's full authority. The prophet/spokesman relationship (Aaron speaks what Moses gives him) mirrors the relationship of word-bearer to the One who sends the word. This is the nature of prophetic office: the prophet speaks with divine authority because the divine word is in his mouth.

### "That the Egyptians Shall Know That I Am the LORD" — The Purpose Stated

**Exodus 7:5** — "And the Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD, when I stretch forth mine hand upon Egypt"

- This phrase or its equivalent ("know that I am the LORD") appears over 60 times in Ezekiel and repeatedly in Exodus — it is God's stated purpose for His acts of judgment and salvation
- **Psalm 9:16** — "The LORD is known by the judgment which he executeth"
- **Isaiah 45:6** — "That they may know from the rising of the sun, and from the west, that there is none beside me"
- **Romans 9:17** — "For this same purpose have I raised thee up, that I might shew my power in thee, and that my name might be declared throughout all the earth"
- **Ezekiel 36:22-23** — "I do not this for your sakes, O house of Israel... but for mine holy name's sake... and I will sanctify my great name... and the heathen shall know that I am the LORD"
- *Interpretation:* The purpose of the plagues is explicitly stated and it is not primarily about Israel's freedom — it is about the declaration of who God is to the entire world. Exodus is a theological manifesto written in history. Paul quotes it directly in Romans 9:17. The pattern: God works through human history — including through the resistance of His enemies — so that His name and nature are declared beyond the community of faith to the nations.

### The Rod/Serpent Before Pharaoh — Aaron's Rod Swallows All Others

**Exodus 7:10-12** — Aaron's rod becomes a serpent; magicians replicate; Aaron's serpent swallows their serpents

- **Exodus 4:3-4** — Moses' private sign with the serpent; now performed publicly before Pharaoh
- **Matthew 12:27-28** — Jesus casts out demons and the Pharisees claim it is done by Beelzebub; Jesus distinguishes true power from counterfeit; the same dynamic as Pharaoh's magicians
- **2 Thessalonians 2:9** — "The coming of the lawless one is after the working of Satan with all power and signs and lying wonders" — the end-time counterfeit of signs
- **Revelation 13:13-14** — The false prophet does "great wonders" to deceive — counterfeit miracle-working is the consistent tool of the adversary
- *Interpretation:* The magicians of Egypt can replicate certain signs — but they cannot reverse them, consume them, or exceed them. Aaron's rod swallowing all others declares: the power of the true God is categorically superior to all imitations. The pattern: wherever God's genuine power operates, the enemy can produce imitations — but the imitations are always consumed by the genuine. The discernment principle is not whether signs occur, but which power ultimately prevails.

### The First Plague — Water Turned to Blood

**Exodus 7:17-21** — The Nile and all waters of Egypt turned to blood; fish die; river stinks; no drinking water

**Judgment on Egypt's gods:**
- The Nile was worshipped in Egypt — the god Hapi was the deity of the Nile's inundation and fertility
- Turning the Nile to blood was a direct strike on the supreme symbol of Egyptian life and religion
- **Numbers 33:4** — "For the Egyptians buried all their firstborn, which the LORD had smitten among them: upon their gods also the LORD executed judgments" — the plagues are explicitly identified as judgments on Egypt's gods
- **Psalm 135:8** — "Who smote the firstborn of Egypt, both of man and beast" — summarizing the plagues as divine judgment

**Revelation Connection:**
- **Revelation 8:8-9** — Second trumpet: a mountain of fire cast into the sea; a third of the sea becomes blood; a third of sea creatures die
- **Revelation 16:3-4** — Second and third bowl judgments: the sea becomes blood, rivers and fountains become blood
- *Interpretation:* The first plague of Exodus 7 is the prototype of the end-time bowl and trumpet judgments of Revelation. The pattern: water to blood is a fundamental judgment form that appears at the beginning of the Exodus and at the end of the age. It is not coincidence — Revelation deliberately mirrors the Exodus plagues. The God who judged Egypt will judge the whole earth with the same categories of judgment.

### "Pharaoh's Heart Was Hardened" — The Hardening Sequence

**Exodus 7:13, 22** — "He hardened Pharaoh's heart"

- In chapters 7-9: Pharaoh hardens his own heart (Exodus 8:15, 32; 9:34) — his own choice
- From chapter 9-14: God hardens Pharaoh's heart (Exodus 9:12; 10:1, 20, 27; 11:10; 14:8) — judicial confirmation
- **Romans 1:24-28** — "God gave them up... God gave them over" — the same pattern; after persistent human rejection, God judicially confirms the choice and removes the restraint
- **Romans 9:17-18** — Paul's explicit treatment: "He hath mercy on whom he will have mercy, and whom he will he hardeneth" — not arbitrary, but judicial
- **2 Thessalonians 2:10-12** — "Because they received not the love of the truth... God shall send them strong delusion" — the New Testament equivalent; God confirms in deception those who chose deception
- *Interpretation:* The hardening of Pharaoh is a two-stage process: Pharaoh repeatedly hardens his own heart (his choice), then God hardens it (judicial confirmation). This is not arbitrary divine predestination to damnation — it is God's confirmation of the direction a man has already chosen. Romans 9 uses this as an example of divine sovereignty operating through human choices, not apart from them.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### PROPHET (Hebrew: nabi)
- **Exodus 7:1** — Aaron as Moses' prophet
- nabi = one who speaks forth; a spokesperson; the mouth of another
- **Deuteronomy 18:18** — "I will raise them up a Prophet from among their brethren, like unto thee, and will put my words in his mouth" — the Messiah as the ultimate nabi
- *Definition here:* The prophet does not originate the message — he delivers it. Aaron speaks Moses' words; Moses speaks God's words. The prophet is the mouthpiece of the one who sends him.

### HARDENED (Hebrew: chazaq)
- **Exodus 7:13** — "He hardened Pharaoh's heart" — chazaq = strengthened, made firm/stiff
- Also hazaq in the sense of strengthening someone in the direction they are already moving
- Distinct from kashah (to be stubborn/obstinate — Pharaoh's own hardening, Exodus 8:15)
- *Definition here:* Divine hardening = God confirming and strengthening the direction a person has chosen for themselves; not creating stubbornness where there was none, but judicially fixing a pattern of willful resistance

### ENCHANTMENTS (Hebrew: lahatim)
- **Exodus 7:11** — "they also did in like manner with their enchantments" — lahatim = flames, secret arts, occult practices
- The magicians of Egypt are using occult/demonic power to produce physical effects
- **Deuteronomy 18:10-12** — God forbids these arts among Israel
- **Acts 8:9-11** — Simon Magus in Samaria: using occult power to command attention
- *Definition here:* Occult/demonic arts that produce real but limited physical effects; they can imitate but cannot exceed or reverse God's genuine acts

- **1 Corinthians 10:20** — "the things which the Gentiles sacrifice, they sacrifice to devils, and not to God" — the gods behind the idols are spiritual powers; the plagues hit both the natural and the spiritual

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### Exodus 7:1 — "I Have Made Thee a God to Pharaoh" Used to Claim Human Divinity

**Verse 1** — "And the LORD said unto Moses, See, I have made thee a god to Pharaoh: and Aaron thy brother shall be thy prophet."

**How it is misused:** Some use this verse to argue that humans can become gods or that there is a category of divine human authority that blurs the line between God and man. It is cited to support teachings of human deification or to give individuals extraordinary authority claims over others.

**What the text actually says:** The context immediately defines what "god to Pharaoh" means — it is a relational, representational role, not an ontological claim:
- **Verse 2** — "Thou shalt speak all that I command thee: and Aaron thy brother shall speak unto Pharaoh" — Moses does not originate the words; he receives and transmits them; this is the role of a commissioned representative, not a deity
- **Exodus 4:16** — "He shall be thy spokesman unto the people: and he shall be, even he shall be to thee instead of a mouth, and thou shalt be to him instead of God" — the identical relationship was already described in chapter 4; it is a functional analogy of authority, not a claim of divine nature
- **John 10:34-35** — Jesus quotes Psalm 82:6 ("I said, Ye are gods") explaining: "If he called them gods, unto whom the word of God came" — those who bear and transmit God's word are described in god-language because they carry His authority; it is representative language, not ontological

*Correction:* "Made thee a god to Pharaoh" means Moses stood before Pharaoh as God's full representative — bearing God's word, authority, and demand. It is an office of commissioned representation, entirely dependent on the One who sent him. Moses has no independent divine nature; his "god-ness" to Pharaoh is borrowed authority. The moment God's commission ends, so does the authority. This is confirmed by Aaron being called Moses' "prophet" — prophets speak for someone else; Moses is speaking for God.

---

### Exodus 7:13 — "God Hardened Pharaoh's Heart" Treated as the Whole Story of Hardening

**Verse 13** — "And he hardened Pharaoh's heart, that he hearkened not unto them; as the LORD had said."

**How it is misused:** This verse — and similar statements in chapters 7 and beyond — is used to argue that Pharaoh's resistance was entirely God's creation, leaving Pharaoh no moral agency and making the plagues an unjust punishment of someone God had predestined to resist. See also the note on Exodus 4:21.

**What the text actually says:** The narrative of hardening runs in two distinct phases, and chapter 7's use of passive language must be read alongside the full sequence:
- **Exodus 7:22** — "Pharaoh's heart was hardened" — passive construction; the immediate agent is not specified
- **Exodus 8:15** — "He hardened his heart" — Pharaoh himself is the explicit agent; his own choice
- **Exodus 8:32** — "Pharaoh hardened his heart at this time also" — again, Pharaoh's own act
- **Exodus 9:34** — "He sinned yet more, and hardened his heart, he and his servants" — Pharaoh and his court choose hardness
- Only from Exodus 9:12 onward does God harden Pharaoh's heart as an explicit divine act — after Pharaoh had already done so repeatedly

*Correction:* The hardening narrative is a two-stage process: self-hardening followed by divine judicial confirmation. Romans 1:24-28 establishes this pattern: "God gave them up" comes after and in response to the human choice to reject the truth. Pharaoh's hardening does not begin with God; it begins with Pharaoh. God's hardening is the confirmation and use of the direction Pharaoh had already established by his own repeated choices.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Blood — Life, Death, and Judgment

- [[01. Genesis/GEN_04_REBUILT|Genesis 4:10]] — "The voice of thy brother's blood crieth unto me from the ground" — blood speaks
- [[03. Leviticus/LEV_17_REBUILT|Leviticus 17:11]] — "The life of the flesh is in the blood: and I have given it to you upon the altar to make an atonement"
- [[02. Exodus/EXO_07_REBUILT|Exodus 7:17]] — The Nile turned to blood = death, not life; judgment, not sustenance
- **Exodus 12** — Blood on the doorposts = life and protection
- [[40. Matthew/MATTHEW_26_REBUILT|Matthew 26:28]] — "This is my blood of the new testament, which is shed for many for the remission of sins"
- [[66. Revelation/REVELATION_16_REBUILT|Revelation 16:6]] — "For they have shed the blood of saints and prophets, and thou hast given them blood to drink"
- *Thread:* Blood in Scripture is simultaneously the most powerful symbol of life (Leviticus 17:11), judgment (Exodus 7), protection (Exodus 12), and redemption (Matthew 26:28). The first plague turning water to blood is the inversion of blessing — what sustained life becomes the sign of judgment. The same blood that saves God's people (Passover) judges His enemies (plague).
