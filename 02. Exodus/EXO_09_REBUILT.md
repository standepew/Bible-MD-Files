## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Three more plagues. Plague 5 (murrain): all Egyptian livestock in the field die; none of Israel's die. Pharaoh sends to check — not one Israelite animal is dead — and hardens his heart. Plague 6 (boils): ashes from the furnace sprinkled toward heaven; boils break on all Egyptians and their animals; the magicians cannot even stand before Moses. God hardens Pharaoh's heart. Plague 7 (hail): God gives advance warning; those who feared His word brought their servants and cattle inside. Devastating hail with fire strikes all of Egypt — the worst since Egypt became a nation. Goshen is spared. Pharaoh summons Moses, confesses "I have sinned this time," asks Moses to pray. Moses agrees but says "I know ye will not yet fear the LORD God." Hail stops; Pharaoh hardens again. Note: barley and flax were smitten; wheat and rie were not yet grown — a detail confirming this is literal history with natural progression.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "For This Same Purpose Have I Raised Thee Up" — Divine Sovereignty and Human Agency

**Exodus 9:16** — "And in very deed for this cause have I raised thee up, for to shew in thee my power; and that my name may be declared throughout all the earth"

- **Romans 9:17** — Paul quotes this verse directly: "For this same purpose have I raised thee up, that I might shew my power in thee, and that my name might be declared throughout all the earth" — Paul uses Exodus 9:16 as the proof text for God's sovereign purpose operating through human resistance
- **Acts 4:27-28** — "For of a truth against thy holy child Jesus, both Herod, and Pontius Pilate, with the Gentiles, and the people of Israel, were gathered together, For to do whatsoever thy hand and thy counsel determined before to be done" — the same principle at the crucifixion; even the enemies' actions fulfill God's predetermined purpose
- **Isaiah 10:5-7** — Assyria is the rod of God's anger; Assyria does not know it is God's instrument; God uses it for His purpose, then judges it for its pride
- *Interpretation:* Exodus 9:16 is the theological keystone of the hardening narrative. God did not arbitrarily create Pharaoh to destroy him — Pharaoh was raised up (given his position, sustained in life during the plagues) so that God's power could be demonstrated and His name declared. Paul's use of this verse in Romans 9 is the Spirit-breathed interpretation. God's sovereignty does not eliminate human responsibility — Pharaoh is guilty for his choices — but it means God's purpose is achieved even through the choices of those who resist Him.

### "Those Who Feared the Word of the LORD" — Faith Within the Enemy Camp

**Exodus 9:20** — "He that feared the word of the LORD among the servants of Pharaoh made his servants and his cattle flee into the houses"

- **Ruth 1:16** — Ruth: a Moabitess who chose Israel's God — faith outside the covenant community
- **Matthew 8:10** — The centurion: "I have not found so great faith, no, not in Israel" — greater faith in a Roman soldier than in Israel
- **Luke 7:9** — Same centurion; Jesus' commendation
- **Acts 10:2** — Cornelius: a devout Gentile who feared God before the gospel arrived
- **Revelation 7:9** — "A great multitude, which no man could number, of all nations, and kindreds, and people, and tongues" — the final harvest of those from outside Israel who believed
- *Interpretation:* Even in Pharaoh's court, there were those who feared God's word and obeyed. Their cattle and servants were saved. This is a pattern throughout Scripture: God always has people within the enemy camp who respond to His word. The Exodus is not a story of all Egyptians condemned and all Israelites saved — it is more complex. Faith in the word of God is the dividing line, not ethnicity. This principle runs from Rahab (Joshua 2) to the Roman centurion (Matthew 8) to Cornelius (Acts 10).

### "The Earth Is the LORD's" — The Theological Basis for the Hail

**Exodus 9:29** — "As soon as I am gone out of the city, I will spread abroad my hands unto the LORD; and the thunder shall cease, neither shall there be any more hail; that thou mayest know how that the earth is the LORD's"

- **Psalm 24:1** — "The earth is the LORD's, and the fulness thereof; the world, and they that dwell therein"
- **Deuteronomy 10:14** — "Behold, the heaven and the heaven of heavens is the LORD's thy God, the earth also, with all that therein is"
- **1 Corinthians 10:26** — Paul quotes Psalm 24:1 in the context of food offered to idols: "the earth is the Lord's, and the fulness thereof"
- **Job 38:4** — "Where wast thou when I laid the foundations of the earth?" — God's ownership established by creation
- *Interpretation:* Moses declares "the earth is the LORD's" as the reason Pharaoh should know that the hail stops by God's command. Egypt believed the earth belonged to their gods — Pharaoh was a god himself. Moses corrects this explicitly: the hail, the land, the Nile — all of it belongs to YHWH. This statement is the theological center of the entire plague narrative. The plagues are not a contest between equals — they are the Owner reclaiming His property and demonstrating His title deed to all of creation.

### Plague 5: Murrain on Cattle — Judgment on Egyptian Worship

**Exodus 9:3-7** — All Egyptian cattle die; none of Israel's die

- The bull Apis and the cow Hathor were among Egypt's most sacred deities — cattle were literally worshipped
- **Numbers 33:4** — "upon their gods also the LORD executed judgments"
- **Psalm 115:4-8** — Idols are the work of men's hands; they cannot act; those who make them become like them
- **Isaiah 46:1-2** — Bel and Nebo bow down; their idols are carried on beasts — "the beasts that bear them are gone into captivity"
- *Interpretation:* The murrain is a direct strike on Egypt's cattle-worship. The sacred animal of their god is killed by the God of Israel, while the cattle of Israel — who do not worship cattle — are untouched. The contrast is the point: the Egyptian gods cannot protect even the animals sacred to them. Israel's God protects the animals of His people who have no religious attachment to cattle. This is one plague where the theological target is unmistakable.

---

### Boils: Ashes from the Furnace — Reversal of Symbolism

**Exodus 9:8-11** — Moses takes ashes from a furnace (kiln) and throws them toward heaven

- The furnace/kiln is the place where Israel was forced to make bricks — the instrument of their oppression
- God uses the ash of their oppression as the material of their enemies' judgment
- **Genesis 50:20** — "Ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good"
- **Romans 8:28** — "And we know that all things work together for good to them that love God"
- *Interpretation:* The ashes of the Egyptian kiln — where Hebrew slaves labored — become the instrument that strikes the Egyptians with boils. What the enemy built to oppress God's people is transformed into the weapon of their judgment. This is a recurring biblical principle: the instrument of oppression becomes the instrument of judgment on the oppressor. The cross — the Roman instrument of execution — became the instrument of sin's destruction.

### Pharaoh's Confession: "I Have Sinned This Time" — False Repentance

**Exodus 9:27** — "And Pharaoh sent, and called for Moses and Aaron, and said unto them, I have sinned this time: the LORD is righteous, and I and my people are wicked"

**Exodus 9:34-35** — "And when Pharaoh saw that the rain and the hail and the thunders were ceased, he sinned yet more, and hardened his heart"

- **Matthew 27:3-4** — Judas: "I have sinned in that I have betrayed the innocent blood" — a confession without repentance that leads to despair, not salvation
- **2 Corinthians 7:10** — "For godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of: but the sorrow of the world worketh death" — the difference between genuine repentance (metanoia) and worldly sorrow (merely being sorry for consequences)
- **1 Samuel 15:24-25** — Saul: "I have sinned... yet honour me now, I pray thee" — confession motivated by desire to preserve position, not genuine turning
- *Interpretation:* Pharaoh says "I have sinned this time" — note "this time." It is sorrow for the specific consequence (the hail), not repentance for the pattern of rebellion. The moment the consequence is removed, he hardens his heart again. This is the pattern of worldly sorrow: crisis produces confession, comfort produces hardening. 2 Corinthians 7:10 defines the difference between this and genuine repentance: genuine repentance produces a change of direction that is "not to be repented of" — it does not reverse when the pressure lifts.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### RAISED THEE UP (Hebrew: he'emadtika)
- **Exodus 9:16** — "for this cause have I raised thee up" — he'emadtika = caused thee to stand, established thee, sustained thee in position
- Not "created you for destruction" but "sustained you through the plagues that could have killed you"
- **Romans 9:17** — Paul's use confirms this: God sustained Pharaoh in order to demonstrate power through the confrontation
- *Definition here:* Divine sustaining of a person in their position specifically so that God's power and purpose can be displayed through the encounter

### MURRAIN (Hebrew: deber)
- **Exodus 9:3** — "the hand of the LORD is upon thy cattle... there shall be a very grievous murrain"
- deber = pestilence, plague; frequently used for epidemic disease in both animals and humans
- **Psalm 91:3** — "He shall deliver thee from the snare of the fowler, and from the noisome pestilence (deber)"
- **Revelation 6:8** — "Death, and Hell followed with him. And power was given unto them over the fourth part of the earth, to kill with sword, and with hunger, and with death (thanatos), and with the beasts of the earth" — deber is one of the four horseman judgments
- *Definition here:* A divinely sent epidemic disease affecting livestock; used as a plague-category throughout Scripture and into Revelation

### FEARED THE WORD (Hebrew: yare et-debar)
- **Exodus 9:20** — "He that feared the word of the LORD"
- yare = reverential awe; debar = the word/speaking
- *Definition here:* Those who took God's spoken warning seriously and acted on it — distinguishing between those who heard and those who heard and believed enough to act; faith here is defined by action in response to the word, not merely acknowledgment of it

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Warning Before Judgment — The Prophetic Principle

- **Exodus 9:19-21** — God warns before the hail; those who believe act; those who don't lose their servants and cattle
- **Amos 3:7** — "Surely the Lord GOD will do nothing, but he revealeth his secret unto his servants the prophets"
- **Ezekiel 33:11** — "As I live, saith the Lord GOD, I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked; but that the wicked turn from his way and live"
- **2 Peter 3:9** — "not willing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance"
- *Type:* The advance warning before the hail plague establishes God's consistent pattern: He warns before He judges. Noah warned; Jonah warned; the prophets warned; John the Baptist warned; Jesus warned (Matthew 24). The warning is mercy — it creates the opportunity to respond. Those who respond to the warning are saved; those who don't perish under the judgment. Revelation's trumpets follow the same pattern: warnings escalate before final bowl judgments.

### The Furnace Ashes as Judgment — Israel's Oppression Reversed

- Israel's labor (bricks, furnaces) was the engine of Egypt's glory
- The ashes of the furnace (Israel's workplace) become the instrument of Egypt's judgment (boils)
- **Isaiah 10:24-27** — The LORD promises to break the Assyrian rod: the instrument of Israel's oppression will be broken
- *Type:* The reversal principle of Exodus 9:8-11 is the gospel principle — Christ took on the curse (our oppression/condemnation) and reversed it into glory. The cross, instrument of Roman execution, became the instrument of sin's defeat.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### Exodus 9:16 — "For This Cause Have I Raised Thee Up" Used to Claim God Creates People for Damnation

**Verse 16** — "And in very deed for this cause have I raised thee up, for to shew in thee my power; and that my name may be declared throughout all the earth."

**How it is misused:** This verse (quoted in Romans 9:17) is used to argue that God arbitrarily creates certain people specifically for the purpose of eternal damnation, with no regard for their choices, making them instruments of wrath with no possibility of mercy. It is used to build a theology of unconditional reprobation where some people are simply made to be destroyed.

**What the text actually says:** The word "raised thee up" in context means sustained in position, not created for destruction — and Pharaoh's resistance is the context of his own repeated choices:
- **Exodus 5:2** — "Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice?" — Pharaoh's defiance is his own stated position from the first encounter; he is not forced into it
- **Exodus 8:15** — "He hardened his heart, and hearkened not unto them; as the LORD had said" — Pharaoh's own repeated acts of hardening precede God's judicial hardening
- **Exodus 9:15** — "For now I will stretch out my hand, that I may smite thee and thy people with pestilence; and thou shalt be cut off from the earth" — God states He *could have* destroyed Pharaoh entirely by now; the fact that Pharaoh still lives is the grace that becomes the stage for demonstration
- **Romans 9:17-18** — Paul's use of this verse in Romans 9 is in the context of God's freedom to show mercy or harden, not a doctrine that specific people are created without any possibility of repentance

*Correction:* "Raised thee up" means God sustained Pharaoh alive through nine plagues that could have killed him, in order to complete the full demonstration of His power and make His name known worldwide. This is not arbitrary damnation — it is God's sovereign use of a man who had already repeatedly chosen his own path of resistance. The verse declares the purpose of the plagues for the nations, not the predetermined eternal fate of a soul who never had a choice.

---

### Exodus 9:27 — "I Have Sinned" Cited as Evidence of Pharaoh's Genuine Repentance

**Verse 27** — "And Pharaoh sent, and called for Moses and Aaron, and said unto them, I have sinned this time: the LORD is righteous, and I and my people are wicked."

**How it is misused:** Pharaoh's confession is sometimes used to argue that he had genuinely repented at this point and God's continued judgment of him afterward was therefore unjust. The words of confession are treated as equivalent to repentance, and the subsequent hardening is presented as God punishing a converted man.

**What the text actually says:** The narrative immediately contradicts any reading of this as genuine repentance:
- **Verse 34** — "And when Pharaoh saw that the rain and the hail and the thunders were ceased, he sinned yet more, and hardened his heart, he and his servants" — the moment the pressure lifted, Pharaoh's behavior changed; genuine repentance does not reverse when consequences are removed
- **Verse 30** — Moses' own assessment precedes the removal of the hail: "But as for thee and thy servants, I know that ye will not yet fear the LORD God" — Moses, who was receiving God's word, did not believe this confession represented genuine repentance even as Pharaoh was making it
- **2 Corinthians 7:10** — "For godly sorrow worketh repentance to salvation not to be repented of: but the sorrow of the world worketh death" — "sorrow of the world" = sorrow over consequences; it produces the appearance of repentance without the reality; Pharaoh's "I have sinned this time" is precisely this: sorrow for the hail, not sorrow for his rebellion against God

*Correction:* Pharaoh's confession in Exodus 9:27 is an example of what 2 Corinthians 7:10 calls "worldly sorrow" — regret for the consequences of sin rather than repentance from the sin itself. The phrase "this time" reveals the scope: he is sorry about the hail specifically, not about his posture toward God. Verse 34 confirms: when the consequence was removed, the confession evaporated. Genuine repentance produces a change of direction that is "not to be repented of" — it does not reverse when the pressure lifts.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Hail and Fire Together — Unprecedented Compound Judgment

- **Exodus 9:24** — "There was hail, and fire mingled with the hail" — natural opposites combined in supernatural judgment
- **Revelation 8:7** — "The first angel sounded, and there followed hail and fire mingled with blood, and they were cast upon the earth" — the Exodus hail plague is the template
- **Psalm 148:8** — "Fire, and hail; snow, and vapours; stormy wind fulfilling his word" — the elements obey the word of God
- *Thread:* The combination of fire and hail — natural enemies that cannot coexist naturally — signals divine rather than natural origin. When God judges, He uses creation in ways that transcend natural laws.
