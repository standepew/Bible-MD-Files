## CHAPTER SUMMARY

God announces to Moses the final, decisive plague — the death of all the firstborn in Egypt, from Pharaoh's throne to the lowest servant, including firstborn of beasts. He instructs the people to borrow silver and gold from their Egyptian neighbors. Moses is noted as being "very great in the land of Egypt" — even Pharaoh's servants respected him. Moses delivers the plague announcement to Pharaoh: at midnight, every firstborn in Egypt will die; there will be a cry such as has never been or will ever be; but against Israel not even a dog will move its tongue. Pharaoh's servants will come bowing to Moses, begging Israel to leave. Moses departs in great anger. God confirms Pharaoh will not listen — so that His wonders may be multiplied.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Yet Will I Bring One Plague More" — The Tenth as the Decisive Act

**Exodus 11:1** — "Yet will I bring one plague more upon Pharaoh, and upon Egypt; afterwards he will let you go"

- **Romans 9:17-18** — God's purpose through the entire plague sequence was declaration of His name; the tenth completes it
- **Hebrews 12:26-27** — "Yet once more I shake not the earth only, but also heaven... that those things which cannot be shaken may remain" — the "yet once more" pattern: one final, decisive act that ends the conflict permanently
- *Interpretation:* The tenth plague is not merely the worst of ten — it is categorically different. The first nine were warnings and demonstrations; the tenth is judgment. The pattern runs through Scripture: God gives repeated opportunities (nine plagues, nine chances) before the final, irreversible act. Hebrews 12 applies this pattern to the end of the age: "yet once more" = the final shaking before the eternal kingdom.

### "Borrow of His Neighbour Jewels of Silver and Gold" — The Spoiling of Egypt Fulfilled

**Exodus 11:2-3** — Israel to borrow silver and gold; God gives them favor

- **Genesis 15:14** — "And also that nation, whom they shall serve, will I judge: and afterward shall they come out with great substance" — prophesied to Abraham 430 years earlier; now being fulfilled
- **Psalm 105:37** — "He brought them forth also with silver and gold: and there was not one feeble person among their tribes"
- **Exodus 3:21-22** — God promised this at the burning bush: "ye shall spoil the Egyptians"
- **Proverbs 13:22** — "The wealth of the sinner is laid up for the just"
- *Interpretation:* The spoiling of Egypt is not an afterthought — it is the fulfillment of a 430-year-old covenant promise made to Abraham. Israel exits slavery and receives what is owed them. The pattern: those who serve God faithfully in affliction ultimately receive the inheritance of those who oppressed them. The silver and gold Israel carried out funded the tabernacle — Egypt's wealth became the house of God.

### "Not a Dog Shall Move His Tongue" — Total Distinction

**Exodus 11:7** — "But against any of the children of Israel shall not a dog move his tongue, against man or beast: that ye may know how that the LORD doth put a difference between the Egyptians and Israel"

- **Exodus 8:22-23** — The Goshen principle established with the flies; now applied at the ultimate level
- **Psalm 91:7-10** — "A thousand shall fall at thy side, and ten thousand at thy right hand; but it shall not come nigh thee... There shall no evil befall thee, neither shall any plague come nigh thy dwelling"
- **John 10:27-28** — "My sheep hear my voice... and they shall never perish, neither shall any man pluck them out of my hand" — the total protection of those belonging to God
- *Interpretation:* "Not a dog shall move his tongue" is the most emphatic expression of total peace and protection in the passage. While Egypt is in the greatest cry in human history, Israel has such complete peace that even the dogs are silent around them. This is the covenant protection principle at its fullest: the same event that brings total destruction to one brings total peace to another, depending entirely on whose blood marks the door.

### Moses Very Great in Egypt — The Deliverer Honored by the Enemy

**Exodus 11:3** — "The man Moses was very great in the land of Egypt, in the sight of Pharaoh's servants, and in the sight of the people"

- **Acts 7:22** — "Moses was learned in all the wisdom of the Egyptians, and was mighty in words and in deeds"
- **Hebrews 11:26** — Moses "esteeming the reproach of Christ greater riches than the treasures in Egypt" — he chose affliction over greatness, yet God gave him greatness anyway
- **Genesis 39:2-4** — Joseph was great in Potiphar's house; God gave him favor even in slavery
- **Daniel 1:19-20** — Daniel and his three friends found ten times better than all others; God gives favor to those who honor Him
- *Interpretation:* The greatness of Moses before Pharaoh's court is God's doing — the same man Pharaoh tried to kill (Exodus 2:15) is now "very great" in Egypt's eyes. This is the reversal principle: what the enemy intended for destruction (the fugitive Moses, the enslaved Israel), God uses for His own exaltation. The deliverer is ultimately honored even in the house of the oppressor.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### DIFFERENCE (Hebrew: pala / palah)
- **Exodus 11:7** — "the LORD doth put a difference between the Egyptians and Israel"
- palah = to separate, distinguish, make wonderful/marvelous; same root as pele (wonder, miracle)
- *Definition here:* God's separating act between His people and Egypt is itself described with the word for miracle — the distinction God makes between those who are His and those who are not is a supernatural act, not a natural one

### GREAT CRY (Hebrew: tse'aqah gedolah)
- **Exodus 11:6** — "there shall be a great cry throughout all the land of Egypt"
- tse'aqah = a cry of distress, anguish; gedolah = great, mighty
- **Genesis 18:20-21** — "Because the cry (tse'aqah) of Sodom and Gomorrah is great" — the same word; the cry that reaches God from the oppressed now falls on the oppressor
- *Definition here:* The great cry of Egypt is the reversal of Israel's great cry under bondage (Exodus 2:23); what Egypt made Israel cry, Egypt now cries; the same word marks both the suffering of the oppressed and the judgment of the oppressor

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Tenth Plague as the Cross

- The death of Egypt's firstborn: life for life; Pharaoh took Hebrew sons — God takes Egypt's
- **Exodus 4:22-23** — "Israel is my son, my firstborn... I will slay thy son, even thy firstborn" — the tenth plague was announced in chapter 4; the entire plague narrative builds to this
- **Romans 8:32** — "He that spared not his own Son, but delivered him up for us all" — God did not spare His own firstborn (Christ) so that those under judgment might be spared
- **John 3:16** — "God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son" — the ultimate firstborn given, so that believers are not under the judgment that fell on Egypt
- *Type:* The death of Egypt's firstborn = judgment on sin falling on the representative firstborn. The Passover lamb = a substitute. The cross = God's own firstborn bearing the judgment so that those marked by His blood are passed over. Exodus 11 is the announcement; Exodus 12 is the instruction; the cross is the ultimate fulfillment.

### "Midnight" — The Hour of Divine Visitation

- **Exodus 11:4** — "About midnight will I go out"
- **Matthew 25:6** — "And at midnight there was a cry made, Behold, the bridegroom cometh" — the parable of the ten virgins; the midnight hour is the hour of the Lord's appearing
- **Acts 16:25** — Paul and Silas praying at midnight; the prison opened
- **Mark 13:35** — "Watch ye therefore... lest coming suddenly he find you sleeping... at midnight, or at the cockcrowing, or in the morning"
- *Thread:* Midnight in Scripture is consistently the hour of divine visitation — both in judgment (Egypt) and in deliverance (Acts 16). The pattern: when human darkness is at its deepest, God moves.
