## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses and Israel sing the Song of Moses — the first great hymn of Scripture — celebrating the Red Sea victory. It declares: the LORD is strength, song, salvation; He is a man of war; He drowned Pharaoh's best; Who is like unto thee, glorious in holiness, fearful in praises, doing wonders? He will plant them in His holy habitation; the LORD shall reign forever and ever. Miriam the prophetess leads the women in dance and timbrel. Three days into the wilderness of Shur, they find no water. They reach Marah — water too bitter to drink. The people murmur. Moses cries to God; God shows him a tree; he casts it into the water; the water becomes sweet. God gives a statute: "If thou wilt diligently hearken unto the voice of the LORD thy God... I will put none of these diseases upon thee... for I am the LORD that healeth thee." They come to Elim — twelve wells and seventy palm trees.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "The LORD Is My Strength and Song, and He Is Become My Salvation"

**Exodus 15:2**

- **Isaiah 12:2** — "Behold, God is my salvation; I will trust, and not be afraid: for the LORD JEHOVAH is my strength and my song; he also is become my salvation" — Isaiah 12 is a direct echo of Exodus 15:2; Israel's end-time song mirrors the Red Sea song
- **Psalm 118:14** — "The LORD is my strength and song, and is become my salvation" — Psalm 118 quotes Exodus 15:2 word for word; this is the Psalm Jesus quoted ("the stone the builders rejected") at Passover
- **Revelation 15:3** — "And they sing the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb, saying, Great and marvellous are thy works, Lord God Almighty; just and true are thy ways, thou King of saints" — the redeemed in Revelation sing "the song of Moses"; Exodus 15 is sung at the end of the age by those who have overcome the beast
- *Interpretation:* Exodus 15 is not merely a historical hymn — it is the song of all redemption. Isaiah 12 uses it for the millennial age; Revelation 15 uses it for the final victory of the saints over the beast. The same song sung at the Red Sea will be sung when the beast's system falls. The pattern: the redeemed always sing after the enemy is drowned.

### "The LORD Is a Man of War" — God as the Warrior

**Exodus 15:3** — "The LORD is a man of war: the LORD is his name"

- **Isaiah 42:13** — "The LORD shall go forth as a mighty man, he shall stir up jealousy like a man of war: he shall cry, yea, roar; he shall prevail against his enemies"
- **Isaiah 63:1-4** — "Who is this that cometh from Edom... travelling in the greatness of his strength?" — the Divine Warrior in end-time garments, having trodden the winepress alone
- **Revelation 19:11-15** — Christ riding on a white horse: "In righteousness he doth judge and make war... and out of his mouth goeth a sharp sword, that with it he should smite the nations"
- **Psalm 24:8** — "Who is this King of glory? The LORD strong and mighty, the LORD mighty in battle"
- *Interpretation:* "The LORD is a man of war" is one of the most important divine self-descriptions in all of Scripture. It directly contradicts the domesticated deity of modern religion. The God of Exodus is the God who fights — and fights to win. Isaiah and Revelation both apply this title to the final coming of Christ. He who drowned Pharaoh in the sea will tread the winepress of God's wrath at the end of the age (Revelation 19:15). Exodus 15:3 is the declaration; Revelation 19 is the fulfillment.

### "Who Is Like Unto Thee?" — The Incomparability of God

**Exodus 15:11** — "Who is like unto thee, O LORD, among the gods? who is like thee, glorious in holiness, fearful in praises, doing wonders?"

- **Psalm 89:6-8** — "For who in the heaven can be compared unto the LORD? who among the sons of the mighty can be likened unto the LORD? God is greatly to be feared in the assembly of the saints"
- **Isaiah 40:18, 25** — "To whom then will ye liken God?... To whom then will ye liken me, or shall I be equal? saith the Holy One"
- **Micah 7:18** — "Who is a God like unto thee, that pardoneth iniquity?" — Micah's answer to Exodus 15:11: the incomparable God is the one who forgives
- **Revelation 15:4** — "Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name? for thou only art holy: for all nations shall come and worship before thee"
- *Interpretation:* "Who is like thee?" is the rhetorical heart of the Song of Moses — and the answer is: no one. The plagues demonstrated it; the Red Sea sealed it. The incomparability of YHWH runs as a thread from Exodus 15 through the prophets to Revelation 15. The song of the redeemed at the end of the age repeats the same question with the same implicit answer.

### The Bitter Waters of Marah — The Tree That Heals

**Exodus 15:25** — "And he cried unto the LORD; and the LORD shewed him a tree, which when he had cast into the waters, the waters were made sweet"

- **Genesis 3:17** — The tree in Eden brought the curse; here a tree reverses bitterness
- **Numbers 19:6** — The purification ritual uses cedar wood, hyssop, and scarlet cast into water — wood in water as purification
- **1 Peter 2:24** — "Who his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree" — Christ is the tree that makes the bitter waters of the curse sweet
- **Galatians 3:13** — "Christ hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us: for it is written, Cursed is every one that hangeth on a tree"
- **Revelation 22:2** — "The tree of life... and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations"
- *Interpretation:* The tree cast into Marah's bitter waters is one of the richest types in Exodus. The tree absorbs the bitterness and the water becomes sweet. Christ on the cross (the tree) absorbed the curse, the bitterness of sin and death, and makes what was bitter (the human condition) sweet. This is the atonement in tree-form. The tree of the curse (Genesis 3) → the tree of the cross (Galatians 3) → the tree of life (Revelation 22) is one continuous thread.

### Jehovah Rapha — The LORD That Healeth

**Exodus 15:26** — "for I am the LORD that healeth thee" — YHWH Rapha

- **Psalm 103:3** — "Who forgiveth all thine iniquities; who healeth all thy diseases" — healing linked to forgiveness
- **Isaiah 53:5** — "with his stripes we are healed" — the Servant's suffering as healing
- **Matthew 8:16-17** — Jesus heals all and Matthew quotes Isaiah 53:4: "Himself took our infirmities, and bare our sicknesses" — Jesus as Jehovah Rapha in flesh
- **James 5:14-15** — "Is any sick among you? let him call for the elders of the church... and the prayer of faith shall save the sick, and the Lord shall raise him up"
- *Interpretation:* Jehovah Rapha is the covenant name of God as Healer — first revealed here at Marah. The context is crucial: the healing of the water comes with a covenant condition — "If thou wilt diligently hearken to the voice of the LORD thy God." Healing in the biblical framework is always connected to the covenant relationship, not to a divine healing machine. The New Testament equivalent: "the prayer of faith shall save the sick" (James 5) — the healing flows through covenant relationship and faith.

### Elim — Twelve Wells and Seventy Palms

**Exodus 15:27** — "And they came to Elim, where were twelve wells of water, and threescore and ten palm trees"

- 12 wells = 12 tribes of Israel; God provides for each tribe
- 70 palms = the 70 elders of Israel (Exodus 24:1); the 70 nations of Genesis 10; the 70 who went into Egypt
- **Numbers 33:9** — Elim is listed as a stopping point; God records it as a place of provision
- *Interpretation:* Elim is not a random campsite — the numbers are covenantal. God does not send Israel to a random oasis; He sends them to a place with exactly 12 wells (one for each tribe) and 70 palms (the number of the complete nation that entered Egypt and the number of the elders). God's provision is precise and covenantal, not general and vague.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### SALVATION / YESHUAH (Hebrew: yeshuah)
- **Exodus 15:2** — "he is become my salvation" — yeshuah = deliverance, rescue, saving act
- The root of the name YESHUA (Jesus) — "thou shalt call his name JESUS: for he shall save his people from their sins" (Matthew 1:21)
- *Definition here:* At the Red Sea, God "became" their salvation — He did not merely offer it; He was the active agent of rescue. This is the name embedded in the person of Christ.

### FEARFUL IN PRAISES (Hebrew: nora tehilloth)
- **Exodus 15:11** — "fearful in praises" — nora = awesome, inspiring reverential fear; tehilloth = praises, songs of praise
- A unique phrase: God is awesome in the praises offered to Him; or: God's praises inspire awe
- *Definition here:* God's nature is such that even the act of praising Him is awesome — the praises are not common speech; they are declarations of overwhelming greatness that inspire reverential fear in those who say them

### RAPHA (Hebrew: rapha)
- **Exodus 15:26** — "I am the LORD that healeth (rapha) thee"
- rapha = to heal, to restore to wholeness, to make whole
- Used for physical healing (2 Kings 5:3), emotional healing (Psalm 147:3 — "He healeth the broken in heart"), national healing (2 Chronicles 7:14 — "and will heal their land"), spiritual healing (Isaiah 53:5)
- *Definition here:* Rapha encompasses the full spectrum of restoration — physical, emotional, spiritual, national; Jehovah Rapha is the God of complete wholeness

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Song of Moses — Sung Three Times in Scripture

1. **Exodus 15** — At the Red Sea; the Exodus victory
2. **Deuteronomy 32** — Moses' final song; prophesying Israel's future unfaithfulness and God's ultimate vindication
3. **Revelation 15:3** — The redeemed in heaven sing "the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb"

*Thread:* The Song of Moses bookends redemptive history — sung at the beginning of the Exodus (Exodus 15) and at the end of the age (Revelation 15). The song is the testimony of God's total victory over His enemies and the complete deliverance of His people. It is the eternal song.

### Miriam the Prophetess — Women and Worship Leadership

- **Exodus 15:20** — Miriam identified as prophetess; leads the women with timbrel and dance
- **Judges 4:4** — Deborah identified as prophetess; judges Israel
- **Luke 2:36** — Anna identified as prophetess; announces the Messiah in the temple
- **Acts 2:17** — "Your sons and daughters shall prophesy" — Joel's prophecy fulfilled at Pentecost
- *Thread:* The role of prophetess in Scripture is consistent and validated. Miriam at the Red Sea is the first named prophetess; she leads corporate worship. This is not in tension with New Testament order — it is the established pattern that Pentecost expands.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Three Days Without Water → Provision — The Resurrection Pattern

- **Exodus 15:22** — Three days in the wilderness, no water → Marah → the tree → sweet water
- **Jonah 1:17** — Three days in the fish → release → Nineveh
- **Matthew 12:40** — Three days in the heart of the earth → resurrection
- *Thread:* Three days of apparent lack/death followed by supernatural provision/resurrection is one of Scripture's most consistent patterns. The three days without water in Exodus 15 follows immediately after the Red Sea — victory followed by testing. This is the pattern of the Christian life: after every great deliverance comes a test of whether the faith that was demonstrated at the Red Sea holds through three days of thirst.
