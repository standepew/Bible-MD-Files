## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Israel encamps at Rephidim — no water. The people quarrel (chide) with Moses: "Give us water." Moses: "Why do you tempt the LORD?" They murmur: "Hast thou brought us up out of Egypt, to kill us and our children and our cattle with thirst?" Moses cries to God: "They will stone me." God tells Moses: go before the people; take the elders; take the rod; "I will stand before thee there upon the rock in Horeb; and thou shalt smite the rock, and there shall come water out of it." Moses does so. He names the place Massah and Meribah — testing and striving — because of Israel's question: "Is the LORD among us, or not?" Then Amalek attacks. Moses sends Joshua to fight. Moses, Aaron, and Hur go up the hill. When Moses holds up his hand, Israel prevails; when his hand drops, Amalek prevails. His hands grow heavy; Aaron and Hur hold them up. Joshua defeats Amalek. God: "Write this for a memorial in a book... for I will utterly put out the remembrance of Amalek." Moses builds an altar: Jehovah-Nissi — The LORD is my banner. "Because the LORD hath sworn that the LORD will have war with Amalek from generation to generation."

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Thou Shalt Smite the Rock" — The Rock as Type of Christ

**Exodus 17:6** — "Behold, I will stand before thee there upon the rock in Horeb; and thou shalt smite the rock, and there shall come water out of it"

- **Numbers 20:8-12** — At Kadesh, God tells Moses to speak to the rock; Moses strikes it instead — this is Moses' sin that costs him entry to Canaan; the rock was not to be struck twice
- **1 Corinthians 10:4** — "And did all drink the same spiritual drink: for they drank of that spiritual Rock that followed them: and that Rock was Christ" — Paul identifies the rock as Christ
- **Isaiah 53:4-5** — "He was wounded for our transgressions... with his stripes we are healed" — Christ smitten; water of life flowing from His side
- **John 19:34** — "One of the soldiers with a spear pierced his side, and forthwith came there out blood and water" — Christ is smitten at the cross; water and blood flow out
- *Interpretation:* The rock struck once in Exodus 17 is the type of Christ crucified once. The water that flows from the smitten rock = the Holy Spirit and life flowing from the cross. This is why Moses' error in Numbers 20 (striking the rock twice) was so serious — it violated the type: Christ is crucified once (Hebrews 9:28; 10:10); to "strike the rock again" would imply a second crucifixion. The rock must be struck once; thereafter it must only be spoken to (Numbers 20:8). The gospel is the proclamation of the once-crucified Rock, not re-crucifixion.

### Massah and Meribah — "Is the LORD Among Us, or Not?"

**Exodus 17:7** — "he called the name of the place Massah, and Meribah... because they tempted the LORD, saying, Is the LORD among us, or not?"

- **Psalm 95:7-11** — "Today if ye will hear his voice, harden not your heart, as in the provocation (Meribah), and as in the day of temptation (Massah) in the wilderness: When your fathers tempted me, proved me, and saw my work. Forty years long was I grieved with this generation... Unto whom I sware in my wrath that they should not enter into my rest"
- **Hebrews 3:7-11** — The Spirit quotes Psalm 95 directly, applying Massah/Meribah to New Testament believers as a warning: "Harden not your hearts, as in the provocation, in the day of temptation in the wilderness"
- **Hebrews 3:17-19** — "with whom was he grieved forty years? was it not with them that had sinned?... And to whom sware he that they should not enter into his rest, but to them that believed not?"
- *Interpretation:* "Is the LORD among us, or not?" is identified by the Holy Spirit (through Psalm 95 and Hebrews 3) as the defining sin of unbelief — demanding proof of God's presence after seeing His works. Hebrews 3 applies this to the danger of New Testament apostasy. The wilderness generation saw the plagues, the Red Sea, the manna, the water from the rock — and still asked "Is the LORD among us?" This is the failure mode of those who require perpetual signs rather than walking in faith.

### Moses' Upraised Hands — Intercessory Prayer and the Cross

**Exodus 17:11-12** — "When Moses held up his hand, Israel prevailed: and when he let down his hand, Amalek prevailed. But Moses' hands were heavy; and they took a stone, and put it under him, and he sat thereon; and Aaron and Hur stayed up his hands"

- **Hebrews 7:25** — "He ever liveth to make intercession for them" — Christ as the ultimate Intercessor whose hands are perpetually raised
- **Romans 8:34** — "Christ Jesus... who also maketh intercession for us" — the New Testament fulfillment
- **Isaiah 53:12** — "He bare the sin of many, and made intercession for the transgressors"
- **Luke 23:34** — "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" — Christ interceding from the cross with arms outstretched
- *Interpretation:* Moses with upraised hands is the type of Christ interceding at the right hand of God. When the intercessor's hands are raised, the battle is won; when they drop, the enemy prevails. This is the theology of intercession: the battle on earth is determined by what happens in the heavenly realm of prayer. Aaron and Hur supporting Moses' hands = the community of believers supporting and participating in intercessory prayer. No one person can sustain the hands alone; the body of Christ bears the intercession together.

### Joshua's First Appearance — The Warrior Who Leads Into the Inheritance

**Exodus 17:9** — "And Moses said unto Joshua, Choose us out men, and go out, fight with Amalek"

- Joshua appears here for the first time; his name is Hoshea (Numbers 13:16 tells us Moses changed it to Joshua/Yehoshua = YHWH is salvation)
- **Joshua 1:1-3** — After Moses dies, Joshua leads Israel into the promised land
- **Matthew 1:21** — Jesus (Greek form of Yehoshua/Joshua): "thou shalt call his name JESUS: for he shall save his people from their sins"
- **Hebrews 4:8** — "For if Jesus (Joshua) had given them rest, then would he not afterward have spoken of another day" — Hebrews distinguishes between Joshua who led into Canaan and Jesus who leads into the true rest
- *Interpretation:* Joshua's first appearance in Scripture is as the warrior sent by Moses to fight Amalek while Moses intercedes on the hill. This is the pattern: Moses (the Law-giver) intercedes; Joshua (Salvation) fights the battle on earth. The Law intercedes; Salvation wins. Christ as the fulfillment of the Joshua type is the One who fights the spiritual battle that the Law could only point toward.

### Jehovah-Nissi — The LORD Is My Banner

**Exodus 17:15** — "And Moses built an altar, and called the name of it Jehovah-nissi"

- Hebrew: Jehovah-Nissi = YHWH is my banner/standard
- A military standard (nissi) is the rallying point around which the army gathers — the flag that marks whose army you belong to
- **Isaiah 11:10, 12** — "there shall be a root of Jesse, which shall stand for an ensign (nissi) of the people... And he shall set up an ensign (nissi) for the nations" — the Messiah as the ultimate banner/standard
- **Isaiah 49:22** — "I will lift up mine hand to the Gentiles, and set up my standard (nissi) to the people" — God's banner over the nations
- **Song of Solomon 2:4** — "his banner over me was love" — the banner is God's love
- **John 12:32** — "And I, if I be lifted up from the earth, will draw all men unto me" — Christ lifted up on the cross = the nissi raised that draws the nations
- *Interpretation:* Jehovah-Nissi declares that the LORD Himself is the rallying banner of His people in warfare. The cross is the ultimate Nissi — the standard lifted up that draws all people to itself (John 12:32). Isaiah 11:10 applies the nissi to the Messianic "root of Jesse" who stands as the rallying point for the nations. The altar Moses built pointing to this name is the type; Christ on the cross is the fulfillment.

### "The LORD Will Have War with Amalek from Generation to Generation"

**Exodus 17:16** — "the LORD will have war with Amalek from generation to generation"

- **1 Samuel 15:2-3** — God commands Saul to utterly destroy Amalek; Saul's failure to do so costs him the kingdom
- **1 Samuel 15:28** — Samuel: "The LORD hath rent the kingdom of Israel from thee this day" — the cost of sparing Amalek
- **Esther 3:1** — Haman is identified as an Agagite (Agag was the Amalekite king Saul spared); the perpetual war with Amalek manifests in Haman's plot to destroy all Jews
- **Revelation 12:17** — "And the dragon was wroth with the woman, and went to make war with the remnant of her seed" — the perpetual enmity continues to the end
- *Interpretation:* Amalek's perpetual warfare with Israel is one of the most significant patterns in the Old Testament. It is not merely historical conflict — it is the Genesis 3:15 enmity in one of its specific historical manifestations. Amalek attacked Israel from behind (Deuteronomy 25:17-18 — they attacked the weak and weary at the rear). This is the enemy's consistent tactic: attack the weak, the stragglers, those not walking in faith. The war with Amalek is the war between the seed of the woman and the seed of the serpent, expressed in military history.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### ROCK (Hebrew: tsur)
- **Exodus 17:6** — "the rock in Horeb" — tsur = a cliff, a rocky crag; a place of refuge and strength
- **Psalm 18:2** — "The LORD is my rock (tsur), and my fortress, and my deliverer; my God, my strength, in whom I will trust"
- **Deuteronomy 32:4** — "He is the Rock (tsur), his work is perfect" — the song of Moses calls God the Rock
- **1 Corinthians 10:4** — "that Rock was Christ" — Paul identifies the tsur of Exodus 17 as Christ
- *Definition here:* The rock is Christ — the firm, immovable foundation that when struck produces living water; the source of the Spirit's outpouring through the once-for-all crucifixion

### BANNER / STANDARD (Hebrew: nes / nissi)
- **Exodus 17:15** — Jehovah-Nissi = YHWH is my nes (banner)
- nes = a pole with a flag; a rallying point; a signal; a wonder/miracle (related word)
- **Numbers 21:8** — "the LORD said unto Moses, Make thee a fiery serpent, and set it upon a pole (nes)" — the same root; the serpent on the pole is a nes/sign/banner
- **John 3:14** — "And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up"
- *Definition here:* The nes/nissi is both the rallying standard and the lifted sign/wonder; Jehovah-Nissi declares God Himself is the standard around which His people rally and the lifted sign that draws the nations to Himself

### AMALEK
- **Exodus 17:8** — Amalek attacks Israel without provocation; the first enemy after the Exodus
- **Genesis 36:12** — Amalek was the grandson of Esau (Esau = the flesh; Amalek = the enemy that comes from the flesh)
- **Deuteronomy 25:17-18** — "Remember what Amalek did unto thee by the way, when ye were come forth out of Egypt; How he met thee by the way, and smote the hindmost of thee, even all that were feeble behind thee, when thou wast faint and weary"
- *Definition here:* The enemy who attacks from behind, targeting the weak and weary; typologically, Amalek represents the flesh/carnal nature that wars against the Spirit (Galatians 5:17); the external manifestation of the internal enemy

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### Exodus 17:6 — "The Rock Was Smitten" Used to Argue Christ Can Be Repeatedly Crucified

**Verse 6** — "Behold, I will stand before thee there upon the rock in Horeb; and thou shalt smite the rock, and there shall come water out of it, that the people may drink."

**How it is misused:** Some approaches to the Lord's Supper, prayers for the dead, or certain penitential systems imply that Christ's sacrifice can be repeated or re-presented, and the repeated smiting of the rock is occasionally offered as a type of this. This confuses the type-antitype relationship.

**What the text actually says:** The typological pattern embedded in the two rock incidents (Exodus 17 and Numbers 20) explicitly guards against the idea of a repeated sacrifice:
- **Exodus 17:6** — the rock is struck once; water flows; the people drink — the single smiting is sufficient
- **Numbers 20:8** — at Kadesh, God tells Moses: "speak ye unto the rock before their eyes; and it shall give forth his water" — the second time, God specifically commands speaking to the rock, not striking it; the rock is not to be struck again
- **Numbers 20:11** — Moses strikes the rock twice anyway; this is identified as Moses' sin and costs him entry to Canaan
- **Hebrews 9:28** — "So Christ was once offered to bear the sins of many" — once; the Greek word hapax (once for all) is the New Testament's interpretive key
- **Hebrews 10:10** — "By the which will we are sanctified through the offering of the body of Jesus Christ once for all"
- **1 Corinthians 10:4** — "that spiritual Rock that followed them: and that Rock was Christ" — Paul's inspired identification of the rock as Christ makes the typological lesson clear: Christ struck once (crucified once); thereafter proclaimed and spoken to, not re-struck

*Correction:* The two-rock narrative of Exodus 17 and Numbers 20 is not incidental — it encodes the most important theological truth about the atonement: Christ was crucified once. Moses' sin of striking the rock the second time violated the type, and God treated it with great seriousness. Hebrews 9:28 and 10:10 are the Spirit's interpretation: the rock is struck once; water flows for all time; thereafter the gospel is the proclamation of the once-crucified Lord, not a repeated offering.

---

### Exodus 17:11 — "When Moses Held Up His Hand, Israel Prevailed" Used to Teach Prayer as Spiritual Leverage

**Verse 11** — "And it came to pass, when Moses held up his hand, that Israel prevailed: and when he let down his hand, Amalek prevailed."

**How it is misused:** This verse is sometimes used to argue that the effectiveness of prayer is purely mechanical — that as long as hands are raised in prayer, God is obligated to act, and that the quantity or posture of prayer determines God's response. It leads to the idea that prayer is a technique of spiritual leverage rather than communion with a sovereign Person.

**What the text actually says:** The passage does not present the upraised hands as a mechanical spiritual formula — it presents them as a God-given means of intercessory connection whose effect is entirely dependent on the One to whom Moses prays:
- **Verse 9** — "Moses said unto Joshua, Choose us out men, and go out, fight with Amalek: to morrow I will stand on the top of the hill with the rod of God in mine hand" — Moses holds the rod of God; the connection to God's authority is what matters; the rod is not a magic instrument but the sign of divine commission
- **Verse 12** — "But Moses' hands were heavy; and they took a stone, and put it under him, and he sat thereon; and Aaron and Hur stayed up his hands" — the community support is required; this is not a solo technique but communal intercession
- **Hebrews 7:25** — "He ever liveth to make intercession for them" — the ultimate fulfillment is Christ's intercession, which is unceasing and unfailing; Christ's hands do not grow heavy; He does not need an Aaron and Hur; the type reveals the limitation of the human intercessor and points to the perfect Intercessor
- **Romans 8:26** — "The Spirit also helpeth our infirmities: for we know not what we should pray for as we ought: but the Spirit itself maketh intercession for us" — the Spirit's intercession is the New Testament equivalent of Aaron and Hur supporting Moses' hands

*Correction:* Exodus 17:11 teaches the necessity and power of intercessory prayer — but not as a mechanical formula. The hands raised toward God represent the intercessor's complete dependence on and alignment with God's purpose. The community bears the intercession together because no single person can sustain it alone. The type is fulfilled in Christ's perpetual intercession (Hebrews 7:25), which never fails because His hands never grow heavy. Human intercession participates in what Christ is always already doing at God's right hand.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Rod of God — The Instrument of Both Judgment and Grace

- **Exodus 7:20** — Rod used to strike the Nile (judgment on Egypt)
- **Exodus 14:16** — Rod stretched over the sea (salvation for Israel)
- **Exodus 17:5-6** — Rod used to strike the rock (water/life for Israel)
- **Numbers 20:11** — Rod strikes the rock again (Moses' sin)
- *Thread:* The rod of God is the instrument of both judgment and grace. The same rod that brought plagues on Egypt brought water to Israel from the rock. The power is in the One who wields the rod, not the rod itself. Christ's cross is similarly both the instrument of God's judgment on sin and the source of salvation for those who believe.
