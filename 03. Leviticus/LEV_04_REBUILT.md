## CHAPTER SUMMARY

God introduces the sin offering (chattat) — specifically for sins committed "through ignorance" (shegagah = inadvertently, without deliberate intent). Four tiers, each with a different animal and procedure. The anointed priest who sins: a young bull; blood sprinkled seven times before the veil and applied to the horns of the incense altar; the whole carcass burned outside the camp — "it is a sin offering." The whole congregation who sins: same as the priest — young bull, same blood procedure, carcass outside the camp. A ruler who sins: a male goat without blemish; blood on the horns of the burnt offering altar only. A common person who sins: a female goat or female lamb; blood on the horns of the burnt offering altar. In each case the fat is burned on the altar. In each case: "the priest shall make an atonement for him... and it shall be forgiven him."

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Sin Through Ignorance — Unintentional Sin Is Still Sin

**Leviticus 4:2** — "If a soul shall sin through ignorance (shegagah) against any of the commandments of the LORD concerning things which ought not to be done"

- Shegagah = inadvertence, error, going astray unintentionally; not the same as zadon (presumptuous/deliberate sin)
- **Numbers 15:27-31** — The contrast is explicit: the shegagah can be atoned (v.27); the "high-handed" sin (yad ramah = raised hand = deliberate defiance) has no sacrifice — "that soul shall be utterly cut off"
- **Psalm 19:12-13** — "Who can understand his errors? cleanse thou me from secret faults. Keep back thy servant also from presumptuous sins; let them not have dominion over me" — David knows the distinction and prays against both
- **Hebrews 9:7** — The high priest entered the Most Holy Place once a year "not without blood, which he offered for himself, and for the errors of the people" — errors (agnoema = things done in ignorance) = shegagah
- **1 John 1:8** — "If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves" — the believer's ongoing need for the sin offering applied through Christ
- **Luke 23:34** — "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" — Jesus intercedes for sins committed in ignorance; the ultimate sin offering for the ultimate shegagah
- *Interpretation:* The distinction between ignorant sin and deliberate sin is not a loophole — it is God's recognition that the fallen human condition produces ongoing transgression that the person may not even be aware of. Leviticus 4 establishes that ignorance does not equal innocence: "if his sin... come to his knowledge... he shall bring his offering." Knowledge brings responsibility; responsibility requires atonement. The New Covenant application is 1 John 1:9: ongoing confession and cleansing for the believer who, as he grows in the light, discovers more of what his sin has been.*

---

### The Priest's Sin — Leadership Sin Has Communal Weight

**Leviticus 4:3** — "If the priest that is anointed do sin according to the sin of the people; then let him bring for his sin... a young bullock without blemish"

- The anointed priest's sin requires the same offering as the whole congregation (both: a young bull; both: blood into the Holy Place; both: carcass outside the camp)
- **James 3:1** — "Be not many masters, knowing that we shall receive the greater condemnation"
- **Numbers 20:12** — Moses' sin at Meribah (striking the rock twice) cost him the promised land — the leader's failure before the people carries amplified consequences
- **Malachi 2:7-8** — "For the priest's lips should keep knowledge... But ye are departed out of the way; ye have caused many to stumble at the law" — priestly sin corrupts the entire community
- *Interpretation:* The anointed priest's sin offering is identical to the congregation's because the leader's sin and the congregation's sin carry equivalent weight before God. This is not a statement about personal guilt but about communal consequence: a corrupted priest produces a corrupted people. James 3:1 is the New Covenant expression: teachers and leaders receive stricter judgment because their failures are not merely personal — they ripple through the entire community they lead.*

---

### Blood Applied Differently by Rank — The Deeper the Sin, the Deeper the Access Required

**Leviticus 4:5-7 (priest), 4:17-18 (congregation)** — Blood taken into the tabernacle, sprinkled seven times before the veil, applied to the incense altar horns

**Leviticus 4:25 (ruler), 4:30 (common person)** — Blood applied to the outer bronze altar horns only

- The priest/congregation sin required blood deeper into the sanctuary — before the veil and on the incense altar
- The ruler/individual sin required blood only on the outer altar
- **Hebrews 9:7** — The high priest alone entered the Most Holy Place with blood; the greater the represented sin, the deeper into the sanctuary the blood must go
- **Hebrews 10:19-22** — "Having therefore, brethren, boldness to enter into the holiest by the blood of Jesus... Let us draw near" — Christ's blood goes all the way in; into the true sanctuary; not just to the outer altar but to the very throne of God
- *Interpretation:* The depth of blood penetration in Leviticus 4 corresponds to the weight of the sin being covered. The priest and congregation — whose sins affect the whole covenant community — require blood that reaches the veil, the threshold of God's immediate presence. This graduates toward the climactic truth: Christ's blood enters not the earthly sanctuary but "into heaven itself, now to appear in the presence of God for us" (Hebrews 9:24). The ultimate sin — the sin of all humanity — requires blood all the way to the throne.*

---

### "Outside the Camp" — The Rejected Bearing of Sin

**Leviticus 4:11-12, 21** — The whole bull for priest and congregation is burned "without the camp unto a clean place"

- **Hebrews 13:11-12** — "For the bodies of those beasts, whose blood is brought into the sanctuary by the high priest for sin, are burned without the camp. Wherefore Jesus also, that he might sanctify the people with his own blood, suffered without the gate"
- The sin offering carcass goes outside because it has become the bearer of what is unacceptable in the camp — sin
- **Hebrews 13:13** — "Let us go forth therefore unto him without the camp, bearing his reproach"
- *Interpretation:* The sin offering carcass burned outside the camp is the type of Christ crucified outside Jerusalem's gate. What bears the sin must bear it outside the place of God's dwelling — sin cannot remain in the camp. Hebrews 13:12 makes the connection explicit: Jesus "suffered without the gate" to fulfill this type. And Hebrews 13:13 gives the call: the believer must also go outside — outside respectable religion, outside social acceptance — to where the sin-bearer died.*

---

### "And It Shall Be Forgiven Him" — The Guarantee

**Leviticus 4:20, 26, 31, 35** — The refrain: "and the priest shall make an atonement for him... and it shall be forgiven him"

- Four times in this chapter the same verdict is pronounced: forgiven
- **Micah 7:18-19** — "Who is a God like unto thee, that pardoneth iniquity... he will have compassion upon us; he will subdue our iniquities; and thou wilt cast all their sins into the depths of the sea"
- **1 John 1:9** — "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins, and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness"
- **Isaiah 43:25** — "I, even I, am he that blotteth out thy transgressions for mine own sake, and will not remember thy sins"
- *Interpretation:* "It shall be forgiven him" is not a tentative possibility — it is the covenant guarantee attached to the properly presented offering. God built the guarantee into the system: bring the right offering in the right way with a contrite heart, and forgiveness is certain. 1 John 1:9 is the New Covenant form of the same guarantee: "he is faithful and just to forgive." The faithfulness is God's; the justice is the cross. The forgiveness is certain.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### SIN OFFERING (Hebrew: chattat)
- From chata = to sin, to miss the mark; the chattat = the offering for the sin that was committed
- Distinct from the asham (trespass/guilt offering, Leviticus 5-6) which addresses the damage done by the sin; the chattat addresses the sin itself
- *Definition here:* The atonement offering for the guilt of sin committed — particularly unintentional sin; the mechanism of forgiveness; fulfilled ultimately in Christ who was "made sin for us" (2 Corinthians 5:21) and who suffered "without the gate" (Hebrews 13:12)*

### IGNORANCE (Hebrew: shegagah)
- From shagah = to wander, go astray, reel; the shegagah = the sin of straying without full awareness
- Opposite of zadon = presumption; yad ramah = high-handed deliberate defiance
- *Definition here:* Sin committed without full knowledge or deliberate intent; neither innocent (it still requires atonement) nor the same as willful rebellion; the category of human failure that ongoing fellowship with God through the sacrificial system was specifically designed to cover*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Isaiah 53:10 — Christ as All Five Offerings

**Isaiah 53:10** — "Yet it pleased the LORD to bruise him; he hath put him to grief: when thou shalt make his soul an offering for sin (asham)"

- The Suffering Servant's soul is specifically designated an asham (Leviticus 5-6 = guilt/trespass offering)
- But the entire context of Isaiah 53 covers all five offerings:
  - v.7 — "brought as a lamb to the slaughter" = burnt offering
  - v.5 — "the chastisement of our peace was upon him" = peace offering
  - v.10 — "an offering for sin (asham)" = trespass offering
  - v.5 — "wounded for our transgressions" = sin offering (chattat)
  - v.11 — "by his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many" = the grain offering of righteous life offered

*One sacrifice. All five offerings. This is the New Testament's claim: Christ's death is not analogous to the sacrificial system — it is the system's singular, permanent, complete fulfillment.*

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### Leviticus 4:2 — "Sins of Ignorance" Used to Minimize or Excuse Willful Sin

**The misuse:** "If a soul shall sin through ignorance" is sometimes misread in two opposite directions: (1) used to argue that ignorance is a complete excuse and no guilt exists, or (2) used to argue that only ignorant/accidental sins are ever forgiven, leaving willful sins in a hopeless category.

**What the text actually says:** Shegagah (inadvertent sin) requires atonement — the ignorance does not remove the guilt; it only determines the category of offering. The provision is FOR ignorant sin precisely because even unintentional transgression is real transgression.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Numbers 15:30-31** — "But the soul that doeth ought presumptuously... the same reproacheth the LORD; and that soul shall be cut off" — the contrast makes clear: shegagah has a sacrifice; high-handed sin (zadon) has none under the Mosaic system
- **Psalm 19:12-13** — David prays for cleansing from BOTH: "cleanse thou me from secret faults" (shegagah) AND "Keep back thy servant also from presumptuous sins" (zadon) — both categories are real dangers requiring different responses
- **1 John 1:9** — "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins" — in the New Covenant, the provision extends even to willful sin through genuine repentance and Christ's blood, which exceeds the Levitical system's scope

---

### The "Outside the Camp" Burning — Not Mere Waste Disposal

**The misuse:** The burning of the sin-offering carcass outside the camp (vv.11-12, 21) is sometimes treated as purely procedural — removing unclean waste.

**What the text actually says:** The sin offering was "most holy" (Leviticus 6:25, 29); yet it is burned outside. The outside location is not contempt but separation — the carcass that has borne sin cannot remain where God dwells.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Hebrews 13:11-12** — "For the bodies of those beasts, whose blood is brought into the sanctuary by the high priest for sin, are burned without the camp. Wherefore Jesus also, that he might sanctify the people with his own blood, suffered without the gate" — the outside-camp burning is explicitly interpreted as a TYPE of Christ's crucifixion outside Jerusalem
- **Hebrews 13:13** — "Let us go forth therefore unto him without the camp, bearing his reproach" — the outside is where Christ died; it is the place of identification, not shame

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Number Seven in Blood Sprinklings

**Leviticus 4:6, 17** — "sprinkle of the blood seven times before the LORD, before the vail"

The seven-times sprinkling thread:
- **Leviticus 4:6, 17** — sin offering for priest/congregation: seven times before the veil
- **Leviticus 14:7, 16, 27** — leper's cleansing: seven sprinklings
- **Leviticus 16:14, 19** — Day of Atonement: seven sprinklings on and before the mercy seat
- **Numbers 19:4** — the red heifer ceremony: seven sprinklings toward the Tabernacle

**What the Bible establishes (seven = completion per the roadmap):** Seven sprinklings = complete, total, comprehensive application of the blood. Nothing is left uncovered; the atonement reaches every point. The blood applied seven times is not redundant application — it is the declaration of total coverage. Where the blood goes seven times, no sin remains unaddressed.

---

### The Horns of the Altar — Authority and Refuge

Blood is applied to the horns of the altars in Leviticus 4 (both the incense altar and the burnt offering altar).

The horn thread:
- **Exodus 27:2** — "And thou shalt make the horns of it upon the four corners thereof: his horns shall be of the same" — the horns are integral, not decorative
- **1 Kings 1:50** — Adonijah "caught hold on the horns of the altar" — the altar horns as a place of refuge; clinging to the horns = claiming the protection of the sacrifice
- **1 Kings 2:28** — Joab "fled unto the tabernacle of the LORD, and caught hold on the horns of the altar"
- **Psalm 118:27** — "Bind the sacrifice with cords, even unto the horns of the altar"
- **Habakkuk 3:4** — "and there was the hiding of his power" — the horns as the place where God's protective strength is concentrated

**What the Bible establishes:** The horns of the altar are the points of God's power and protection converging with the place of sacrifice. Blood on the horns = the atonement applied at the point of maximum authority and refuge. When a fugitive clung to the horns, he was claiming the protection of the blood that had been applied there. The New Covenant equivalent: clinging by faith to Christ crucified is clinging to the only true altar whose blood provides permanent refuge.
