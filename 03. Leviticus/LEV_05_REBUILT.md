## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Chapter 5 extends the sin offering provisions to specific obligation cases and introduces the graduated scale: anyone who can afford a lamb brings one; if not, two turtledoves or two pigeons (one sin offering, one burnt offering); if even that is too much, a tenth of an ephah of fine flour — no oil, no frankincense (it is a sin offering, not a grain offering). All receive the same verdict: "and it shall be forgiven him." The cases addressed: failing to testify when required (sins of omission), touching unclean things unknowingly, making a rash oath. Then the Trespass Offering (asham) is introduced for a different category: trespass against the holy things of God (defilement of what is set apart). This requires a ram valued in silver plus restitution of the harm plus 20%. Finally: unknown guilt — even what a person did not know he did makes him guilty and requires atonement when it comes to light.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Graduated Offering — No One Excluded by Poverty

**Leviticus 5:7, 11** — If you cannot bring a lamb → bring two birds; if you cannot bring two birds → bring fine flour

- **Luke 2:24** — Mary and Joseph brought "two turtledoves" — they used the birds provision from this chapter
- **Isaiah 55:1** — "Ho, every one that thirsteth, come ye to the waters, and he that hath no money; come ye, buy, and eat; yea, come, buy wine and milk without money and without price" — the New Covenant fulfillment: access to God at no cost to the one who comes
- **Revelation 22:17** — "And whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely" — the final form of the sliding scale: no price at all
- *Interpretation:* The sliding scale is one of the most important statements in Leviticus: God built poverty accommodation into the atonement system from the beginning. The same forgiveness — "it shall be forgiven him" — applies to the lamb-bringer and the flour-bringer. The New Covenant eliminates the scale entirely: Christ's one sacrifice is available to all at the same price — nothing. The God who told the poor they could bring flour to be forgiven ultimately provided forgiveness at His own expense with no minimum offering requirement.*

### Sin of Omission — The Witness Who Does Not Speak

**Leviticus 5:1** — "And if a soul sin, and hear the voice of swearing, and is a witness, whether he hath seen or known of it; if he do not utter it, then he shall bear his iniquity"

- The person who has witnessed something and is obligated to testify but fails to do so — their silence makes them guilty
- **Proverbs 24:11-12** — "If thou forbear to deliver them that are drawn unto death, and those that are ready to be slain; If thou sayest, Behold, we knew it not; doth not he that pondereth the heart consider it?"
- **Ezekiel 3:18** — "When I say unto the wicked, Thou shalt surely die; and thou givest him not warning, nor speakest to warn the wicked from his wicked way, to save his life; the same wicked man shall die in his iniquity; but his blood will I require at thine hand"
- **James 4:17** — "Therefore to him that knoweth to do good, and doeth it not, to him it is sin"
- *Interpretation:* Leviticus 5:1 is the foundational text for the sin of omission. The obligation to testify when one has witnessed something carries legal and moral weight — silence when speech is required is culpable. Ezekiel 3:18 extends this to the prophetic watchman; James 4:17 makes it a general New Covenant principle. The sin of omission — failing to do what is known and required — is not less guilty than commission; it simply requires its own acknowledgment and atonement.*

### "Though He Wist It Not, Yet Is He Guilty" — Unknown Sin Is Still Sin

**Leviticus 5:17** — "And if a soul sin, and commit any of these things which are forbidden to be done by the commandments of the LORD; though he wist it not, yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity"

- This is the strongest statement in Leviticus 5: ignorance of the violation does not remove the guilt of the violation
- **Romans 5:13** — "for until the law sin was in the world: but sin is not imputed when there is no law" — the principle works in reverse: where there IS law, sin is imputed even when unknown
- **Psalm 19:12** — "Who can understand his errors? cleanse thou me from secret faults" — David's prayer for the sins he does not know he has committed
- **Acts 17:30** — "And the times of this ignorance God winked at; but now commandeth all men every where to repent" — prior ignorance of God's revelation does not remove moral culpability; it only changes the immediate accountability
- *Interpretation:* "He wist it not, yet is he guilty" is God's declaration that moral reality is not determined by the sinner's awareness of it. A man can incur guilt he doesn't know he has. This is why the sin offering was offered regularly — not only for known sins but as ongoing coverage for the field of unknown transgression. Psalm 19:12 is the prayer that arises from this truth: "cleanse me from secret faults." The believer who prays 1 John 1:9 does so partly for sins they are not yet aware of, trusting Christ's blood to cover what has not yet come to light.*

### The Asham — Trespass Against Holy Things Requires Restitution

**Leviticus 5:15-16** — "If a soul commit a trespass, and sin through ignorance, in the holy things of the LORD; then he shall bring for his trespass unto the LORD a ram without blemish... And he shall make amends for the harm that he hath done in the holy thing, and shall add the fifth part thereto"

- The asham (guilt/trespass offering) addresses the damage done, not just the guilt incurred
- It requires the ram (atonement for guilt) PLUS restitution of the full value PLUS 20% (compensation for the period of deprivation)
- **Zacchaeus in Luke 19:8** — "And Zacchaeus stood, and said unto the Lord; Behold, Lord, the half of my goods I give to the poor; and if I have taken any thing from any man by false accusation, I restore him fourfold" — Zacchaeus voluntarily applied the Levitical restitution principle as evidence of genuine repentance
- **Isaiah 53:10** — "thou shalt make his soul an offering for sin (asham)" — Christ is the asham; He not only removes guilt but restores what sin took from God and from the injured
- *Interpretation:* The asham principle is that genuine atonement for wrong done to others requires both vertical reconciliation (the ram, the blood before God) and horizontal restitution (restoring what was taken, plus 20%). You cannot be right with God while leaving unaddressed the damage your sin caused to others. Zacchaeus demonstrates this principle in practice: his fourfold restitution is the fruit of genuine encounter with Christ — not a condition of salvation but the evidence of it.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### TRESPASS OFFERING / GUILT OFFERING (Hebrew: asham)
- Asham = guilt, offense; also the state of being guilty and the offering that addresses it
- Distinct from the chattat (sin offering = the act of sin) — the asham addresses the damage/debt created by the sin
- **Isaiah 53:10** — "his soul an offering for sin" = asham — the Servant's soul itself becomes the guilt payment
- *Definition here:* The offering that covers both the guilt of the offense and compensates for the damage done; the atonement that addresses not only what was done wrong but what was owed and is now restored; fulfilled in Christ who both removes guilt and restores what sin took from God (His glory) and from the injured (Romans 5:11 — reconciliation)*

### WIST NOT (Hebrew: lo yada)
- **Leviticus 5:17** — "though he wist it not" = lo yada = he did not know; yet the guilt (asham) is real
- *Definition here:* The state of moral culpability without conscious awareness; the condition that requires ongoing atonement even for the righteous person who walks as closely with God as possible; the reason why Christ's one offering covers not only known sins but the full measure of human transgression*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Flour Sin Offering — Poverty No Barrier to Forgiveness

**Leviticus 5:11** — The flour offering carries no oil (the Spirit's anointing) and no frankincense (the ascending fragrance of delight) — because it is a sin offering, not a worship offering; the absence is a statement of the gravity of the sin, not the unworthiness of the offerer

- Yet: "it shall be forgiven him" — identical verdict to the lamb-bringer
- *No one stands before God unforgiven because they couldn't afford the required animal. God built His own grace into the Law's requirements. The New Covenant simply removes the cost entirely: "the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord" (Romans 6:23).*

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### Leviticus 5:17 — "Though He Wist It Not, Yet Is He Guilty" — Misread in Both Directions

**The misuse (direction 1):** "He wist it not" is sometimes used to argue complete exoneration — if you didn't know it was wrong, no guilt attaches. This is the logic of "I didn't know, so I'm fine."

**The misuse (direction 2):** Conversely, the verse is sometimes used to generate paralysis — "I might be guilty of things I don't even know about" — used to undermine assurance.

**What the text actually says:** "Though he wist it not, yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity" — the verse explicitly rejects direction 1: ignorance does not equal innocence. But the provision of the trespass offering (vv.18-19) explicitly addresses direction 2: there IS an atonement for unknown guilt — bring the offering, be forgiven.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Psalm 19:12** — "Who can understand his errors? cleanse thou me from secret faults" — David's prayer acknowledges the category of unknown guilt and brings it to God for cleansing, rather than either ignoring it or despairing over it
- **1 John 1:9** — "cleanse us from ALL unrighteousness" — the "all" covers what is known and unknown; the New Covenant provision is comprehensive
- **Acts 17:30** — "the times of this ignorance God winked at; but now commandeth all men every where to repent" — the principle that ignorance does not permanently excuse; when light comes, response is required

---

### Leviticus 5:1 — The Sin of Not Speaking — Often Missed

**The misuse:** This verse (the witness who does not speak up) is rarely misquoted — it is more commonly ignored entirely, as if Leviticus 5 is only about ritual impurity.

**What the text actually says:** "And if a soul sin, and hear the voice of swearing, and is a witness, whether he hath seen or known of it; if he do not utter it, then he shall bear his iniquity" — silence when speech is required is a named, atonement-requiring sin.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Ezekiel 3:18** — "When I say unto the wicked, Thou shalt surely die; and thou givest him not warning... his blood will I require at thine hand" — the prophet as required witness; silence = guilt
- **James 4:17** — "Therefore to him that knoweth to do good, and doeth it not, to him it is sin" — the New Covenant general principle; the sin of omission is a real category
- **Proverbs 24:11-12** — "If thou forbear to deliver them that are drawn unto death... doth not he that pondereth the heart consider it?" — God assesses the omission even when the person claims ignorance of obligation

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Sliding Scale as a Symbolic Thread — Access for All

**Leviticus 5:6-11** — Lamb → two birds → fine flour; the same "it shall be forgiven him" at the end of each tier.

This is not just economy — it is the first fully explicit statement in Leviticus that the cost of access to God adjusts to the worshipper's means. The thread:

- **Leviticus 1:3, 10, 14** — The burnt offering scale: bull → sheep/goat → birds
- **Leviticus 5:6-11** — The sin offering scale: lamb → two birds → fine flour
- **Leviticus 12:6-8** — Birth purification: lamb → two birds (Mary and Joseph used this tier — Luke 2:24)
- **Leviticus 14:21-32** — Leper's cleansing: three lambs → one lamb + two birds
- **Isaiah 55:1** — "Ho, every one that thirsteth, come ye to the waters, and he that hath no money; come ye, buy, and eat... without money" — the sliding scale reaches its New Covenant limit: no price at all
- **Revelation 22:17** — "And whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely" — the scale has collapsed to zero cost; Christ's one sacrifice covers all tiers simultaneously

**What the Bible establishes:** The sliding scale of Leviticus 5 is not merely practical accommodation — it is the enacted declaration that the identity of God makes provision for every person who comes to Him. The New Covenant removes the scale entirely without removing the principle: no one is excluded from access to God by what they lack.

---

### The Ram Without Blemish — Value Assessed in Shekels

**Leviticus 5:15, 18** — The trespass offering requires "a ram without blemish out of the flocks, with thy estimation by shekels of silver, after the shekel of the sanctuary"

This introduction of silver valuation ties into a broader thread:

- **Exodus 30:12-16** — The half-shekel ransom: "every man a ransom for his soul... the rich shall not give more, and the poor shall not give less" — silver as the medium of ransom, with a fixed value per soul
- **Leviticus 27** — The entire chapter on valuations in silver shekels; every person, animal, field assigned a silver value
- **Numbers 3:47** — Five shekels per firstborn redeemed
- **Zechariah 11:12-13** — "So they weighed for my price thirty pieces of silver" — the price of the Shepherd
- **Matthew 26:15** — Thirty pieces of silver for Christ — precisely the valuation assigned to a slave gored by an ox (Exodus 21:32); the lowest human valuation in the Law applied to the Son of God
- **1 Peter 1:18-19** — "ye were not redeemed with corruptible things, as silver and gold... But with the precious blood of Christ" — the silver system is superseded; what silver valued is now priced in blood

**What the Bible establishes:** Silver = the medium of human valuation and ransom throughout the Law. When Christ is sold for thirty pieces of silver, the Law's own valuation system is applied to Him — and then superseded, because His blood purchases what no amount of silver could.
