## CHAPTER SUMMARY

The eighth day after the seven days of consecration. Moses calls Aaron, his sons, and the elders. Aaron is commanded to offer a young calf and a ram for himself first (sin offering, then burnt offering), then the people's offerings: a goat for sin, a calf and lamb for burnt offering, a bullock and ram for peace offerings, and a grain offering with oil. The stated purpose: "for to day the LORD will appear unto you." The congregation draws near. Aaron first atones for himself, then for the people — working through all five categories of offering. He blesses the people. Moses and Aaron enter the Tabernacle together, come out, and bless the people. Then: "And there came a fire out from before the LORD, and consumed upon the altar the burnt offering and the fat: which when all the people saw, they shouted, and fell on their faces."

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Eighth Day — New Beginning Beyond the Seven

**Leviticus 9:1** — "And it came to pass on the eighth day"

- Seven = completion of the created order; eight = new beginning beyond the cycle; the first day of what comes after the world's week
- **Leviticus 23:36, 39** — the eighth day as the closing solemn assembly after the Feast of Tabernacles — the day beyond the feast
- **Luke 9:28** — the Transfiguration on "about an eight days after" Peter's confession
- **John 20:26** — "And after eight days again his disciples were within, and Thomas with them: then came Jesus" — the Resurrection appearances bracket on the eighth day
- **Colossians 2:11-12** — circumcision performed on the eighth day (Genesis 17:12) as the type of the circumcision of Christ (death and resurrection)
- *Interpretation:* The eighth day in Scripture is always the day of new creation — what exists after the old order is complete. The Tabernacle system begins its actual operation on the eighth day: the seven days of consecration were preparation; the eighth day is the launch of the new order. Christ's resurrection on the first/eighth day of the week is the ultimate eighth-day event — the new creation breaking through.*

---

### "For To Day the LORD Will Appear Unto You"

**Leviticus 9:4, 6** — The appearance of God's glory is the explicit purpose of the entire sacrificial procedure

- **Exodus 24:15-17** — "the glory of the LORD abode upon mount Sinai... And the sight of the glory of the LORD was like devouring fire" — the pattern from Sinai
- **John 1:14** — "And the Word was made flesh, and dwelt among us, and we beheld his glory" — the ultimate fulfillment: God appearing among His people not in fire above an altar but in incarnate form
- **John 17:24** — "Father, I will that they also, whom thou hast given me, be with me where I am; that they may behold my glory"
- **Revelation 22:4** — "And they shall see his face" — the eternal fulfillment: perpetual vision of the glory
- *Interpretation:* The entire sacrificial system — all the blood, all the procedures, all the priestly service — existed for one stated purpose: that God would appear among His people. This is the deepest motive of all worship: not to maintain a religious system, not to manage guilt, but to encounter the living God. The fire that fell in Leviticus 9 was the system working as designed — access to God achieved through atonement. The New Covenant answer is the incarnation and ultimately Revelation 22:4.*

---

### The Priest for Himself First, Then for the People

**Leviticus 9:7-8** — "Go unto the altar, and offer thy sin offering, and thy burnt offering, and make an atonement for thyself, and for the people"

- Aaron could not represent the people before offering for himself first
- **Hebrews 7:27** — "Who needeth not daily, as those high priests, to offer up sacrifice, first for his own sins, and then for the people's: for this he did once, when he offered up himself" — the contrast with Christ: Aaron needed his own sin offering; Christ had none
- **Hebrews 5:2-3** — "Who can have compassion on the ignorant, and on them that are out of the way; for that he himself also is compassed with infirmity. And by reason hereof he ought, as for the people, so also for himself, to offer for sins"
- *Interpretation:* Every Aaronic priest entered the Most Holy Place with blood for himself before blood for the people — because every Aaronic priest was a sinner. This structural requirement built the limitation of the old priesthood directly into the liturgy. Hebrews 7:27 uses this to declare the superiority of Christ: He offered for the people only, because He had no sin of His own requiring atonement. The limitation that was liturgically embedded in Leviticus 9 is fulfilled by elimination in Christ.*

---

### Fire From the LORD — Divine Acceptance Made Visible

**Leviticus 9:24** — "And there came a fire out from before the LORD, and consumed upon the altar the burnt offering and the fat: which when all the people saw, they shouted, and fell on their faces"

- **Genesis 15:17** — "a burning lamp that passed between those pieces" — the LORD walking through the covenant sacrifice at Abraham's cutting
- **1 Kings 18:38** — Elijah on Carmel: "Then the fire of the LORD fell, and consumed the burnt sacrifice" — the same pattern: God's fire as the answer to faithful sacrifice and the defeat of false gods
- **2 Chronicles 7:1** — "Now when Solomon had made an end of praying, the fire came down from heaven, and consumed the burnt offering" — the Temple dedication; the same pattern a third time
- **Acts 2:3** — "And there appeared unto them cloven tongues like as of fire, and it sat upon each of them" — the Pentecost fire; the New Covenant divine acceptance made visible
- *Interpretation:* The fire from the LORD in Leviticus 9 is not a special effect — it is God's signature of acceptance on the sacrifice. Three major moments in Israel's history repeat this pattern: Leviticus 9 (Tabernacle launch), 1 Kings 18 (Elijah's confrontation), 2 Chronicles 7 (Temple dedication). Each time, the fire declares: this is acceptable; I am present; this is My house. Pentecost is the New Covenant version — the fire falls, not on an altar, but on people who are now the temple (1 Corinthians 3:16).*

---

### The People Shouted and Fell on Their Faces

**Leviticus 9:24** — The dual response: vocal praise and prostrate reverence

- **Revelation 5:13-14** — "And every creature which is in heaven, and on the earth... heard I saying, Blessing, and honour, and glory, and power, be unto him that sitteth upon the throne... And the four beasts said, Amen. And the four and twenty elders fell down and worshipped"
- **Isaiah 6:1-5** — Isaiah sees the LORD in the Temple: "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts: the whole earth is full of his glory... Then said I, Woe is me! for I am undone" — shouting glory, falling in terror
- **Revelation 1:17** — "And when I saw him, I fell at his feet as dead"
- *Interpretation:* The response to the manifest glory of God is always two things simultaneously: exultant praise and overwhelming prostration. The people shout — because what they have been waiting for has arrived, the promises have been fulfilled, the system works, God is present. They fall — because the presence that is so desired is also overwhelming, holy, and consuming. Every authentic encounter with God in Scripture produces both.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### EIGHTH DAY (Hebrew: yom hash-shemini)
- **Leviticus 9:1** — the day after the complete seven; first day of operation after full preparation
- *Definition here:* The day of new beginning beyond the completed old cycle; the type of resurrection and new creation; the day the consecrated system begins its actual function; pointing forward to the first/eighth day of the week when Christ rose*

### GLORY OF THE LORD (Hebrew: kevod YHWH)
- **Leviticus 9:6, 23** — the visible manifestation of God's weighty, consuming, luminous presence; here appearing in fire consuming the sacrifice
- *Definition here:* The tangible outward expression of God's being — His weight, His splendor, His holiness made perceptible; the stated goal of the Tabernacle and its entire sacrificial system; fulfilled ultimately in the incarnation (John 1:14) and the New Jerusalem (Revelation 21:23)*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Sequence: Moses and Aaron Enter Together, Then Come Out and Bless

**Leviticus 9:23** — Moses and Aaron go into the Tabernacle, come out, bless the people — then the glory appears

This sequence foreshadows the high-priestly pattern of Hebrews: the priest enters the presence of God on behalf of the people, comes out having completed the work, and the result is blessing upon the people.

- **Hebrews 9:11-12** — "But Christ being come an high priest of good things to come, by a greater and more perfect tabernacle... by his own blood he entered in once into the holy place, having obtained eternal redemption for us"
- **Numbers 6:24-26** — "The LORD bless thee, and keep thee: The LORD make his face shine upon thee... The LORD lift up his countenance upon thee, and give thee peace" — the Aaronic benediction, the blessing the high priest pronounces upon coming out

*Moses and Aaron entering the Tabernacle together and coming out to bless the people is the type of Christ entering the true sanctuary with His own blood and emerging as the mediator of blessing for all who are His. The fire that fell after the blessing is the Spirit poured out at Pentecost — the divine ratification of the completed priestly work.*
