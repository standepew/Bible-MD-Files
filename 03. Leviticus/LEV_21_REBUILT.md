## CHAPTER SUMMARY

God addresses the priests through Moses: the ordinary priest may not defile himself by contact with a dead person except for the closest family (mother, father, son, daughter, brother, unmarried sister). He must not adopt pagan mourning rites (baldness, beard-shaving, body-cutting). He must not marry a prostitute, a profane woman, or a divorcée — because he is holy and offers the bread of God. His daughter who profanes herself is burned. The high priest exceeds all: he cannot uncover his head, rend his clothes, or enter any house where there is a dead person — not even for his own father or mother; he cannot go out of the sanctuary during the mourning period; he must marry only a virgin of his own people. Then the blemish law: any descendant of Aaron with a physical blemish — blind, lame, flat-nosed, limb deformity, broken foot or hand, hunchback, dwarf, eye blemish, scurvy, scabbed, broken testicle — cannot approach the altar to offer. But he may eat the bread of God, both the most holy and the holy things. He simply may not serve at the altar or go within the veil.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture) (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Higher Standard for Those Who Draw Near

**Leviticus 21:1-3, 10-12** — The priest's restrictions exceed the congregation's; the high priest's exceed the priest's

- **Luke 12:48** — "For unto whomsoever much is given, of him shall be much required"
- **James 3:1** — "My brethren, be not many masters, knowing that we shall receive the greater condemnation"
- **Hebrews 5:2-4** — "Who can have compassion on the ignorant... for that he himself also is compassed with infirmity. And by reason hereof he ought, as for the people, so also for himself, to offer for sins. And no man taketh this honour unto himself"
- *Interpretation: Proximity to God increases accountability. The priest who touches what is holy carries a stricter prohibition on what is unclean. This is not punitive — it is the logic of holiness: the nearer you are to the fire, the more completely everything combustible must be cleared away.*

### The Blemished Priest — Disqualified from the Altar, Not from God's Provision

**Leviticus 21:17-23** — A priest with a blemish may eat the holy bread but may not approach the altar or the veil

- **Hebrews 7:26** — "For such an high priest became us, who is holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from sinners, and made higher than the heavens" — Christ is the unblemished priest; no blemish in Him disqualifies Him
- **Hebrews 4:15** — "For we have not an high priest which cannot be touched with the feeling of our infirmities; but was in all points tempted like as we are, yet without sin"
- *Interpretation: The blemish law of Leviticus 21 built the requirement of physical perfection into the priestly type — pointing to the One who would be the perfect, unblemished priest. The blemished priest's access to eat the holy food (v.22) is significant: even those disqualified from altar-service may eat what God provides. The grace of provision is wider than the restriction of service.*

### "I the LORD Do Sanctify Them"

**Leviticus 21:8, 15, 23** — Three times: "I the LORD which sanctify you / him / them"

- **1 Corinthians 1:30** — "But of him are ye in Christ Jesus, who of God is made unto us wisdom, and righteousness, and sanctification, and redemption"
- **1 Thessalonians 5:23** — "And the very God of peace sanctify you wholly"
- **Jude 1** — "to them that are sanctified by God the Father"
- *Interpretation: The sanctification of the Levitical priests was not self-achieved — it was conferred by the LORD. The same pattern governs New Covenant sanctification. No person sanctifies himself; God sanctifies those He calls into His service.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### BLEMISH (Hebrew: mum)
- **Leviticus 21:17-23** — a physical defect or deformity; the twelve listed conditions cover visual, skeletal, skin, and reproductive impairment
- *Definition here: A physical disqualifier for altar-service in the Aaronic priesthood; pointing typologically to the requirement that the ultimate priest and sacrifice be "without blemish" (Hebrews 9:14; 1 Peter 1:19). The blemish law is not contempt for the disabled — it is the establishment of the unblemished standard that Christ alone meets perfectly.*

### PROFANE (Hebrew: chalal)
- **Leviticus 21:4, 6, 9, 12, 14, 15, 23** — to defile, to make common what is holy, to violate the sacred
- *Definition here: The opposite of qadosh (holy/set apart). To profane is to erase the distinction between the holy and the common — to treat the sacred as ordinary. The repeated chalal in Leviticus 21 defines the entire chapter: the priest's conduct, family, and physical presentation must not erase the distinction between the one who serves God and the world around him.*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Unblemished Priest — The Complete Fulfillment

Every disqualifying blemish listed in Leviticus 21:17-21 points to the same truth by negation: the true high priest must be without any of these. Hebrews declares the fulfillment:

- **Hebrews 7:26** — "holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from sinners, and made higher than the heavens"
- **Hebrews 9:14** — "who through the eternal Spirit offered himself without spot to God"
- **1 Peter 1:19** — "But with the precious blood of Christ, as of a lamb without blemish and without spot"

The twelve conditions listed in Leviticus 21:18-20 were never meant to exclude the disabled from God's presence — they were meant to establish the standard that no fallen human priest could fully meet, pointing to the One who would meet it perfectly.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES PASSAGES

### Leviticus 21:17-23 — Used to Exclude Disabled People from Ministry

**The misuse:** This passage is occasionally cited to argue that people with physical disabilities are disqualified from Christian ministry or from full standing before God.

**What the text actually says:** Even the blemished priest "shall eat the bread of his God, both of the most holy, and of the holy" (v.22). The restriction is from altar-service and the veil — not from the presence of God or from receiving God's provision. The blemish law governed a specific typological function (the shadow of the unblemished sacrifice) in the Mosaic economy.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Luke 14:13, 21** — Jesus specifically includes "the poor, the maimed, the lame, the blind" at the eschatological banquet — the very categories Leviticus 21 lists as disqualifying for altar-service are the categories Jesus calls to His table
- **Acts 3:2-8** — The lame man at the Beautiful Gate is healed and enters the Temple — crossing the barrier that the Levitical system maintained; Christ's power restores what the Law marked as restricted
- **Galatians 3:28** — "There is neither Jew nor Greek... ye are all one in Christ Jesus" — the distinctions of the Mosaic system do not govern the New Covenant community's inclusion

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The High Priest Who Cannot Mourn — A Thread to Gethsemane

**Leviticus 21:10-12** — The high priest cannot uncover his head, rend his clothes, or defile himself for any dead person — not even his own father or mother. He cannot leave the sanctuary.

This requirement produces a remarkable typological echo:

- **John 11:33-35** — Jesus "groaned in the spirit... and Jesus wept" at Lazarus's tomb — the great High Priest who was also fully human, entering into human grief before He conquered it
- **Hebrews 5:7-8** — "Who in the days of his flesh, when he had offered up prayers and supplications with strong crying and tears unto him that was able to save him from death... Though he were a Son, yet learned he obedience by the things which he suffered"
- **Hebrews 4:15** — "touched with the feeling of our infirmities"

The Aaronic high priest could not mourn because he was the type: the one who represents the living God before a death-marked people. Christ, the antitype, entered death fully — and conquered it — something no Aaronic high priest could do. The restriction of Leviticus 21:10-12 is the shadow; Hebrews 4:15 and 5:7-8 show the substance: a High Priest who is both perfectly holy AND touched with human grief.
