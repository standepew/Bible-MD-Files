## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Three distinct laws. First: all lepers, all with a bodily issue, and all defiled by a dead body are put outside the camp — because God dwells in the midst. Second: when a man or woman commits a trespass against the LORD (sinning against a person), they must confess, make full restitution plus a fifth to the wronged party. If the wronged person has no kinsman to receive it, the payment goes to the priest plus an atonement ram. Third: the law of jealousy. If a husband suspects his wife of adultery but has no witness, he brings her to the priest with a barley meal offering (no oil, no frankincense — it is an offering of memorial, bringing iniquity to remembrance). The priest takes holy water in an earthen vessel, mixes in dust from the Tabernacle floor, uncovers the woman's head, puts the offering in her hands, and holds the bitter water. He charges her by oath: if innocent, be free; if guilty, may the LORD make your thigh to rot and your belly to swell. The woman says amen, amen. The priest writes the curses in a book, blots them into the bitter water, waves the barley offering on the altar, then makes the woman drink. If guilty, the physical signs of corruption appear; if innocent, she is free and shall conceive. The man is guiltless; the woman bears her own iniquity.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture) (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "In the Midst Whereof I Dwell" — Holiness Required by Presence

**Numbers 5:3** — "that they defile not their camps, in the midst whereof I dwell"

- **Leviticus 15:31** — "Thus shall ye separate the children of Israel from their uncleanness; that they die not in their uncleanness, when they defile my tabernacle that is among them"
- **Deuteronomy 23:14** — "For the LORD thy God walketh in the midst of thy camp, to deliver thee, and to give up thine enemies before thee; therefore shall thy camp be holy: that he see no unclean thing in thee, and turn away from thee"
- **2 Corinthians 6:16-7:1** — "for ye are the temple of the living God; as God hath said, I will dwell in them, and walk in them... Having therefore these promises, dearly beloved, let us cleanse ourselves from all filthiness of the flesh and spirit, perfecting holiness in the fear of God" — Paul quotes the indwelling-God principle and draws the same conclusion as Numbers 5: where God dwells, purity is required
- *Interpretation: The logic of Numbers 5:3 is the logic of all biblical holiness requirements. God's presence does not lower the standard; it raises it. The New Covenant community, indwelt by the Spirit (1 Corinthians 3:16), faces the same principle in the same force: the one who dwells within us is holy.*

### Restitution Plus a Fifth — The Principle Established

**Numbers 5:6-8** — sin against a person is a trespass against the LORD; restitution is principal plus a fifth; if no kinsman, it goes to the priest

- **Leviticus 6:1-7** — the foundational trespass law; Numbers 5 extends it to the case of no surviving kinsman
- **Luke 19:8** — Zacchaeus: "Behold, Lord, the half of my goods I give to the poor; and if I have taken any thing from any man by false accusation, I restore him fourfold" — he goes beyond the required fifth; genuine repentance exceeds the minimum
- **Matthew 5:23-24** — "if thou bring thy gift to the altar, and there rememberest that thy brother hath ought against thee; Leave there thy gift before the altar, and go thy way; first be reconciled to thy brother, and then come and offer thy gift" — Jesus cites the Leviticus/Numbers restitution principle as prerequisite to worship
- *Interpretation: Numbers 5:6 explicitly states that trespass against a person is trespass against the LORD. This is the theological foundation of Matthew 25:40 — "inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least of these my brethren, ye have done it unto me." Sin against a person has a vertical dimension; restitution to a person is restitution to the LORD.*

### The Bitter Water Ordeal — God as Witness When None Exists

**Numbers 5:11-31** — the ordeal of jealousy; no human witness; God adjudicates directly

- **Proverbs 5:3-4** — "the lips of a strange woman drop as an honeycomb... But her end is bitter as wormwood" — the bitter water is the enacted proverb of adultery's consequence
- **Revelation 8:10-11** — "And the name of the star is called Wormwood: and the third part of the waters became wormwood; and many men died of the waters, because they were made bitter" — bitter water as the symbol of divine judgment on corruption
- **Jeremiah 9:15; 23:15** — God gives the unfaithful people water of gall and wormwood to drink — the same bitter-water judgment extended to the whole nation for corporate spiritual adultery
- **John 8:3-11** — the woman taken in adultery brought before Jesus; the scribes and Pharisees demand the Law's verdict; Jesus writes in the dust (the same dust of the Tabernacle floor that goes into the bitter water) and says "he that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone" — the ordeal of Numbers 5 and Jesus writing in the dust on the ground are the same scene in different form
- *Interpretation: In Numbers 5, the priest writes the curses in a book and blots them into the water — the writing is dissolved into the water the woman drinks. In John 8, Jesus writes in the dust of the ground (where there was a Temple, the equivalent of the Tabernacle floor). The connection is not explicit in John's text but is established by the consistent Scripture-to-Scripture pattern.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### SPIRIT OF JEALOUSY (Hebrew: ruach qin'ah)
- **Numbers 5:14, 30** — the jealous spirit that comes upon a husband when he suspects unfaithfulness
- *Definition here: Qin'ah is the same word used for God's own jealousy — "I the LORD thy God am a jealous God" (Exodus 20:5). The husband's jealousy is not merely human suspicion; it is a reflection of God's covenantal jealousy over His people. The ordeal of Numbers 5 is the legal mechanism for resolving what the spirit of jealousy has raised but cannot itself adjudicate. God's jealousy over Israel (Ezekiel 16) is the national application of the same principle: God demands exclusive covenant faithfulness and will not share His people with other gods.*

### BITTER WATER (Hebrew: mayim ha-marim)
- **Numbers 5:18-24** — the water mixed with dust and dissolved curses
- *Definition here: Water that tests what is hidden — if unclean, it makes the corruption visible; if clean, it has no effect. The bitterness is the medium of divine verdict. Water in Scripture is a consistent testing/cleansing agent: the flood (judgment and cleansing), the Red Sea (judgment on Egypt, passage for Israel), the waters of Marah (bitter → sweetened by the tree, Exodus 15:23-25), the bitter water of Numbers 5. The tree that sweetened Marah's waters (Exodus 15:25) is the typological precursor of the cross that transforms bitter judgment into cleansing.*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Israel as the Unfaithful Wife — The Ordeal Applied to the Nation

The ordeal of Numbers 5 operates individually in its context. But the entire book of the prophet Hosea applies the same structure to the nation:

- **Hosea 2:2-13** — God charges Israel with spiritual adultery; she has gone after other lovers (Baals); God will expose her shame
- **Hosea 2:14-23** — after the ordeal, God woos Israel back: "I will betroth thee unto me for ever"
- **Ezekiel 16** — the extended allegory of Israel as the unfaithful wife: God found her, clothed her, adorned her, betrothed her — and she played the harlot with every nation
- **Jeremiah 3:1-10** — Israel as the divorced wife who has played the harlot; God calls her to return

The bitter-water ordeal is the prophetic template for what God does with Israel through the exile: the curses are administered (Leviticus 26; Deuteronomy 28), the corruption is exposed, and then — for the innocent remnant — the promise of conception: "she shall conceive seed" (Numbers 5:28; Isaiah 66:7-8 — the woman who travails and brings forth before the travail).

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES PASSAGES

### Numbers 5:27-28 — The Ordeal Used to Argue God Punishes by Miraculous Illness

**The misuse:** The physical consequences of the bitter water are sometimes used to argue that all illness is direct divine punishment for specific sin, or conversely, that the ordeal is primitive superstition and evidence of biblical unreliability.

**What the text actually says:** The ordeal is explicitly a divinely-administered verdict in the absence of human witnesses. God is the judge; the physical sign is His verdict. It is not a general principle about illness — it is a specific judicial mechanism for a specific covenant violation in a specific covenantal community.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **John 9:2-3** — "Who did sin, this man, or his parents, that he was born blind? Jesus answered, Neither hath this man sinned, nor his parents: but that the works of God should be made manifest in him" — Jesus explicitly rejects the automatic sin-to-illness equation
- **Job 1-2** — Job's suffering is not punishment for sin; it is the context for the revelation of God's justice

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Writing Curses and Blotting Them Out — The Forgiveness Thread

[[04. Numbers/NUM_05_REBUILT|Numbers 5:23]] — "the priest shall write these curses in a book, and he shall blot them out with the bitter water"

The curses are written — then blotted (dissolved) into the water the woman drinks. If guilty, they enter her and do their work. If innocent, the dissolved curses passed through her with no effect.

This write-and-blot pattern runs through Scripture:

- [[02. Exodus/EXO_32_REBUILT|Exodus 32:32-33]] — Moses offers to be blotted from God's book; God responds that He blots out those who sin against Him — there is a book; there is a blotting
- **Psalm 51:1, 9** — "blot out my transgressions... blot out all mine iniquities" — the psalmist asks for the same blotting
- [[51. Colossians/COLOSSIANS_02_REBUILT|Colossians 2:14]] — "Blotting out the handwriting of ordinances that was against us, which was contrary to us, and took it out of the way, nailing it to his cross" — Christ blots out the written record of charges against believers; the curses that should enter and destroy are dissolved at the cross
- [[66. Revelation/REVELATION_03_REBUILT|Revelation 3:5]] — "I will not blot out his name out of the book of life"

**What the Bible establishes:** There is a divine book. Names and charges are written in it. Blotting is the consistent biblical word for the removal of what was written — whether in judgment or in mercy. The bitter-water ceremony is the ceremonial preview of the cross-event of Colossians 2:14: the record of charges dissolved into the death of Christ so that they enter Him rather than those who are in Him.
