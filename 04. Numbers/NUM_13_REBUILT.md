## CHAPTER SUMMARY

God commands Moses to send men to search out Canaan — one ruler per tribe. Twelve named men are sent from the wilderness of Paran, including Caleb of Judah and Oshea (Joshua) of Ephraim, whose name Moses changes to Jehoshua. They are instructed to go up through the south into the mountain, to observe the land, the people, the cities, the soil, and to bring back fruit — it is the time of the first ripe grapes. They search from the wilderness of Zin to Rehob by Hamath. They reach Hebron where the children of Anak dwell. At the brook Eshcol they cut a single cluster of grapes requiring two men and a staff to carry it, plus pomegranates and figs. After forty days they return to Kadesh. Their report to Moses, Aaron, and all the congregation: the land indeed flows with milk and honey — here is the fruit. But — the people are strong, the cities walled and great, the children of Anak are there, Amalekites in the south, Hittites/Jebusites/Amorites in the mountains, Canaanites by the sea and Jordan. Caleb alone speaks up: "Let us go up at once, and possess it; for we are well able to overcome it." The other ten immediately disagree: "We be not able to go up against the people; for they are stronger than we." They bring an evil report: the land devours its inhabitants; all the people are men of great stature; there we saw the giants, the sons of Anak — and we were in our own sight as grasshoppers, and so we were in their sight.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture) (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Caleb's Spirit — "Another Spirit"

**Numbers 13:30** — "And Caleb stilled the people before Moses, and said, Let us go up at once, and possess it; for we are well able to overcome it"

- **Numbers 14:24** — God's own explanation: "But my servant Caleb, because he had another spirit with him, and hath followed me fully, him will I bring into the land" — the "another spirit" is what distinguishes Caleb from the ten
- **Joshua 14:8** — Caleb at 85 recalls: "I wholly followed the LORD my God" — forty-five years later, his self-assessment is consistent with God's assessment in Numbers 14:24
- **Hebrews 3:12-14** — "Take heed, brethren, lest there be in any of you an evil heart of unbelief, in departing from the living God... For we are made partakers of Christ, if we hold the beginning of our confidence stedfast unto the end" — the writer of Hebrews uses the Numbers 13-14 generation as the warning against departing from faith mid-journey
- *Interpretation: The "another spirit" of Caleb (Numbers 14:24) is the spirit of faith — the spirit that looks at the giants and says God is bigger. The ten looked at the giants and measured themselves against the giants. Caleb looked at the giants and measured them against God. The same geography; the same physical observations; radically different conclusions — because of whose lens they used.*

### "We Were as Grasshoppers" — The Self-Assessment of Unbelief

**Numbers 13:33** — "and we were in our own sight as grasshoppers, and so we were in their sight"

The ten spies define the problem precisely. They did not report what God said about them — they reported what they thought the giants thought of them. Self-assessment in the mirror of the enemy.

- **Isaiah 40:22** — "It is he that sitteth upon the circle of the earth, and the inhabitants thereof are as grasshoppers" — God's perspective on ALL the inhabitants of the earth; from God's vantage, every Canaanite giant is also a grasshopper. The ten spies apply the grasshopper self-assessment to themselves; God applies it to everyone including the giants
- **1 Samuel 17:42-45** — Goliath disdains David as a youth; David's reply: "Thou comest to me with a sword, and with a spear, and with a shield: but I come to thee in the name of the LORD of hosts, the God of the armies of Israel" — David has the Caleb spirit; he does not measure himself against Goliath but measures Goliath against God
- **Romans 8:31** — "If God be for us, who can be against us?" — the doctrinal statement of what Caleb enacted: the measure of an opposition is not its size but whether God is present

### Moses Renames Oshea — Jehoshua (The LORD Saves)

**Numbers 13:16** — "And Moses called Oshea the son of Nun Jehoshua"

- Oshea = salvation (from the Hebrew root yasha — to save)
- Jehoshua = YHWH saves; YHWH is salvation
- **Matthew 1:21** — "thou shalt call his name JESUS [Greek: Iesous = Hebrew: Yehoshua]: for he shall save his people from their sins" — Jesus bears the full form of Joshua's name; Joshua leads Israel into the earthly promised land; Jesus leads His people into the eternal rest
- **Hebrews 4:8** — "For if Jesus [Joshua] had given them rest, then would he not afterward have spoken of another day" — the writer of Hebrews plays on the shared name: the first Joshua's rest was incomplete; the second Joshua (Jesus) provides the true rest
- *Interpretation: Moses renaming Oshea to Jehoshua is prophetically loaded. The man who would lead Israel into the promised land is given the name that means "YHWH is salvation" — pointing forward to the one who would lead God's people into the ultimate rest under the same name.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### EVIL REPORT (Hebrew: dibbah — slander, whispering, defamation)
- **Numbers 13:32** — "they brought up an evil report of the land"
- *Definition here: Dibbah is not merely a negative assessment — it is a deliberate defamatory misrepresentation. The same word used in Genesis 37:2 — Joseph brings his father an "evil report" about his brothers. In Numbers 13:32, the evil report is against the land God has given — it slanders what God has declared good. Proverbs 10:18 — "he that uttereth a slander, is a fool." The ten spies are not merely pessimistic; they are slanderers of God's gift.*

### GIANTS / NEPHILIM (Hebrew: nephilim — fallen ones)
- **Numbers 13:33** — "there we saw the giants, the sons of Anak, which come of the giants"
- *Definition here: Numbers 13:33 connects the Anakim to the nephilim of Genesis 6:4 — "there were giants [nephilim] in the earth in those days." The Anakim are descended from Anak, who is descended from the Nephilim line. The same term appears only in Genesis 6:4 and Numbers 13:33 in the entire Torah. The ten spies are not merely reporting large people; they are reporting the survival of the pre-flood giant line in Canaan — which is why Joshua 11:21-22 records Joshua specifically cutting off the Anakim from the hill country.*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Cluster of Grapes on a Staff — Two Men, One Burden

**Numbers 13:23** — "they bare it between two upon a staff"

The cluster of grapes from Eshcol (meaning "cluster") required two men carrying it between them on a pole — the fruit of the promised land was so abundant it could not be carried by one. This image has been recognized within Scripture's typological framework:

- The cluster between two poles is a type of Christ crucified between two criminals — the true fruit of the promised land, lifted on a pole
- **Numbers 21:8-9** — the bronze serpent lifted on a pole: "And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up" (John 3:14) — the lifted-pole pattern in Numbers
- **John 15:1** — "I am the true vine" — Jesus as the vine whose fruit is carried out of the promised land to all nations
- **Deuteronomy 8:8** — the promised land described as "a land of... vines and fig trees and pomegranates" — exactly the fruits the spies brought back

The Eshcol cluster, requiring two carriers on a staff, is the only individual piece of the land's fruit given extended description. It is the sign of the land's abundance — and it required a cross-shaped transport: two bearers, one staff, one burden between them.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES PASSAGES

### Numbers 13:27 — "It Floweth with Milk and Honey" Used in Isolation

**The misuse:** The phrase "it floweth with milk and honey" is widely quoted as a simple promise of blessing — detached from the "nevertheless" of verse 28. The full report has two parts.

**What the text actually says:** The spies affirm the land's goodness — the evidence is in their hands, the fruit they carried back. But the ten immediately follow with "nevertheless" — the goodness of the land is not in question; the question is whether God is able to give it to them. The ten's failure is not that they reported difficulty; it is that they concluded the difficulty was greater than God.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Numbers 14:8** — Joshua and Caleb: "If the LORD delight in us, then he will bring us into this land, and give it us; a land which floweth with milk and honey" — the same phrase; the same land; completely different conclusion
- **The issue is never the land; the issue is always whether God will give it** — and that depends on whether Israel believes Him

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Forty Days — The Testing Number

[[04. Numbers/NUM_13_REBUILT|Numbers 13:25]] — "they returned from searching of the land after forty days"

The forty days of the spies' search becomes the forty years of wandering in Numbers 14:34 — "each day for a year." The forty-day number connects to the Bible's consistent testing pattern:

- [[01. Genesis/GEN_07_REBUILT|Genesis 7:4]] — forty days of flood; the world tested and judged
- **Exodus 24:18; 34:28** — Moses forty days on Sinai; the Law received
- [[04. Numbers/NUM_13_REBUILT|Numbers 13:25]] — forty days searching the land; faith tested
- [[04. Numbers/NUM_14_REBUILT|Numbers 14:34]] — forty days → forty years; the consequence of failing the test
- [[11. 1 Kings/1_KINGS_19_REBUILT|1 Kings 19:8]] — Elijah forty days to Horeb; the prophet tested
- [[40. Matthew/MATTHEW_04_REBUILT|Matthew 4:2]] — Jesus forty days in the wilderness; tested by the devil

**What the Bible establishes through the forty pattern:** Forty is the duration of a testing-unto-qualification. Moses' forty days on Sinai = qualified to receive the Law. The spies' forty days = tested for faith and failed = forty years of consequence. Jesus' forty days = tested in the wilderness and passed = qualified to begin His ministry. The forty is always a trial; whether it produces faith or unbelief determines the outcome.
