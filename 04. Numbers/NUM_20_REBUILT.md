## CHAPTER SUMMARY

The congregation comes to the wilderness of Zin in the first month — the fortieth year. Miriam dies and is buried at Kadesh. Immediately the people strive against Moses for water: the same complaint as the wilderness of Rephidim (Exodus 17), nearly word for word. Moses and Aaron go to the Tabernacle door and fall on their faces; the glory of the LORD appears. God's command: take the rod, gather the congregation, and speak to the rock before their eyes — it will give water. Moses takes the rod. He addresses the congregation: "Hear now, ye rebels; must we fetch you water out of this rock?" He lifts his hand and strikes the rock twice. Water flows abundantly. God's verdict: "Because ye believed me not, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel, therefore ye shall not bring this congregation into the land." This place is called Meribah (striving). Moses sends to the king of Edom — a detailed appeal: we are your brother Israel; we have suffered in Egypt; the LORD brought us out; let us pass through your land on the king's highway, touching nothing. Edom says no; threatens to come out with the sword. Israel offers to pay for any water — Edom still refuses, comes out in force. Israel turns away from them. They come to Mount Hor at the edge of Edom. God tells Moses: Aaron is to be gathered to his people; he shall not enter the land because of the Meribah rebellion. Take Aaron and Eleazar up the mountain; strip Aaron of his garments and put them on Eleazar. Aaron dies on the top of Mount Hor. Moses and Eleazar come down. All Israel mourns Aaron thirty days.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture) (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Moses Strikes the Rock — The One Act That Bars Him from the Land

**Numbers 20:11-12** — Moses strikes the rock twice; God's verdict: "ye believed me not, to sanctify me"

- **Exodus 17:6** — the first rock at Rephidim: "Behold, I will stand before thee there upon the rock in Horeb; and thou shalt smite it, and there shall come water out of it" — God explicitly commanded striking the first time
- **1 Corinthians 10:4** — "And did all drink the same spiritual drink: for they drank of that spiritual Rock that followed them: and that Rock was Christ" — the rock is Christ; Paul's statement means the type of Christ was struck once, providing water throughout the wilderness journey. The rock that was to be struck was already struck; now it was to be spoken to
- **Numbers 20:8** — God's command is "speak ye unto the rock" — not strike; the change of instruction is the theological point: the rock that was struck (the crucifixion, once for all) now gives water when spoken to (prayer and the Spirit)
- **Hebrews 6:6** — "seeing they crucify to themselves the Son of God afresh, and put him to an open shame" — striking the rock a second time is the typological statement of crucifying Christ again, which the New Testament explicitly condemns; Moses' double striking is the violation of the type
- **Hebrews 9:28** — "Christ was once offered to bear the sins of many" — once; not twice; the rock struck once, spoken to thereafter
- *Interpretation: Moses' sin is not merely anger or impatience — it is a typological violation. God told him to speak because the type had already been enacted (the first striking). To strike again was to misrepresent the once-for-all nature of Christ's death. "Ye believed me not, to sanctify me" — Moses' striking implied that speaking was insufficient, that God's word alone would not produce the water. It was an act of public unbelief that misrepresented the character of God to the congregation.*

### Edom Refuses Israel — Brother Against Brother

**Numbers 20:14-21** — Edom (descendants of Esau/Edom) refuses passage to Israel (descendants of Jacob/Israel)

- **Genesis 25:24-26** — Jacob and Esau: twin brothers from birth; Jacob supplants Esau; the sibling rivalry begins in the womb
- **Genesis 36:1** — "Esau, who is Edom" — Edom is the national name of Esau's descendants
- **Numbers 20:14** — Moses' appeal: "Thus saith thy brother Israel" — the kinship appeal; we are brothers
- **Obadiah 10-12** — God's indictment of Edom: "For thy violence against thy brother Jacob shame shall cover thee... thou shouldest not have looked on the day of thy brother in the day that he became a stranger... thou shouldest not have rejoiced over the children of Judah in the day of their destruction" — Edom's refusal at Numbers 20 is part of the long pattern of Edomite hostility toward Jacob that culminates in Obadiah's prophecy
- **Malachi 1:2-3** — "Was not Esau Jacob's brother? saith the LORD: yet I loved Jacob, And I hated Esau" — cited in Romans 9:13 as the statement of God's sovereign election
- *Interpretation: Edom's refusal to let Israel pass is not merely a geopolitical decision — it is the continuation of the Esau/Jacob conflict from the womb. The brother-nation that should have shown kinship mercy instead threatens with the sword. Obadiah pronounces judgment on this specific posture. The nations that stand against God's covenant people in the posture of Edom will face the Obadiah verdict.*

### Aaron's Death — The Priestly Garments Transferred

**Numbers 20:25-28** — Aaron stripped of his garments; Eleazar clothed; Aaron dies on the mount

- **Exodus 28:1-3** — Aaron's garments made for glory and for beauty; consecrated as the high priestly garments
- **Hebrews 7:23-24** — "And they truly were many priests, because they were not suffered to continue by reason of death: But this man, because he continueth ever, hath an unchangeable priesthood" — Aaron's death and garment transfer is the specific limitation Hebrews 7 addresses; the Aaronic priesthood is interrupted by death at every generation; Christ's priesthood is indestructible
- **Psalm 133:2** — "It is like the precious ointment upon the head, that ran down upon the beard, even Aaron's beard: that went down to the skirts of his garments" — Aaron's garments as the image of unity and blessing; the garments he wore were inseparable from his identity as priest
- *Interpretation: The transfer of the high priestly garments from Aaron to Eleazar on the summit of Mount Hor — in the sight of all Israel — is the public ceremony of the priesthood's continuity through death. Aaron does not die in office; he is formally transferred. The Aaronic priesthood does not end with Aaron's death — it passes to Eleazar. But it will eventually end, because every priest dies. Hebrews 7 is the theological consequence: the only priesthood that does not end is the one held by the one who does not die.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### SANCTIFY ME (Hebrew: qadash — to set apart, to declare holy)
- **Numbers 20:12** — "Because ye believed me not, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel"
- *Definition here: To sanctify God before the people is to demonstrate His character accurately — His holiness, His power, His faithfulness. Moses' striking of the rock failed to sanctify God because it implied that God's spoken word was insufficient, that only force would produce the result. The congregation watched; what they saw was Moses' anger producing water, not God's word being obeyed. The moment was a public misrepresentation. Leviticus 10:3 — "I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the people I will be glorified" — the same principle stated after Nadab and Abihu's death. Those who come nearest to God bear the highest responsibility for representing Him accurately.*

### MERIBAH (Hebrew: meribah — strife, contention)
- **Numbers 20:13** — "This is the water of Meribah; because the children of Israel strove with the LORD"
- *Definition here: Meribah means the place of striving/quarreling. Exodus 17:7 names the first rock-incident Massah-Meribah (testing and striving). Numbers 20:13 names this second incident Meribah. Psalm 95:8 — "Harden not your heart, as in the provocation, as in the day of temptation in the wilderness" — cited in Hebrews 3:8 as the defining warning about Israel's rebellion. Psalm 106:32-33 — "They angered him also at the waters of strife, so that it went ill with Moses for their sakes: Because they provoked his spirit, so that he spake unadvisedly with his lips" — the psalm's explanation of Numbers 20:10-12; Moses' outburst was provoked by Israel's rebellion, but the provocation does not remove the accountability.*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Rock Struck Once, Spoken to Thereafter — Christ's Once-for-All Sacrifice

The two rock incidents in the Pentateuch together form a single progressive revelation:

**First rock** (Exodus 17:6) — God commands: strike the rock. Water flows. Type: the crucifixion — the once-for-all blow.

**Second rock** (Numbers 20:8) — God commands: speak to the rock. Moses strikes instead. Type destroyed: the rock that has been struck must now be spoken to, not struck again.

- **Hebrews 10:10** — "By the which will we are sanctified through the offering of the body of Jesus Christ once for all"
- **1 Peter 3:18** — "For Christ also hath once suffered for sins, the just for the unjust"
- **Hebrews 7:27** — "who needeth not daily, as those high priests, to offer up sacrifice, first for his own sins, and then for the people's: for this he did once, when he offered up himself"

The rock struck once → water that sustained Israel for forty years → spoken to ever after → perpetual provision through the once-completed sacrifice. The New Covenant relationship with Christ is not a repeated sacrifice; it is ongoing speech — prayer, the Word, the Spirit — flowing from the one who was struck once.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES PASSAGES

### Numbers 20:12 — "Ye Believed Me Not" — The Nature of Moses' Sin Debated

**The observation:** Moses' exact sin at Meribah has been extensively debated — was it anger? Was it taking credit ("must WE fetch water")? Was it striking instead of speaking? Was it striking twice?

**What the text says:** God's own statement is the definitive answer: "Because ye believed me not, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel." Two components: (1) unbelief — they did not trust that God's command to speak would be sufficient; (2) failure to sanctify — the act did not glorify God accurately before the people. Both components are in the text. The striking was the expression of the unbelief; the unbelief was the root.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Psalm 106:32-33** — "They angered him also at the waters of strife, so that it went ill with Moses for their sakes: Because they provoked his spirit, so that he spake unadvisedly with his lips" — the Psalm identifies the vehicle: unadvisedly with his lips (the "ye rebels" address)
- **Deuteronomy 1:37; 3:26** — Moses himself says "the LORD was angry with me for your sakes" — he connects his exclusion to the people's provocation; yet he does not dispute the justice of God's verdict

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Mountain Deaths — Moses, Aaron, Miriam — The Wilderness Generation Passes

Numbers 20 records the deaths of two of the three leaders of the Exodus: Miriam at Kadesh (v.1), Aaron on Mount Hor (v.28). Moses will die on Mount Nebo (Deuteronomy 34). All three die in the wilderness without entering the land. The original leadership is being stripped away so that only the new generation enters Canaan under new leadership — Joshua.

- **Deuteronomy 34:5-8** — Moses dies on Mount Nebo; thirty days mourning — same as Aaron
- **Matthew 17:3** — at the Transfiguration, Moses and Elijah appear with Jesus: the two who died without entering the earthly land appear on the mountain of transformation, having entered the heavenly reality
- **Hebrews 11:13** — "These all died in faith, not having received the promises, but having seen them afar off" — Moses and Aaron are among those who died in faith; their deaths in the wilderness are not defeats but acts of faith in the promise they did not receive in their own lifetime

**What the Bible establishes:** The three wilderness leaders — Miriam, Aaron, Moses — all die before the crossing of Jordan. The Jordan crossing is reserved for the new generation under Joshua (Jesus). The old creation leadership does not enter the rest; the new creation leader brings his people through. This is not their failure — it is the structure of the type. Moses and Aaron were never meant to be what Joshua was; they were the scaffold, not the building.
