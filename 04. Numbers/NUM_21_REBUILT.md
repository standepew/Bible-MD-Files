## CHAPTER SUMMARY

King Arad of Canaan fights Israel and takes prisoners. Israel vows a total ban — if God delivers Canaan, they will utterly destroy the cities. God hearkens; Hormah (destruction) is named. Then the long detour around Edom discourages the people; they speak against God and Moses: no bread, no water, we loathe the manna. God sends fiery serpents; many die. The people confess and ask Moses to pray. God's remedy: make a bronze serpent, set it on a pole — every bitten person who looks lives. The march continues with place-names: Oboth, Ije-abarim, valley of Zared, the Arnon. At Beer God promises water — instead of striving, Israel sings: "Spring up, O well; sing ye unto it: The princes digged the well, the nobles of the people digged it." From Beer to Mattanah, Nahaliel, Bamoth, Pisgah. Then Israel sends to Sihon king of the Amorites for passage — same request as to Edom — Sihon refuses and marches out against Israel. Israel destroys Sihon at Jahaz and occupies all his land including Heshbon. The ancient poetic taunt-song of Heshbon is cited. Then Og king of Bashan comes against Israel at Edrei. God tells Moses: "Fear him not: for I have delivered him into thy hand." Israel destroys Og and all his people and possesses his land.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture) (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Bronze Serpent — Look and Live

**Numbers 21:8-9** — the serpent on the pole; every bitten person who looks lives

- **John 3:14-15** — "And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, even so must the Son of man be lifted up: That whosoever believeth in him should not perish, but have eternal life" — Jesus Himself identifies the bronze serpent as the type of His crucifixion; this is the most explicit type-to-antitype connection in the Gospels made by Christ's own mouth
- **John 12:32-33** — "And I, if I be lifted up from the earth, will draw all men unto me. This he said, signifying what death he should die" — the lifting up = crucifixion; the same lifting-up as Numbers 21:8
- **2 Kings 18:4** — Hezekiah destroys the bronze serpent (Nehushtan) when Israel begins burning incense to it — the type was never meant to be worshipped; when the symbol displaces the substance it must be destroyed
- *Interpretation: The structure of the Numbers 21 remedy is the structure of the gospel: (1) the people are dying of a bite they cannot cure; (2) God provides a remedy that looks offensive (a serpent image = the very thing biting them); (3) the remedy requires only looking, not effort or merit; (4) everyone who looks lives; everyone who does not look dies. John 3:14-15 is not a creative reinterpretation — it is the intended meaning. The serpent on the pole is the one who became sin for us (2 Corinthians 5:21) so that looking to Him produces life.*

### "Our Soul Loatheth This Light Bread" — The Second Rejection of Manna

**Numbers 21:5** — the people loathe the manna, again; this time they are bitten

- **Numbers 11:6** — the first rejection of manna: "our soul is dried away; there is nothing at all, beside this manna" — same complaint; different judgment (quail until it came out their nostrils)
- **1 Corinthians 10:9** — "Neither let us tempt Christ, as some of them also tempted, and were destroyed of serpents" — Paul explicitly cites Numbers 21 as a warning for the New Covenant community: tempting Christ by despising His provision
- *Interpretation: "Light bread" (qeloqel — contemptible, worthless bread) is what the people call the bread from heaven. The manna they describe as worthless is, by Psalm 78:25, "angels' food" and by John 6:35 the type of Christ Himself — the bread of life. To call the bread of heaven loathsome is to call Christ loathsome. The serpents are the consequence of that assessment.*

---

### The Song of the Well — Singing Over a Gift

**Numbers 21:17** — "Then Israel sang this song, Spring up, O well; sing ye unto it"

The contrast with every previous water narrative is striking: at Marah, they murmured (Exodus 15:24); at Rephidim, they strove (Exodus 17:2); at Meribah, they strove again (Numbers 20:3). Here, for the only time in the wilderness, Israel sings over water — because they received it as a gift that God promised, not something they demanded.

- **Psalm 42:1-2** — "As the hart panteth after the water brooks, so panteth my soul after thee, O God: My soul thirsteth for God, for the living God" — the water of Beer becomes, in the Psalms, the image of holy thirst for God
- **Isaiah 12:3** — "Therefore with joy shall ye draw water out of the wells of salvation" — the eschatological singing at the well; the Beer song as its prototype
- **John 4:14** — "the water that I shall give him shall be in him a well of water springing up into everlasting life" — Jesus promises a well that springs up from within — the ultimate Beer ("well") where the people sing

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### FIERY SERPENTS (Hebrew: nachashim seraphim — burning/fiery serpents)
- **Numbers 21:6** — "the LORD sent fiery serpents"
- *Definition here: Seraphim — the same word as the seraphim of Isaiah 6:2 ("above it stood the seraphim"). The fiery serpents of Numbers 21 are called seraphim, and they burn — presumably with venom that produces burning sensation. The word seraph (to burn) applied both to the deadly serpents of judgment and to the heavenly burning beings around the throne. The bronze serpent on the pole — a seraph shape, elevated — anticipates the seraphic presence at the throne and the lifting up of the one who would take the burning judgment of sin.*

### BAN / HEREM (Hebrew: cherem — devoted to destruction, utterly banned)
- **Numbers 21:2-3** — "I will utterly destroy their cities" — Israel vows the cherem
- *Definition here: The cherem is total consecration to God through destruction — nothing of the enemy is kept; all is given to God, usually through fire. It is the covenant warfare principle: what God gives to Israel to destroy is returned to Him as an offering by destruction. Achan's sin (Joshua 7) is precisely violating the cherem — taking what was devoted to God. The Hormah naming (cherem → Hormah, place of destruction) marks the first full implementation of covenant warfare in Numbers.*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Bronze Serpent Destroyed by Hezekiah — The Danger of the Type Outliving Its Purpose

**Numbers 21:9** — Moses makes a bronze serpent as God commands
**2 Kings 18:4** — Hezekiah "brake in pieces the brasen serpent that Moses had made: for unto those days the children of Israel did burn incense to it: and he called it Nehushtan" (a piece of bronze)

The bronze serpent that God ordained as a means of healing became, centuries later, an idol. Hezekiah names it contemptuously: Nehushtan — mere bronze. The instrument of divine healing had become an object of false worship. Hezekiah's destruction of it is praised: "he did that which was right in the sight of the LORD" (2 Kings 18:3).

**What the Bible establishes:** Every God-ordained type must eventually give way to its fulfilment. The bronze serpent was meant to point forward; worshipping it is to mistake the signpost for the destination. John 3:14-15 is the completion that renders the bronze serpent obsolete — Christ has been lifted up; the Nehushtan is no longer needed. This principle applies to every shadow: the Sabbath points to rest in Christ (Hebrews 4); the Temple points to Christ's body (John 2:19-21); the sacrificial system points to His once-for-all offering. When the reality arrives, clinging to the shadow is idolatry.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES PASSAGES

### Numbers 21:8 — "Make Thee a Fiery Serpent" — God Commands Making an Image

**The observation:** God commands Moses to make a representation of the very thing causing death — a serpent image. This appears to contradict Exodus 20:4 ("thou shalt not make unto thee any graven image... of any thing... in the earth beneath").

**What the text establishes:** The bronze serpent is not made for worship — it is made for looking at. The distinction the Torah draws is not between representation and no-representation but between making something to worship it and making something as a divinely-commanded instrument. The Tabernacle is full of crafted images: cherubim on the ark, woven into the veil, embroidered on the curtains. These are not idolatry because they are commanded by God for a specific purpose. The sin of idolatry is worshipping what is made; the sin proved by 2 Kings 18:4 is Israel eventually doing exactly that.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### "As Moses Lifted Up the Serpent" — The Lifting-Up Thread

The lifting-up of the bronze serpent establishes a typological pattern that John's Gospel develops explicitly:

- [[04. Numbers/NUM_21_REBUILT|Numbers 21:8-9]] — serpent lifted on a pole; those who look live
- [[43. John/JOHN_03_REBUILT|John 3:14]] — "as Moses lifted up the serpent... so must the Son of man be lifted up"
- [[43. John/JOHN_08_REBUILT|John 8:28]] — "When ye have lifted up the Son of man, then shall ye know that I am he"
- [[43. John/JOHN_12_REBUILT|John 12:32]] — "And I, if I be lifted up from the earth, will draw all men unto me"

Three times in John's Gospel Jesus speaks of being "lifted up" — each time referring to the crucifixion, each time echoing Numbers 21. The cross is the pole; the crucified one is the serpent-image of sin (2 Corinthians 5:21 — "made him to be sin for us"); the remedy is looking — faith. The wilderness generation that looked at bronze lived physically for a season; those who look to the crucified Christ live eternally.
