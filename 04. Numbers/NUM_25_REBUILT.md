## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Israel camps at Shittim. The people begin committing whoredom with Moabite women. The women invite Israel to their sacrifices; Israel eats and bows to their gods. Israel joins himself to Baal-Peor; the LORD's anger burns. God commands Moses: hang the heads of the people before the LORD against the sun to turn away His fierce anger. Moses commands the judges to slay every man joined to Baal-Peor. Then, in full public view — before the entire weeping congregation at the Tabernacle door — one Israelite named Zimri, prince of a Simeonite house, brings a Midianite woman named Cozbi, daughter of a Midianite chief, into the camp. Phinehas, son of Eleazar son of Aaron, sees it. He rises from the congregation, takes a javelin, goes into the tent after them, and thrusts both through. The plague is stayed. 24,000 have died. God speaks to Moses: Phinehas has turned My wrath from the children of Israel while he was zealous for My sake — he made atonement for the children of Israel. Therefore I give him My **covenant of peace** and his seed after him — the **covenant of an everlasting priesthood** — because he was zealous for his God. The names: the Israelite was Zimri son of Salu, a prince of Simeon; the Midianite woman was Cozbi daughter of Zur, a head of a Midianite house. God commands: vex the Midianites and smite them — for they beguiled Israel in the matter of Peor and in the matter of Cozbi.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture) (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The Doctrine of Balaam — What the Curse Could Not Do, the Counsel Did

**Numbers 25:1-3** — Balaam's curse failed four times; the seduction through Moabite women succeeds.

- **Numbers 31:16** — Moses identifies the source: "Behold, these caused the children of Israel, through the counsel of Balaam, to commit trespass against the LORD in the matter of Peor" — it was Balaam's counsel to Balak; he advised that Israel could only be defeated by internal corruption
- **Revelation 2:14** — "thou hast there them that hold the doctrine of Balaam, who taught Balac to cast a stumblingblock before the children of Israel, to eat things sacrificed unto idols, and to commit fornication" — Jesus to the church at Pergamos; the doctrine of Balaam is the strategy of introducing sexual immorality and idol-food into the covenant community to accomplish from within what could not be accomplished from without
- *Interpretation: Numbers 25 is the direct consequence of Numbers 22-24. The curse strategy failed; Balaam's alternative counsel succeeded. This is one of the most important strategic lessons in Scripture: the enemy who cannot defeat God's people through direct spiritual opposition may succeed through inducing them to defeat themselves through compromise with the surrounding culture. The doctrine of Balaam is not a curse — it is a seduction.*

### Phinehas and the Atonement

**Numbers 25:13** — "he was zealous for his God, and made an atonement for the children of Israel"

- **Numbers 16:46-48** — Aaron running with the censer stands between the dead and the living; the plague stays. Here Phinehas runs with a javelin; the plague stays. Both are acts of atonement — the plague arrested by a priestly action.
- **Psalm 106:30-31** — "Then stood up Phinehas, and executed judgment: and so the plague was stayed. And that was counted unto him for righteousness unto all generations for evermore" — the same language as Genesis 15:6 (Abraham's faith counted for righteousness); Phinehas's zeal is counted as righteousness across all generations
- **Romans 4:3** — the faith-counted-as-righteousness formula; the same accounting principle applied to Abraham, applied to Phinehas
- *Interpretation: Phinehas's act is called atonement (kipper — to cover, to make propitiation). He did not offer a sacrifice — he executed judgment. The plague was stayed not by a ritual act but by a righteous act of zeal against the specific sin causing the plague. This establishes that judgment against sin can function as atonement — covering the community by removing the defilement. It prefigures the divine act by which God's own judgment against sin, executed on Christ, becomes the atonement for the community.*

### The Covenant of Peace — The Everlasting Priesthood

**Numbers 25:12-13** — Phinehas receives the covenant of peace (shalom) and the everlasting priesthood

- **Malachi 2:4-5** — "And ye shall know that I have sent this commandment unto you, that my covenant might be with Levi, saith the LORD of hosts. My covenant was with him of life and peace; and I gave them to him for the fear wherewith he feared me, and was afraid before my name" — the covenant of peace of Numbers 25 is recalled in Malachi as the foundational Levitical covenant; it is the reference point for what a faithful priest looks like
- **Hebrews 7:24** — "But this man, because he continueth ever, hath an unchangeable priesthood" — Christ's priesthood is the ultimate everlasting priesthood; Phinehas's covenant of everlasting priesthood points to the one whose priesthood is permanent by the power of indestructible life
- **Isaiah 54:10** — "my covenant of peace shall not be removed" — the same phrase; the shalom-covenant is permanent

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### JOINED TO BAAL-PEOR (Hebrew: tsamad — yoked to, attached, bound to)
- **Numbers 25:3** — "Israel joined himself unto Baal-peor"
- *Definition here: Tsamad is not casual association — it is the yoke-word, the attachment of one thing to another. Israel became yoked to Baal-Peor. Hosea 9:10 — "they went to Baal-peor, and separated themselves unto that shame; and their abominations were according as they loved" — the attachment is characterised in Hosea as a love-attachment, a deep covenantal yoking to an idol. 2 Corinthians 6:14 — "Be ye not unequally yoked together with unbelievers" — the New Covenant prohibition uses the same imagery.*

### ZEALOUS (Hebrew: qanna / qin'ah — jealous zeal, burning passion)
- **Numbers 25:11** — "while he was zealous for my sake"
- *Definition here: The same root as God's own jealousy — "I the LORD thy God am a jealous God" (Exodus 20:5). Phinehas's zeal is the human reflection of the divine jealousy — a consuming passion for God's honour that cannot coexist with the violation of the covenant. John 2:17 — "The zeal of thine house hath eaten me up" — Christ's zeal for the Temple; the same qin'ah enacted in the cleansing of the Temple.*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Phinehas as Type of Christ's Zeal

Aaron ran with incense and stood between the dead and the living (Numbers 16:48). Phinehas ran with a javelin and thrust through the source of the plague. Both are priestly acts of atonement; both stay a plague; both are types of Christ.

- The incense of Aaron = Christ's intercession absorbing judgment
- The javelin of Phinehas = Christ's righteous execution of judgment against sin, once for all, in His own body

**1 Peter 2:24** — "Who his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree" — the one in whom judgment and intercession converge: He bore the sin (Aaron's incense posture) and executed judgment against it in His own person (Phinehas's javelin posture). The atonement is both.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES PASSAGES

### Numbers 25:7-8 — Phinehas Used to Justify Vigilante Violence in God's Name

**The misuse:** The Phinehas story has been cited to justify extrajudicial killings of those perceived as spiritually corrupting the community — "Phinehas priests" in extremist religious literature.

**What the text establishes:** The context is a national emergency — a plague is killing people in real time; Moses and the judges have already been commanded to act (v.4-5); the execution of the stated command is already underway. Phinehas acts in that specific context, in continuity with the commanded judgment already in process. His act is not private vengeance; it is the priestly extension of a publicly declared judicial proceeding.

**The correction from within Scripture:**
- **Romans 12:19** — "Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord" — private vengeance is prohibited
- **Romans 13:4** — legitimate civil authority bears the sword; individuals do not deputise themselves
- The Phinehas principle in the New Covenant is applied to internal discipline of the community (1 Corinthians 5:1-5, 11-13) through proper community authority, not individual violence

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Weeping Congregation at the Tabernacle Door

[[04. Numbers/NUM_25_REBUILT|Numbers 25:6]] — "the congregation of the children of Israel, who were weeping before the door of the tabernacle of the congregation"

The congregation is weeping — presumably in intercession, in grief, in response to the plague and the commanded executions. Into this scene of communal prayer and mourning, Zimri walks brazenly past with Cozbi. The brazenness is the point: the open, public defiance of the covenant in the very moment of corporate repentance.

- [[29. Joel/JOEL_02_REBUILT|Joel 2:17]] — "Let the priests, the ministers of the LORD, weep between the porch and the altar, and let them say, Spare thy people, O LORD" — the priestly weeping between the Tabernacle entrance and the altar; the intercession posture
- [[04. Numbers/NUM_25_REBUILT|Numbers 25:6-8]] — the congregation weeps at the door; Zimri walks through; Phinehas rises from among the congregation — the priest who is in the posture of intercession is also the one who rises to execute zeal

**What the Bible establishes:** Priestly intercession and priestly zeal are not opposites — they are two aspects of the same office. The priest who weeps for the people must also be willing to act for the people when their corruption threatens the entire community. The sequence — weeping, then rising, then acting — is the Phinehas model.
