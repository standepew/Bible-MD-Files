## CHAPTER SUMMARY

God speaks to Moses: command Israel to observe the offering calendar — my offering, my bread for my sacrifices made by fire, in their due season. The daily offering (tamid): two unblemished yearling lambs — one in the morning, one at evening — with a tenth ephah of flour mixed with beaten oil and a quarter hin of wine poured in the holy place. This was ordained at Sinai; it is perpetual. The Sabbath doubles the daily: two additional lambs with doubled flour, added to the continual offering. The New Moon adds: two young bulls, one ram, seven yearling lambs, with proportional flour and oil; one goat for sin offering — all beside the continual burnt offering. The Passover is the fourteenth of the first month. The Feast of Unleavened Bread opens on the fifteenth — seven days of eating unleavened bread; the first day is a holy convocation with no servile work; a burnt offering of two bulls, one ram, seven lambs, with flour and oil; one goat for atonement — beside the morning continual offering. This same offering daily for seven days; the seventh day is another holy convocation. Then the Feast of Firstfruits (Weeks/Pentecost): when the new grain offering is brought after your weeks are complete — a holy convocation with no servile work; two bulls, one ram, seven yearling lambs; proportional flour and oil; one goat for atonement — all unblemished, all beside the continual offering.

- *Interpretation: The language of "my bread" in Numbers 28:2 establishes that the sacrificial system is, from God's perspective, a meal — a table where He receives. The priests are the stewards of His table. Every unblemished animal, every measure of flour and oil and wine, is provision for divine communion. Christ as the bread of life is the living fulfilment of every grain offering brought to God's table.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### CONTINUAL BURNT OFFERING / TAMID (Hebrew: olah tamid — ascending offering, continuous/perpetual)
- **Numbers 28:3, 6, 10, 15, 23, 24, 31** — "beside the continual burnt offering" — repeated phrase throughout the chapter
- *Definition here: Tamid means unbroken, perpetual, without interruption. The olah (burnt offering — literally "ascending") that goes up morning and evening is the anchor around which every other offering is organized. Every festival addition is "beside the continual" — the base offering is never replaced, only supplemented. This is the theological architecture: there is an unceasing offering to God around which all other worship is structured. The removal of the tamid (Daniel 8:11) is the worst possible religious catastrophe.*

### SWEET SAVOUR (Hebrew: reach nichoach — scent of rest/satisfaction)
- **Numbers 28:2, 6, 8, 13, 24, 27** — "a sweet savour unto the LORD"
- *Definition here: The phrase means literally the scent of God's settling down, His satisfaction — the smell of the burning sacrifice that causes God to "rest" or be "pleased." Genesis 8:21 — "the LORD smelled a sweet savour" after Noah's offering and promised never to destroy the earth again. Ephesians 5:2 — "Christ also hath loved us, and hath given himself for us an offering and a sacrifice to God for a sweetsmelling savour" — Paul applies the exact terminology of Numbers 28 to Christ's self-offering. The fragrance that satisfies God is ultimately Christ's offering.*

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Morning and Evening — The Bookends of Every Day

Two lambs daily — one at dawn, one at dusk — frame every day of Israel's existence with sacrifice. No day begins without a lamb ascending; no day ends without one. This is not convenience worship; it is total-day consecration.

- **Psalm 92:1-2** — "It is a good thing to give thanks unto the LORD, and to sing praises unto thy name, O most High: To shew forth thy lovingkindness in the morning, and thy faithfulness every night" — the Psalm for the Sabbath Day reflects the tamid rhythm in prayer
- **Daniel 6:10** — Daniel prays three times a day — the tamid rhythm extended to three; morning, noon, and evening intercession
- **Acts 3:1** — Peter and John going to the Temple "at the hour of prayer, being the ninth hour" — the afternoon tamid time; the apostles still ordering their day around the offering rhythm even after the cross
- *Interpretation: The tamid establishes that worship is not an event but a structure — the architecture of every day, not an occasional addition to it. The morning and evening frames are the Old Covenant form of "pray without ceasing."*

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES PASSAGES

### Numbers 28:2 — "My Bread" Used to Argue That God Literally Eats

**The misuse:** The phrase "my bread" is occasionally read in a crude sense — as if God were literally hungry and needed feeding.

**What the text establishes:** The offering is called God's bread because it is given to Him as a meal is given to an honoured guest — the language of provision and table-fellowship, not of divine hunger. Psalm 50:12-13 — "If I were hungry, I would not tell thee: for the world is mine, and the fulness thereof. Will I eat the flesh of bulls, or drink the blood of goats?" — God explicitly denies needing food. The "bread" language is covenantal and relational, not metabolic. The Tabernacle is God's dwelling; the offerings are the household provision that expresses the relationship, not its nutritional content.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Beside-the-Continual Thread — All Worship Builds on the Foundation

Seven times in this chapter (verses 10, 15, 23, 24, 31) the phrase "beside the continual burnt offering" (or equivalent) appears. Every addition — Sabbath, New Moon, Passover, Firstfruits — is beside, not instead of, the tamid.

**What the Bible establishes:** The architectural principle of worship is that special seasons supplement but never supplant constant devotion. The festivals are the high points; the tamid is the foundation. A church that is fervently alive at Christmas and Easter but has no daily, weekly rhythm of prayer and Word is structurally deficient in exactly the way Numbers 28 warns against — the special additions are present, but the continual is missing.

- **Luke 18:1** — "men ought always to pray, and not to faint" — the tamid principle in New Covenant form
- **1 Thessalonians 5:17** — "Pray without ceasing" — three words that are the New Testament tamid
