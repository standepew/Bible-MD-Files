## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Israel turns to Bashan and defeats Og, the last king of the Rephaim (giants), whose iron bedstead measured nine cubits long. Sixty fenced cities and many unwalled towns fall to Israel — not one too strong for the LORD. Moses distributes the territory east of Jordan: the plain and Gilead to Reuben and Gad, and all Bashan (the kingdom of Og) to the half-tribe of Manasseh. He then commands the eastern tribes to cross armed before their brethren until all Israel possesses the western territory. Moses charges Joshua to see in these two victories what the LORD will do to all kingdoms across Jordan — "the LORD your God he shall fight for you." Moses then recounts his prayer for the privilege of crossing Jordan, and God's firm refusal: the LORD was wroth for Israel's sake. Moses is commanded to view the land from the top of Pisgah but told clearly he shall not cross Jordan. He is to encourage and strengthen Joshua instead.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Og — "The Remnant of Giants" — Last of the Rephaim

**Deuteronomy 3:11** — "For only Og king of Bashan remained of the remnant of giants; behold, his bedstead was a bedstead of iron; is it not in Rabbath of the children of Ammon? nine cubits was the length thereof, and four cubits the breadth of it, after the cubit of a man."

- **Numbers 21:33-35** — The original account of Og's defeat: "And the LORD said unto Moses, Fear him not: for I have delivered him into thy hand, and all his people, and his land"
- **Joshua 12:4-5** — Og is listed among the defeated kings: "the coast of Og king of Bashan, which was of the remnant of the giants"
- **Joshua 13:12** — "all the kingdom of Og in Bashan, which reigned in Ashtaroth and in Edrei, who remained of the remnant of the giants"
- **Amos 2:9** — "Yet destroyed I the Amorite before them, whose height was like the height of the cedars, and he was strong as the oaks"
- **1 Samuel 17:4** — Goliath of Gath: "whose height was six cubits and a span" — a later descendant of the same giant line
  - *Interpretation:* The iron bedstead of Og (nine cubits ≈ 13.5 feet at 18 inches per cubit) is preserved in Rabbath of Ammon as a historical artifact attesting to his size. This is not myth; Moses states it is still there in his day. Og is identified as the last of the Rephaim (the giant race). His defeat continues the pattern of God systematically removing the giant seed from the land, culminating eventually in David's killing of Goliath and the subsequent defeats of giant descendants in 2 Samuel 21:15-22.

### Sixty Cities of Argob — Total Victory Recorded

**Deuteronomy 3:4-5** — "All these cities were fenced with high walls, gates, and bars; beside unwalled towns a great many."

- **1 Kings 4:13** — "The son of Geber, in Ramoth-gilead; to him pertained the towns of Jair the son of Manasseh... threescore great cities with walls and brasen bars" — Solomon's administrative record confirms the sixty cities of Bashan still intact
- **Joshua 13:30** — Confirms "all the kingdom of Og in Bashan... threescore cities, all the region of Argob" — Joshua's record matches Moses' account
  - *Interpretation:* The specific count of sixty cities is not hyperbole. It is confirmed by later administrative records in Joshua and Kings. Deuteronomy's historical claims are verifiable within the Bible itself. The "high walls, gates, and bars" that made these cities formidable to human armies were nothing before the LORD — "there was not a city which we took not from them."

### Eastern Tribes' Obligation to Cross Armed Before Their Brethren

**Deuteronomy 3:18-20** — "ye shall pass over armed before your brethren the children of Israel... Until the LORD have given rest unto your brethren, as well as unto you, and until they also possess the land"

- **Numbers 32:16-32** — The original negotiation where Reuben and Gad ask for the east Jordan land; Moses conditionally grants it if they fight alongside Israel in Canaan
- **Joshua 1:12-15** — Joshua repeats the condition to the eastern tribes before the Jordan crossing; they affirm their commitment: "All that thou commandest us we will do"
- **Joshua 22:1-4** — After the conquest is complete, Joshua releases the eastern tribes to return home with blessing: "the LORD your God hath given rest unto your brethren, as he promised them"
  - *Interpretation:* The obligation of the eastern tribes to cross armed before the rest of Israel is a covenant commitment — they received their land first, but they must fight for their brothers' land before enjoying their own rest fully. This pattern embeds a principle: those who receive God's blessing early bear a responsibility to help secure the blessing for others. "Until they also possess the land" — received grace creates obligation to serve.

### "What God Is There in Heaven or in Earth, That Can Do According to Thy Works?"

**Deuteronomy 3:24** — "O Lord GOD, thou hast begun to shew thy servant thy greatness, and thy mighty hand: for what God is there in heaven or in earth, that can do according to thy works, and according to thy might?"

- **Exodus 15:11** — "Who is like unto thee, O LORD, among the gods? who is like thee, glorious in holiness, fearful in praises, doing wonders?" — Moses' song immediately after the Red Sea
- **2 Samuel 7:22** — "Wherefore thou art great, O LORD God: for there is none like thee, neither is there any God beside thee, according to all that we have heard with our ears."
- **Isaiah 40:18** — "To whom then will ye liken God? or what likeness will ye compare unto him?"
- **Isaiah 46:9** — "I am God, and there is none else; I am God, and there is none like me"
- **Jeremiah 32:17** — "Ah Lord GOD! behold, thou hast made the heaven and the earth by thy great power and stretched out arm, and there is nothing too hard for thee"
  - *Interpretation:* Moses' rhetorical question is the great declaration of God's uniqueness that runs through the whole Bible. No other god can do what the LORD does. The question "what God is there?" expects and demands the answer "none." This is not merely comparative theology; it is the foundational confession of Scripture: the God of Israel is without rival. The mighty acts Moses has just recounted — Sihon's defeat, Og's defeat — are the evidence base for the confession.

### Moses Denied Entry — Bearing the People's Consequence

**Deuteronomy 3:26-27** — "But the LORD was wroth with me for your sakes, and would not hear me: and the LORD said unto me, Let it suffice thee; speak no more unto me of this matter. Get thee up into the top of Pisgah, and lift up thine eyes westward, and northward, and southward, and eastward, and behold it with thine eyes: for thou shalt not go over this Jordan."

- **Numbers 20:10-12** — The original incident: Moses struck the rock twice at Meribah when God said "speak to the rock." God's verdict: "Because ye believed me not, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel, therefore ye shall not bring this congregation into the land which I have given them."
- **Deuteronomy 4:21** — Moses states again: "Furthermore the LORD was angry with me for your sakes, and sware that I should not go over Jordan"
- **Numbers 27:12-14** — God shows Moses the land from Mount Abarim and repeats the verdict with the same words as Numbers 20
- **Psalm 106:32-33** — "They angered him also at the waters of strife, so that it went ill with Moses for their sakes: Because they provoked his spirit, so that he spake unadvisedly with his lips."
  - *Interpretation:* Moses' exclusion from Canaan is one of Scripture's most sobering passages. His fault at Meribah (Numbers 20) — failing to sanctify God's specific word before the people — cost him the land. But Moses himself in Deuteronomy consistently attributes it to Israel's sake ("for your sakes" — not excusing himself, but connecting his fate to the people's persistent rebellion). The mediator bears the consequences of the people's sin in ways that exceed what seems proportional. This is a shadow of the greater Mediator who bore far more than He deserved for His people's sake.

### "Let It Suffice Thee" — God's Closed Doors

**Deuteronomy 3:26** — "the LORD said unto me, Let it suffice thee; speak no more unto me of this matter."

- **2 Corinthians 12:8-9** — Paul prayed three times for the thorn in the flesh to be removed; God's answer: "My grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness" — a gracious but firm closed door
- **Matthew 26:39** — Jesus in Gethsemane: "O my Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me: nevertheless not as I will, but as thou wilt" — the Son submits to a closed door for the greater purpose
  - *Interpretation:* "Let it suffice thee" is not cruelty — it is God's sovereign determination that a greater purpose is served by this closed door. Moses is given something in place of entry: he sees the land from Pisgah. God does not leave him empty — He gives Moses the vision even while closing the entrance. The pattern: when God closes a door, He often gives a vision. What is withheld in one form is granted in another.

### Joshua Commissioned — Encourage and Strengthen

**Deuteronomy 3:28** — "But charge Joshua, and encourage him, and strengthen him: for he shall go over before this people, and he shall cause them to inherit the land which thou shalt see."

- **Deuteronomy 31:7-8** — Moses charges Joshua publicly: "Be strong and of a good courage... the LORD he it is that doth go before thee; he will be with thee, he will not fail thee, neither forsake thee: fear not, neither be dismayed."
- **Joshua 1:1-9** — God Himself charges Joshua after Moses' death: "Be strong and of a good courage... Have not I commanded thee? Be strong and of a good courage; be not afraid, neither be thou dismayed: for the LORD thy God is with thee whithersoever thou goest."
- **1 Chronicles 22:13** — David charges Solomon the same way: "Be strong, and of good courage; dread not, nor be dismayed."
- **Isaiah 41:10** — "Fear thou not; for I am with thee: be not dismayed; for I am thy God: I will strengthen thee; yea, I will help thee"
  - *Interpretation:* The charge to "encourage and strengthen" Joshua runs from Moses to God Himself to the conquest. Courage in God's service is not natural boldness — it is a commanded state, grounded not in one's own strength but in the presence of the LORD who goes before. The three-fold charge — encourage, strengthen, fear not — becomes the standard commissioning language throughout Scripture for those entering impossible tasks.
