## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses concludes miscellaneous laws: (1) Judicial justice—judges must justify the righteous and condemn the wicked. If the wicked deserves beating, the judge administers it publicly, limited to forty stripes to prevent excessive punishment and the brother appearing vile. (2) Animal rights—don't muzzle the ox while it treads grain, allowing it to eat while working. (3) Levirate marriage—if brothers dwell together and one dies childless, the living brother must marry the widow and perform the duty of a husband's brother, ensuring the firstborn carries the dead brother's name. If the brother refuses, the widow goes to elders who confirm his refusal; she removes his sandal and spits in his face as public shame, and his household is called "the house of him whose sandal is removed." (4) Assaulting woman—if a wife intervenes in her husband's fight and seizes the attacker's genitals, her hand is cut off without pity. (5) Honest commerce—no dishonest weights or measures; use perfect and just weights and measures for long life in the land, as dishonesty is abominable to God. (6) Amalek's destruction—remember Amalek's cowardly attack on feeble Israelites at the rear; when God gives rest from enemies, Israel must blot out Amalek's memory from under heaven.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Forty stripes he may give him, and not exceed" — Punishment Limits

**Deuteronomy 25:1-3** — "If there be a controversy between men... then they shall justify the righteous, and condemn the wicked... Forty stripes he may give him, and not exceed: lest, if he should exceed, and beat him above these with many stripes, then thy brother should seem vile unto thee"

- **2 Corinthians 11:24** — "Of the Jews five times received I forty stripes save one."
  - *Interpretation:* Paul received 39 stripes from the Jews five times, showing they carefully observed this limitation to avoid exceeding the 40-stripe maximum.

- **Leviticus 19:35-36** — "Ye shall do no unrighteousness in judgment, in meteyard, in weight, or in measure. Just balances, just weights, a just ephah, and a just hin, shall ye have: I am the LORD your God, which brought you out of the land of Egypt."
  - *Interpretation:* Leviticus establishes the principle of just judgment and honest measures, which Moses extends to include punishment limits.

- **Proverbs 20:10** — "Divers weights, and divers measures, both of them are alike abomination to the LORD."
  - *Interpretation:* Wisdom literature affirms the principle of honesty in commerce, showing these moral principles transcended ceremonial law.

### "Thou shalt not muzzle the ox when he treadeth out the corn" — Animal Rights

**Deuteronomy 25:4** — "Thou shalt not muzzle the ox when he treadeth out the corn."

- **1 Corinthians 9:9-10** — "For it is written in the law of Moses, Thou shalt not muzzle the mouth of the ox that treadeth out the corn. Doth God take care for oxen? Or saith he it altogether for our sakes? For our sakes, no doubt, this is written: that he that ploweth should plow in hope; and that he that thresheth in hope should be partaker of his hope."
  - *Interpretation:* Paul applies this principle to ministerial support, arguing that just as working oxen may eat, ministers deserve material support from their labor.

- **1 Timothy 5:18** — "For the scripture saith, Thou shalt not muzzle the ox that treadeth out the corn. And, The labourer is worthy of his reward."
  - *Interpretation:* Paul combines this principle with Jesus' teaching that workers deserve their wages to establish the right of ministers to receive support.

- **Luke 10:7** — "And in the same house remain, eating and drinking such things as they give: for the labourer is worthy of his hire."
  - *Interpretation:* Jesus establishes the principle that workers deserve compensation, which Paul connects to the ox-muzzling law.

### "Her husband's brother shall go in unto her" — Levirate Marriage

**Deuteronomy 25:5-10** — "If brethren dwell together, and one of them die, and have no child, the wife of the dead shall not marry without unto a stranger: her husband's brother shall go in unto her, and take her to him to wife... And if the man like not to take his brother's wife... Then shall his brother's wife come unto him in the presence of the elders, and loose his shoe from off his foot, and spit in his face"

- **Genesis 38:8-10** — "And Judah said unto Onan, Go in unto thy brother's wife, and marry her, and raise up seed to thy brother. And Onan knew that the seed should not be his; and it came to pass, when he went in unto his brother's wife, that he spilled it on the ground, lest that he should give seed to his brother. And the thing which he did displeased the LORD: wherefore he slew him also."
  - *Interpretation:* Genesis provides the earliest example of levirate marriage, though Onan's refusal shows the practice predates Moses' formal regulations.

- **Ruth 4:1-10** — "Then went Boaz up to the gate, and sat him down there: and, behold, the kinsman of whom Boaz spake came by; unto whom he said, Ho, such a one! turn aside, sit down here. And he turned aside, and sat down... And this was the custom in former time in Israel concerning redeeming and concerning changing, to confirm the whole thing: a man plucked off his shoe, and gave it to his neighbour"
  - *Interpretation:* Ruth's story demonstrates the levirate marriage practice in action, with Boaz fulfilling the duty after the nearer kinsman refuses.

- **Matthew 22:23-28** — "The same day came to him the Sadducees, which say that there is no resurrection, and asked him, Saying, Master, Moses said, If a man die, having no children, his brother shall marry his wife, and raise up seed unto his brother. Now there were with us seven brethren: and the first, when he had married a wife, died, and, having no seed, left his wife unto his brother"
  - *Interpretation:* The Sadducees reference this law in their hypothetical question about resurrection, showing this was well-known in Jesus' time.

### "Thou shalt cut off her hand" — Assault Punishment

**Deuteronomy 25:11-12** — "When men strive together one with another, and the wife of the one draweth near for to deliver her husband out of the hand of him that smiteth him, and putteth forth her hand, and taketh him by the secrets: Then thou shalt cut off her hand, thine eye shall not pity her"

- **Deuteronomy 22:13-21** — "If any man take a wife, and go in unto her, and hate her, and give occasions of speech against her... Then shall the father of the damsel, and her mother, take and bring forth the tokens of the damsel's virginity unto the elders of the city in the gate"
  - *Interpretation:* The context of sexual offenses shows this severe punishment relates to protecting reproductive capability and family honor.

- **Exodus 21:22-25** — "If men strive, and hurt a woman with child, so that her fruit depart from her, and yet no mischief follow: he shall be surely punished, according as the woman's husband will lay upon him; and he shall pay as the judges determine... Eye for eye, tooth for tooth, hand for hand, foot for foot"
  - *Interpretation:* Exodus establishes the lex talionis principle that Moses applies here, though the punishment seems more severe than the offense.

- **Matthew 5:38-39** — "Ye have heard that it hath been said, An eye for an eye, and a tooth for a tooth: But I say unto you, That ye resist not evil: but whosoever shall smite thee on thy right cheek, turn to him the other also."
  - *Interpretation:* Jesus transforms the lex talionis principle from civil justice to personal ethics, though the civil principle remains valid for maintaining order.

### "Perfect and just weight, a perfect and just measure" — Honest Commerce

**Deuteronomy 25:13-16** — "Thou shalt not have in thy bag divers weights, a great and a small... But thou shalt have a perfect and just weight, a perfect and just measure shalt thou have: that thy days may be lengthened in the land which the LORD thy God giveth thee"

- **Proverbs 11:1** — "A false balance is abomination to the LORD: but a just weight is his delight."
  - *Interpretation:* Wisdom literature affirms this principle, showing it's a fundamental moral requirement beyond ceremonial law.

- **Proverbs 16:11** — "A just weight and balance are the LORD's: but all the weights of the bag are his work."
  - *Interpretation:* The psalmist shows that honest measures belong to God's character, making dishonesty spiritually offensive.

- **Hosea 12:7** — "He is a merchant, the balances of deceit are in his hand: he loveth to oppress."
  - *Interpretation:* Hosea uses false balances as a symbol of Israel's spiritual corruption, showing the principle has prophetic significance.

**Deuteronomy 25:17-19** — "Remember what Amalek did unto thee by the way...   - *Interpretation:* Exodus provides the historical account of Amalek's cowardly attack and God's command to destroy them, which Moses now repeats.

  - *Interpretation:* Haman is an Agagite, descendant of Agag, king of the Amalekites (1 Samuel 15), showing the Amalekite threat continued through Haman's attempted genocide of the Jews.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Forty stripes he may give him, and not exceed"
**Common misinterpretation:** This promotes harsh physical punishment
**Biblical context:** This is a limitation, not a requirement. The purpose is to prevent excessive punishment and maintain the brother's dignity. Paul received 39 stripes (2 Corinthians 11:24), showing they carefully avoided the maximum.

### "Thou shalt cut off her hand"
**Common misinterpretation:** This promotes brutal punishment for minor offenses
**Biblical context:** This refers to a specific situation where a wife intervenes in a fight by seizing her husband's attacker's genitals, attempting to permanently disable him. The severe punishment reflects the seriousness of attempting to destroy reproductive capacity and family continuity.
