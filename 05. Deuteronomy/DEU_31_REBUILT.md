## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses announces his transition at 120 years old, unable to cross Jordan, but God will go before Israel, destroying nations, with Joshua leading them. He commands Israel to be strong and courageous, for God won't fail or forsake them. Moses publicly commissions Joshua with the same charge before all Israel. Moses writes the law and delivers it to priests and elders, commanding that every seven years during the Feast of Tabernacles, the entire law be read publicly to all Israel—men, women, children, and strangers—so they may learn and fear God. God tells Moses he will soon die and predicts Israel will turn to foreign gods, forsaking Him and breaking His covenant, bringing divine anger and hiding of God's face. God commands Moses to write a song as a witness against Israel when they prosper in Canaan then turn to other gods. Moses writes the song that day and teaches it to Israel, giving Joshua his final charge to be strong and courageous. When Moses finishes writing the law, he commands Levites to place the book beside the ark of the covenant as a witness. Moses acknowledges Israel's rebellion and predicts they will corrupt themselves after his death, bringing evil in latter days. He calls heaven and earth to witness, then speaks the song to all Israel.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Be strong and of a good courage" — Leadership Charge

**Deuteronomy 31:6-8, 23** — "Be strong and of a good courage, fear not, nor be afraid of them: for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee... And he gave Joshua the son of Nun a charge, and said, Be strong and of a good courage"

- **Joshua 1:6-9** — "Be strong and of a good courage: for unto this people shalt thou divide for them an inheritance... Have not I commanded thee? Be strong and of a good courage; be not afraid, neither be thou dismayed: for the LORD thy God is with thee whithersoever thou goest."
  - *Interpretation:* God repeats this charge directly to Joshua, showing the importance of strength and courage in leadership. The promise of God's presence accompanies this charge.

- **1 Chronicles 28:20** — "And David said to Solomon his son, Be strong and of good courage, and do it: fear not, nor be dismayed: for the LORD God, even my God, will be with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee, until thou hast finished all the work for the service of the house of the LORD."
  - *Interpretation:* David gives Solomon the same charge, showing this became the standard leadership transfer formula in Israel.

- **2 Timothy 2:1** — "Thou therefore, my son, be strong in the grace that is in Christ Jesus."
  - *Interpretation:* Paul adapts this principle for spiritual leadership, showing that strength comes from God's grace rather than human courage.

### "This people will rise up, and go a whoring after the gods of the strangers" — Future Apostasy

**Deuteronomy 31:16-18** — "And the LORD said unto Moses, Behold, thou shalt sleep with thy fathers; and this people will rise up, and go a whoring after the gods of the strangers of the land... and will forsake me, and break my covenant which I have made with them... - **Judges 2:11-13** — "And the children of Israel did evil in the sight of the LORD, and served Baalim: And they forsook the LORD God of their fathers, which brought them out of the land of Egypt, and followed other gods, of the gods of the people that were round about them, and bowed themselves unto them, and provoked the LORD to anger. And they forsook the LORD, and served Baal and Ashtaroth."
  - *Interpretation:* Judges records the exact fulfillment of Moses' prophecy, showing Israel's repeated cycle of apostasy after entering Canaan.

- **1 Kings 11:4-8** — "For it came to pass, when Solomon was old, that his wives turned away his heart after other gods: and his heart was not perfect with the LORD his God, as was the heart of David his father... And the LORD was angry with Solomon, because his heart was turned from the LORD God of Israel, which had appeared unto him twice"
  - *Interpretation:* Even wise Solomon fell into this apostasy, confirming Moses' prediction that prosperity would lead to turning to other gods.

- **Romans 1:21-25** — "Because that, when they knew God, they glorified him not as God, neither were thankful; but became vain in their imaginations, and their foolish heart was darkened... Who changed the truth of God into a lie, and worshipped and served the creature more than the Creator"
  - *Interpretation:* Paul shows that Israel's apostasy pattern reflects universal human tendency to turn from Creator to creation.

  - *Interpretation:* Paul shows that preserved Scripture continues to serve as witness and instruction for believers.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Be strong and of a good courage"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about human self-confidence or positive thinking
**Biblical context:** This is a divine charge accompanied by the promise "for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee." The courage comes from God's presence, not human strength.

### "I will hide my face from them"
**Common misinterpretation:** This means God completely abandons His people
**Biblical context:** This is temporary judgment for covenant violation. God's hiding leads Israel to recognize their need for Him ("Are not these evils come upon us, because our God is not among us?"). It's corrective, not permanent abandonment.

**Chapter 31 marks the transition from Moses to Joshua:** Moses, at 120, acknowledges his limitations while commissioning Joshua with divine promises of presence and success. He establishes systems for preserving God's word—public reading every seven years and placing the law book beside the ark. God predicts Israel's future apostasy but provides the song as a witness. Moses' realism about Israel's rebellion contrasts with his faithfulness in completing his ministry. The chapter shows that leadership transition requires both divine commissioning and practical systems for preserving truth, while acknowledging human weakness and the need for divine grace.
