## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses' song calls heaven and earth to witness his doctrine that drops like rain, publishing God's name and greatness. God is the Rock—perfect in work, just in ways, true and without iniquity. Israel corrupted themselves, becoming perverse. Moses asks them to remember God's ancient care: when the Most High divided nations' inheritance according to Israel's number, Jacob became God's portion. God found Israel in wilderness, led them, kept them as apple of His eye, like an eagle stirring her nest, bearing her young on wings. He brought them to high places, providing honey from rock, oil from flint, butter, milk, fat lambs, Bashan rams, goats, and wheat, and pure grape blood. But Jeshurun (Israel) waxed fat and kicked, forsaking God who made them, lightly esteeming their Rock of salvation. They provoked God with strange gods and abominations, sacrificing to devils and new gods. God abhorred them, hid His face, and promised judgment: fire burning to hell, consuming earth's increase, burning mountain foundations. He would send hunger, burning heat, beasts, serpents, sword without, terror within, destroying all ages. He would scatter them but stopped fearing enemies would claim credit. God promises vengeance and recompense: their foot will slide when calamity comes. He will judge His people and have mercy when their power is gone, asking where their gods are who ate their sacrifices. God declares He alone is God, killing and making alive, wounding and healing. He will whet His sword, render vengeance, make arrows drunk with blood. Nations should rejoice with God's people as He avenges His servants' blood and shows mercy. Moses concludes by commanding Israel to set their hearts on these words as life, for through them they'll prolong their days in Canaan.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### JESHURUN
- **Meaning:** "Upright one," poetic name for Israel
- **Usage:** Ironically describes Israel when prosperous
- **Prophetic significance:** Shows contrast between ideal and reality
- **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual Israel's ups and downs

### APPLE OF HIS EYE
- **Meaning:** Pupil of the eye, most protected part
- **Significance:** Utmost care and protection
- **Biblical usage:** God's tender care for His people
- **Modern application:** Precious and protected relationship

### LOWEST HELL
- **Hebrew:** "Sheol" - realm of the dead
- **Meaning:** Complete destruction, not eternal punishment
- **Context:** Total judgment on apostate Israel
* **New Testament distinction:** Different from eternal hell (Gehenna)

### DRAGONS AND ASPS
- **Dragons:** "Tannin" - large serpents or sea monsters
- **Asps:** Venomous snakes, symbols of deadly poison
- **Metaphorical:** Spiritual poison of idolatry
* **New Testament application:** Spiritual deception (Revelation 12:9)

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "I will render vengeance to mine enemies"
**Common misinterpretation:** This promotes personal revenge
**Biblical context:* This is God's exclusive right to vengeance, not human permission. Paul quotes this in Romans 12:19 to teach believers NOT to take vengeance themselves but to leave it to God. The context is divine justice, not human retaliation.

### "Jeshurun waxed fat, and kicked"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about physical obesity
**Biblical context:** "Jeshurun" means "upright one," a poetic name for Israel. "Waxed fat" means became prosperous, not literally obese. "Kicked" means rebelled against God, showing that prosperity led to spiritual pride and apostasy.

**Chapter 32 presents Moses' final song as covenant testimony:** The song calls heaven and earth to witness God's perfection and Israel's corruption. It reviews God's faithful care from wilderness wandering to Canaan's abundance, then describes Israel's apostasy when prosperity led to pride. God promises severe judgment but also mercy and ultimate vindication. The song serves as a witness that would be remembered when Israel strayed. Moses concludes by emphasizing that God's word is life itself, not vain instruction. This poetic summary of Deuteronomy's themes provides a memorable testimony that would endure through Israel's future generations.
