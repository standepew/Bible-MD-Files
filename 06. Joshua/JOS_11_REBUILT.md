## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Jabin king of Hazor, hearing of Israel's victories, sends to Jobab of Madon, Shimron, Achshaph, and northern kings, including Canaanites, Amorites, Hittites, Perizzites, Jebusites in mountains, and Hivites under Hermon. They gather a massive army with many horses and chariots, camp at Merom waters to fight Israel. God tells Joshua not to fear, promising to deliver them all slain the next day, commanding Joshua to hough their horses and burn chariots. Joshua attacks suddenly at Merom, and God delivers the enemy into Israel's hand, who chases them to Zidon, Misrephoth-maim, and Mizpeh valley, leaving none remaining. Joshua obeys God by houghing horses and burning chariots. Joshua then turns back, takes Hazor, and kills its king, burning the city since Hazor was head of all kingdoms. Joshua destroys all inhabitants with the sword, burning Hazor. He takes all cities and kings, destroying them as Moses commanded, but only burns cities still standing in strength, except Hazor. Israel takes spoil and cattle as prey but destroys all people. Joshua perfectly obeys all God's commands through Moses. Joshua takes all land from Halak to Baal-gad, conquering all kings. Joshua warred a long time with these kings. Only Gibeon made peace; all others were taken in battle because God hardened their hearts to destroy them utterly as Moses commanded. Joshua cuts off the Anakims from mountains, destroying them and their cities, leaving only some in Gaza, Gath, and Ashdod. Joshua takes the whole land as God told Moses, giving it to Israel by tribal divisions, and the land rests from war.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Thou shalt hough their horses, and burn their chariots with fire" — Military Disarmament

**Joshua 11:6, 9** — "And the LORD said unto Joshua, Be not afraid because of them: for to morrow about this time will I deliver them up all slain before Israel: thou shalt hough their horses, and burn their chariots with fire... So Joshua came, and all the people of war with him, against them by the waters of Merom suddenly... And Joshua did unto them as the LORD bade him: he houghed their horses, and burnt their chariots with fire"

- **Deuteronomy 17:16** — "But he shall not multiply horses to himself, nor cause the people to return to Egypt, to the end that he should multiply horses: forasmuch as the LORD hath said unto you, Ye shall henceforth return no more that way."
  - *Interpretation:* Moses' law forbade multiplying horses, which Joshua now implements by destroying enemy horses, showing obedience to divine military principles.

- **Psalm 20:7** — "Some trust in chariots, and some in horses: but we will remember the name of the LORD our God."
  - *Interpretation:* The psalmist contrasts trust in military hardware with trust in God, which Joshua demonstrates by destroying horses and chariots.

- **Isaiah 31:1** — "Woe to them that go down to Egypt for help; and stay on horses, and trust in chariots, because they are many; and in horsemen, because they are very strong; but they look not unto the Holy One of Israel, neither seek the LORD!"
  *Interpretation:* Isaiah warns against trusting in military strength, which Joshua addresses by removing the temptation through destruction.

### "Hazor beforetime was the head of all those kingdoms" — Strategic Victory

**Joshua 11:10, 13** — "And Joshua at that time turned back, and took Hazor, and smote the king thereof with the sword: for Hazor beforetime was the head of all those kingdoms... And they smote all the souls that were therein with the edge of the sword, utterly destroying them: there was not any left to breathe: and he burnt Hazor with fire"

- **Judges 4:2-3** — "And the LORD sold them into the hand of Jabin king of Canaan, that reigned in Hazor; the captain of whose host was Sisera... And the children of Israel cried unto the LORD: for he had nine hundred chariots of iron; and twenty years he mightily oppressed the children of Israel."
  - *Interpretation:* Judges shows that Hazor later regains power under Jabin, demonstrating that Joshua's destruction was not permanent, requiring later deliverance through Deborah and Barak.

- **1 Kings 9:15** — "And this is the reason of the levy which king Solomon raised; for to build the house of the LORD, and his own house, and Millo, and the wall of Jerusalem, and Hazor, and Megiddo, and Gezer."
  - *Interpretation:* Solomon rebuilds Hazor as a fortified city, showing it remained strategically important even after Joshua's destruction.

- **Jeremiah 49:28-30** — "Concerning Kedar, and concerning the kingdoms of Hazor, which Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon shall smite... Arise, get you up unto the wealthy nation, that dwelleth without care, saith the LORD, which have neither gates nor bars, which dwell alone."
  *Interpretation:* Jeremiah prophesies against Hazor in Babylonian times, showing its continued significance.

### "For it was of the LORD to harden their hearts" — Divine Sovereignty

**Joshua 11:20** — "For it was of the LORD to harden their hearts, that they should come against Israel in battle, that he might destroy them utterly, and that they might have no favour, but that he might destroy them, as the LORD commanded Moses"

- **Exodus 9:16** — "And in very deed for this cause have I raised thee up, for to shew in thee my power; and that my name may be declared throughout all the earth."
  - *Interpretation:* God told Pharaoh He raised him up to demonstrate His power, which parallels God hardening Canaanite hearts to demonstrate His judgment.

- **Romans 9:17-18** — "For the scripture saith unto Pharaoh, Even for this same purpose have I raised thee up, that I might shew my power in thee, and that my name might be declared throughout all the earth. Therefore hath he mercy on whom he will have mercy, and whom he will he hardeneth."

- **John 12:40** — "He hath blinded their eyes, and hardened their heart; that they should not see with their eyes, nor understand with their heart, and be converted, and I should heal them."
  *Interpretation:* John applies this principle to those who rejected Christ, showing God's hardening serves His purposes of judgment.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### HOUGH
- **Meaning:** To cut the tendons of the legs, cripple
- **Military purpose:** Disable horses for future use
- **Symbolic meaning:** Remove military power
* **Spiritual application:** Disable spiritual enemies (Ephesians 6:12)

### ANAKIMS
- **Meaning:** Descendants of Anak, giants
- **Historical significance:** Caused Israel's fear in wilderness
- **Geographic distribution:** Mountain regions, later Philistine cities
* **Spiritual meaning:** Seemingly insurmountable obstacles

### RESTED FROM WAR
- **Meaning:** Military campaign completed
- **Significance:** Conquest phase finished
- **Temporary nature:** Rest until new conflicts arise
* **Spiritual application:** Peace through obedience (Philippians 4:7)

### HARDEN HEARTS
- **Meaning:** Make resistant to persuasion
- **Divine purpose:** Bring about judgment
- **Human responsibility:** People responsible for their choices
* **Biblical principle:** God's sovereignty with human responsibility

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "For it was of the LORD to harden their hearts"
**Common misinterpretation:** This means God forces people to sin
**Biblical context:** God's hardening works with people's existing rebellion to bring about His purposes of judgment. The Canaanite kings were already hostile; God ensured they wouldn't repent to avoid the judgment they deserved.

### "The land rested from war"
**Common misinterpretation:** This means all fighting ended forever
**Biblical context:** This refers to the completion of the conquest phase. Later books show continued conflicts (Judges), but the major conquest of Canaan was complete, giving Israel possession of the land.

**Chapter 11 completes the major conquest of Canaan:** The northern coalition under Jabin represents the final organized resistance to Israel's conquest. God's command to hough horses and burn chariots shows that military victory requires removing reliance on human strength. Hazor's destruction as the head kingdom demonstrates strategic targeting of leadership. Joshua's perfect obedience to all God's commands through Moses shows the importance of complete compliance with divine instructions. The hardening of Canaanite hearts demonstrates God's sovereignty in ensuring judgment comes upon those who deserve it. The defeat of the Anakim shows that what once caused Israel's fear is now overcome through divine power. The statement that "the land rested from war" marks the completion of the conquest phase, though not all conflict ends. This chapter demonstrates that complete victory comes through complete obedience, divine power, and the removal of human reliance on military strength. The conquest fulfills God's promises to Moses and establishes Israel in the land as their inheritance.
