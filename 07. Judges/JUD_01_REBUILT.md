## CHAPTER SUMMARY

After Joshua's death, Israel asks the LORD who should fight the Canaanites first. The LORD says Judah shall go up, promising He has delivered the land into Judah's hand. Judah invites Simeon to help, promising mutual assistance. Together they defeat Canaanites and Perizzites, killing 10,000 men in Bezek. They capture Adoni-bezek, cut off his thumbs and great toes. Adoni-bezek acknowledges divine retribution, as he had done this to 70 kings. They bring him to Jerusalem where he dies. Judah conquers Jerusalem, burning it. Judah then attacks Canaanites in the mountain, south, and valley, conquering Hebron (formerly Kirjath-arba) and killing Sheshai, Ahiman, and Talmai. Caleb offers his daughter Achsah to whoever takes Kirjath-sepher (Debir). Othniel, Caleb's nephew, captures it and receives Achsah as wife. Achsah asks her father for springs of water in addition to her south land, and Caleb gives her upper and nether springs. The Kenites, Moses' father-in-law's descendants, go with Judah to the wilderness south of Arad and dwell among the people. Judah and Simeon destroy Zephath, renaming it Hormah. Judah takes Gaza, Askelon, and Ekron with their coasts. The LORD is with Judah, helping them drive out mountain inhabitants, but they cannot drive out valley inhabitants due to iron chariots. Hebron is given to Caleb as Moses promised, and he expels the three sons of Anak. Benjamin fails to drive out Jebusites from Jerusalem, who dwell there to this day. The house of Joseph attacks Beth-el (Luz), sending spies who learn the entrance from a man in exchange for mercy. They capture the city but let the man go, who rebuilds Luz in Hittite land. Manasseh fails to drive out inhabitants of Beth-shean, Taanach, Dor, Ibleam, and Megiddo. When Israel becomes strong, they put Canaanites to tribute instead of driving them out. Ephraim fails to drive out Gezer's Canaanites. Zebulun fails to drive out Kitron and Nahalol inhabitants, who become tributaries. Asher fails to drive out Accho, Zidon, Ahlab, Achzib, Helbah, Aphik, and Rehob inhabitants, dwelling among them. Naphtali fails to drive out Beth-shemesh and Beth-anath inhabitants, who become tributaries. Amorites force Dan into the mountain, dwelling in mount Heres, Aijalon, and Shaalbim, but the house of Joseph makes them tributaries. The Amorites' coast extends from Akrabbim upward.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "After the death of Joshua" — Leadership Transition

**Judges 1:1** — "Now after the death of Joshua it came to pass, that the children of Israel asked the LORD, saying, Who shall go up for us against the Canaanites first, to fight against them?"

- **Joshua 24:29** — "And it came to pass after these things, that Joshua the son of Nun, the servant of the LORD, died, being an hundred and ten years old"
  - *Interpretation:* Joshua 24 records Joshua's death, which Judges 1 references as the beginning of the new era without his leadership.

- **Deuteronomy 31:23** - "And he gave Joshua the son of Nun a charge, and said, Be strong and of a good courage: for thou shalt bring the children of Israel into the land which I sware unto them: and I will be with thee."
  -Interpretation:* Moses originally commissioned Joshua, and now Israel must seek new leadership after Joshua's death.

### "Judah shall go up: behold, I have delivered the land into his hand" — Divine Promise

**Judges 1:2** — "And the LORD said, Judah shall go up: behold, I have delivered the land into his hand"

- **Genesis 49:8-10** — "Judah, thou art he whom thy brethren shall praise: thy hand shall be in the neck of thine enemies; thy father's children shall bow down before thee. The sceptre shall not depart from Judah, nor a lawgiver from between his feet, until Shiloh come"
  - *Interpretation:* Jacob's blessing prophesied Judah's leadership and victory, which God now confirms in directing Judah to lead the conquest.

- **Hebrews 7:14** - "For it is evident that our Lord sprang out of Juda; of which tribe Moses spake nothing concerning priesthood"
  -Interpretation:* Hebrews references Judah's preeminence, showing this tribe's special role in Israel's history.

### "Caleb said, He that smiteth Kirjath-sepher, and taketh it, to him will I give Achsah my daughter to wife" — Caleb's Promise

**Judges 1:12-15** — "And Caleb said, He that smiteth Kirjath-sepher, and taketh it, to him will I give Achsah my daughter to wife... And Othniel the son of Kenaz, Caleb's younger brother, took it: and he gave him Achsah his daughter to wife"

  -Interpretation:* Numbers records God's special promise to Caleb, which is now being fulfilled in his possession of Hebron and his daughter's marriage.

### "They could not drive out the inhabitants of the valley, because they had chariots of iron" — Military Limitation

**Judges 1:19** — "And the LORD was with Judah; and he drave out the inhabitants of the mountain; but could not drive out the inhabitants of the valley, because they had chariots of iron"

- **Joshua 17:16** — "And the children of Joseph said, The hill is not enough for us: and all the Canaanites that dwell in the land of the valley have chariots of iron, both they who are of Bethshean and her towns, and they who are of the valley of Jezreel."
  - *Interpretation:* Joshua 17 mentions the iron chariots as a significant military challenge, showing this was a recurring problem for Israel.

- **Deuteronomy 20:1** - "When thou goest out to battle against thine enemies, and seest horses, and chariots, and a people more than thou, be not afraid of them: for the LORD thy God is he that doth go with thee, to fight for thee against thine enemies, to save thee."
  -Interpretation:* Deuteronomy commands Israel not to fear chariots because God fights for them, making Judah's failure here a matter of insufficient faith rather than military impossibility.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "They could not drive out the inhabitants of the valley, because they had chariots of iron"
**Common misinterpretation:** This shows God's limitation
**Biblical context:** This shows Israel's lack of faith, not God's limitation. Deuteronomy 20:1 commands Israel not to fear chariots because God fights for them. The problem was insufficient faith, not insufficient divine power.

### "When Israel was strong, that they put the Canaanites to tribute"
**Common misinterpretation:** This was wise compromise
**Biblical context:* This was disobedience to God's command to drive out all inhabitants. It led to ongoing idolatry and problems, as later books show.

**Chapter 1 establishes the post-Joshua period with incomplete conquest:** After Joshua's death, Israel seeks divine guidance for continuing the conquest. God directs Judah to lead, fulfilling Jacob's prophecy about Judah's preeminence. The Judah-Simeon alliance shows tribal cooperation, but the pattern of incomplete obedience begins with failure to drive out valley inhabitants due to iron chariots. Adoni-bezek's capture and punishment demonstrate divine retribution principle. Caleb's continued faithfulness contrasts with other tribes' failures, showing that individual faithfulness can succeed where national effort fails. Achsah's request for springs shows the importance of seeking God's best provision. The Kenites' settlement shows peaceful integration of faithful foreigners. The systematic listing of tribal failures (Benjamin, Manasseh, Ephraim, Zebulun, Asher, Naphtali, Dan) demonstrates widespread disobedience to God's command for complete conquest. The pattern of making Canaanites tributaries instead of driving them out sets up future problems with idolatry. This chapter establishes the cyclical pattern of the Judges period: initial success followed by incomplete obedience leading to ongoing problems. It shows that partial obedience to God's commands leads to compromise and future difficulties, while complete obedience brings full blessing. The chapter serves as a foundation for understanding why the judges became necessary and why Israel repeatedly fell into idolatry.
