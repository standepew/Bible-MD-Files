## CHAPTER SUMMARY

An angel of the LORD comes from Gilgal to Bochim, reminding Israel that God brought them from Egypt to the promised land and promised never to break His covenant. God commanded them not to make treaties with inhabitants or allow their altars, but Israel disobeyed. Therefore God declares He will not drive out the remaining nations, which will become thorns and snares to Israel. When the angel speaks, Israel weeps and names the place Bochim, sacrificing to the LORD. After Joshua lets the people go, each tribe goes to possess their inheritance. Israel serves the LORD throughout Joshua's days and the elders' days who witnessed God's works. Joshua dies at 110 and is buried in Timnath-heres. That generation dies, and a new generation arises that doesn't know the LORD or His works. Israel does evil, serving Baalim and Ashtaroth, forsaking the LORD who brought them from Egypt, following surrounding nations' gods and provoking God's anger. God's anger burns, and He delivers Israel into spoilers' hands, selling them to enemies so they cannot stand. Wherever they go, God's hand is against them for evil, fulfilling His word, causing great distress. Nevertheless, God raises up judges who deliver them from oppressors. But Israel won't listen to judges, going after other gods and quickly turning from their fathers' faithful ways. When God raises judges, He is with them and delivers Israel from enemies during the judge's lifetime, relenting because of their groanings. When the judge dies, Israel returns to corruption worse than their fathers, serving and bowing to other gods without ceasing. God's anger burns again because Israel transgressed His covenant and wouldn't listen to His voice. God declares He will no longer drive out the nations Joshua left, to test Israel whether they will keep God's ways as their fathers did. Therefore God leaves those nations without driving them out hastily or delivering them into Joshua's hand.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "I made you to go up out of Egypt, and have brought you unto the land which I sware unto your fathers" — Covenant Faithfulness

**Judges 2:1** — "And an angel of the LORD came up from Gilgal to Bochim, and said, I made you to go up out of Egypt, and have brought you unto the land which I sware unto your fathers; and I said, I will never break my covenant with you"

- **Exodus 20:2** — "I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage."
  - *Interpretation:* Exodus records God's deliverance from Egypt as the basis of His covenant relationship, which the angel now references in reminding Israel of their obligations.

- **Genesis 17:7-8** - "And I will establish my covenant between me and thee and thy seed after thee in their generations for an everlasting covenant, to be a God unto thee, and to thy seed after thee. And I will give unto thee, and to thy seed after thee, the land wherein thou art a stranger, all the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession"
  -Interpretation:* Genesis records God's original covenant promise to Abraham about the land, which the angel now references as fulfilled.

### "Ye shall make no league with the inhabitants of this land; ye shall throw down their altars" — Command Against Compromise

**Judges 2:2** — "And ye shall make no league with the inhabitants of this land; ye shall throw down their altars: but ye have not obeyed my voice: why have ye done this?"

- **Deuteronomy 7:2-4** — "And when the LORD thy God shall deliver them before thee; thou shalt smite them, and utterly destroy them; thou shalt make no covenant with them, nor shew mercy unto them: Neither shalt thou make marriages with them; thy daughter thou shalt not give unto his son, nor his daughter shalt thou take unto thy son. For they will turn away thy son from following me, that they may serve other gods: so the anger of the LORD be kindled against you, and destroy thee suddenly."
  - *Interpretation:* Deuteronomy provides the original command against making treaties with Canaanites, explaining that intermarriage would lead to idolatry, which Judges now shows happening.

- **Exodus 34:12-13** - "Take heed to thyself, lest thou make a covenant with the inhabitants of the land whither thou goest, lest it be for a snare in the midst of thee: But ye shall destroy their altars, break their images, and cut down their groves"
  -Interpretation:* Exodus also commands destroying Canaanite religious sites, showing this was a consistent requirement.

### "They shall be as thorns in your sides, and their gods shall be a snare unto you" — Consequences of Disobedience

**Judges 2:3** — "Wherefore I also said, I will not drive them out from before you; but they shall be as thorns in your sides, and their gods shall be a snare unto you"

  -Interpretation:* Numbers also warned about remaining inhabitants becoming thorns, showing this was consistently taught.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### BOCHIM
- **Meaning:** "Weepers"
- **Context:** Place named for Israel's weeping after rebuke
- **Significance:** Moment of repentance that didn't last
* **Spiritual application:** Godly sorrow (2 Corinthians 7:10)

### BAALIM AND ASHTAROTH
- **Meaning:** Canaanite male and female deities
- **Context:** Gods Israel served instead of LORD
- **Significance:** Complete abandonment of true worship
* **New Testament parallel:** Idolatry (Colossians 3:5)

### JUDGES
- **Meaning:** Divinely raised deliverers
- **Context:** Temporary leaders who rescued Israel
- **Significance:** Pattern of divine mercy despite rebellion
* **Christological parallel:** Christ as ultimate deliverer (Hebrews 2:15)

### THORNS IN YOUR SIDES
- **Meaning:** Constant sources of pain and trouble
- **Context:** Remaining Canaanites as ongoing problem
- **Significance:** Consequences of incomplete obedience
* **Spiritual application:** Unresolved sin (Hebrews 12:1)

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "There arose another generation after them, which knew not the LORD"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about intellectual ignorance
**Biblical context:** This is about experiential knowledge through relationship and obedience, not just intellectual awareness. The generation didn't know God through personal experience and faithfulness.

### "The LORD raised up judges"
**Common misinterpretation:** This shows God's approval
**Biblical context:* This shows God's mercy despite Israel's sin. The judges were a temporary solution to a deeper problem of Israel's unfaithfulness, not a sign of God's approval of their behavior.

**Chapter 2 establishes the cyclical pattern of the Judges period:** The angel's message at Bochim reminds Israel of God's covenant faithfulness and their disobedience. God's declaration that remaining Canaanites will become "thorns in your sides" and "snares" establishes the principle that incomplete obedience leads to ongoing problems. Israel's weeping at Bochim shows temporary repentance without lasting change. The transition from Joshua's faithful generation to one that "knew not the LORD" demonstrates the failure to teach children about God's works. The pattern of idolatry, divine judgment, deliverance through judges, and return to idolatry establishes the cyclical nature of this period. The judges are raised up as temporary solutions, but Israel returns to corruption "more than their fathers" when each judge dies. God's explanation that He leaves the nations to test Israel shows divine sovereignty even in allowing difficult circumstances. This chapter provides the theological framework for understanding the entire Judges period: God remains faithful to His covenant while Israel repeatedly breaks it, leading to divine judgment tempered with mercy through the judges. The chapter demonstrates that spiritual decline comes from failing to pass knowledge of God to the next generation, and that incomplete obedience to God's commands creates ongoing problems that affect future generations. It establishes the pattern that will repeat throughout the book: sin → judgment → cry for help → deliverance → return to sin.
