## CHAPTER SUMMARY

God left specific nations to test Israel: five Philistine lords, Canaanites, Sidonians, and Hivites from mount Baal-hermon to Hamath. This was to teach war to generations that hadn't known Canaan's wars and to test whether Israel would obey God's commands through Moses. Israel dwelt among Canaanites, Hittites, Amorites, Perizzites, Hivites, and Jebusites, intermarrying and serving their gods. Israel did evil, forgetting the LORD and serving Baalim and groves. God's anger burned, and He sold Israel to Chushan-rishathaim, Mesopotamian king, for eight years. When Israel cried out, God raised Othniel (Caleb's brother) as deliverer. The Spirit of the LORD came upon Othniel, who judged Israel and defeated Chushan-rishathaim, bringing forty years of rest until Othniel died. Israel again did evil, so God strengthened Eglon, Moab's king, against Israel. Eglon gathered Ammon and Amalek, conquered Israel, and possessed the city of palm trees. Israel served Eglon eighteen years. When Israel cried out, God raised Ehud (left-handed Benjamite) as deliverer. Israel sent tribute to Eglon through Ehud, who made a double-edged dagger a cubit long, girding it under his raiment on his right thigh. After presenting tribute, Ehud sent the bearers away, then returned to Eglon claiming a secret message. Alone in Eglon's summer parlour, Ehud said he had a message from God. As Eglon rose, Ehud thrust the dagger into his belly with his left hand. The fat closed over the blade, preventing removal, and filth came out. Ehud locked the parlour doors and escaped. When servants found locked doors, they assumed Eglon was covering his feet. After waiting shamefully long, they opened the doors and found Eglon dead. Ehud escaped to Seirath, then blew a trumpet in mount Ephraim. Israel followed him, taking Jordan fords toward Moab and preventing escape. They killed about ten thousand valiant Moabites, with none escaping. Moab was subdued that day, bringing eighty years of rest. After Ehud came Shamgar (son of Anath), who killed six hundred Philistines with an ox goad and delivered Israel.

### "To prove Israel by them, even as many of Israel as had not known all the wars of Canaan" — Divine Testing

**Judges 3:1, 4** — "Now these are the nations which the LORD left, to prove Israel by them, even as many of Israel as had not known all the wars of Canaan... Only that the generations of the children of Israel might know, to teach them war, at the least such as before knew nothing thereof"

- **Deuteronomy 8:2-3** — "And thou shalt remember all the way which the LORD thy God led thee these forty years in the wilderness, to humble thee, and to prove thee, to know what was in thine heart, whether thou wouldest keep his commandments, or no. And he humbled thee, and suffered thee to hunger, and fed thee with manna, which thou knewest not, neither did thy fathers know; that he might make thee know that man doth not live by bread only, but by every word that proceedeth out of the mouth of the LORD doth man live."
  - *Interpretation:* Deuteronomy explains that God tests His people to reveal their hearts and teach them dependence on Him, which Judges now applies to military testing through remaining nations.

- **James 1:2-4** - "My brethren, count it all joy when ye fall into divers temptations; Knowing this, that the trying of your faith worketh patience. But let patience have her perfect work, that ye may be perfect and entire, wanting nothing."
  -Interpretation:* James teaches that testing produces spiritual maturity, which aligns with God's purpose in leaving nations to test Israel.

- **Judges 20:16** — "Among all this people there were seven hundred chosen men lefthanded; every one could sling stones at an hair breadth, and not miss."

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "To prove Israel by them"
**Common misinterpretation:** This means God tempted Israel to sin
**Biblical context:** This means God tested Israel's faithfulness, not tempted them to evil. Testing reveals character while tempting encourages sin, which God never does (James 1:13).

### "A man lefthanded"
**Common misinterpretation:** This indicates weakness or defect
**Biblical context:* This indicates unusual skill that enabled surprise attack. Benjamites were known for ambidextrous warriors, making left-handedness a tactical advantage, not a weakness.
