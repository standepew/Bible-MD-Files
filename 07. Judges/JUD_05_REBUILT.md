## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Deborah and Barak sing this victory song, praising the LORD for avenging Israel when people willingly offered themselves. They call kings and princes to hear them sing to the LORD God of Israel. The song describes God's awesome presence when He went out from Seir and Edom - earth trembled, heavens dropped water, mountains melted before the LORD, even Sinai. In the days of Shamgar and Jael, highways were unoccupied and travelers used byways while village life ceased until Deborah arose as a mother in Israel. Israel chose new gods, bringing war to their gates; no shield or spear was found among forty thousand in Israel. Deborah's heart is toward governors who offered themselves willingly among the people. She addresses those riding white asses, sitting in judgment, and walking by the way. Those delivered from archers at watering places will rehearse the LORD's righteous acts toward Israel's villages, then God's people will go to the gates. Deborah calls herself and Barak to awake and sing, and Barak to lead captivity captive. The LORD made the remaining have dominion over nobles and gave Deborah dominion over the mighty. Ephraim provided a root against Amalek, Benjamin among his people, Machir provided governors, and Zebulun provided writers. Issachar's princes were with Deborah and Barak, sent on foot into the valley. Reuben had great heart-searchings about participating. Gilead stayed beyond Jordan, Dan remained in ships, and Asher stayed on the sea shore. But Zebulun and Naphtali jeoparded their lives to death in high places. The kings of Canaan fought at Taanach by Megiddo's waters but gained no money. They fought from heaven - stars in their courses fought against Sisera. The ancient Kishon river swept them away. Horse hooves broke from prancings of mighty ones. The angel of the LORD curses Meroz bitterly because they didn't help the LORD against the mighty. Jael is blessed above women in the tent - she gave Sisera milk when he asked for water, brought butter in a lordly dish, then used hammer and nail to smite Sisera, piercing his temples. He bowed, fell, and lay dead at her feet. Sisera's mother looks through the lattice, wondering why his chariot delays. Her wise ladies answer that they must have divided the spoil - each man a damsel or two, Sisera getting colorful needlework spoil. The song ends with prayer that all God's enemies perish but those who love Him be as the sun going forth in might. The land had rest forty years.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "They fought from heaven; the stars in their courses fought against Sisera"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is astrology or fortune-telling
**Biblical context:** This is poetic language describing God's control over creation for His purposes. The "stars" represent celestial bodies following God's direction in battle, not astrological influence.

### "Curse ye Meroz"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about personal vengeance
**Biblical context:** This is divine judgment on neutrality. Meroz failed to help God's people in battle, showing that not taking sides in spiritual warfare is equivalent to opposing God.

**Chapter 5 is the victory song celebrating God's deliverance through unlikely means:** Deborah and Barak's song begins with praising God for avenging Israel when people willingly offered themselves, showing that victory comes through human cooperation with divine power. The description of God's awesome presence recalls Sinai, establishing that the same power that gave the law now delivers His people. The contrast between the dark period of Shamgar and Jael with Deborah's rise shows that God raises up leaders when needed. The criticism of tribes that didn't participate (Reuben, Gilead, Dan, Asher) demonstrates that partial commitment is insufficient in God's battles. The cosmic warfare description shows that all creation participates in God's deliverance. The curse on Meroz establishes the principle that neutrality in spiritual warfare equals opposition to God. Jael's blessing celebrates her decisive action, showing that God uses whoever is willing to act for His purposes. The ironic scene of Sisera's mother expecting victory while her son is dead demonstrates the futility of opposing God's will. The final prayer contrasts the fate of God's enemies with the blessing of those who love Him, using the sun as a metaphor for faithful witness. This chapter serves as both historical record and theological statement about how God works: He uses willing people, all creation serves His purposes, neutrality is condemned, and decisive action brings blessing. The forty years of rest shows the completeness of the victory when God's people fully cooperate with His plans.
