## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Israel does evil, so the LORD delivers them to Midian for seven years. Midian prevails, forcing Israel into mountain dens, caves, and strongholds. When Israel sows, Midianites, Amalekites, and eastern peoples invade, destroying crops to Gaza and leaving no sustenance. They come with countless cattle and camels like grasshoppers, devastating the land. Israel becomes greatly impoverished and cries to the LORD. God sends a prophet reminding Israel He delivered them from Egypt and all oppressors, gave them their land, and commanded them not to fear Amorite gods, but they disobeyed. An angel appears under an oak in Ophrah (Joash the Abi-ezrite's property) where Gideon threshes wheat to hide from Midianites. The angel calls Gideon a mighty man of valor, saying the LORD is with him. Gideon questions why all this happened if God is with them, asking where God's miracles are now that He has forsaken them to Midian. God tells Gideon to go in his might and save Israel, asking "Have not I sent thee?" Gideon protests his family is poor in Manasseh and he's the least in his father's house. God promises His presence and that Gideon will strike Midianites as one man. Gideon asks for a sign, preparing a kid and unleavened cakes. The angel has him lay flesh and cakes on a rock and pour broth, then touches them with his staff. Fire consumes the offering, and the angel departs. Gideon realizes he saw the angel face to face and fears death, but God assures him peace. Gideon builds an altar called Jehovah-shalom. That night, God commands Gideon to destroy his father's Baal altar and grove, build an altar to the LORD on the rock, and offer the second bullock as burnt sacrifice with the grove's wood. Gideon does this by night, fearing his father's household and city men. In the morning, the city discovers the destroyed altar and demands Gideon's death. Joash defends his son, challenging them to plead for Baal if he's a god. He renames Gideon Jerubbaal ("Let Baal plead") because he destroyed Baal's altar. The Midianites gather with Amalekites and eastern peoples in Jezreel valley. The Spirit comes upon Gideon, he blows a trumpet, and Abi-ezer gathers. He sends messengers to Manasseh, Asher, Zebulun, and Naphtali, who come to meet them. Gideon asks God for a sign with a fleece - dew only on the fleece, dry ground elsewhere. God does this. Gideon asks for the reverse sign - dry fleece, dewy ground - which God also does.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "The LORD is with thee, thou mighty man of valour" — Divine Calling

**Judges 6:12** — "And the angel of the LORD appeared unto him, and said unto him, The LORD is with thee, thou mighty man of valour"

- **Judges 7:2** — "And the LORD said unto Gideon, The people that are with thee are too many for me to give the Midianites into their hands, lest Israel vaunt themselves against me, saying, Mine own hand hath saved me."
  - *Interpretation:* God later reduces Gideon's forces to ensure the victory is clearly divine, showing that "mighty man of valour" refers to God's empowerment, not Gideon's natural ability.

- **1 Samuel 16:7** - "But the LORD said unto Samuel, Look not on his countenance, or on the height of his stature; because I have refused him: for the LORD seeth not as man seeth; for man looketh on the outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart."
  -Interpretation:* Samuel shows that God sees potential beyond outward appearance, which He does in calling Gideon despite his humble status.

### "If now I have found grace in thy sight, then shew me a sign" — Request for Confirmation

**Judges 6:17** — "And he said unto him, If now I have found grace in thy sight, then shew me a sign that thou talkest with me"

- **Judges 6:36-40** — "And Gideon said unto God, If thou wilt save Israel by mine hand, as thou hast said, Behold, I will put a fleece of wool in the floor; and if the dew be on the fleece only, and it be dry upon all the earth beside, then shall I know that thou wilt save Israel by mine hand, as thou hast said"
  - *Interpretation:* Gideon's fleece signs show his continuing need for confirmation, demonstrating that even after the fire sign, he still struggles with faith.

- **Luke 1:18-20** - "And Zacharias said unto the angel, Whereby shall I know this? for I am an old man, and my wife well stricken in years. And the angel answering said unto him, I am Gabriel, that stand in the presence of God; and am sent to speak unto thee, and to shew thee these glad tidings."
  -Interpretation:* Zacharias also asks for a sign when told of John's birth, showing that godly people sometimes need confirmation of divine messages.

### "Will ye plead for Baal? will ye save him? he that will plead for him, let him be put to death" — Challenge to False Gods

**Judges 6:31** — "And Joash said unto all that stood against him, Will ye plead for Baal? will ye save him? he that will plead for him, let him be put to death whilst it is yet morning: if he be a god, let him plead for himself, because one hath cast down his altar"

- **1 Kings 18:21** — "And Elijah came unto all the people, and said, How long halt ye between two opinions? if the LORD be God, follow him: but if Baal, then follow him. And the people answered him not a word."
  - *Interpretation:* Elijah also challenges Israel to choose between God and Baal, showing this was a recurring spiritual battle in Israel's history.

- **Jeremiah 10:5** - "They are upright as the palm tree, but speak not: they must needs be borne, because they cannot go. Be not afraid of them, for they cannot do evil, neither also is it in them to do good."
  -Interpretation:* Jeremiah describes idols as helpless, which Joash demonstrates by challenging Baal to defend himself.

### "The Spirit of the LORD came upon Gideon, and he blew a trumpet" — Divine Empowerment

**Judges 6:34** — "Then all the Midianites and the Amalekites and the children of the east were gathered together, and went over, and pitched in the valley of Jezreel. But the Spirit of the LORD came upon Gideon, and he blew a trumpet; and Abi-ezer was gathered after him"

- **Judges 3:10** — "And the Spirit of the LORD came upon him, and he judged Israel, and went out to war: and the LORD delivered Chushan-rishathaim king of Mesopotamia into his hand; and his hand prevailed against Chushan-rishathaim."
  - *Interpretation:* The Spirit coming upon judges is a consistent pattern in Judges, showing divine empowerment is necessary for victory.

- **Acts 1:8** - "But ye shall receive power, after that the Holy Ghost is come upon you: and ye shall be witnesses unto me both in Jerusalem, and in all Judaea, and in Samaria, and unto the uttermost part of the earth."
  -Interpretation:* Jesus promises the Holy Spirit's power for witness, which continues the pattern of divine empowerment for God's work.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### MIGHTY MAN OF VALOUR
- **Meaning:** Warrior of great courage
- **Context:** Angel's description of Gideon
- **Significance:** God sees potential beyond current reality
* **New Testament parallel:** Mighty in spirit (Luke 1:69)

### JEHOVAH-SHALOM
- **Meaning:** "The LORD is Peace"
- **Context:** Name of Gideon's altar
- **Significance:** God brings peace despite circumstances
* **New Testament parallel:** Prince of Peace (Isaiah 9:6)

### JERUBBAAL
- **Meaning:** "Let Baal contend" or "Baal's opponent"
- **Context:** New name given to Gideon
- **Significance:** Victory over false gods brings new identity
* **Spiritual application:** New identity in Christ (2 Corinthians 5:17)

### FLEECE
- **Meaning:** Wool sheath for testing
- **Context:** Gideon's sign for confirmation
- **Significance:** God accommodates human weakness while building faith
* **Spiritual application:** Testing God's will (James 1:5)

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "The LORD is with thee, thou mighty man of valour"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about Gideon's natural courage
**Biblical context:** This is about God's perspective and calling, not Gideon's current state. Gideon is hiding from Midianites, showing he's not yet courageous. God sees his potential, not his present reality.

### "If the dew be on the fleece only, and it be dry upon all the earth beside"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about proper methods for seeking God's will
**Biblical context:** This shows Gideon's weak faith. God graciously accommodates his request, but the fleece signs are not presented as the normative way to discern God's will. They show God's patience with human weakness.

God's prophetic message reminds Israel of His past faithfulness before calling Gideon. Gideon's call shows God sees potential beyond current circumstances - calling a hiding man a "mighty man of valor." Gideon's questions and requests for signs show his honest struggle with faith, which God patiently accommodates. The fire sign demonstrates God's power and confirms the divine message. Gideon's fear of death shows his humanity, but God's assurance of peace shows divine grace. The altar named Jehovah-shalom establishes that God brings peace even in turmoil. The nighttime destruction of Baal's altar shows both courage and fear - Gideon obeys but still fears men. Joash's defense of Gideon demonstrates that false gods cannot defend themselves, establishing the principle that true God needs no human defense. The name Jerubbaal becomes a testimony of victory over false gods. The Spirit's empowerment shows that divine enablement follows obedience. The fleece signs show God's patience with weak faith while building it stronger. This chapter establishes important principles: God calls unlikely people, sees potential beyond current reality, accommodates human weakness while building faith, false gods cannot defend themselves, and divine empowerment follows obedience.
