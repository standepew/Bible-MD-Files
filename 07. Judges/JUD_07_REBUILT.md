## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Jerubbaal (Gideon) and his men camp by Harod well, facing Midianites north of Moreh hill in the valley. God says Gideon's army is too large - if Israel wins with many, they'll claim victory by their own strength. God tells Gideon to proclaim that fearful men should return from mount Gilead. Twenty-two thousand leave, leaving ten thousand. God says they're still too many and brings them to water for testing. Those who lap water like dogs (using their hands) are separated from those who bow to drink. Three hundred men lap, while the rest kneel. God says He'll save Israel through the three hundred lappers. The others go home with provisions and trumpets, while the three hundred remain against Midianites below. That night, God tells Gideon to attack the camp, promising victory. If Gideon fears, he can go with servant Phurah to hear what the enemy says, which will strengthen his hands. Gideon goes with Phurah to the camp's edge. The Midianites, Amalekites, and eastern peoples lie in the valley like grasshoppers with countless camels like sand by the sea. Gideon hears a man telling his fellow a dream: a barley cake tumbled into Midian's camp, struck a tent, and overturned it. The friend interprets this as God delivering Midian into Gideon's hand. Gideon worships and returns to Israel's camp, announcing God's victory. He divides the three hundred into three companies, giving each man a trumpet, empty pitcher, and lamp inside. He instructs them to follow his lead. When he blows his trumpet at the middle watch, they should blow theirs all around, shouting "The sword of the LORD, and of Gideon." Gideon and his hundred men reach the camp's edge at the beginning of the middle watch, blow trumpets, and break pitchers. The three companies blow trumpets, break pitchers, hold lamps in left hands and trumpets in right hands, shouting "The sword of the LORD, and of Gideon." They stand around the camp. The enemy runs, cries, and flees. The LORD causes each man's sword to turn against his fellow throughout the host. The enemy flees to Beth-shittah, Zererath, Abel-meholah border, and Tabbath. Israel's men from Naphtali, Asher, and Manasseh pursue Midianites. Gideon sends messengers to Ephraim, asking them to take the waters at Beth-barah and Jordan. Ephraim's men gather, take those waters, and capture two Midianite princes - Oreb and Zeeb. They kill Oreb on rock Oreb and Zeeb at winepress of Zeeb, pursue Midian, and bring their heads to Gideon across Jordan.

### "The people that are with thee are too many for me to give the Midianites into their hands" — Divine Strategy

**Judges 7:2** — "And the LORD said unto Gideon, The people that are with thee are too many for me to give the Midianites into their hands, lest Israel vaunt themselves against me, saying, Mine own hand hath saved me"

- **2 Corinthians 12:9** — "And he said unto me, My grace is sufficient for thee: for my strength is made perfect in weakness. Most gladly therefore will I rather glory in my infirmities, that the power of Christ may rest upon me."
  - *Interpretation:* Paul teaches that God's power is perfected in human weakness, which aligns with God reducing Gideon's forces to ensure divine glory.

- **John 3:30** - "He must increase, but I must decrease."
  -Interpretation:* John the Baptist's statement reflects the principle that human strength must decrease for divine power to increase.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### TOO MANY
- **Meaning:** Sufficiently large to cause human pride
- **Context:** God's reason for reducing army size
- **Significance:** God ensures He receives all glory
* **New Testament parallel:** Strength in weakness (2 Corinthians 12:9)

### LAP WITH THE TONGUE
- **Meaning:** Drinking like a dog without kneeling
- **Context:** Character test for alertness and readiness
- **Significance:** Vigilance and military preparedness
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual alertness (1 Peter 5:8)

### BARLEY CAKE
- **Meaning:** Humble bread made from barley
- **Context:** Symbol of Gideon's humble origins
- **Significance:** God uses the humble to defeat the proud
* **New Testament parallel:** Humble instruments (1 Corinthians 1:27)

### EMPTY PITCHERS
- **Meaning:** Clay jars with nothing inside
- **Context:** Part of unconventional warfare strategy
- **Significance:** God uses empty vessels for His glory
* **Spiritual application:** Empty vessels for God (2 Timothy 2:21)

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 Then Jerubbaal, who is Gideon, and all the people that were with him, rose up early, and pitched beside the well of Harod: so that the host of the Midianites were on the north side of them, by the hill of Moreh, in the valley. 

2 And the LORD said unto Gideon, The people that are with thee are too many for me to give the Midianites into their hands, lest Israel vaunt themselves against me, saying, Mine own hand hath saved me. 

3 Now therefore go to, proclaim in the ears of the people, saying, Whosoever is fearful and afraid, let him return and depart early from mount Gilead. And there returned of the people twenty and two thousand; and there remained ten thousand. 

4 And the LORD said unto Gideon, The people are yet too many; bring them down unto the water, and I will try them for thee there: and it shall be, that of whom I say unto thee, This shall go with thee, the same shall go with thee; and of whomsoever I say unto thee, This shall not go with thee, the same shall not go. 

5 So he brought down the people unto the water: and the LORD said unto Gideon, Every one that lappeth of the water with his tongue, as a dog lappeth, him shalt thou set by himself; likewise every one that boweth down upon his knees to drink. 

6 And the number of them that lapped, putting their hand to their mouth, were three hundred men: but all the rest of the people bowed down upon their knees to drink water. 

7 And the LORD said unto Gideon, By the three hundred men that lapped will I save you, and deliver the Midianites into thine hand: and let all the other people go every man unto his place. 

8 So the people took victuals in their hand, and their trumpets: and he sent all the rest of Israel every man unto his tent, and the three hundred men: and the host of Midian was beneath him in the valley. 

9 And it came to pass the same night, that the LORD said unto him, Arise, get thee down unto the host; for I have delivered it into your hand. 

10 But if thou fear to go down, go thou with Phurah thy servant down to the host: 

11 And thou shalt hear what they say; and afterward shall thine hands be strengthened to go down unto the host. Then went he down with Phurah his servant unto the outside of the armed men that were in the host camp. 

12 And the Midianites and the Amalekites and all the children of the east lay along in the valley like grasshoppers for multitude; and their camels were without number, as the sand by the sea side for multitude. 

13 And when Gideon was come, he fell, and, behold, there was a man that told a dream unto his fellow, and said, Behold, I dreamed a dream, and, lo, a cake of barley bread tumbled into the host of Midian, and came unto a tent, and smote it that it fell, and overturned it, that the tent lay along. 

14 And his fellow answered and said, This is nothing else save the sword of Gideon the son of Joash, a man of Israel: for into his hand hath God delivered Midian, and all the host. 

15 And it was so, when Gideon heard the telling of the dream, and the interpretation thereof, that he worshipped, and returned into the host of Israel, and calling, Arise; for the LORD hath delivered into your hand the host of Midian. 

16 And he divided the three hundred men into three companies, and he put a trumpet in every man's hand, with empty pitchers, and lamps within the pitchers. 

17 And he said unto them, Look on me, and do likewise: and, behold, when I come to the outside of the camp, it shall be that, as I do, so shall ye do. 

18 When I blow with a trumpet, I and all that are with me, then blow ye the trumpets also on every side of all the camp, and say, The sword of the LORD, and of Gideon. 

19 So Gideon, and the hundred men that were with him, came unto the outside of the camp in the beginning of the middle watch; and they had but newly set the watch: and they blew the trumpets, and brake the pitchers that were in their hands. 

20 And the three companies blew the trumpets, and brake the pitchers, and held the lamps in their left hands, and the trumpets in their right hands to blow withal: and they cried, The sword of the LORD, and of Gideon. 

21 And they stood every man in his place round about the camp: and their hands were strengthened, and all the host ran, and cried, and fled. 

22 And the three hundred blew the trumpets, and the LORD set every man's sword against his fellow, even throughout all the host: and the host fled to Beth-shittah in Zererath, and to the border of Abel-meholah, unto Tabbath. 

23 And the men of Israel gathered themselves together out of Naphtali, and out of Asher, and out of all Manasseh, and pursued after the Midianites. 

24 And Gideon sent messengers throughout all mount Ephraim, saying, Come down against the Midianites, and take before them the waters unto Beth-barah and Jordan. Then all the men of Ephraim gathered themselves together, and took the waters unto Beth-barah and Jordan. 

25 And they took two princes of the Midianites, Oreb and Zeeb; and they slew Oreb upon the rock Oreb, and Zeeb they slew at the winepress of Zeeb, and pursued Midian, and brought the heads of Oreb and Zeeb to Gideon on the other side Jordan.

**Chapter 7 demonstrates God's strategy of using weakness to display divine strength:** God's reduction of Gideon's army from 32,000 to 300 shows that divine victory requires human dependence, not strength. The first reduction removes the fearful, establishing that courage is essential for God's battles. The water testing separates the vigilant from the casual, showing that God values readiness and alertness in His service. The barley cake dream reveals that God uses humble means (barley was poor people's food) to defeat the proud, demonstrating His preference for using the weak to shame the strong. The unconventional warfare strategy - trumpets, empty pitchers, and lamps - shows that God's weapons are not conventional but supernatural. The enemy's panic and self-destruction demonstrate that God fights battles in ways humans cannot understand. Ephraim's participation shows that after the initial victory, others can join in completing the work. The capture of Oreb and Zeeb (whose names mean "raven" and "wolf") shows the defeat of scavenging predators. This chapter establishes crucial principles: God reduces human strength to display His power, He tests character rather than appearance, He uses the humble to defeat the proud, His methods are supernatural not conventional, and He allows others to share in victory after the initial breakthrough. The chapter shows that divine strategy often appears foolish to human wisdom but accomplishes what human strength cannot achieve. It demonstrates that God's battles require divine weapons and divine timing, and that the victory ultimately belongs to the LORD.
