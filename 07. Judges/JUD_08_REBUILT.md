## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Ephraim's men complain sharply that Gideon didn't call them to fight Midianites. Gideon diplomatically replies that Ephraim's gleaning is better than Abi-ezer's vintage, and that God delivered Midian's princes Oreb and Zeeb into Ephraim's hands. This appeases their anger. Gideon and his three hundred men, faint but pursuing, cross Jordan. He asks Succoth's men for bread for his weary men pursuing Midianite kings Zebah and Zalmunna. Succoth's princes refuse, asking if the kings are already captured. Gideon vows to tear their flesh with thorns when God delivers the kings. Penuel's men answer similarly, so Gideon vows to break their tower when he returns. Zebah and Zalmunna are in Karkor with fifteen thousand men - all that remain of the eastern hosts after 120,000 swordsmen fell. Gideon attacks the secure host by way of tent-dwellers east of Nobah and Jogbehah. When the kings flee, Gideon captures them and defeats their host. Before sunrise, Gideon returns from battle, captures a young man from Succoth, and learns the identities of Succoth's 67 princes and elders. He confronts Succoth's men with the captured kings, reminding them of their refusal to help his weary men. He takes the elders, thorns, and briers, and teaches the men of Succoth with them. He breaks down Penuel's tower and kills the city's men. Gideon asks the kings what kind of men they killed at Tabor. They answer they resembled king's children. Gideon reveals they were his mother's sons and brothers, saying he wouldn't have killed them if they'd been spared. He commands his firstborn Jether to kill the kings, but the youth fears and won't draw his sword. The kings tell Gideon to kill them himself, as a man's strength matches his character. Gideon kills them and takes their camel neck ornaments. Israel's men offer Gideon and his descendants rule over them for delivering them from Midian. Gideon refuses, declaring the LORD shall rule over them. He requests each man's earrings from the prey. The people willingly give them, casting 1,700 shekels of gold earrings into a garment, plus ornaments, collars, purple raiment, and camel chains. Gideon makes an ephod from the gold and places it in Ophrah, where all Israel worships it, becoming a snare to Gideon and his house. Midian is subdued, and Israel enjoys forty years of peace in Gideon's days. Jerubbaal returns to his home, fathering seventy sons through many wives, and Abimelech through his Shechem concubine. Gideon dies in good old age and is buried in Joash's sepulchre in Ophrah. Immediately after his death, Israel turns to Baalim and Baal-berith, forgetting the LORD who delivered them from all enemies, and showing no kindness to Gideon's house despite his goodness to Israel.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

**Common misinterpretation:** This is about rejecting all government
**Biblical context:** This is about rejecting human kingship in favor of direct divine rule. Gideon isn't an anarchist but recognizes that God should be Israel's ultimate authority.

### "Gideon made an ephod thereof, and put it in his city... which thing became a snare unto Gideon"
**Common misinterpretation:* This shows Gideon's complete fall into idolatry
**Biblical context:** This shows how even faithful leaders can create spiritual stumbling blocks. The ephod becomes a snare to Israel and Gideon's house, but Gideon himself isn't necessarily the primary worshipper.
