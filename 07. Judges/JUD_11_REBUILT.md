## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Jephthah the Gileadite, a mighty warrior, is Gilead's son by a harlot. Gilead's legitimate sons grow up and expel Jephthah, denying him inheritance because he's a "strange woman's" son. Jephthah flees to Tob land, where vain men gather around him. When Ammon makes war against Israel, Gilead's elders fetch Jephthah from Tob to be their captain against Ammon. Jephthah reminds them they hated and expelled him, asking why they come now in distress. The elders promise to make him head over all Gilead inhabitants if he leads them to victory. Jephthah agrees, and the people make him head and captain. He utters his words before the LORD at Mizpeh. Jephthah sends messengers to Ammon's king, asking why he attacks Israel. The Ammonite king claims Israel took his land from Arnon to Jabbok to Jordan when coming from Egypt, demanding restoration. Jephthah responds that Israel didn't take Moabite or Ammonite land. He recounts Israel's journey from Egypt through wilderness to Red Sea and Kadesh, where Edom and Moab refused passage. Israel traveled east of Moab without entering their territory, then asked Sihon king of the Amorites for passage. Sihon gathered forces to fight Israel, but God delivered Sihon into Israel's hands, giving them possession of Amorite land from Arnon to Jabbok and wilderness to Jordan. Jephthah argues that since God dispossessed the Amorites for Israel, Ammon has no claim. He challenges Ammon to possess what their god Chemosh gives them, while Israel will possess what God drives out. Jephthah asks if Ammon is better than Balak who never fought Israel during their 300-year occupation of the area, questioning why they didn't recover the land earlier. He declares his innocence and calls the LORD as Judge between Israel and Ammon. The Ammonite king rejects Jephthah's words. The Spirit of the LORD comes upon Jephthah, who passes through Gilead, Manasseh, and Mizpeh to fight Ammon. Jephthah vows that if God delivers Ammon into his hands, whatever comes from his house to meet him upon his return will be the LORD's and offered as burnt offering. God delivers Ammon into Jephthah's hands, and he defeats them from Aroer to Minnith, taking twenty cities and the plain of vineyards with great slaughter, subduing Ammon. When Jephthah returns home, his only daughter comes out to meet him with timbrels and dances. He tears his clothes, lamenting that she has brought him low and troubled him, since he opened his mouth to the LORD and cannot go back. His daughter accepts his vow, asking for two months to bewail her virginity on the mountains with her companions. Jephthah agrees, and she spends two months mourning. At the end, she returns, and Jephthah fulfills his vow - she knew no man. This becomes Israel's custom: daughters yearly lament Jephthah's daughter for four days.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### MIGHTY MAN OF VALOUR
- **Meaning:** Warrior of great courage and strength
- **Context:** Jephthah's character description
- **Significance:** God values courage regardless of social status
* **New Testament parallel:** Mighty in spirit (Luke 1:69)

### STRANGE WOMAN
- **Meaning:** Woman of questionable background or status
- **Context:** Jephthah's mother, cause of his rejection
- **Significance:** Social prejudice based on birth circumstances
* **Spiritual application:** God's acceptance regardless of background (Galatians 3:28)

### VOW
- **Meaning:** Solemn promise to God
- **Context:** Jephthah's rash promise for victory
- **Significance:** Rash promises can have tragic consequences
* **New Testament parallel:** Careful speech (James 5:12)

### BURNT OFFERING
- **Meaning:** Sacrifice completely consumed by fire
- **Context:** Tragic fulfillment of Jephthah's vow
- **Significance:** Complete devotion to God, even at great cost
* **Christological parallel:** Christ's sacrifice (Hebrews 10:12)

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "He was the son of an harlot"
**Common misinterpretation:** This disqualifies Jephthah from leadership
**Biblical context:** This shows God's grace in using people regardless of their background. Jephthah's character and courage, not his birth, determine his fitness for leadership.

### "And she knew no man"
**Common misinterpretation:* This means she died as a virgin
**Biblical context:** This phrase indicates she remained unmarried and chaste, but doesn't necessarily specify death. The focus is on her virginity as the sacrifice, not necessarily her death.

**Chapter 11 demonstrates God's use of unlikely people and the danger of rash vows:** Jephthah's background as a harlot's son who is rejected by his family shows that God calls leaders based on character and courage, not social status. His exile to Tob and gathering of "vain men" shows that God can work through imperfect circumstances and people. The elders' desperation shows that human need often overcomes prejudice. Jephthah's challenge to their hypocrisy shows wisdom in confronting past wrong while being willing to help. His diplomatic message demonstrates knowledge of Israel's history and ability to argue from divine perspective rather than military might. The detailed historical defense shows that Israel's conquest was legitimate and divinely sanctioned, not aggression. Jephthah's appeal to God as Judge shows confidence in divine justice over human courts. The Spirit's empowerment shows that divine enablement follows obedience. The tragic vow demonstrates the danger of making rash promises to God, even with good intentions. The daughter's submission shows remarkable faith and acceptance of God's will, even at great personal cost. The two-month mourning period shows respect for human grief while maintaining commitment to divine promises. The annual lamentation shows that tragic events can become teaching moments for future generations. This chapter establishes crucial principles: God uses unlikely people, human prejudice cannot thwart divine calling, wisdom requires confronting past wrong, divine justice ultimately prevails, rash promises have serious consequences, and faith accepts God's will even at great cost. It demonstrates that God's grace extends to all backgrounds, that leadership requires both courage and wisdom, and that faithfulness sometimes requires painful sacrifice. The chapter shows that human weakness doesn't disqualify from divine service, but that human weakness can lead to tragic consequences that require divine grace to endure.
