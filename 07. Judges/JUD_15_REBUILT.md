## CHAPTER SUMMARY

During wheat harvest, Samson visits his wife with a kid, wanting to go into her chamber. Her father refuses, explaining he thought Samson hated her, so he gave her to Samson's companion. He offers the younger sister instead. Samson declares he'll be more blameless than the Philistines despite doing them displeasure. He catches three hundred foxes, ties them tail-to-tail with firebrands between their tails, sets the brands on fire, and releases them into the Philistines' standing corn, burning shocks, standing corn, vineyards, and olives. The Philistines learn Samson did this because his wife was given to his companion. They come and burn his wife and father with fire. Samson vows revenge, then strikes them "hip and thigh" with great slaughter and dwells on Etam rock. The Philistines camp in Judah, spreading in Lehi. Judah's men ask why they've come, and the Philistines explain they've come to bind Samson to do to him what he did to them. Three thousand Judah men go to Etam rock and confront Samson, noting Philistine rule and asking what he's done. Samson replies he did to them as they did to him. Judah explains they've come to bind him for the Philistines. Samson makes them swear not to attack him themselves. They agree to bind him fast and deliver him without killing him. They bind him with two new cords and bring him from the rock. At Lehi, the Philistines shout against him. The Spirit of the LORD comes mightily upon him, and his bonds become like burnt flax, falling from his hands. He finds a fresh jawbone of an ass, takes it, and kills a thousand men. Samson declares he slew a thousand men with an ass's jawbone, heaps upon heaps. He casts away the jawbone and names the place Ramath-lehi. Being very thirsty, he calls to the LORD, acknowledging God's great deliverance but fearing he'll die of thirst and fall to the uncircumcised. God cleaves a hollow place in the jaw, water comes out, and Samson drinks, reviving. He names it En-hakkore in Lehi. Samson judges Israel twenty years during Philistine oppression.

**Judges 15:4-5** — "And Samson went and caught three hundred foxes, and took firebrands, and turned tail to tail, and put a firebrand in the midst between two tails: And when he had set the brands on fire, he let them go into the standing corn of the Philistines, and burnt up both the shocks, and also the standing corn, with the vineyards and olives"

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### FOXES
- **Meaning:** Small wild canines
- **Context:** Samson's weapon of agricultural destruction
- **Significance:** Creative but destructive revenge method
* **Spiritual application:** Destructive anger (James 1:20)

### FIREBRANDS
- **Meaning:** Burning torches
- **Context:** Tools for crop destruction
- **Significance:** Perverting useful tools for destructive purposes
* **Spiritual application:** Misused gifts (1 Corinthians 12)

### JAWBONE
- **Meaning:** Lower jaw bone
- **Context:** Unconventional weapon for killing thousand men
- **Significance:** God using humble means for great victory
* **New Testament parallel:** Weak made strong (2 Corinthians 12:9)

### EN-HAKKORE
- **Meaning:** "Well of the caller" or "spring of the caller"
- **Context:** Place where God provided water for thirsty Samson
- **Significance:** God's provision in desperate need
* **Spiritual application:** God's provision (Philippians 4:19)

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Now shall I be more blameless than the Philistines, though I do them a displeasure"
**Common misinterpretation:** This shows righteous justification
**Biblical context:** This is self-justification for revenge. Samson rationalizes his destructive actions as being "more blameless" than the Philistines, but this is human rationalization, not divine approval.

### "With the jawbone of an ass, heaps upon heaps"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about celebrating violence
**Biblical context:** This is Samson's boast about victory through divine enablement. While God gave the victory, Samson's pride in the method shows his character flaws even in divine success.

**Chapter 15 demonstrates the cycle of revenge, divine empowerment, and human weakness in Samson's life:** Samson's attempt to reconcile with his wife shows his continued attachment despite her betrayal. His father-in-law's explanation about giving the wife to his companion shows the cultural practice of treating women as property. Samson's declaration of being "more blameless" shows his self-justification for revenge. The fox-and-firebrand attack demonstrates creative but destructive revenge that harms the Philistines economically but escalates the conflict. The Philistines' burning of his wife and father shows how personal conflicts have deadly consequences. Samson's "hip and thigh" slaughter shows his tendency toward excessive violence. His retreat to Etam rock shows both wisdom in avoiding confrontation and cowardice in facing consequences. Judah's confrontation shows how Samson's actions affect his own people, who suffer under Philistine rule. Their willingness to bind him shows their fear of Philistine power and desire for peace at any cost. Samson's demand for protection from his own people shows his distrust even of fellow Israelites. The Spirit's mighty coming shows divine enablement for deliverance when human strength fails. The jawbone victory demonstrates God's use of humble means for great victories and Samson's pride in his own strength. His boast about the victory shows his failure to give proper credit to God. Samson's thirst and cry to God show a moment of humility and dependence on divine provision. God's miraculous water from the jawbone shows His provision even in desperate situations and His willingness to answer prayer despite human weakness. The twenty-year judgeship shows that despite his flaws, Samson fulfills his calling to begin delivering Israel from Philistines. This chapter establishes crucial patterns: revenge escalates conflicts, divine power works through human weakness, human pride follows divine victories, God provides in desperate need, and flawed leaders can still fulfill divine purposes. It demonstrates that Samson's calling is genuine but his character consistently undermines his effectiveness, requiring repeated divine intervention.
