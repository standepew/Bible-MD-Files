## CHAPTER SUMMARY

In the days when judges ruled, famine struck the land. Elimelech of Bethlehem-judah went to sojourn in Moab with his wife Naomi and sons Mahlon and Chilion. They were Ephrathites of Bethlehem-judah who settled in Moab. Elimelech died, leaving Naomi with two sons. The sons married Moabite women - Orpah and Ruth - and lived there about ten years. Both Mahlon and Chilion died, leaving Naomi without husband or sons. Having heard the LORD had provided bread for His people, Naomi arose with her daughters-in-law to return from Moab to Judah. Naomi urged her daughters-in-law to return to their mothers' houses, praying the LORD would deal kindly with them as they had dealt with the dead and with her, and that each would find rest in her husband's house. She kissed them, and they wept. They said they would return with her to her people. Naomi asked why they would go with her when she had no more sons in her womb to be their husbands. She told them to return, being too old for a husband. Even if she married and bore sons that night, they wouldn't wait for them to grow up to have husbands. It grieved her that the LORD's hand was against her. They wept again. Orpah kissed Naomi goodbye, but Ruth clung to her. Naomi said Ruth's sister-in-law had returned to her people and gods, telling Ruth to follow her. Ruth begged not to be left or to stop following: wherever Naomi went, she would go; wherever Naomi lodged, she would lodge; Naomi's people would be her people, Naomi's God her God. Where Naomi died, Ruth would die and be buried. May the LORD deal with her and more if anything but death separated them. Seeing Ruth was determined, Naomi stopped speaking. They went to Bethlehem, where the whole city was stirred, asking if this was Naomi. She said to call her Mara, not Naomi, for the Almighty had dealt bitterly with her. She went out full but returned empty, with the LORD testifying against her and the Almighty afflicting her. Naomi and Ruth the Moabitess returned from Moab to Bethlehem at barley harvest's beginning.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### FAMINE
- **Meaning:** Extreme scarcity of food
- **Context:** God's judgment during judges period
- **Significance:** Test of faith leading to migration
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual hunger (Matthew 5:6)

### SOJOURN
- **Meaning:** Temporary residence as foreigner
- **Context:** Elimelech's move to Moab
- **Significance:** Living as strangers in foreign lands
* **Spiritual application:** Pilgrims and strangers (1 Peter 2:11)

### EPHRATHITES
- **Meaning:** People from Ephrath (ancient Bethlehem)
- **Context:** Family's geographical and tribal identity
- **Significance:** Connection to Davidic lineage
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual heritage (Hebrews 11)

### MARA
- **Meaning:** "Bitter"
- **Context:** Naomi's new name reflecting her suffering
- **Significance:** Human perspective on divine providence
* **Spiritual application:** Bitterness turned to sweetness (Exodus 15:23)

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 Now it came to pass in the days when the judges ruled, that there was a famine in the land. And a certain man of Beth-lehem-judah went to sojourn in the country of Moab, he, and his wife, and his two sons. 

2 And the name of the man was Elimelech, and the name of his wife Naomi, and the name of his two sons Mahlon and Chilion, Ephrathites of Beth-lehem-judah. And they came into the country of Moab, and continued there. 

3 And Elimelech Naomi's husband died; and she was left, and her two sons. 

4 And they took them wives of the women of Moab; the name of the one was Orpah, and the name of the other Ruth: and they dwelled there about ten years. 

5 And Mahlon and Chilion died also both of them; and the woman was left of her two sons and her husband. 

6 Then she arose with her daughters in law, that she might return from the country of Moab: for she had heard in the country of Moab how that the LORD had visited his people in giving them bread. 

7 Wherefore she went forth out of the place where she was, and her two daughters in law with her; and they went on the way to return unto the land of Judah. 

8 And Naomi said unto her two daughters in law, Go, return each to her mother's house: the LORD deal kindly with you, as ye have dealt with the dead, and with me. 

9 The LORD grant you that ye may find rest, each of you in the house of her husband. Then she kissed them; and they lifted up their voice, and wept. 

10 And they said unto her, Surely we will return with thee unto thy people. 

11 And Naomi said, Turn again, my daughters: why will ye go with me? are there yet any more sons in my womb, that they may be your husbands? 

12 Turn again, my daughters, go your way; for I am too old to have an husband. If I should say, I have hope, if I should have an husband also to night, and should also bear sons; 

13 Would ye tarry for them till they were grown? would ye stay for them from having husbands? nay, my daughters; for it grieveth me much for your sakes that the hand of the LORD is gone out against me. 

14 And they lifted up their voice, and wept again: and Orpah kissed her mother in law; but Ruth clave unto her. 

15 And she said, Behold, thy sister in law is gone back unto her people, and unto her gods: return thou after thy sister in law. 

16 And Ruth said, Intreat me not to leave thee, or to return from following after thee: for whither thou goest, I will go; and where thou lodgest, I will lodge: thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God: 

17 Where thou diest, will I die, and there will I be buried: the LORD do so to me, and more also, if ought but death part thee and me. 

18 When she saw that she was stedfastly minded to go with her, then she left speaking unto her. 

19 So they two went until they came to Beth-lehem. And it came to pass, when they were in Beth-lehem, that all the city was moved about them, and they said, Is this Naomi? 

20 And she said unto them, Call me not Naomi, call me Mara: for the Almighty hath dealt very bitterly with me. 

21 I went out full, and the LORD hath brought me home again empty: why then call ye me Naomi, seeing the LORD hath testified against me, and the Almighty hath afflicted me? 

22 So Naomi returned, and Ruth the Moabitess, her daughter in law, with her, which returned out of the country of Moab: and they came to Beth-lehem in the beginning of barley harvest.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Whither thou goest, I will go; and where thou lodgest, I will lodge"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about romantic love
**Biblical context:** This is Ruth's covenant commitment to Naomi's God and people, not romantic love. It's a spiritual conversion and loyalty pledge.

### "Call me not Naomi, call me Mara: for the Almighty hath dealt very bitterly with me"
**Common misinterpretation:* This shows Naomi's loss of faith
**Biblical context:** This shows Naomi's honest expression of grief while still returning to God's people. Her bitterness is human emotion, not necessarily loss of faith.

**Chapter 1 demonstrates God's providence working through human suffering and foreign inclusion:** The story begins during the chaotic judges period, showing God's grace operating even in spiritual anarchy. The famine represents God's judgment that tests faith, leading Elimelech to make the difficult decision to sojourn in Moab. The deaths of all the men represent complete human helplessness and the end of human plans. Naomi's decision to return upon hearing the LORD has provided bread shows faith in God's provision despite personal loss. Her counsel to her daughters-in-law shows wisdom in releasing them from obligations while blessing them. Orpah's departure represents the natural choice to return to familiar culture and religion. Ruth's covenant commitment represents extraordinary faith and loyalty that transcends cultural and religious boundaries. Her declaration that Naomi's God will be her God shows true conversion to faith in the LORD. Naomi's bitterness shows honest human emotion in the face of suffering, yet she still returns to Bethlehem, showing underlying faith. The city's response shows the communal nature of Israelite society. Naomi's name change to Mara reflects her perspective on divine providence, though God's greater plan is at work. The arrival at barley harvest's beginning shows God's timing and provision. This chapter establishes crucial themes: God's providence works through suffering, foreign inclusion in God's people, extraordinary faith through covenant commitment, and divine timing in provision. It demonstrates that God's redemptive plan includes Gentiles who demonstrate true faith, that human suffering can lead to divine blessing, and that loyalty to God and His people transcends cultural boundaries. The chapter shows that even in the darkest circumstances, God is working to fulfill His greater purposes, including establishing the lineage of King David and ultimately the Messiah. Ruth's inclusion points forward to the inclusion of all nations in God's redemptive plan through Christ.
