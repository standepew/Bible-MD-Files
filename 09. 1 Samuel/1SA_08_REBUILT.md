## CHAPTER SUMMARY

When Samuel was old, he made his sons judges over Israel. His firstborn Joel and second Abiah judged in Beer-sheba, but his sons didn't walk in his ways, turning aside after lucre, taking bribes, and perverting judgment. All Israel's elders gathered and came to Samuel at Ramah, saying he was old and his sons didn't walk in his ways, asking to make them a king like all nations. The request displeased Samuel, so he prayed to the LORD. The LORD said to hearken to the people's voice, for they hadn't rejected Samuel but rejected God from reigning over them. According to all their works since Egypt - forsaking God and serving other gods - they did the same to Samuel. He should hearken to their voice but protest solemnly and show them the manner of king that would reign over them. Samuel told all the LORD's words to the people asking for a king. The king would take their sons for chariots, horsemen, and running before chariots; appoint captains over thousands and fifties; set them to ear his ground, reap his harvest, make war instruments and chariot instruments. He would take daughters for confectionaries, cooks, and bakers. He would take fields, vineyards, oliveyards (even the best) for his servants. He would take the tenth of seed and vineyards for officers and servants. He would take menservants, maidservants, goodliest young men, and asses for his work. He would take the tenth of sheep, making them his servants. They would cry out that day because of their chosen king, but the LORD wouldn't hear them. Nevertheless, the people refused to obey Samuel, saying they wanted a king to be like all nations, with their king to judge them, go before them, and fight their battles. Samuel heard all their words and rehearsed them to the LORD. The LORD said to hearken to their voice and make them a king. Samuel told Israel's men to go to their cities.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### LUCRE
- **Meaning:** Money, gain, profit
- **Context:** Samuel's sons' motivation for corruption
- **Significance:** Corrupting influence of greed
* **New Testament parallel:** Love of money (1 Timothy 6:10)

### NATIONS
- **Meaning:** Other countries with human governments
- **Context:** Israel's desire to be like other nations
- **Significance:** Rejection of divine government
* **Spiritual application:** Worldly conformity (Romans 12:2)

### CHARIOTS
- **Meaning:** Military vehicles
- **Context:** King's military requirements
- **Significance:** Human military power vs. divine power
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual warfare (2 Corinthians 10:4)

### TENTH
- **Meaning:** Ten percent tax or tithe
- **Context:** King's taxation of people
- **Significance:** Burden of human government
* **Spiritual application:** God's tithe (Malachi 3:10)

1 And it came to pass, when Samuel was old, that he made his sons judges over Israel. 

2 Now the name of his firstborn was Joel; and the name of his second, Abiah: they were judges in Beer-sheba. 

3 And his sons walked not in his ways, but turned aside after lucre, and took bribes, and perverted judgment. 

4 Then all the elders of Israel gathered themselves together, and came to Samuel unto Ramah, 

5 And said unto him, Behold, thou art old, and thy sons walk not in thy ways: now make us a king to judge us like all the nations. 

6 But the thing displeased Samuel, when they said, Give us a king to judge us. And Samuel prayed unto the LORD.

8 According to all the works which they have done since the day that I brought them up out of Egypt even unto this day, wherewith they have forsaken me, and served other gods, so do they also unto thee. 

9 Now therefore hearken unto their voice: howbeit yet protest solemnly unto them, and shew them the manner of the king that shall reign over them. 

10 And Samuel told all the words of the LORD unto the people that asked of them a king. 

11 And he said, This will be the manner of the king that shall reign over you: He will take your sons, and appoint them for himself, for his's chariots, and to be his horsemen; and some shall run before his chariots. 

12 And he will appoint him captains over thousands, and captains over fifties; and will set them to ear his ground, and to reap his harvest, and to make his instruments of war, and instruments of his chariots. 

13 And he will take your daughters to be confectionaries, and to be cooks, and to be bakers. 

14 And he will take your fields, and your vineyards, and your oliveyards, even the best of them, and give them to his servants. 

16 And he will take the tenth of your seed, and of your vineyards, and and give to his officers, and to his servants. 

17 And he will take your menservants, and your maidservants, and your goodliest young men, and your asses, and put them to his work. 

18 He will take the tenth of your sheep: and ye shall be his servants. 

19 And ye shall cry out in that day because of your king which ye shall have chosen you; and the LORD will not hear you in that day.  

20 Nevertheless the people refused to obey the voice of Samuel; and they said, Nay; but we will have a king over us;  

21 That we also may be like all the nations; and that our king may judge us, and go out before us, and fight our battles.  

22 And Samuel heard all the words of the people, and he rehearsed them in the ears of the LORD.  

23 And the LORD said to Samuel, Hearken unto their voice, and make them a king. And Samuel said unto the men of Israel, Go ye every man unto his city.

**Chapter 8 demonstrates the human tendency to reject divine government in favor of human authority:** Samuel's old age and his sons' corruption show the failure of the current leadership system. The sons' turning aside after lucre, taking bribes, and perverting judgment shows how power corrupts when not properly exercised. The elders' gathering shows corporate recognition of leadership failure. Their request for a king "like all the nations" shows rejection of Israel's unique theocratic system in favor of worldly government. Samuel's displeasure shows his understanding that this request represents spiritual failure. His prayer shows proper response to difficult situations - seeking divine guidance. God's explanation reveals the spiritual reality behind Israel's request: they're rejecting God's kingship, not just Samuel's leadership. The comparison to their history since Egypt shows this as a pattern of rejecting God for other gods. God's instruction to hearken but protest shows His permissive will - allowing human choice while warning of consequences. Samuel's detailed warning about the king's rights shows the cost of human government - military conscription, forced labor, taxation, loss of property and freedom. The specific examples (sons for chariots, daughters for service, fields taken, tithes demanded) show comprehensive loss of freedom. The warning that they would cry out but God wouldn't hear shows the consequences of rejecting divine protection. The people's refusal to heed the warning shows human stubbornness and desire to be like others. Their desire to be like nations shows conformity to worldly patterns. Their desire for a king to fight battles shows reliance on human military power rather than divine protection. God's permission to give them a king shows His respect for human choice even when it's wrong. This chapter establishes crucial themes: human leadership fails without divine guidance, people often reject divine government for human authority, human government brings loss of freedom, and God allows human choice but warns of consequences. It demonstrates that the desire to be like others often leads to rejecting God's best, that human authority always costs more than promised, and that rejecting divine kingship brings divine silence in times of trouble. The chapter shows the transition from theocracy to monarchy as Israel's choice, not God's preference. It illustrates the principle that when people reject divine leadership, God often allows them to experience the consequences of their choice to teach them the value of divine rule. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfect King who combines divine authority with perfect leadership, showing that only divine government can truly provide what human government promises but fails to deliver. The people's desire to be like nations contrasts with Christ's call to be a holy nation, separate from worldly patterns.
