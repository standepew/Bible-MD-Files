## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Samuel told Israel he had hearkened to their voice and made a king over them. The king now walked before them; Samuel was old and grayheaded with his sons among them, having walked before them from childhood. He presented himself as witness before the LORD and His anointed, asking if he had taken ox or ass, defrauded or oppressed anyone, or received bribes to blind his eyes, offering to restore anything. They said he hadn't defrauded, oppressed, or taken anything. Samuel said the LORD and His anointed were witnesses they found nothing in his hand, and they agreed. Samuel told Israel the LORD advanced Moses and Aaron, bringing their fathers from Egypt. He asked them to stand while he reasoned of the LORD's righteous acts. When Jacob came to Egypt and fathers cried to the LORD, He sent Moses and Aaron to bring them out to this place. When they forgot the LORD, He sold them to Sisera, Philistines, and Moab's king who fought them. They cried to the LORD, confessing sin in forsaking Him for Baalim and Ashtaroth, asking deliverance to serve Him. The LORD sent Jerubbaal, Bedan, Jephthah, and Samuel, delivering them from enemies so they dwelt safely. When Nahash king of Ammon came, they asked for a king instead of the LORD their God. Samuel told them to behold their chosen and desired king, whom the LORD set over them. If they feared and served the LORD, obeying His voice and not rebelling, both they and their king would continue following the LORD. But if they rebelled against the LORD's commandment, His hand would be against them as with their fathers. He told them to stand and see the great thing the LORD would do. Since it was wheat harvest, he would call to the LORD who would send thunder and rain so they'd perceive their wickedness in asking for a king. Samuel called to the LORD, who sent thunder and rain that day. The people greatly feared the LORD and Samuel, asking Samuel to pray they wouldn't die for adding this evil to their sins. Samuel told them not to fear despite their wickedness, but to turn not aside from following the LORD, serving Him with all their heart and not going after vain things that can't profit or deliver. The LORD wouldn't forsake His people for His great name's sake since it pleased Him to make them His people. Samuel said he wouldn't sin against the LORD by ceasing to pray for them, but would teach the good and right way. They should only fear the LORD and serve Him in truth with all their heart, considering His great works. If they still did wickedly, both they and their king would be consumed.

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 And Samuel said unto all Israel, Behold, I have hearkened unto your voice in all that ye said unto me, and have made a king over you.  

2 And now, behold, the king walketh before you: and I am old and grayheaded; and, behold, my sons are with you: and I have walked before you from my childhood unto this day.  

3 Behold, here I am: witness against me before the LORD, and before his anointed: whose ox have I taken? or whose ass have I taken? or whom have I defrauded? whom have I oppressed? or of whose hand have I received any bribe to blind mine eyes therewith? and I will restore it you.  

4 And they said, Thou hast not defrauded us, nor oppressed us, neither hast thou taken ought of any man's hand.  

5 And he said unto them, The LORD is witness against you, and his anointed is witness this day, that ye have not found ought in my hand. And they answered, He is witness.  

6 And Samuel said unto the people, It is the LORD that advanced Moses and Aaron, and that brought your fathers up out of the land of Egypt.  

7 Now therefore stand still, that I may reason with you before the LORD of all the righteous acts of the LORD, which he did to you and to your fathers.  

8 When Jacob was come into Egypt, and your fathers cried unto the LORD, then the LORD sent Moses and Aaron, which brought forth your fathers out of Egypt, and made them dwell in this place.  

9 And when they forgat the LORD their God, he sold them into the hand of Sisera, captain of the host of Hazor, and into the heart of the Philistines, and to the the hand of the king of Moab, and they fought against them.  

10 And they cried unto the LORD, and said, We have sinned, because we have forsaken the good LORD, and have served Baalim and Ashtaroth: but now deliver us out of the hand of our enemies, and we will serve thee.  

11 And the LORD sent Jerubbaal, and Bedan, and Jephthah, and Samuel, and delivered you out of the hand of your enemies on every side, and ye dwelled safe.  

12 And when ye saw that Nahash the king of the children of Ammon came against you, ye said unto me, Nay; but a king shall reign over us: when the LORD your God was your king.  

13 Now therefore behold the king whom ye have chosen, and whom ye have desired! and, behold, the LORD hath set a king over you.  

14 If ye will fear the LORD, and serve him, and obey his voice, and not rebel against the commandment of the LORD, then shall both ye and also the king that reigneth over you continue following the LORD your God:  

15 But if ye will not obey the voice of the LORD, but rebel against the commandment of the LORD, then shall the hand of the LORD be against you, as it was against your fathers.  

16 Now therefore stand and see this great thing, which the LORD will do before your eyes.  

17 Is it not wheat harvest to day? I will call unto the LORD, and he shall send thunder and rain; that ye may perceive and see that your wickedness is great, which ye have done in the sight of the LORD, in asking you a king.  

18 So Samuel called unto the LORD; and the LORD sent thunder and rain that day: and all the people greatly feared the LORD and Samuel.  

19 And all the people said unto Samuel, Pray for thy servants unto the LORD thy God, that we die not: for we have added unto all our sins this evil, to ask us a king.  

20 And Samuel said unto the people, Fear not: ye have done all this wickedness: yet turn not aside from following the LORD, but serve the LORD with all your heart:  

21 And turn ye not aside: for then should ye go after vain things, which cannot profit nor deliver; for they are vain.  

22 For the LORD will not forsake his people for his great name's sake: because it hath pleased the LORD to make you his people.  

23 Moreover as for me, God forbid that I should sin against the LORD in ceasing to pray for you: but I will teach you the good and the right way:  

24 Only fear the LORD, and serve him in truth with all your heart: for consider how great things he hath done for you.  

25 But if ye shall still do wickedly, ye shall be consumed, both ye and your king.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Whose ox have I taken? or whose ass have I taken?"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about personal inventory
**Biblical context:** This is Samuel's integrity testimony - he's asking Israel to examine his entire leadership for any corruption, showing transparency and accountability.

### "I will call unto the LORD, and he shall send thunder and rain"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about weather control
**Biblical context:** This is a divine sign to confirm God's displeasure at Israel's request for a king - thunder and rain during wheat harvest was supernatural.

**Chapter 12 demonstrates Samuel's faithful farewell address, reviewing Israel's history and warning about their choice of king:** Samuel begins by acknowledging he has fulfilled their request for a king despite his earlier warnings. His statement about being old and grayheaded shows his long service and transition to new leadership. His integrity testimony shows transparent leadership - inviting examination of his entire service for any corruption. The people's confirmation of his integrity shows his blameless leadership record. Calling the LORD and His anointed as witnesses shows divine accountability. His historical review shows Israel's pattern of forgetting God, being oppressed, crying out, and receiving deliverance. The reference to Moses and Aaron shows the foundation of Israel's deliverance. The cycle of forgetting God, serving Baalim and Ashtaroth, being sold to enemies, then crying out shows Israel's repeated spiritual failure. The mention of deliverers (Jerubbaal/Gideon, Bedan, Jephthah, Samuel) shows God's faithfulness in sending leaders. The statement that they rejected the LORD as king in favor of human monarchy shows the spiritual reality behind their request. His warning about obedience and rebellion shows the conditional nature of blessing under the new system. The promise that both people and king would continue following the LORD if obedient shows that monarchy could work under divine blessing. The warning of divine judgment if rebellious shows that the new system doesn't change God's standards. The thunder and rain during wheat harvest shows supernatural confirmation of God's displeasure. The people's fear shows recognition of divine power and their error. Their request for prayer shows acknowledgment of sin and need for intercession. Samuel's encouragement not to fear but to continue serving God shows grace despite their failure. His warning against vain things shows the futility of false worship. His promise that God won't forsake them for His name's sake shows divine faithfulness despite human failure. His commitment to continue praying and teaching shows his ongoing spiritual leadership. His final warning that both people and king would be consumed if wicked shows the seriousness of obedience. This chapter establishes crucial themes: leadership requires integrity and transparency, God's faithfulness continues despite human failure, divine blessing is conditional on obedience, and spiritual leadership continues regardless of political changes. It demonstrates that true leadership includes honest assessment of past performance, that historical perspective provides wisdom for present decisions, and that God's purposes continue through different leadership structures. The chapter shows Samuel as a model of faithful leadership - blameless in service, faithful in intercession, committed to teaching, and concerned for the people's spiritual welfare. It illustrates the principle that leadership transitions require proper accountability, that divine standards don't change with political systems, and that God's faithfulness to His name provides security for His people. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfect King who combines divine authority with perfect leadership, who provides eternal deliverance rather than temporary relief, and whose intercession never ceases. The thunder sign points forward to Christ's authority over nature and His role as divine witness to truth. The chapter demonstrates that faithful leadership includes both honest confrontation of sin and gracious encouragement to continue serving God, that historical perspective provides wisdom for present choices, and that God's purposes prevail despite human errors in leadership selection.
