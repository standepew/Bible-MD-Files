## CHAPTER SUMMARY

David said in his heart he would perish one day by Saul's hand; nothing better than speedily escaping into Philistines' land; Saul would despair of seeking him in Israel's coast; he'd escape from his hand. David arose, passed over with six hundred men with him to Achish son of Maoch, king of Gath. David dwelt with Achish at Gath, he and men, every man with household, even David with two wives, Ahinoam Jezreelitess and Abigail Carmelitess, Nabal's wife. It was told Saul David fled to Gath; he sought no more again for him. David told Achish, if he found grace in his eyes, let them give place in some town in country to dwell there; why should servant dwell in royal city with him? Achish gave him Ziklag that day; Ziklag pertains to kings of Judah to this day. Time David dwelt in Philistines' country was full year and four months. David and men went up, invaded Geshurites, Gezrites, Amalekites; those nations were old inhabitants of land as going to Shur even to Egypt. David smote land, left neither man nor woman alive, took away sheep, oxen, asses, camels, apparel, returned, came to Achish. Achish asked where they made road that day. David said against south of Judah, south of Jerahmeelites, south of Kenites. David saved neither man nor woman alive to bring tidings to Gath, lest they tell on them, saying David did this, would be his manner while dwelling in Philistines' country. Achish believed David, saying he made his people Israel utterly abhor him; therefore he'd be his servant forever.

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 And David said in his heart, I shall now perish one day by the hand of Saul: there is nothing better for me than that I should speedily escape into the land of the Philistines; and Saul shall despair of me, to seek me any more in any coast of Israel: so shall I escape out of his hand.  

2 And David arose, and he passed over with the six hundred men that were with him unto Achish, the son of Maoch, king of Gath.  

3 And David dwelt with Achish at Gath, he and his men, every man with his household, even David with his two wives, Ahinoam the Jezreelitess, and Abigail the Carmelitess, Nabal's wife.  

4 And it was told Saul that David was fled to Gath: and he sought no more again for him.  

5 And David said unto Achish, If I have now found grace in thine eyes, let them give me a place in some town in the country, that I may dwell there: for why should thy servant dwell in the royal city with thee?  

6 Then Achish gave him Ziklag that day: wherefore Ziklag pertaineth unto the kings of Judah unto this day.  

7 And the time that David dwelt in the country of the Philistines was a full year and four months.  

8 And David and his men went up, and invaded the Geshurites, and the Gezrites, and the Amalekites: for those nations were of old the inhabitants of the land, as thou goest to Shur, even unto the land of Egypt.  

9 And David smote the land, and left neither man nor woman alive, and took away the sheep, and the oxen, and the asses, and the camels, and the apparel, and returned, and came to Achish.  

10 And Achish said, Whither have ye made a road to day? And David said, Against the south of Judah, and against the south of the Jerahmeelites, and to the south of the Kenites.  

11 And David saved neither man nor woman alive, to bring tidings to Gath, saying, Lest they should tell on us, saying, So did David, and so will be his manner all the while he dwelleth in the country of the Philistines.  

12 And Achish believed David, saying, He hath made his people Israel utterly to abhor him; therefore he shall be my servant for ever.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "I shall now perish one day by the hand of Saul"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about accurate prediction
**Biblical context:** This is about human despair - David's assessment comes from fear, not divine revelation, showing how human perspective differs from divine reality.

### "He hath made his people Israel utterly to abhor him; therefore he shall be my servant for ever"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about David's betrayal
**Biblical context:** This is about deceptive success - Achish believes David has betrayed Israel, showing how deception can create false security.

**Chapter 27 demonstrates David's human weakness and strategic thinking in desperate circumstances:** David's conclusion that he would perish by Saul's hand shows human despair rather than divine perspective. His decision to flee to Philistines shows strategic thinking based on fear rather than faith. The movement with six hundred men shows continued leadership despite circumstances. The dwelling with Achish shows seeking refuge among former enemies. The inclusion of households shows David's concern for family and followers' welfare. Saul's cessation of pursuit shows David's strategy achieved immediate goal. David's request for town in country shows wisdom in avoiding direct conflict with Achish. Achish's gift of Ziklag shows divine provision through unexpected means. The duration of stay shows extended period of this arrangement. The raids against ancient enemies show David's military activity and strategic deception. The complete destruction shows thoroughness in eliminating witnesses. The taking of spoils shows practical provision for his men. The deception about raid locations shows strategic wisdom in maintaining Achish's trust. The elimination of witnesses shows careful planning to maintain deception. Achish's belief shows success of David's strategy. His conclusion that David was permanently alienated from Israel shows how deception creates false security. His expectation of permanent service shows how misunderstanding serves David's purposes. This chapter establishes crucial themes: human despair leads to strategic decisions, divine purposes work through human weakness, deception can provide temporary protection, and God uses unexpected means for His purposes. It demonstrates that even God's chosen experience human weakness and make strategic decisions based on circumstances, that divine purposes continue through imperfect human actions, and that God provides through unexpected channels. The chapter shows David as strategic leader who uses deception for protection while maintaining his men's welfare. It illustrates the principle that human weakness doesn't disqualify from divine purposes, that strategic thinking can provide temporary solutions, and that God works through imperfect human decisions. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfect refuge who provides eternal protection, whose truth brings perfect security, and whose wisdom provides perfect guidance. The deception points forward to how Christ's truth overcomes all deception. The chapter demonstrates that when God's chosen face desperate circumstances, they may make strategic decisions based on human weakness, but divine purposes continue to work through these decisions, showing God's sovereignty over human affairs and His ability to use even imperfect strategies for His ultimate purposes.
