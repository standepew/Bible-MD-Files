## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### GILBOA
- **Meaning:** "Agony" or "Bubbling spring"
- **Context:** Mountain where Saul and Jonathan died
- **Significance:** Place of tragic defeat and loss
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual defeat (Psalm 44:22)

### UNCIRCUMCISED
- **Meaning:** Those outside covenant
- **Context:** Philistines as enemies of Israel
- **Significance:** Spiritual opposition to God's people
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual uncircumcision (Romans 2:29)

### ASHTAROTH
- **Meaning:** "Star" or "Goddess"
- **Context:** Pagan goddess whose house received Saul's armor
- **Significance:** Victory claimed by false gods
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual idolatry (1 John 5:21)

### JABESH-GILEAD
- **Meaning:** "Dry heap of testimony"
- **Context:** City that showed loyalty to Saul
- **Significance:** Gratitude for past deliverance
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual loyalty (Revelation 2:10)

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 Now the Philistines fought against Israel: and the men of Israel fled from before the Philistines, and fell down slain in mount Gilboa.  

2 And the Philistines followed hard upon Saul and upon his sons; and the Philistines slew Jonathan, and Abinadab, and Malchi-shua, Saul's sons.  

3 And the battle went sore against Saul, and the archers hit him; and he was sore wounded of the archers.  

4 Then said Saul unto his armourbearer, Draw thy sword, and thrust me through therewith; lest these uncircumcised come and thrust me through, and abuse me. But his armourbearer would not; for he was sore afraid. Therefore Saul took a sword, and fell upon it.  

5 And when his armourbearer saw that Saul was dead, he fell likewise upon his sword, and died with him.  

6 So Saul died, and his three sons, and his armourbearer, and all his men, that same day together.  

7 And when the men of Israel that were on the other side of the valley, and they that were on the other side Jordan, saw that the men of Israel fled, and that Saul and his sons were after they forsook the cities, and fled; and the Philistines came and dwelt in them.  

8 And it came to pass on the morrow, when the Philistines came to strip the slain, that they found Saul and his sons fallen in mount Gilboa.  

9 And they cut off his head, and stripped off his armour, and sent into the land of the Philistines round about, to publish it in the house of their idols, and among the people.  

10 And they put his armour in the house of Ashtaroth: and they fastened his body to the wall of Beth-shan.  

11 And when the inhabitants of Jabesh-gilead heard of that which the Philistines had done to Saul;  

12 All the valiant men arose, and went all night, and took the body of Saul and the bodies of his sons from the wall of Beth-shan, and came to Jabesh, and burnt them there.  

13 And they took their bones, and buried them under a tree at Jabesh, and fasted seven days.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Draw thy sword, and thrust me through therewith; lest these uncircumcised come and thrust me through, and abuse me"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about courage
**Biblical context:** This is about avoiding shame - Saul chooses suicide to prevent abuse by enemies, showing how fear of shame leads to desperate actions.

### "All the valiant men arose, and went all night, and took the body of Saul"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about military action
**Biblical context:** This is about loyalty in death - Jabesh-gilead remembers Saul's deliverance and honors him in death, showing gratitude transcends circumstances.

**Chapter 31 demonstrates the tragic end of Saul's reign and the consequences of rejecting divine guidance:** Philistines' attack shows final judgment on Saul's disobedience. Israel's flight shows loss of leadership and divine protection. The death of Saul's sons shows tragic family consequences of leadership failure. Jonathan's death shows loss of godly leadership potential. Saul's wounds show physical manifestation of spiritual defeat. His suicide shows final act of despair rather than repentance. The armourbearer's death shows loyalty unto death. The complete defeat shows total judgment on Saul's reign. Israel's abandonment of cities shows loss of national identity. Philistine occupation shows enemy dominance. The desecration of Saul's body shows enemy triumph over God's anointed. The display in pagan temple shows false gods claiming victory. Jabesh-gilead's loyalty shows gratitude for past deliverance. Their nighttime action shows courage in dangerous circumstances. The proper burial shows respect despite Saul's failures. The seven-day fast shows proper mourning. This chapter establishes crucial themes: rejecting divine guidance brings tragic consequences, leadership failure affects entire nation, despair leads to final defeat, and gratitude transcends circumstances. It demonstrates that Saul's complete rejection of divine guidance led to complete defeat, that his leadership failure brought national disaster, and that even in defeat, human loyalty shows gratitude for past deliverance. The chapter shows Saul as tragic figure who began well but ended in despair, showing the importance of maintaining divine guidance to the end. It illustrates the principle that rejecting divine guidance brings judgment, that leadership failure has national consequences, and that human loyalty can show grace even to the fallen. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfect king who provides perfect leadership, whose death brings victory rather than defeat, and whose resurrection brings eternal life. The tragic death points forward to Christ's victorious death. The chapter demonstrates that when leaders reject divine guidance completely, the result is tragic defeat that affects entire nations, but human gratitude can still show honor even to the fallen, pointing forward to the perfect honor given to Christ through His resurrection.
