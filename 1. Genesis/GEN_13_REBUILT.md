## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Abram came up out of Egypt — "went up" (alah) — with Sarai, Lot, and all his accumulated wealth: very rich in cattle, silver, and gold. He retraced his steps to Bethel, to the exact place of his first altar, and called on the name of the LORD — restoration after failure begins at the altar of first devotion. Both Abram and Lot possessed great substance; the land could not sustain them together. Strife (riv) broke out between their herdsmen, while the Canaanite and Perizzite still occupied the land — an external pressure making internal unity more urgent. Abram appealed to Lot from covenant kinship: "Let there be no strife between me and thee... for we be brethren" — and surrendered his right of first choice, offering Lot the whole land to pick from. Lot lifted up his eyes and saw the plain of Jordan — well-watered everywhere, like the garden of the LORD, like the land of Egypt — and chose it for himself, journeying east. He pitched his tent toward Sodom. But the men of Sodom were wicked and sinners before the LORD exceedingly. After Lot's departure, the LORD spoke to Abram: lift up your eyes northward, southward, eastward, westward — all you see, I give to you and your seed forever. I will make your seed as the dust of the earth. Arise and walk through the land — it is yours. Abram moved to Hebron, to the plain of Mamre, and built an altar there.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Abram Went Up Out of Egypt" — The Upward Direction of Leaving the World

**Genesis 13:1** — "And Abram went UP (alah) out of Egypt... into the south"

- **Alah** = to go up, to ascend, to rise — the standard direction of departure from Egypt in Scripture; leaving Egypt is always "going up"; entering Egypt is always "going down"; the geography and the theology align
- **Genesis 45:25; 46:29** — Joseph's brothers "went up out of Egypt"; Israel "went up" in the tribal registers
- **Exodus 13:18** — Israel "went up harnessed out of Egypt"
- **Numbers 32:11** — "None of the men that came up out of Egypt shall see the land"
- **1 Kings 10:28-29** — Horses brought up from Egypt by trade
- **Hosea 11:1; Matthew 2:15** — "Out of Egypt have I called my son" — the direction of calling is always upward and outward from Egypt
- *Interpretation:* The consistent "up from Egypt" language is not merely geographic — Egypt sits below sea level in places, but the real height is spiritual. To leave Egypt is to ascend toward God, toward the promise, toward the land. To go to Egypt is to descend. This is the directional theology of the world: the world pulls down; God calls up. Abram's "went up from Egypt" is his restoration — he is ascending back to the covenant path after his descent in Genesis 12.

### "He Returned to the Altar" — Restoration After Failure

**Genesis 13:3-4** — "Unto the place of the altar, which he had made there at the first: and there Abram called on the name of the LORD"

- The altar at Bethel (Genesis 12:8) was the last place of genuine covenant worship before Abram's Egypt detour; his return is to the exact location of first devotion — not a new altar but the old one
- **Revelation 2:4-5** — "Nevertheless I have somewhat against thee, because thou hast left thy first love. Remember therefore from whence thou art fallen, and repent, and do the FIRST WORKS" — the letter to Ephesus parallels Abram's return; restoration requires returning to where you left off; the first works, the first altar, the first love
- **Hosea 2:14-15** — "I will allure her, and bring her into the wilderness, and speak comfortably unto her. And I will give her her vineyards from thence, and the valley of Achor for a door of hope" — God's restoration pattern: He brings His people back through the wilderness to the beginning, where the covenant was fresh and the relationship was uncontaminated
- **Psalm 51:12** — "Restore unto me the joy of thy salvation; and uphold me with thy free spirit" — David's post-failure prayer; the prayer of return to first love; this is the spirit of Genesis 13:3-4
- *Interpretation:* After Egypt's compromise, Abram did not build a new altar in a new location — he returned to the old one. Genuine restoration in the believer's life is not reinvention but return: back to the Word, the altar, the name called upon. The geography of renewal runs through the place of first devotion.

### "For We Be Brethren" — Covenant Kinship as the Ground of Peace

**Genesis 13:8** — "Let there be no strife, I pray thee, between me and thee... for we be brethren (achim)"

- **Achim** = brothers — literally; Abram was Lot's uncle (Genesis 11:27 — Lot was Haran's son, Haran was Abram's brother), but "brothers" is used broadly for covenant kin; the appeal is relational before it is legal
- **Psalm 133:1** — "Behold, how good and how pleasant it is for brethren to dwell together in unity!" — the same word (achim); this Psalm is the commentary on Genesis 13:8; Abram embodied it; Lot did not
- **Romans 12:18** — "If it be possible, as much as lieth in you, live peaceably with all men" — Abram exhausted every peaceable option; he surrendered a legal right (as the elder, the land choice was properly his) to prevent strife
- **Philippians 2:3-4** — "Let nothing be done through strife or vainglory; but in lowliness of mind let each esteem other better than themselves. Look not every man on his own things, but every man also on the things of others" — this is the Abram principle; he esteemed Lot's interests above his own legal right; the anti-strife posture
- **James 4:1-2** — "From whence come wars and fightings among you? come they not hence, even of your lusts that war in your members?" — the herdsmen's strife came from territorial competition (lust for land); Abram refused to let the servants' strife become his own; the man of faith dealt with strife at its root by releasing the cause of it
- *Interpretation:* "For we be brethren" is one of the most powerful peace-making arguments in Scripture. Abram did not appeal to law, rank, age, or prior claim — all of which favored him. He appealed to relationship. The strongest argument for peace among God's people is always the covenant that binds them. Strife between brethren dishonors the covenant more than it damages the property; Abram saw this and gave up the better land to protect the better relationship.

### "Lot Lifted Up His Eyes" — The Choice by Sight

**Genesis 13:10-11** — "And Lot lifted up his eyes, and beheld all the plain of Jordan... Then Lot chose him all the plain of Jordan"

- **The contrast with v.14:** Two "lift up eyes" moments in the same chapter
  - v.10 — Lot lifts up his eyes: sees prosperity; chooses for himself; journeys east; tent toward Sodom
  - v.14 — The LORD says to Abram: "Lift up now thine eyes" — God directs the vision; the promise is given; the four directions are God's gift
  - Lot's sight-choice produces loss. God's promise-vision produces inheritance.
- **Genesis 3:6** — "When the woman saw that the tree was good for food, and that it was pleasant to the eyes... she took of the fruit thereof, and did eat" — the pattern: see → desire → take → consequences; Lot's choice follows the exact pattern of Eve's; both look at something visually attractive and take it without asking God
- **1 John 2:15-16** — "Love not the world, neither the things that are in the world... For all that is in the world, the lust of the flesh, and the lust of the eyes, and the pride of life, is not of the Father, but is of the world" — Lot's choice was driven by "the lust of the eyes"; the plain looked like everything good
- **2 Corinthians 5:7** — "For we walk by faith, not by sight" — Abram walked by faith (receiving by promise what he did not see yet); Lot walked by sight (taking what he could see without consulting God); the two men in one chapter are the two ways of life
- **Numbers 13:33** — The ten faithless spies: "We saw the giants... and we were in our own sight as grasshoppers" — the principle that seeing by the eye alone produces fear or wrong desire, never accurate covenant vision
- *Interpretation:* Lot's "lifted up his eyes" is the biblical archetype of the worldly choice — visually rational, materially optimal, spiritually catastrophic. Every element of his assessment was accurate (the plain WAS well-watered; it DID look like Eden and Egypt) except the one that mattered: the men of Sodom were wicked and sinners before the LORD exceedingly (v.13). Visual prosperity concealed moral destruction. What looks like the garden of the LORD may contain Sodom.

### "Like the Garden of the LORD, Like the Land of Egypt"

**Genesis 13:10** — "It was well watered every where... even as the garden of the LORD, like the land of Egypt, as thou comest unto Zoar"

- **Two comparisons that should have given pause:**
  1. "Like the garden of the LORD" — Eden; the original paradise; but Eden was lost through disobedience (Genesis 3); no place on earth is Eden; what looks like Eden after the fall contains the serpent
  2. "Like the land of Egypt" — the world's provision system; Abram had just experienced the cost of Egypt; to Lot, this was a recommendation; the plain looked like the place Abram had just been delivered from
- **The paradox:** The plain was compared to both the highest (Eden) and the lowest (Egypt) of Lot's reference points, and both comparisons pointed to danger, not safety
- **Ezekiel 31:8-9** — Pharaoh's Egypt compared to trees in Eden: "The cedars in the garden of God could not hide him... I have made him fair by the multitude of his branches: so that all the trees of Eden... envied him" — God's own ironic comparison of Egypt's glory to Eden; both comparisons point to pride, not safety; what resembles Eden in human terms is often what God will bring low
- **Revelation 17:4** — The Great Harlot: "Arrayed in purple and scarlet colour, and decked with gold and precious stones and pearls" — she too looks like abundance and beauty; she too is what she appears not to be; Lot's Jordan plain is the prototype of the world-system that appears glorious but conceals destruction
- *Interpretation:* The double comparison — like Eden AND like Egypt — is Scripture's built-in warning label on Lot's choice. Eden and Egypt are not compatible sources of blessing. One is God's original creation; the other is the world-system of bondage. To find a place that resembles both simultaneously is to find a place that has the appearance of abundance without the substance of covenant. The plain of Jordan was beautiful, well-watered, and on the road to destruction.

### "Lot Journeyed East" — The Direction of Departure from God

**Genesis 13:11** — "Lot journeyed east (qidmah): and they separated themselves the one from the other"

- **Qidmah** = eastward, toward the east — the directional word; in Genesis, "east" consistently marks movement away from God's covenant presence
- **The East pattern in Genesis:**
  - Genesis 3:24 — "He placed... east of the garden of Eden a flaming sword" — east of Eden is where God's presence is blocked; east = the direction of exile from paradise
  - Genesis 4:16 — "Cain went out from the presence of the LORD, and dwelt in the land of Nod, on the east of Eden" — east again = departure from God's presence
  - Genesis 11:2 — "As they journeyed from the east" (or: "they journeyed eastward") — the Babel builders moving east to settle in Shinar; the direction of the anti-God project
  - Genesis 13:11 — Lot journeys east → toward Sodom
  - Genesis 25:6 — Abraham sent his other sons "unto the east country" — away from the covenant land
  - **Contrast: Genesis 12:8** — Abram between Bethel (west) and Hai (east); he positioned himself with the house of God (Bethel) to his west/back and the "heap of ruins" (Ai) to the east; Lot moved in the direction of the ruins
- **The theological east:** Eden's gate faced east (the Tabernacle and Temple also faced east for the priests to face toward sunrise, but the congregation faced west toward the Holy of Holies — east was the direction of exile; west was the direction of the presence); to journey east is to journey in the direction the exiles were expelled from Eden
- **Ezekiel 11:23** — "And the glory of the LORD went up from the midst of the city, and stood upon the mountain which is on the east side of the city" — the glory departing to the east; the east as the direction the Shekinah traveled when it left the temple in judgment
- *Interpretation:* "Lot journeyed east" is one word that carries enormous symbolic weight. He moved in the direction of exile, the direction of Cain, the direction of Babel. He moved away from Bethel (house of God) toward Sodom. The direction of the choice reveals its spiritual character before Sodom is even reached.

### "The Men of Sodom Were Wicked and Sinners Before the LORD Exceedingly"

**Genesis 13:13** — "But the men of Sodom were wicked and sinners before the LORD exceedingly"

- **Three degrees of condemnation in one verse:** "wicked" (ra — evil in character), "sinners" (chatta'im — active transgressors), "exceedingly" (me'od — to a very great degree); this stacking of terms is unusual and deliberate; it marks Sodom as a special category of evil even among post-flood humanity
- **The specific sins of Sodom identified by Ezekiel:** **Ezekiel 16:49-50** — "Behold, this was the iniquity of thy sister Sodom, pride, fulness of bread, and abundance of idleness was in her and in her daughters, neither did she strengthen the hand of the poor and needy. And they were haughty, and committed abomination (toevah) before me: therefore I took them away as I saw good" — Ezekiel identifies a cluster: pride, material excess, social indifference, haughtiness, and then toevah (abomination — the sexual sin specified in Genesis 19:4-5)
- **Genesis 18:20** — "The cry of Sodom and Gomorrah is great, and because their sin is very grievous" — God hears a "cry" (tsa'aqah) from Sodom; this is the same word used for the cry of the oppressed/afflicted (Exodus 3:7 — Israel's cry in Egypt; Genesis 4:10 — Abel's blood crying from the ground); Sodom's victims cry out to God
- **Genesis 19:4-5** — "The men of the city, even the men of Sodom, compassed the house round, both old and young, all the people from every quarter: And they called unto Lot, and said unto him, Where are the men which came in to thee this night? bring them out unto us, that we may know them" — the specific sexual sin: attempted homosexual rape of the angelic visitors; "know" (yada) in the sexual sense; confirmed by Jude 7
- **Jude 7** — "Even as Sodom and Gomorrha, and the cities about them in like manner, giving themselves over to fornication, and going after strange flesh, are set forth for an example, suffering the vengeance of eternal fire" — Jude defines the sin: fornication (porneia) and "strange flesh" (sarkos heteras — different/other flesh); the New Testament identifies the same specific sexual sin that Genesis 19 records
- **Luke 17:28-30** — Jesus: "Likewise also as it was in the days of Lot; they did eat, they drank, they bought, they sold, they planted, they builded... Even thus shall it be in the day when the Son of man is revealed" — the condition of Sodom (normalcy, prosperity, no expectation of judgment) is the condition of the world at Christ's return; Sodom is the eschatological type
- *Interpretation:* Genesis 13:13 is the Bible's first comprehensive moral verdict on Sodom: wicked, sinners, exceedingly. The word "exceedingly" (me'od) is the same word used of God's assessment of creation — "very (me'od) good" (Genesis 1:31). Sodom is the anti-creation: what God called "very good," Sodom has made "very evil." The rest of Scripture fills in the specifics: pride, excess, social cruelty, sexual abomination. Sodom is not a cautionary tale about one sin but about the complete inversion of the created order.

### "The LORD Said unto Abram, After That Lot Was Separated from Him" — God Speaks After Separation

**Genesis 13:14** — "And the LORD said unto Abram, AFTER THAT LOT WAS SEPARATED FROM HIM, Lift up now thine eyes"

- **The timing is explicit and theological:** God did not speak to Abram while Lot was present; it was "after that Lot was separated from him" that God gave the expanded land promise; the separation from Lot preceded the fullness of the revelation
- **The principle of separation and revelation:** When Lot (who represents the compromise of worldly association) is removed, God speaks more fully; the encumbrance of the wrong association was limiting the covenant vision
- **2 Corinthians 6:14, 17-18** — "Be ye not unequally yoked together with unbelievers... Wherefore come out from among them, and be ye separate, saith the Lord, and touch not the unclean thing; and I will receive you, And will be a Father unto you, and ye shall be my sons and daughters" — the structure of 2 Corinthians 6 mirrors Genesis 13: separation from ungodly yoke → God's fuller revelation and adoption; the New Covenant form of Abram's post-Lot vision
- **Genesis 22:4** — "Then on the third day Abraham lifted up his eyes, and saw the place afar off" — "lifted up his eyes" on Moriah after complete obedience; the same directional vision of faith leads to the covenant altar
- **John 15:2** — "Every branch in me that beareth not fruit he taketh away; and every branch that beareth fruit, he purgeth it, that it may bring forth more fruit" — the Father's pruning is the Lot-separation; what appears to be loss (separating from the prosperous nephew) becomes the moment of greater fruitfulness
- *Interpretation:* "After that Lot was separated from him" is one of the most quietly significant phrases in the Abrahamic narrative. God waited for the separation before He expanded the vision. Lot's presence — and the compromise his presence represented — was limiting what God could show Abram. Obedient separation creates the conditions for enlarged revelation. The believer who cannot hear God's full voice may need to examine who is sitting beside them.

### The Land Promise — Four Directions, Forever, and the Seed as Dust

**Genesis 13:14-16** — "Northward, and southward, and eastward, and westward: For all the land which thou seest, to thee will I give it, and to thy seed for ever. And I will make thy seed as the dust of the earth"

- **Four directions = total possession:** To be told to look in all four directions is to be told the totality of what is seen belongs to the covenant; the land in every direction is the inheritance; this is the cosmic scope of the Abrahamic promise
- **"For ever" (olam):** The same berith olam language of previous covenant promises; the land is not a lease but a permanent inheritance; **Psalm 105:8-11** — "He hath remembered his covenant for ever... unto thee will I give the land of Canaan, the lot of your inheritance" — God's perpetual memory of the land covenant
- **Galatians 3:16-18** — "The seed is Christ... the land is ultimately Christ's inheritance, and believers inherit with Him"; **Romans 4:13** — "the promise, that he should be the heir of the WORLD, was not to Abraham, or to his seed, through the law, but through the righteousness of faith" — Paul expands the land promise from Canaan to the world; what Abram saw in four directions from his hilltop is a type of the global inheritance Christ receives and believers share
- **The three images of the seed's multitude:**
  - Genesis 13:16 — "as the dust of the earth" — earthly descendants; innumerable; the nation of Israel
  - Genesis 15:5 — "as the stars of heaven" — heavenly/spiritual descendants; innumerable in another realm; the spiritual seed of faith (Galatians 3:7-9)
  - Genesis 22:17 — "as the sand which is upon the sea shore" — innumerable in another dimension; **Hebrews 11:12** applies this to all who are born of one man's faith
  - **Revelation 7:9** — "A great multitude, which no man could number, of all nations, and kindreds, and people, and tongues" — the dust/stars/sand image at its final fulfillment; a multitude beyond calculation from every direction

### "Arise and Walk Through the Land" — The Act of Faith-Possession

**Genesis 13:17** — "Arise, walk through the land in the length of it and in the breadth of it; for I will give it unto thee"

- **The act of walking the land is the act of faith-possession:** Abram had no legal deed, no army, no government recognition of his claim; God told him to walk it; the walking was the covenant stake
- **Joshua 1:3** — "Every place that the sole of your foot shall tread upon, that have I given you" — the same principle applied to Israel's conquest; the walk is the claim; faith-possession precedes legal-possession
- **Joshua 14:9** — "Surely the land whereon thy feet have trodden shall be thine inheritance, and thy children's for ever" — Caleb claimed the mountain because his feet had walked it (Numbers 13:30); the act of faith-walking establishes the inheritance claim
- **Ephesians 1:3** — "Blessed with all spiritual blessings in heavenly places in Christ" — the believer's inheritance is already given "in Christ" before it is experientially possessed; "arise and walk" is the call to claim by faith what is already legally given by covenant

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### STRIFE (Hebrew: riv)

**Genesis 13:7-8** — "There was a STRIFE (riv) between the herdmen... Let there be no STRIFE (riv)"

- **Riv** = strife, dispute, contention, legal controversy — from yarav (to strive, to contend); riv is not casual disagreement but a serious confrontation that, if left unresolved, escalates to permanent division; it is used for both interpersonal and legal disputes
- **The cause of this riv:** Excessive wealth; the land could not sustain both; prosperity without sufficient space produced competition; **Proverbs 13:10** — "Only by pride cometh contention (matsah)" — under the surface of the herdsmen's territorial dispute was the pride that says "my cattle's need matters more than our unity"
- **The riv in covenant context:** Abram named the covenant relationship ("brethren") as the reason strife was intolerable; to strive with a brother is to damage the covenant that defines you; **Proverbs 17:14** — "Leave off contention, before it be meddled with: for the beginning of strife is as when one letteth out water" — riv is like releasing a flood: easy to start, almost impossible to stop; Abram stopped it at the source
- **Psalm 55:9** — "Destroy, O Lord, and divide their tongues: for I have seen violence and strife (riv) in the city" — David prays against riv in the city; Genesis 13's riv was between traveling covenant men; its settlement prevented the city-conflict pattern
- **Philippians 2:3** — "Let nothing be done through strife (eritheia) or vainglory; but in lowliness of mind let each esteem other better than themselves" — Paul's eritheia (strife driven by self-interest) is the Greek equivalent of Genesis 13's riv; the solution is the same: esteem the other above yourself
- **James 4:1-2** — "From whence come wars and fightings among you? come they not hence, even of your lusts?" — the source of riv is always internal (lust, pride, self-interest) before it is external (territorial competition); Abram addressed the source (the relationship) rather than the symptom (whose cattle graze where)
- *Interpretation:* The riv of Genesis 13 is the first recorded conflict between covenant people. Its resolution — Abram surrendering his legal priority — establishes the pattern for all covenant conflict resolution: the stronger party yields; the relationship is valued above the right; peace is purchased at personal cost. This is the anti-litigation posture of 1 Corinthians 6:7 — "Now therefore there is utterly a fault among you, because ye go to law one with another. Why do ye not rather take wrong? why do ye not rather suffer yourselves to be defrauded?"

### LOT'S PROGRESSIVE COMPROMISE — PITCHED TOWARD SODOM (Hebrew: masa' oholo / va-ye'ehal)

**Genesis 13:12** — "And pitched his tent toward Sodom"

**The five-stage descent of Lot:**

| Stage | Text | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 1. He saw | Genesis 13:10 | "Lot lifted up his eyes, and beheld all the plain" — the look of desire |
| 2. He chose | Genesis 13:11 | "Lot chose him all the plain of Jordan" — the decision for self |
| 3. He moved toward | Genesis 13:12 | "Pitched his tent toward Sodom" — proximity, not yet residence |
| 4. He lived in | Genesis 14:12 | "Lot, who dwelt in Sodom" — full residency established |
| 5. He led in | Genesis 19:1 | "Lot sat in the gate of Sodom" — civic authority, integration |

- **The progression is never sudden:** No one goes from Abram's altar to Sodom's gate in one step; there are always intermediate stages; each stage seems reasonable from the previous stage; the final destination is incomprehensible from the starting point but logical from the penultimate step
- **Psalm 1:1** — "Blessed is the man that walketh not in the counsel of the ungodly, nor standeth in the way of sinners, nor sitteth in the seat of the scornful" — the three verbs of Psalm 1:1 (walks, stands, sits) trace the same progressive stages of compromise that Lot exemplifies; walking in ungodly counsel → standing with sinners → sitting with the scornful; the psalm describes Lot's descent in reverse order (the blessed man avoids what Lot entered)
- **1 Corinthians 15:33** — "Evil communications (companionships) corrupt good manners (morals)" — the Greek homiliai kakai phtherousin ethe chrestha; Lot's story is the biblical case study; the wickedness of Sodom's "communications" (the daily environment) corrupted his moral formation; his daughters acted like Sodomites (Genesis 19:30-38)
- **2 Peter 2:7-8** — "Just Lot, vexed with the filthy conversation of the wicked: (For that righteous man dwelling among them, in seeing and hearing, vexed his righteous soul from day to day with their unlawful deeds)" — the righteous soul can be perpetually vexed (tortured, worn down) by the environment it chooses to inhabit; Lot was "righteous" (dikaiou) but not well; his soul was being ground down daily; righteousness of position does not prevent damage from unwise location
- **Luke 17:28-30** — Christ uses Lot's days (normalcy, prosperity, no expectation of judgment: "they did eat, they drank, they bought, they sold, they planted, they builded") as the type of the world's condition at His return; the pre-judgment normalcy of Sodom is the eschatological warning
- *Interpretation:* "Pitched his tent toward Sodom" is one of the most significant phrases in the Lot narrative — not because of where Lot was (not yet in Sodom) but because of the direction his tent door faced. Lot had not yet arrived in Sodom, but his tent was oriented toward it. What a man faces, what he looks at, what direction his home opens toward — these reveal his trajectory before his feet arrive. The tent-toward-Sodom is the believer who has not yet compromised but has oriented their life toward the compromise.

### SODOM (Hebrew: Sedom; meaning: burning / scorched)

**Genesis 13:10, 12-13** — "Like the garden of the LORD, like the land of Egypt... toward Sodom... the men of Sodom were wicked and sinners before the LORD exceedingly"

- **The name Sodom** — likely from a root meaning "burning" or "scorched ground" — the name itself is prophetic of the city's end (Genesis 19:24 — "the LORD rained upon Sodom and upon Gomorrah brimstone and fire")
- **The first moral verdict on Sodom (Genesis 13:13):** Three terms stacked — ra (evil), chatta'im (habitual sinners), me'od (exceedingly) — this triple condemnation is the Bible's own severity-marker; Sodom is not merely sinful but definitively, exceptionally wicked in God's moral assessment
- **Sodom's full sin profile from Scripture:**
  - Pride (ga'on — arrogance, self-exaltation) — Ezekiel 16:49
  - Fulness of bread (sova lechem — food excess, gluttony) — Ezekiel 16:49
  - Abundance of idleness (shokat hashqet — ease, complacency, indifference) — Ezekiel 16:49
  - Social cruelty (failure to strengthen the poor and needy) — Ezekiel 16:49
  - Haughtiness (gavah — pride that led to action) — Ezekiel 16:50
  - Abomination (toevah — the sexual sin) — Ezekiel 16:50; specified in Genesis 19:4-5 and Jude 7
- **The "cry" of Sodom:** Genesis 18:20-21 — "the cry (tsa'aqah) of Sodom and Gomorrah is great" — the same word as the cry of Abel's blood (Genesis 4:10), the cry of Israel in Egypt (Exodus 2:23); Sodom had victims whose cry reached God; the sin was not only self-destructive but harmed others
- **Sodom as eschatological type:** Luke 17:28-30; Matthew 10:15 — "It shall be more tolerable for the land of Sodom and Gomorrha in the day of judgment, than for that city" — towns that rejected the gospel are judged more severely than Sodom; Sodom sets the lower bound, not the upper; the gospel context makes rejecting Christ worse than being Sodom
- **Isaiah 1:9-10** — "Except the LORD of hosts had left unto us a very small remnant, we should have been as Sodom... Hear the word of the LORD, ye rulers of Sodom" — Isaiah applies the Sodom label to Jerusalem; the city that has the Word and rejects it becomes Sodom in God's assessment
- **Revelation 11:8** — "The great city, which spiritually is called Sodom and Egypt, where also our Lord was crucified" — Jerusalem at the time of the two witnesses is called "spiritually Sodom"; Jerusalem crucified the Lord; Sodom becomes the spiritual name for any city/culture that has received God's presence and rejected it in sexual immorality and pride
- *Interpretation:* Sodom in Scripture is not primarily a geographic location but a moral category. Ezekiel shows it is a composite of interconnected sins: pride producing excess, excess producing idleness, idleness producing social cruelty, social cruelty producing the abomination. The sexual sin named in Genesis 19 and Jude 7 is the expression of the whole character, not the isolated cause. Any culture that follows the Sodom pattern — pride, excess, idleness, contempt for the vulnerable, sexual inversion of the created order — is walking toward Sodom's judgment regardless of its geographic location.

### FOREVER (Hebrew: olam)

**Genesis 13:15** — "To thee will I give it, and to thy seed for EVER (olam)"

- **Olam** = perpetual, age-lasting, to the vanishing point of time — from alam (to be hidden, to be veiled); olam refers to what is hidden from view because it extends so far it cannot be seen; it is not necessarily "absolute eternity" in all uses (as GEN_09 showed with the Noahic berith olam bounded by "while the earth remaineth") but its meaning in each context must be read against the promise's content
- **The land promise's olam:** The land given to Abraham's seed "forever" is grounded in the covenant God makes with Himself; **Psalm 105:8-11** — "He hath remembered his covenant for ever, the word which he commanded to a thousand generations. Which covenant he made with Abraham... Saying, Unto thee will I give the land of Canaan, the lot of your inheritance" — God's memory of this covenant is itself "for ever"
- **How olam operates in the land promise:**
  - Physical/national level: Israel's title to the land of Canaan is an olam covenant; **Genesis 17:8** — "all the land of Canaan, for an everlasting possession"
  - Christological level: **Romans 4:13** — "that he should be the heir of the world" — the land expands to global scope in Christ; what Abram was promised in Canaan, Christ inherits in the whole world; the olam of the land is fulfilled in the global reign of Christ
  - Eternal level: **Hebrews 11:10, 16** — Abraham "looked for a city which hath foundations, whose builder and maker is God... a better country, that is, an heavenly" — Abraham understood the olam land promise pointed beyond Canaan to the heavenly country; the ultimate fulfillment is the new earth (Revelation 21:1-3) where God dwells with His people forever
- **The Noahic bound and the Abrahamic olam:** The Noahic berith olam is bounded "while the earth remaineth"; the Abrahamic olam is not similarly bounded in the text — it expands (Romans 4:13; Revelation 21) rather than contracts; the two "forevers" operate in different scopes
- *Interpretation:* "To thy seed for ever" in Genesis 13:15 is not merely the title deed to Canaan — it is the promise of the entire inheritance that the Seed (Christ — Galatians 3:16) will receive and that believers in Christ (the spiritual seed — Galatians 3:29) will share. The olam of the land runs from a tent in Canaan to a city "whose builder and maker is God" (Hebrews 11:10) to the new earth where the covenant people dwell with God in the new Jerusalem (Revelation 21:3). Every step of the olam is larger than the previous.

### HEBRON / CHEBER (Hebrew: Chevron — fellowship, communion)

**Genesis 13:18** — "Then Abram removed his tent, and came and dwelt in the plain of MAMRE, which is in HEBRON, and built there an altar unto the LORD"

- **Chevron** = from chavar (to join, to unite, to be in fellowship, to be in compact) — Hebron means "fellowship" or "communion" or "alliance"; the name of the place Abram chose is the name of the relationship he sought with God
- **Mamre** = fatness, strength, robustness — the name of the Amorite whose plains Abram inhabited (Genesis 14:13 — "Mamre the Amorite, brother of Eshcol, and brother of Aner... confederate with Abram"); the place of strength and abundance
- **Hebron in Abraham's covenant life:**
  - Genesis 13:18 — Abram's first home in Hebron; he builds an altar
  - Genesis 14:13 — God's messengers find Abraham at the terebinths of Mamre (Hebron)
  - Genesis 18:1 — "The LORD appeared unto him in the plains of Mamre" — the Trinitarian visit; the intercession for Sodom
  - Genesis 23:2 — Sarah died at Kirjath-arba (another name for Hebron)
  - Genesis 23:19; 25:9 — Abraham buried at Machpelah near Mamre/Hebron
  - Genesis 35:27 — Jacob came to his father Isaac at Mamre/Hebron
  - Genesis 49:29-33 — Jacob commanded to be buried at Machpelah/Hebron
- **Hebron in Israel's history after Abraham:**
  - Numbers 13:22 — Hebron was built before Zoan of Egypt (ancient); the Anakim/Nephilim-related giants dwelt there
  - Joshua 14:12-15 — Caleb drove out the Anakim and claimed Hebron; **Joshua 14:14** — "Hebron therefore became the inheritance of Caleb the son of Jephunneh the Kenezite unto this day, because that he wholly followed the LORD God of Israel"
  - 2 Samuel 2:1-4 — David first anointed king at Hebron; reigned there seven years before Jerusalem
  - 2 Samuel 15:10 — Absalom's rebellion began at Hebron; the city of fellowship became the site of family betrayal
- **The theological significance:** Abram settled in the place of fellowship (Hebron) and built an altar; he chose communion with God over the prosperous plains Lot had taken; fellowship with God is Abram's inheritance after Lot's departure; this is the consistent pattern: what looks like loss (losing Lot's company) becomes fellowship with God (dwelling in Chevron)
- *Interpretation:* Abram's choice of Hebron is his theological response to Lot's choice of Jordan. Lot chose by sight — well-watered, like Eden, like Egypt. Abram ended up in the place of fellowship (Chevron/Hebron) — not by choosing it for its appearance but by following God's direction after Lot's departure. The believer who lets go of the world's attractive plain and walks in covenant obedience finds himself in the place of chebar — deep fellowship with God. The name of Abram's final resting place (Genesis 23:19 — buried at Hebron) is the name that defined his life: fellowship with the covenant God.

### EAST (Hebrew: qidmah / qedem)

**Genesis 13:11** — "And Lot journeyed east (qidmah)"

- **Qedem / qidmah** = east, eastward, before, in front of — from a root meaning "what is in front" or "the ancient/primordial direction"; in the ancient Near East, one oriented by facing the sunrise (east), so "before" and "east" shared the same root; east is the direction of the sunrise, the beginning, the ancient — but in Genesis, it consistently marks departure from God's covenant presence
- **The east symbolism in Genesis:**
  - Genesis 2:8 — "God planted a garden eastward (qedem) in Eden" — but the point of reference is always from God's presence; the garden itself was at the east
  - Genesis 3:24 — "He placed at the east (qedem) of the garden of Eden Cherubims, and a flaming sword" — east of the garden = the direction of exile; the cherubim block the eastward way back into Eden for fallen man
  - Genesis 4:16 — "Cain went out from the presence of the LORD, and dwelt in the land of Nod, on the east (qedem) of Eden" — east = departure from God's face (paneh YHWH)
  - Genesis 11:2 — "As they journeyed from the east (qedem)" or "eastward" to Shinar — the Babel builders' eastern movement
  - Genesis 13:11 — Lot journeyed east (qidmah) toward Sodom
  - Genesis 25:6 — Abraham sent his other sons "eastward, unto the east country" — away from Isaac, away from the covenant
  - Genesis 29:1 — Jacob "went on his journey, and came into the land of the people of the east" — exile from the covenant land
- **The tabernacle/temple reversal:** The tabernacle and temple faced east (Ezekiel 8:16 — "five and twenty men with their backs toward the temple of the LORD, and their faces toward the east" — the condemnation is that they faced east in worship, away from God's presence); the congregation entered from the east but faced west toward the Holy of Holies; the Priest of God served from west to east, moving toward the people; the east was the direction of the worshipers' approach and the exiles' expulsion simultaneously
- *Interpretation:* In Genesis, east is consistently the direction a person moves when departing from God's covenant presence: Adam and Eve expelled eastward, Cain exiled east, Babel builders east, Lot east toward Sodom. The geographic symbolism is consistent enough to be intentional. To move east in Genesis is to move in the direction of exile, of Cain, of Babel, of Sodom. When God directs Abram to look "northward, and southward, and eastward, and westward" (v.14), He is sovereign over all four directions — including east. What Lot chose (east/Sodom) God gave to Abram in the promise; the entire landscape, including what Lot took, belongs to the covenant.

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Abram and Lot — Type of the Faithful vs. the Worldly Believer

**Genesis 13** — The two paths diverge at the Jordan / Hebron fork

| Abram (Faith) | Lot (Sight) |
|---|---|
| Surrenders the choice | Takes first choice |
| Remains in Canaan (covenant land) | Moves to Jordan plain (looks like Eden/Egypt) |
| Dwells in a tent (pilgrim) | Dwells in cities (settler) |
| Faces Bethel (House of God) | Faces Sodom (burning) |
| Moves toward Hebron (fellowship) | Moves toward Sodom (scorched) |
| Builds an altar | Builds no altar (none mentioned) |
| God speaks to him after separation | Lot receives only rescue, not a word |
| Inherits everything (v.14-17) | Loses everything (Genesis 19) |

- **1 Corinthians 3:12-15** — "If any man build upon this foundation gold, silver, precious stones, wood, hay, stubble... he shall suffer loss: but he himself shall be saved; yet so as by fire" — Lot is the 1 Corinthians 3:15 believer; "saved, yet so as by fire"; righteous (2 Peter 2:7) but his life's work consumed; the righteous soul who made wrong choices and lost everything except his own life at the judgment
- **2 Peter 2:7-9** — Lot is called "just" (dikaion) and the LORD delivered him; his righteousness was real; his wisdom was absent; the New Testament does not endorse his choices — it records his faith and mourns his loss

### Lot's Five-Stage Descent — Type of Incremental Worldliness

**Genesis 13-19** — The complete Lot narrative as the pattern of progressive compromise

- **Stage 1 (Gen 13:10)** → Looked with desire: the beginning of all worldly compromise is the sustained, desiring look
- **Stage 2 (Gen 13:11)** → Chose: the will follows the eye; desire becomes decision
- **Stage 3 (Gen 13:12)** → Tent toward Sodom: orientation before arrival; facing the wrong direction
- **Stage 4 (Gen 14:12)** → Dwelt in Sodom: the tent toward Sodom became the house in Sodom
- **Stage 5 (Gen 19:1)** → Sat in the gate: civic integration; positional authority in the system that was about to be destroyed

The Psalm 1:1 parallel: "walketh not... standeth... sitteth" — walk → stand → sit; Lot walked toward, stood in, sat at the gate; the progressive settling of the soul into the wrong place.

---

### The Abram-Lot Separation — Type of the Great Separation

**Genesis 13:14** — "After that Lot was separated from him"

- Every major covenant advance in Scripture involves a separation:
  - Abram from Lot (Genesis 13) → land promise expanded
  - Isaac from Ishmael (Genesis 21) → covenant line clarified
  - Jacob from Esau (Genesis 32-33) → wrestling with God, new name
  - Israel from Egypt (Exodus) → covenant at Sinai
  - The church from the world (2 Corinthians 6:14-18) → fullness of relationship with God as Father
- **Revelation 18:4** — "Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues" — the final separation command; the pattern established at Abram-Lot reaches its eschatological climax; separation from Babylon is the precondition for not receiving her plagues

---

### Sodom — Type of the World Before Judgment

**Genesis 13:10, 13** — "Before the LORD destroyed Sodom... the men of Sodom were wicked exceedingly"

- **Luke 17:28-30** — Christ uses Sodom's pre-judgment normalcy as the sign of His return: "they did eat, they drank, they bought, they sold... Even thus shall it be in the day when the Son of man is revealed" — the world at Christ's return will not be obviously apocalyptic; it will be conducting business as usual; the very normalcy of Sodom before the fire is the warning sign
- **Matthew 10:15** — "It shall be more tolerable for the land of Sodom and Gomorrha in the day of judgment, than for that city" — towns that rejected Christ's apostles are judged more severely than Sodom; Sodom received no gospel; gospel-rejecting cities have less excuse
- **2 Peter 2:6** — God "turned the cities of Sodom and Gomorrha into ashes, condemned them with an overthrow, making them an ensample (hupodeigma — example, pattern) unto those that after should live ungodly" — Sodom is an example, a pattern; it is not merely a historical event but a template of divine judgment on entrenched unrepentant sin

---

### Hebron — Type of Deep Fellowship With God

**Genesis 13:18** — Abram builds an altar at Hebron (fellowship/communion)

- **The altar at Hebron** is where Abraham received the divine visit of Genesis 18 (the three men at Mamre); the place of fellowship became the place of the greatest face-to-face encounter with God in the patriarchal narratives; the fellowship (chebar) with God deepens at the altar of fellowship (Chevron/Hebron)
- **Psalm 25:14** — "The secret of the LORD is with them that fear him; and he will shew them his covenant" — Abram dwelling at Hebron (fellowship) received God's secrets (Genesis 18:17 — "Shall I hide from Abraham that thing which I do?"); the man who lives in chebar with God receives God's confidences
- **James 2:23** — "And the scripture was fulfilled which saith, Abraham believed God, and it was imputed unto him for righteousness: and he was called the Friend of God" — Abraham = the Friend of God (philos Theou); the Hebrew background is chebar; Abraham the fellowship-dweller is Abraham the friend-of-God; Hebron is where that friendship is deepest

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "Lot Was Righteous" — Therefore His Life Choices Were Approved by God

**The Misuse:** 2 Peter 2:7-8 calling Lot "righteous" and "just" is used to suggest his choices in Sodom were valid, or that the story does not critique him.

**What Scripture says:**

- **The righteousness was forensic, not experiential:** 2 Peter 2:7 says Lot was "just" (dikaion — righteous in position before God); this is the same forensic righteousness all believers have through faith; it does not mean every decision is righteous
- **The cost of his choices was total:** Lost his home, his property, his wife (Genesis 19:26), his sons-in-law who would not leave (Genesis 19:14), his dignity (Genesis 19:30-38 — his daughters' actions showed Sodom's moral formation in them); he was "saved, yet so as by fire" (1 Corinthians 3:15 — the principle if not the specific application)
- **Peter's account is descriptive, not prescriptive:** 2 Peter 2:7-8 says his soul was "vexed" (basanizomenos — tortured, distressed) daily by the wickedness around him; being righteous and being daily tortured by your environment is not a commendation of the choice to live there; it is a record of the cost
- **The daughters' Sodom-formation:** Genesis 19:30-38 — Lot's daughters devised and executed a plan of sexual manipulation that produced the Moabites and Ammonites; the moral formation of Sodom had been absorbed through their years of residence; Lot's righteous soul could not protect his children from the formation of the city they lived in
- *Correction from Scripture:* Lot's righteousness confirms that God saves by faith, not works. It does not confirm that Lot's choices were wise. Scripture's own verdict is that Lot chose by sight, lost by Sodom, and was saved only by grace and Abraham's intercession (Genesis 19:29 — "when God destroyed the cities of the plain... God remembered Abraham, and sent Lot out").

---

### "The Men of Sodom Were Wicked" — Sodom's Sin Was Only Inhospitality

**The Misuse:** A prominent interpretive tradition argues that Sodom's sin was exclusively social — inhospitality to strangers — and that Genesis 19's account of the men of Sodom demands is about social aggression, not sexual sin; some use Ezekiel 16:49 (pride, excess, idleness) to replace rather than complement the sexual identification.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Jude 7 is the definitive New Testament interpretation:** "Even as Sodom and Gomorrha, and the cities about them in like manner, giving themselves over to fornication (ekporneuein), and going after strange flesh (sarkos heteras), are set forth for an example, suffering the vengeance of eternal fire" — the apostle Jude explicitly names the sin as porneia and "strange/other flesh"; these are sexual terms; the New Testament does not support the inhospitality-only reading
- **2 Peter 2:6-8** — "Turning the cities of Sodom and Gomorrha into ashes... And delivered just Lot, vexed with the filthy conversation (aselgeia — licentiousness, debauchery) of the wicked" — aselgeia specifically refers to sexual licentiousness; Peter's word for the surrounding wickedness is a sexual-ethics term
- **Genesis 19:4-5** — "The men of Sodom, compassed the house round, both old and young, all the people from every quarter... and said... bring them out unto us, that we may know (yada) them" — yada in the sexual sense is confirmed by Lot's response (v.8 — he offers his daughters instead, "which have not known (yada) man"); the sexual intent is unmistakable
- **Ezekiel 16:49-50 adds to, not replaces:** Ezekiel lists pride, excess, idleness, social cruelty AND toevah (abomination); he is expanding the sin profile, not substituting it; Jude 7 and 2 Peter 2:7 make clear the toevah was sexual
- **The "inhospitality" reading ignores Lot's response:** If the demand was for social introduction, Lot's offer of his virgin daughters as an alternative (Genesis 19:8) makes no sense; the substitution of daughters only makes sense if the demand was sexual
- *Correction from Scripture:* Sodom's sin was comprehensive: pride, excess, social cruelty, and sexual abomination (as Ezekiel 16:49-50 lists). Jude 7 defines the sexual abomination as fornication and going after "strange/other flesh." The inhospitality reading is contradicted by Jude 7, 2 Peter 2:7-8, Lot's own response in Genesis 19:8, and the plain meaning of yada in context.

---

### "Pitched His Tent Toward Sodom" — Used to Justify Proximity to Ungodliness as Strategy

**The Misuse:** Some argue Lot's proximity to Sodom was a deliberate mission strategy — being salt and light; his presence in the gate (Genesis 19:1) is reread as civic influence for good.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Scripture never commends Lot's proximity:** The narrative presents it as the outcome of a sight-driven choice; there is no divine commendation of Lot's strategy; his city-gate position in Genesis 19 is mentioned as a fact, not praised as faithfulness
- **The mission strategy failed:** Lot had zero influence on Sodom; his sons-in-law "mocked" his warning (Genesis 19:14); his daughters were morally formed by Sodom, not the reverse; he could not find ten righteous people in the city to prevent its destruction; whatever "salt" he was had lost its savor
- **1 Corinthians 15:33** — "Evil communications corrupt good manners" — the consistent principle is that sustained ungodly association corrupts the believer, not the reverse; Lot's case is the proof-text
- **Psalm 1:1** — The blessed man does not "sit in the seat of the scornful"; the Hebrew blessing begins with not sitting where Lot sat (the gate = the seat of city authority)
- **The distinction:** There is a proper Christian engagement with the world (Matthew 5:13-16 — salt and light; John 17:15 — not taken out of the world but kept from evil); this is not the same as choosing to live in the environment of Sodom; the distinction is between presence for mission and residence by worldly choice; Lot chose residence by sight, not mission by calling
- *Correction from Scripture:* Lot's city-gate presence was the result of his sight-driven choice, not a Spirit-led mission strategy. Scripture's own verdict (2 Peter 2:7-8 — his soul was daily vexed, not daily fruitful) shows his presence accomplished no redemptive work. The proper response to Sodom is Abram's: intercession from outside (Genesis 18:23-33), not integration from within.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### Two Visions — Lot's Sight and God's Promise

**Two "lift up your eyes" moments in Genesis 13:**

- **v.10** — Lot lifted up his eyes: saw prosperity; chose self; journeyed east; lost everything
- **v.14** — God told Abram to lift up his eyes: saw promise; received inheritance; stayed in Canaan; received everything

**Thread through Scripture:**
- Genesis 22:4 — "Abraham lifted up his eyes, and saw the place" (Moriah) — the faith-vision on the way to the ultimate test
- Genesis 22:13 — "Abraham lifted up his eyes, and looked, and behold behind him a ram" — the provision-vision after obedience
- Numbers 13:27-33 — the ten spies lifted up their eyes and saw the giants; the two faithful spies (Caleb and Joshua) lifted up their eyes and saw the inheritance; same land, different vision; the eyes see what the heart is set on
- **Hebrews 12:2** — "Looking unto Jesus the author and finisher of our faith" — the ultimate "lift up your eyes"; fixing the gaze on Christ rather than on the Jordan plain or the giants; the final form of the faith-vision

---

### East — The Direction of Exile

**Genesis 13:11** — "Lot journeyed east"

**Thread through Scripture:**
- Genesis 3:24 — east of Eden: exile of Adam
- Genesis 4:16 — east of Eden: exile of Cain
- Genesis 11:2 — eastward to Shinar: Babel's rebellion
- Genesis 13:11 — east toward Sodom: Lot's compromise
- Genesis 25:6 — east country: the non-covenant sons
- 1 Kings 11:7; 2 Kings 23:13 — "the mount that is before Jerusalem" (the east) — where Solomon built altars to foreign gods; the east as the site of idol worship surrounding the city
- Ezekiel 10:18-19; 11:23 — the glory of God departs east from the temple: the ultimate exile judgment
- *Reversal:* Ezekiel 43:1-4 — "The glory of the God of Israel came from the way of the east" — the glory RETURNS from the east; the direction of exile becomes the direction of restoration; Zechariah 14:4 — the LORD stands on the Mount of Olives (east of Jerusalem) at His return; what east means in exile God redeems at the restoration

---

### Tent vs. City — Pilgrim Identity vs. World Settlement

**Genesis 13:12** — "Abram dwelled in the land of Canaan, and Lot dwelled in the cities of the plain"

**Thread through Scripture:**
- Abram: tent (ohel) — pilgrim; no permanent structure; always ready to move at God's direction; builds altars, not houses
- Lot: cities (arim) — settler; integrated into the world's urban system; no altars mentioned
- **Cain** built the first city (Genesis 4:17) after his exile; the city is associated from its first mention with man's attempt to build permanence without God
- **Nimrod** built cities (Genesis 10:10-12) — Babel, Nineveh; the city as the seat of human power
- **Abraham's testimony:** Hebrews 11:9-10 — "he sojourned in the land of promise, as in a strange country, dwelling in tabernacles (tents)... For he looked for a city which hath foundations, whose builder and maker is God" — the tent is not an inferior dwelling; it is the declaration that the earthly city is not ultimate; the tent-dweller is the one looking for the right city
- **The resolution:** Revelation 21:2 — "the holy city, new Jerusalem, coming down from God out of heaven" — the city God builds descends; Babel's city was built from the ground up and destroyed; God's city comes down from heaven; the tent-dwelling believer finally receives the city he was always looking for

### Altar Markers — Abram's Covenant Stakes in the Land

**Genesis 12:7, 8; 13:18** — Three altars in Abram's first Canaan journey

**The altar-map of Abram's faith journey:**
1. Shechem (Genesis 12:7) — first theophany; land promise; first covenant stake
2. Between Bethel and Ai (Genesis 12:8) — second altar; called on the name of the LORD
3. Hebron/Mamre (Genesis 13:18) — third altar; after Lot's departure; fellowship established

**No altar for Lot:** The contrast is in what is absent; Lot builds no altar at the Jordan, at the plain, at Sodom; his life in the best-watered land of all lacks the one act that marks the covenant journey; the land without the altar is the world without God

**The altar thread leads to:**
- Genesis 22:9 — Moriah; the ram; the near-sacrifice; the type of Christ's sacrifice
- 2 Chronicles 3:1 — Solomon built the temple "in mount Moriah... where David had prepared, in the threshingfloor of Ornan the Jebusite" — the site of Abraham's altar becomes the site of the temple; the covenant stakes Abram drove with altars across Canaan climax at the house of God on Moriah

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Conflict Between Covenant People Over Material Resources

**Genesis 13:7** — "There was a strife between the herdmen of Abram's cattle and the herdmen of Lot's cattle"
- First recorded conflict between members of the covenant household
- Caused by material abundance, not scarcity — "their substance was great"
- First mention establishes: prosperity without spiritual maturity produces conflict; the covenant people are not immune to resource competition; material blessing without relational priority creates strife; the resolution model (surrender the right to preserve the relationship) becomes the New Covenant standard of 1 Corinthians 6:7

### First "Lift Up Your Eyes" Vision From God

**Genesis 13:14** — "Lift up now thine eyes, and look from the place where thou art"
- First time God directs the covenant man to survey the land in four directions
- The promise scope is the horizon of vision — as far as you can see in every direction
- First mention establishes: God's covenant promise exceeds human vision in every direction; faith sees what God points to, not what the eye naturally lands on; the God-directed gaze and the self-directed gaze (Lot's "lifted up his eyes" in v.10) are the two ways of seeing in one chapter

### First "Forever" (Olam) Land Promise

**Genesis 13:15** — "To thee will I give it, and to thy seed for ever (olam)"
- First use of olam in connection with the land promise to Abraham
- Establishes the perpetual character of the Abrahamic land covenant
- First mention establishes: the land is not temporary or conditional on generation-by-generation obedience; the olam covenant grounds the land title in God's eternal commitment, not in Israel's performance; Romans 4:13 extends this olam to "heir of the world"

### First Record of God Speaking Only After Separation From a Compromising Association

**Genesis 13:14** — "The LORD said unto Abram, AFTER THAT LOT WAS SEPARATED from him"
- First recorded instance of God's word coming specifically in connection with a completed separation
- The timing ("after that") is theologically significant; God did not expand the vision while Lot was present
- First mention establishes: some of God's fullest revelations come only after obedient separations; the 2 Corinthians 6:14-18 principle ("be ye separate... and I will receive you, and will be a Father unto you") has its first expression in Genesis 13:14

### First Complete Moral Verdict on Sodom

**Genesis 13:13** — "The men of Sodom were wicked and sinners before the LORD exceedingly"
- First comprehensive moral assessment of Sodom before its destruction
- The triple condemnation (wicked, sinners, exceedingly) sets the standard by which all subsequent Sodom references are measured
- First mention establishes: Sodom represents the comprehensive moral inversion of God's created order; it is not merely a city with one vice but a city that is defined by its thorough wickedness; all later references to Sodom build on this baseline verdict

### First Record of Abram Surrendering a Legal Right for Peace

**Genesis 13:8-9** — "Let there be no strife... Is not the whole land before thee? separate thyself from me"
- First instance in the patriarchal narrative of a covenant man surrendering a legitimate prior claim for relational peace
- As the elder, the choice was Abram's by right; he offered it to Lot
- First mention establishes: the maturity of faith includes the willingness to surrender legal rights for covenant relationships; this is the pattern Christ embodies (Philippians 2:5-8 — He who had the right of heaven surrendered it); the anti-litigation, anti-strife posture of the New Covenant believer is rooted in Abram's Genesis 13 choice

## NAME MEANINGS

| Name | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Hebron (Chevron) | Fellowship, Communion, Alliance | From chavar — to join; Abram chose to dwell in the place of fellowship; his deepest covenant encounters with God happened here; he is buried here |
| Mamre | Fatness, Robustness, Strength | The Amorite ally of Abram; his plains provided the setting for the greatest theophany to Abraham (Genesis 18); the strength of fellowship |
| Sodom (Sedom) | Burning, Scorched | The name is prophetic of the city's end; Genesis 19:24 — fire and brimstone from heaven |
| Gomorrah (Amorah) | Submersion, Flooding | Also prophetic; the two cities named for burning and submersion both meet their ends by fire from heaven |
| Zoar | Small, Little | The small city to which Lot fled; "let it be little" (Genesis 19:20); Lot's appeal for mercy — just a small request; his life at Zoar is not recorded as blessed |
| Jordan | Descender, The Descending River | The Jordan flows down from Galilee to the Dead Sea (below sea level); the plain of Jordan was well-watered but led to Sodom and the cities destroyed; the river whose name means "descend" flows through the territory Lot chose; the descent continues |
