## CHAPTER SUMMARY

In the days of Amraphel king of Shinar, Arioch king of Ellasar, Chedorlaomer king of Elam, and Tidal king of nations — four kings made war against five: the kings of Sodom, Gomorrah, Admah, Zeboiim, and Bela. For twelve years the five served Chedorlaomer; in the thirteenth year they rebelled; in the fourteenth year Chedorlaomer returned with his allies and swept through the land — smiting the Rephaim, Zuzim, Emim, Horites, Amalekites, and Amorites — before defeating the five kings in the vale of Siddim. Sodom and Gomorrah were plundered. Lot, who now dwelt in Sodom, was taken captive with all his goods — the direct fruit of his sight-driven choice of Genesis 13. A fugitive reported to "Abram the Hebrew" — the first use of the word "Hebrew" (Ivri) in Scripture, marking Abram's covenant identity as distinct from all the surrounding peoples. Abram armed 318 trained servants born in his own house, allied with Mamre, Aner, and Eshcol, pursued the kings to Dan, divided his forces by night, and recovered everything — all goods, all people, all women, and Lot. Returning victorious, Abram was met in the valley of Shaveh by two kings: the king of Sodom, and Melchizedek king of Salem. Melchizedek — king of righteousness, king of peace, priest of the most high God (El Elyon) — brought forth bread and wine, blessed Abram of the most high God, and praised El Elyon who had delivered Abram's enemies. Abram gave him tithes of all. The king of Sodom then made his offer: "Give me the persons, take the goods." Abram refused everything — having sworn to the LORD, El Elyon, possessor of heaven and earth, that he would not take from a thread to a shoelatchet, "lest thou shouldest say, I have made Abram rich." God alone would be the source of Abram's wealth.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### The War of Four Kings Against Five — The First International Conflict

**Genesis 14:1-9** — "Four kings with five"

- **First war recorded in any detail in Scripture** — nine named kings, multiple nations, coalition warfare, international tribute and rebellion; the same pattern Matthew 24:7 names as a sign of the end: "For nation shall rise against nation, and kingdom against kingdom"
- **The twelve/thirteen/fourteen year pattern:** Twelve years of service → thirteenth year of rebellion → fourteenth year of judgment; the number thirteen is associated with rebellion throughout Scripture (Genesis 14:4 is the first instance); the rebellion brought the immediate return of the oppressor with greater force — disobedience to a lesser authority resulted in its reassertion with violence; **Romans 13:2** — "They that resist shall receive to themselves damnation" — the principle is consistent; rebellion against a governing authority (however evil) without God's express command results in judgment
- **Chedorlaomer king of Elam** — Elam is identified in Genesis 10:22 as a son of Shem; the first military oppressor of the Sodom region was Semitic; this is not Gentile-vs-Semite warfare but Semitic imperial power; **Isaiah 21:2** — "Go up, O Elam: besiege, O Media" — Elam reappears as a military power in prophecy; **Jeremiah 49:34-39** — the LORD's judgment and restoration of Elam in the latter days
- **Amraphel king of Shinar** — Shinar is Babel/Babylonia (Genesis 10:10; 11:2; Daniel 1:2); some ancient sources identified Amraphel with Hammurabi, though this is not certain; Shinar is always the territory of the anti-God kingdom system; a king of Shinar leading the coalition is consistent with the Babel/Babylon thread
- **The Rephaim (v.5)** — "Smote the Rephaims in Ashteroth Karnaim" — the Rephaim are the giants, the post-flood remnant of Nephilim-class warriors; **Deuteronomy 3:11** — Og king of Bashan was the last of the Rephaim; **Numbers 13:33** — the spies saw "the sons of Anak, which come of the giants (Nephilim)"; the fact that the four kings smote the Rephaim en route establishes the military supremacy of the eastern coalition; Abram's 318 servants defeated what these giants could not hold

### "Abram the Hebrew" — Covenant Identity Declared

**Genesis 14:13** — "And there came one that had escaped, and told Abram the Hebrew (Ha-Ivri)"

- **First use of "Hebrew" (Ivri) in Scripture** — Abram is identified to the outside world by this covenant-ethnic designation; it comes from a foreigner's mouth (the escaped fugitive), showing this was the recognized external identification of Abram's people
- **Ivri** = from Eber (Genesis 10:21; 11:14-17) — one who descends from Eber; also related to abar (to cross over, to pass through, to ford a river) — the crossing-over people; Abram crossed the Euphrates at God's call; Joshua 24:2-3 — "your fathers dwelt on the other side of the flood (the River)... and I took your father Abraham from the other side of the river" — Abram is ha-ivri because he crossed from Mesopotamia to Canaan at God's word; the Hebrew identity is rooted in obedient crossing-over
- **Genesis 39:14, 17** — Potiphar's wife calls Joseph "an Hebrew man (ish ivri)" — the Egyptian perspective on the covenant people; Hebrew is the designation used when the covenant people are identified by outsiders or in situations requiring distinction from the surrounding peoples
- **Exodus 1:15-16; 2:11** — "Hebrew midwives," Moses' "Hebrew brethren" — the covenant identity defined by descent from Abraham/Eber
- **Exodus 3:18** — "The LORD God of the Hebrews hath met with us" — God Himself is identified by the Hebrews when speaking to Pharaoh; the covenant name and the covenant people are bound together
- **Philippians 3:5** — Paul: "An Hebrew of the Hebrews" — the double superlative; identity traceable all the way back to Eber and across to Genesis 14:13's first use; the title carries the full weight of covenant descent from Abraham through to the New Testament era
- *Interpretation:* The first appearance of "Hebrew" occurs in a military crisis, spoken by a non-Hebrew to identify Abram as the covenant man. This sets the pattern: the Hebrew identity is most visible when the covenant people are under pressure, in combat, or in foreign territory. The name carries Eber's meaning (crossing over) and the covenant's significance (the people defined by obedient passage through whatever God commands).

### Abram Arms His Trained Servants — The Warrior Patriarch

**Genesis 14:14** — "He armed his trained servants, born in his own house, three hundred and eighteen, and pursued them unto Dan"

- **318 trained servants born in his own house** — not mercenaries but household-born men trained for combat; Abram was not a wandering shepherd but the chieftain of a significant household force; the size of his military capacity confirms the Genesis 13:2 wealth assessment ("very rich")
- **"Born in his own house"** — the Hebrew ylidei beyto (children/sons of his house) — these men were covenant members of Abram's household, raised under his authority; their loyalty was absolute; they were trained (chanikh — exercised, experienced) specifically in warfare
- **1 Samuel 14:6** — "There is no restraint to the LORD to save by many or by few" — Jonathan's principle; Abram's 318 against a coalition that had just defeated five kings and the Rephaim; the disproportion is the point; God did not need 3,000 or 30,000
- **Judges 7:7** — Gideon's 300 vs. the Midianite host — the same principle; God consistently uses small numbers to ensure His glory is not shared with human military strength (Judges 7:2 — "Lest Israel vaunt themselves against me, saying, Mine own hand hath saved me")
- **The night attack (v.15):** "He divided himself against them, he and his servants, by night" — dividing the force, attacking by night, creating the impression of a larger and multi-directional force; Abram demonstrated both faith and military wisdom; faith without prudence is presumption; **Luke 14:31** — "Or what king, going to make war against another king, sitteth not down first, and consulteth whether he be able with ten thousand to meet him that cometh against him with twenty thousand?" — the LORD commends military calculation; Abram calculated and acted with wisdom

### Two Kings in the Valley of Shaveh — The Contrast

**Genesis 14:17-21** — The king of Sodom and Melchizedek both meet Abram after the victory

- **The valley of Shaveh (the king's dale)** — a flat valley, identified in 2 Samuel 18:18 as the location where Absalom erected his monument ("Absalom's pillar"); the place of earthly memorial
- **Two kings, two offers, two different spirits:**
  - Melchizedek: brings bread and wine (sustenance and joy), blesses Abram in God's name, praises God who delivered the enemies, receives tithes — the spirit of worship and covenant recognition
  - King of Sodom: makes a transactional offer ("Give me the persons, take the goods") — the spirit of the world's commerce; no blessing of God, no recognition of divine deliverance, just a deal
- **The contrast is the chapter's moral climax:** Abram had just won a great victory; two responses came from two directions; Melchizedek's blessing pointed Abram back to God; the king of Sodom's offer would have entangled Abram with Sodom; Abram accepted the worship-offer (tithes) and refused the commercial offer (spoils) — distinguishing between what is God's and what is the world's
- **Genesis 15:1** — "After these things the word of the LORD came unto Abram in a vision, saying, Fear not, Abram: I am thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward" — God immediately responded to Abram's refusal of the king of Sodom with a direct promise: "I am... thy exceeding great reward"; what the world offered (the spoils), God replaced with Himself as the reward

### Melchizedek Blesses Abram — The Greater Blesses the Lesser

**Genesis 14:19-20** — "And he blessed him... And blessed be the most high God"

- **Hebrews 7:7** — "And without all contradiction the less is blessed of the better" — the principle is irrefutable; the one who blesses is greater than the one blessed; Melchizedek blessed Abraham; therefore Melchizedek > Abraham; therefore the Melchizedek priesthood > the Levitical priesthood; therefore Christ's priesthood (after the order of Melchizedek) > Aaron's priesthood
- **The double blessing:** Melchizedek blessed Abram (v.19) AND the most high God (v.20) — the priest blessed the man going upward to God and blessed God going downward to man; the priest stands between, connecting earth and heaven; this is the definition of the priestly role
- **Numbers 6:24-26** — the Aaronic blessing ("The LORD bless thee and keep thee...") — given after this text; it follows the pattern established by Melchizedek; the priest pronounces blessing from God upon the people; Melchizedek originated the form before Aaron codified it
- **Galatians 3:9** — "So then they which be of faith are blessed with faithful Abraham" — through Christ (the Melchizedek priest), the blessing flows to Abraham's spiritual seed; the blessing of Genesis 14:19 reaches every believer who is IN Abraham's Seed

### Abram's Tithe — Pre-Law, Pre-Levi, to the Greater Priest

**Genesis 14:20** — "And he gave him tithes of all"

- **Hebrews 7:4-10** — "Now consider how great this man was, unto whom even the patriarch Abraham gave the tenth of the spoils... And as I may so say, Levi also, who receiveth tithes, payed tithes in Abraham: For he was yet in the loins of his father, when Melchizedek met him"
  - The argument: Levi (the Levitical priesthood) was biologically inside Abraham when Abraham tithed; therefore Levi tithed through Abraham; therefore the Levitical priesthood tithed to Melchizedek's priesthood; therefore the Melchizedek order is superior to the Levitical order; Hebrews 7 makes the case entirely from Genesis 14:20
- **Pre-Law establishment:** Abraham tithed 430 years before the Mosaic Law codified tithing (Leviticus 27:30-32; Numbers 18:21-26); the practice precedes the legislation; **Galatians 3:17** — the covenant cannot be voided by the Law; what was established in Abraham precedes and outlasts what was established in Moses
- **Genesis 28:22** — Jacob's vow: "Of all that thou shalt give me I will surely give the tenth unto thee" — the tithing principle passed from Abraham to Jacob without Mosaic legislation; it was a covenant instinct before it was a legal requirement
- **Malachi 3:10** — "Bring ye all the tithes into the storehouse... and prove me now herewith, saith the LORD of hosts, if I will not open you the windows of heaven, and pour you out a blessing, that there shall not be room enough to receive it" — the Malachi tithes go into the storehouse of the temple (Levitical system), not to Melchizedek directly; but the principle of returning the tenth to God's appointed priest precedes Sinai and outlasts it
- *Interpretation:* Abram's tithe in Genesis 14:20 is the foundational act that establishes three theological realities simultaneously: (1) the Melchizedek priesthood is superior to the Levitical; (2) tithing is a pre-Law covenant response, not merely a Mosaic regulation; (3) the believer's proper response to covenant victory is to honor the priest of God before accepting any reward from the world.

### "Not a Thread Nor a Shoelatchet" — The Total Refusal

**Genesis 14:22-23** — "I will not take from a thread even to a shoelatchet, and that I will not take any thing that is thine, lest thou shouldest say, I have made Abram rich"

- **The oath:** "I have lift up mine hand unto the LORD" — the formal gesture of oath-swearing in the ancient world; Abram did not merely decline the offer; he had pre-sworn an oath before God that he would not accept anything from Sodom's king; the oath made the refusal binding and formal, not just personal preference
- **"Thread to shoelatchet"** — from the cheapest thread (chut) to the cheapest leather strap (serok na'al) — a merismus for everything from the least to the most; not a partial refusal ("I'll take some but not all") but an absolute refusal of everything from the most trivial to the most valuable
- **The reason:** "Lest thou shouldest say, I have made Abram rich" — Abram was protecting God's reputation as his sole provider; if Abram accepted Sodom's wealth, Sodom's king could claim credit for Abram's prosperity; the source of blessing matters as much as the blessing itself
- **Genesis 15:1** — "I am... thy exceeding great reward" — God answered Abram's refusal of Sodom's wealth by declaring Himself to BE the reward; what the world offered (perishable goods), God replaced with Himself; the God-is-my-reward statement is the direct divine response to the thread-and-shoelatchet refusal
- **Proverbs 22:1** — "A good name is rather to be chosen than great riches, and loving favour rather than silver and gold" — Abram chose the good name (God alone enriches me) over great riches (Sodom's spoils)
- **2 Corinthians 6:14-16** — "What fellowship hath righteousness with unrighteousness? and what communion hath light with darkness?... what part hath he that believeth with an infidel?" — Paul's principle is Abram's practice; the believer does not become indebted to or enriched by the world system; the thread-and-shoelatchet refusal is the Old Testament form of 2 Corinthians 6's principle
- **The allowance for his allies:** v.24 — Abram excluded Aner, Eshcol, and Mamre from his oath; they were his Amorite allies who had fought alongside him; they were not under his oath, and he would not impose his covenant convictions on non-covenant men; this is a mark of spiritual wisdom: Abram's convictions governed Abram's conduct without being imposed on those outside the covenant
- *Interpretation:* The thread-and-shoelatchet refusal is one of the most decisive moments in the Abrahamic narrative. It shows that Abram distinguished not only between good and evil (he would not take Sodom's wealth) but between good sources and tainted sources of the same good. The spoils were legitimately won; Abram had earned every thread and shoelatchet. His refusal was not legal but covenantal: I have sworn that God alone will be the credit for my wealth. This is the anti-Lot posture — where Lot moved toward Sodom for its material abundance, Abram refused Sodom's wealth to keep his covenant testimony pure.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### HEBREW / IVRI (Hebrew: ha-Ivri — הָעִבְרִי)

**Genesis 14:13** — "And there came one that had escaped, and told Abram the HEBREW (ha-Ivri)"

- **First use of "Hebrew" (Ivri) applied to a person in Scripture** — this designation identifies Abram's covenant-ethnic identity in the eyes of the surrounding peoples; it is the external name, spoken by outsiders (or in contexts where distinction from non-covenant peoples is required)
- **Two roots, both applicable:**
  1. From **Eber** (Genesis 10:21; 11:14) — Abram descended from Eber through the Shem → Arphaxad → Salah → Eber → Peleg → ... → Terah → Abram line; "Ivri" = "one from Eber"; the ethnic/genealogical identity
  2. From **abar** (to cross over, to pass through, to ford) — the "crosser-over" people; Joshua 24:2-3 — "your fathers dwelt on the other side of the River (me'ever ha-nahar)... I took your father Abraham from beyond the River"; Abram crossed the Euphrates at God's command; the Hebrews are defined by the crossing-over obedience
  - Both roots are embedded in the word; Ivri = descended from Eber + characterized by crossing over
- **The contexts in which "Hebrew" appears:**
  - Genesis 14:13 — by a military fugitive in a crisis; Abram's identity under pressure
  - Genesis 39:14, 17 — by Potiphar's wife to identify Joseph in accusation; Hebrew identity in foreign accusation
  - Genesis 40:15; 41:12 — Joseph identifies himself and is identified as Hebrew; covenant identity in captivity
  - Exodus 1:15-16; 2:6, 7, 11, 13 — in the context of slavery and birth-control genocide; Hebrew identity under oppression
  - Exodus 3:18; 5:3; 7:16 — "the LORD God of the Hebrews" — God's self-identification in relation to His covenant people before Pharaoh
  - Jonah 1:9 — "I am an Hebrew" — Jonah's self-identification to Gentile sailors in crisis
  - Philippians 3:5 — Paul: "An Hebrew of the Hebrews" — the deepest genealogical-covenant identity claim; tracing all the way to Genesis 14:13
- **"Hebrew" vs. "Israelite" vs. "Jew":** "Hebrew" (Ivri) = ethnic/genealogical/covenant identity from Eber, used primarily when contrasting with Gentiles; "Israelite" (Yisraeli) = descendant of Jacob/Israel, used primarily within covenant community discourse; "Jew" (Yehudi) = from Judah, the primary designation after the Babylonian exile; all three trace to Genesis 14:13's first Ivri
- *Interpretation:* The first use of "Hebrew" in Scripture identifies Abram by his covenant distinctiveness in the context of a military rescue mission. The Hebrews are the crossing-over people — those who, like Abram, obey God's call to ford the rivers of their known world and enter the unknown at His word. Every "Hebrew" in Scripture carries this first-use definition: covenant identity marked by obedient crossing-over under God's direction.

### MELCHIZEDEK (Hebrew: Malki-Tzedeq — מַלְכִּי־צֶדֶק)

**Genesis 14:18** — "And Melchizedek king of Salem brought forth bread and wine: and he was the priest of the most high God"

- **The name:** Malki (my king, from melek — king) + Tzedeq (righteousness, from tzadak — to be righteous, just); Melchizedek = "My King Is Righteousness" or "King of Righteousness"
- **Hebrews 7:2** — "First being by interpretation King of righteousness, and after that also King of Salem, which is, King of peace" — the inspired New Testament interpretation of both the name (righteousness) and his title (peace); righteousness first, then peace — the consistent biblical order (Isaiah 32:17 — "the work of righteousness shall be peace")
- **The unique combination:** King AND priest — no other figure in the Hebrew Bible holds both offices simultaneously except Christ; **1 Samuel 13:9-14** — Saul was judged for performing a priestly function; **2 Chronicles 26:16-21** — Uzziah was struck with leprosy for the same; the combination was reserved for Melchizedek (pre-Mosaic) and for Christ (post-Mosaic, after the order of Melchizedek — Hebrews 7)
- **"Without father, without mother, without descent" (Hebrews 7:3):** Not a literal statement about his origin but a statement about the genealogical record; Levitical priests required documented genealogy to serve (Ezra 2:62 — priests excluded because "they could not shew their father's house"); Melchizedek had no such recorded lineage; his priesthood was not based on birth-qualification but on divine appointment; this is why the phrase is "made like unto the Son of God" — not literally eternal but typologically resembling the eternal priest whose priesthood requires no human genealogy to validate it
- **The Christophany question:** Two main positions:
  1. **Type of Christ (majority view):** Melchizedek was a historical king of pre-Israelite Jerusalem, a genuine human being, whose specific historical characteristics (no recorded genealogy, king-priest combination, bread and wine, blessing Abraham, receiving tithes) were divinely arranged to typify Christ's eternal priesthood; Hebrews 7:3 says "made LIKE unto the Son of God" — if he WERE the Son of God, it would say "the Son of God"
  2. **Christophany (minority view):** Melchizedek was the pre-incarnate Christ appearing in history; Hebrews 7:3's "no beginning of days nor end of life" is literal, not merely typological; "made like unto the Son of God" is the author's way of describing an actual appearance; the combination of no recorded genealogy, simultaneous king-priest, and eternal priesthood supports literal identification
  - **The text does not require us to choose dogmatically:** Hebrews 7 uses the typological language ("made like," "after the similitude of") that is consistent with both readings; the theological point is identical either way: Christ's priesthood is of Melchizedek's order, not Aaron's, and is therefore superior
- **Psalm 110:4** — "The LORD hath sworn, and will not repent, Thou art a priest for ever after the order of Melchizedek" — the only psalm expressly attributed to David; the pre-Davidic oath of the Father to the Son establishing the eternal priesthood; quoted in Hebrews 5:6, 6:20, and 7:17, 21; this psalm is the Old Testament foundation of the entire Hebrews 7 argument
- **The Melchizedek-Aaron contrast that Hebrews 7 builds from Genesis 14:**

| Aaronic/Levitical Priesthood | Melchizedek / Christ Priesthood |
|---|---|
| Based on genealogy (tribe of Levi) | Based on divine appointment (no recorded genealogy) |
| Temporary (priests died, needed successors) | Permanent ("abideth a priest continually" — Hebrews 7:3) |
| Offered animal blood (imperfect, repeated) | Offered Himself once (Hebrews 7:27) |
| Received tithes from Israel | Received tithes FROM ABRAHAM (who contained Levi) |
| Blessed by Abraham's greater | Blessed Abraham |
| Within the Mosaic covenant | Pre-Mosaic, therefore pre-Law |
| Served in earthly tabernacle | Serves in heavenly tabernacle (Hebrews 8:2) |

- *Interpretation:* Melchizedek's appearance in Genesis 14 is one of the most theologically loaded events in the entire Pentateuch. His name (righteousness), title (king of peace/Salem), role (priest of El Elyon), actions (bread and wine, blessing), and the response he received (tithes from Abraham) are all precisely calibrated to typify Christ's eternal priesthood. Hebrews 5-7 is the Spirit's own commentary on Genesis 14:18-20. To read Genesis 14 without reading Hebrews 7 is to read the shadow without the substance.

### SALEM (Hebrew: Shalem — שָׁלֵם)

**Genesis 14:18** — "Melchizedek king of Salem"

- **Shalem** = peace, wholeness, completeness — from shalom (peace, wholeness, well-being); "Salem" is the poetic/abbreviated form of Jerusalem (Yerushalem = "city of peace" or "foundation of peace")
- **Psalm 76:2** — "In Salem also is his tabernacle, and his dwelling place in Zion" — Psalm 76 equates Salem with Zion (Jerusalem); the tabernacle/temple is in Salem; Melchizedek was king of what would become David's Jerusalem
- **The progression of Salem:**
  - Genesis 14:18 — Salem: Melchizedek's city; pre-Israelite king-priest reigns here
  - 2 Samuel 5:6-9 — Jerusalem: David conquers the Jebusite city; establishes it as the capital of the Davidic kingdom
  - 2 Samuel 6:17 — Ark brought to Jerusalem; Zion becomes the dwelling place of God's name
  - Psalm 76:2 — Salem = Zion = God's dwelling
  - Hebrews 12:22 — "But ye are come unto mount Sion, and unto the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem" — the New Covenant heavenly Jerusalem; Salem elevated to its ultimate reality
  - Revelation 21:2 — "The holy city, new Jerusalem, coming down from God out of heaven" — the eternal Salem; peace fully realized; the city of the eternal Melchizedek-King

### EL ELYON (Hebrew: El Elyon — אֵל עֶלְיוֹן)

**Genesis 14:18-20, 22** — "Priest of the most high God (El Elyon)... the most high God, possessor of heaven and earth"

- **First use of "El Elyon" in Scripture** — introduced by Melchizedek (v.18-19) and immediately adopted by Abram in his oath to the king of Sodom (v.22); the fact that Abram identified the LORD (YHWH) with Melchizedek's El Elyon confirms that the two names refer to the same God
- **El** = God (the generic term for deity in Hebrew); **Elyon** = highest, most high, uppermost — from alah (to go up); El Elyon = the God who is above all others; the divine title makes a supremacy claim — there is no higher authority
- **"Possessor of heaven and earth" (qoneh shamayim va-aretz):** Not merely "creator" (bare') but "possessor/owner" (qanah — to acquire, to possess, to own); God owns everything because He made everything and because He has redeemed/acquired it; **Psalm 24:1** — "The earth is the LORD's, and the fulness thereof"; **Psalm 50:10-12** — "Every beast of the forest is mine... the world is mine, and the fulness thereof"
- **The significance of Abram's adoption of the title:** When Abram said to the king of Sodom, "I have lift up mine hand unto the LORD (YHWH), the most high God (El Elyon), the possessor of heaven and earth" (v.22) — he was doing two things: (1) identifying YHWH with Melchizedek's El Elyon (a theological claim of monotheistic supremacy over all local deities), and (2) invoking the possessor of EVERYTHING as his witness; if God owns everything, Sodom's king owns nothing that is not already God's; Abram is refusing a gift from someone who has no ultimate ownership of what he is offering
- **El Elyon throughout Scripture:**
  - Numbers 24:16 — Balaam's oracle: "the knowledge of the most High"
  - Deuteronomy 32:8 — "When the Most High divided to the nations their inheritance"
  - Psalm 7:17 — "I will praise the LORD according to his righteousness: and will sing praise to the name of the LORD most high"
  - Psalm 47:2 — "For the LORD most high is terrible; he is a great King over all the earth"
  - Psalm 83:18 — "That men may know that thou, whose name alone is JEHOVAH, art the most high over all the earth"
  - Psalm 91:1 — "He that dwelleth in the secret place of the most High"
  - Daniel 4:17, 24-25, 32 — "The most High ruleth in the kingdom of men" — used seven times in Daniel 4 alone; Nebuchadnezzar's humiliation is the demonstration that El Elyon is sovereign over earthly kings
  - **Luke 1:32, 35** — "He shall be called the Son of the Highest (Elyon)... the power of the Highest shall overshadow thee" — Gabriel uses the El Elyon title for God and for Christ in the Annunciation; the Son of El Elyon entering Mary's womb is the incarnation of the God Melchizedek served in Genesis 14
  - **Acts 16:17** — "These men are the servants of the most high God, which shew unto us the way of salvation" — a demon-possessed girl identifies Paul and Silas by the El Elyon title; even demons know and name El Elyon
- *Interpretation:* El Elyon is the title Scripture uses when asserting God's absolute supremacy over all powers, all kings, all nations, and all competing deities. Melchizedek introduced this title to Abraham in the context of a military victory over four kings. The lesson: the One who delivered the enemies into Abram's hand is not merely the God of Abram's household but El Elyon — owner of heaven and earth, supreme over all. Every competing claim to Abram's loyalty (the king of Sodom's wealth) is nullified by the ownership claim of El Elyon.

### KOHEN (Hebrew: kohen — כֹּהֵן) — PRIEST

**Genesis 14:18** — "He was the PRIEST (kohen) of the most high God"

- **First use of "kohen" (priest) in Scripture** — the entire priestly office, which will dominate Leviticus, Numbers, and much of the prophetic literature, begins here; and it begins not with a Levite, not with an Israelite, but with Melchizedek, who predates both; the first priest in Scripture belongs to no Mosaic institution
- **Kohen** = one who officiates, who stands near (to God), who serves in sacred office — the root is debated; some connect it to kun (to stand firm, to establish); the kohen is the one who stands in God's presence on behalf of the people and stands before the people on behalf of God
- **The priesthood trajectory in Scripture:**
  - Genesis 14:18 — First kohen: Melchizedek; pre-Israelite; no genealogical requirement; king-priest; serves El Elyon
  - Exodus 19:6 — "A kingdom of priests (mamlechet kohanim)": all Israel called to be a priestly nation; the Melchizedek pattern (all-encompassing priestly identity) applied to a whole people
  - Exodus 28-29 — Aaron and his sons consecrated as the Levitical kohanim; the institutional priesthood; genealogy-based; garments; sacrifices; incense; the full Mosaic elaboration
  - 1 Samuel 2:27-36 — God's covenant with Eli's house; prediction of its ending; "I will raise me up a faithful priest" — pointing beyond the Aaronic
  - Psalm 110:4 — "A priest for ever after the order of Melchizedek" — the eternal priesthood announced by David
  - Hebrews 5-7 — Christ as the eternal kohen who supersedes the Levitical order; goes back to the Genesis 14:18 foundation
  - Revelation 1:6; 5:10; 20:6 — "Made us kings and priests (hiereis) unto God" — the Melchizedek pattern (king-priest) restored to all believers; what Melchizedek alone was in Genesis 14, every believer becomes in Christ
- *Interpretation:* The first kohen in Scripture is also the last word on the priesthood: Melchizedek's order, which Christ fulfills, is the beginning and end of the priestly office. The Levitical priesthood is the middle parenthesis — necessary for the typological education of Israel and the demonstration that no human priesthood could make the final atonement. The first priest had no genealogy; the final Priest (Christ) needed none; the first priest brought bread and wine; the final Priest made His body and blood the bread and wine.

### BREAD AND WINE (Hebrew: lechem va-yayin)

**Genesis 14:18** — "And Melchizedek king of Salem brought forth bread and wine"

- **Lechem** = bread, food, the basic staff of life — bread in the ancient world was not a luxury but the sustaining element of human life; to bring bread is to bring life-sustenance after the battle
- **Yayin** = wine, fermented grape — the element of celebration, covenant, and joy; to bring wine after victory is to mark the moment as one of consecrated joy
- **Why a priest would bring bread and wine:** In the ancient world, priests conducted covenant meals; bread and wine were the elements of sacred fellowship between the divine and the human; Melchizedek brought the covenant meal to Abram — the priest mediating the covenant between El Elyon and His servant after the battle
- **The Lord's Supper connection:**
  - **Matthew 26:26-28** — "Jesus took bread, and blessed it, and brake it, and gave it to the disciples... And he took the cup, and gave thanks, and gave it to them, saying, Drink ye all of it; For this is my blood of the new testament"
  - **Luke 22:19-20** — "This is my body which is given for you: this do in remembrance of me... This cup is the new testament in my blood"
  - **1 Corinthians 11:23-26** — The Lord's Supper as the New Covenant form of what Melchizedek brought in the Abrahamic context
  - The first communion was served by the Melchizedek-type priest; the final establishment of the Lord's Supper was by the Melchizedek-reality Priest (Christ); the bread and wine of Genesis 14:18 and the bread and cup of Matthew 26 are the same elements, the same priest (in type and antitype), the same covenant context (victory — Christ's victory over sin and death)
- **John 6:35, 55** — "I am the bread of life... For my flesh is meat indeed, and my blood is drink indeed" — Christ claimed to be what the bread and wine of Melchizedek pointed toward; He is not merely the priest who serves the covenant meal; He IS the covenant meal
- *Interpretation:* The bread and wine of Genesis 14:18 is the first Lord's Supper in Scripture — served by the first priest, to the father of faith, after a military victory over the enemies of God's people. Every Lord's Supper since is a renewal of what Melchizedek began: the priest-king feeding the covenant people with the elements of his own victory-sacrifice.

### TITHE / MAASER (Hebrew: maaser — מַעֲשֵׂר)

**Genesis 14:20** — "And he gave him TITHES (maaser) of all"

- **Maaser** = tenth part — from asar (to give a tenth); the tithe is definitionally ten percent; it is not "a generous amount" or "a significant portion" — it is the specific fraction of ten percent dedicated to God through His appointed priest
- **The tithe given here is from battle spoils** — not from regular income or agricultural produce; the context is unique; Abram did not tithe to Melchizedek annually from his livestock — this was a specific post-battle tithe from the recovered goods; this distinction is important for understanding the extent to which Genesis 14:20 establishes tithing as a universal ongoing requirement vs. a specific covenant act
- **Pre-Mosaic tithing:**
  - Genesis 14:20 — Abram to Melchizedek (from battle spoils)
  - Genesis 28:22 — Jacob's vow: "Of all that thou shalt give me I will surely give the tenth unto thee" — Jacob did not wait for the Law to establish tithing; it was already a covenant instinct; but this is a vow, not necessarily a regular practice
- **Mosaic tithing (three distinct tithes in the Law):**
  - Leviticus 27:30-32 — The LORD's tithe: a tenth of all produce and livestock; given to the Levites
  - Numbers 18:26 — The Levites gave a tenth of their tithe to the priests (Aaron's sons) — a tithe of the tithe
  - Deuteronomy 14:22-27 — The festival tithe: a tenth consumed in rejoicing before the LORD in the place He chose
  - Deuteronomy 14:28-29 — The third-year tithe: a tenth for the Levite, stranger, fatherless, widow in the local town
- **Hebrews 7:4-10 — the theological argument from Genesis 14:20:** Levi (who received tithes from Israel under the Law) was "in the loins of his father" (Abraham) when Abraham tithed to Melchizedek; therefore Levi tithed through Abraham; therefore the Melchizedek priesthood is greater than the Levitical priesthood; the tithes of all Israel across all their history were foreshadowed in the single tithe of Genesis 14:20
- **Malachi 3:10; Matthew 23:23** — the later use and commendation of tithing; Christ commended tithing while saying greater matters (justice, mercy, faith) should not be neglected
- *Interpretation:* The tithe of Genesis 14:20 is the foundational tithing act in Scripture. It establishes that the practice precedes the Mosaic Law by 430 years (Galatians 3:17). Hebrews 7's argument that Levi paid tithes through Abraham is only possible because Genesis 14:20 existed. The New Covenant application of tithing must engage both the pre-Law nature of the practice (not merely a Mosaic regulation to be discarded) and Paul's "cheerful giver" principle (2 Corinthians 9:7 — not by compulsion); both streams flow from Genesis 14's single tithe event.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "Without Father, Without Mother" — Melchizedek Was a Divine Being or Eternal Entity

**The Misuse:** Hebrews 7:3 is read as a literal claim that Melchizedek had no biological parents and never died, making him an eternal pre-existent being distinct from both humanity and Christ.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Hebrews 7:3** says "made LIKE UNTO the Son of God" — not "the Son of God" himself; if Melchizedek WERE the Son of God in literal pre-incarnate appearance, the author would not need the simile "like unto"; the language is typological, not literal identification
- **"Without father, without mother, without descent"** refers to the genealogical record, not biological origin; the context (Hebrews 7:4-10) is entirely about the genealogical basis of priestly qualifications; Levitical priests served by genealogy (Ezra 2:62 — priests excluded because "they could not shew their father's house"); Melchizedek's priesthood had no genealogical requirement; "without father, without mother" = no genealogical record on which to base the priesthood
- **"Neither beginning of days, nor end of life"** — again, in the record; Genesis 14 does not record his birth or his death; in this it is unlike every other figure in Genesis (who have genealogies); this unrecorded-end is used to make the typological point: "made like unto the Son of God, abideth a priest continually" — not that Melchizedek literally lives forever, but that the record presents him as one whose priestly office has no terminus, typifying Christ whose priesthood genuinely has no end
- **Hebrews 7:8** — "Here men that die receive tithes; but there he receiveth them, of whom it is witnessed that he liveth" — the "he liveth" is the typological testimony of the record (no death recorded), not a biographical statement that Melchizedek never died
- *Correction from Scripture:* Melchizedek was a historical human figure whose specific historical circumstances — no recorded genealogy, no recorded death, simultaneous king-priest, serving El Elyon, bringing bread and wine, blessing Abraham, receiving tithes — were divinely arranged to typify Christ. Hebrews 7 uses these typological correspondences to argue for Christ's superior priesthood. The text does not make Melchizedek himself divine or eternal; it makes him a deliberate shadow of the One who is.

---

### Genesis 14:20 Proves Tithing is Mandatory for New Covenant Believers at 10%

**The Misuse:** Because Abraham tithed before the Law (and is therefore a pre-Law, non-Mosaic precedent), tithing of 10% of income is binding on all believers in all ages.

**What Scripture says:**

- **What the text actually establishes:** Abram tithed from battle spoils — a single, specific, post-victory act; the text does not record Abram tithing regularly from his income, livestock, or agricultural produce; Genesis 14:20 is one event, not a pattern or command
- **The Hebrews 7 use of this text:** Hebrews 7 uses Genesis 14:20 to argue the superiority of Melchizedek's priesthood over the Levitical, NOT to establish tithing as a universal New Covenant obligation; the argument is entirely about priestly hierarchy, not giving percentage
- **Jacob's tithe (Genesis 28:22)** was a conditional vow — "if God will be with me... I will surely give the tenth" — a personal vow, not a universal command
- **The Levitical tithe is addressed to Israel under the Mosaic covenant** — Leviticus 27:30-32; Numbers 18:21-26; these are covenant obligations tied to the Levitical system; when the Levitical system ended with the destruction of the temple (70 AD), the specific Levitical tithe structure ended with it
- **Malachi 3:10** is frequently applied directly to New Covenant believers; but Malachi was addressing post-exilic Israel under the Mosaic covenant with the temple system functioning; the "storehouse" is the Levitical temple storehouse, not a church budget
- **The New Covenant principle:** 2 Corinthians 9:6-7 — "He which soweth sparingly shall reap also sparingly... Every man according as he purposeth in his heart, so let him give; not grudgingly, or of necessity: for God loveth a cheerful giver" — Paul establishes the principle of proportionate, willing, generous, non-compulsory giving; he does not invoke the 10% figure
- **Matthew 23:23** — "Ye pay tithe of mint and anise and cummin, and have omitted the weightier matters of the law, judgment, mercy, and faith: these ought ye to have done, and not to leave the other undone" — Jesus commended tithing in the context of the Mosaic covenant still operative during His ministry (before the cross); He did not explicitly mandate it for the post-cross church
- *Correction from Scripture:* Genesis 14:20 establishes that the principle of giving a portion to God through His appointed priest is pre-Mosaic and covenant-instinctive. It does not establish a universal mandatory 10% obligation for New Covenant believers. New Covenant giving is governed by 2 Corinthians 9:6-7 (proportionate, cheerful, generous, non-compulsory) and by the recognition that God owns everything (El Elyon, possessor of heaven and earth — v.22). The percentage is the floor of the principle, not the ceiling; the New Covenant call is to "give yourself first to the Lord" (2 Corinthians 8:5) — which exceeds any percentage.

### Abram's Refusal of Sodom's Wealth Proves Christians Must Refuse All Material Blessing From Unbelievers

**The Misuse:** Some argue that Christians should refuse money, employment, or business dealings with non-believers because Abram refused the king of Sodom's goods.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Abram's refusal was oath-based and specific:** "I have lift up mine hand unto the LORD" — he had pre-sworn a specific oath; his refusal was not a general principle for all interactions with pagans but a specific covenant-protection decision for this specific context
- **He made an allowance for his allies:** v.24 — Aner, Eshcol, and Mamre (pagan Amorites) were explicitly allowed to take their portion; Abram did not impose his covenant convictions on non-covenant men working alongside him
- **The context was identity-protection:** The specific reason was "lest thou shouldest say, I have made Abram rich" — this is not a general wealth-from-pagans prohibition but a specific reputation-protection for God's glory in Abram's case
- **Paul explicitly allows business dealings with unbelievers:** 1 Corinthians 5:9-10 — "I wrote unto you in an epistle not to company with fornicators: Yet not altogether with the fornicators of this world... for then must ye needs go out of the world" — total separation from the economic and social world is not the New Covenant standard
- **The New Covenant principle:** The concern is not the source of the material but whether receiving it creates ungodly indebtedness, compromises gospel testimony, or gives the world ground to claim credit for the believer's provision
- *Correction from Scripture:* Abram's thread-and-shoelatchet refusal was a specific, oath-bound decision to protect God's reputation as his sole provider in the context of a Sodom-tainted victory. The principle it establishes is: do not accept resources that would give worldly sources credit for God's covenant provision of His people. This is applied with wisdom, not as a blanket prohibition of all economic engagement with non-believers.

**Thread:**
- Genesis 14:18-22 — Melchizedek and Abram name El Elyon as possessor of heaven and earth
- Deuteronomy 32:8 — "When the Most High divided to the nations" — El Elyon as sovereign over national distribution
- Psalm 47:2 — "The LORD most high is terrible; he is a great King over all the earth" — El Elyon as cosmic king
- Psalm 91:1 — "He that dwelleth in the secret place of the most High" — El Elyon as refuge
- Daniel 4:17, 25, 32, 34-35 — "The most High ruleth in the kingdom of men" — seven times in the chapter of Nebuchadnezzar's humiliation; the king of the greatest human empire on earth bows to El Elyon
- Luke 1:32, 35 — "Son of the Highest"/"power of the Highest" — Christ named as the Son and Power of El Elyon in the Annunciation
- Acts 16:17 — "Servants of the most high God" — even demons call Paul and Silas by the El Elyon identification

*Interpretation:* El Elyon is the title Scripture uses when it needs to make the absolute supremacy claim: above all kings, all nations, all powers, all competing deities. First introduced by Melchizedek in the context of a military victory over four kings — the occasion was precisely right: after Abram had defeated kings, the priest named the One above all kings. El Elyon is the theological answer to every human claim of ultimate authority.

### Two Kingdoms in the Valley — The Recurring Choice

**Genesis 14:17-21** — King of Sodom and Melchizedek both meet Abram

**Thread:**
- Genesis 14 — After Abram's victory: king of Sodom (goods and persons) vs. Melchizedek (bread, wine, blessing, tithes)
- Matthew 4:1-11 — After Christ's baptism (the Spirit's anointing): Satan offers kingdoms and glory vs. angels minister after refusal
- Revelation 13-14 — The beast's mark (buy and sell in the world's system) vs. the Lamb's seal (worship God alone); the eschatological form of the valley of Shaveh choice

*Interpretation:* Every believer who wins a spiritual victory is met in the valley of Shaveh by two kingdom representatives: the covenant priest offering worship and covenant sustenance, and the world's king offering goods with strings attached. The thread-and-shoelatchet decision must be made in every generation.

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First War Recorded in Detailed History

**Genesis 14:1-9** — "These made war"
- First detailed account of organized international warfare in Scripture
- Coalition of four vs. coalition of five; tribute, rebellion, punitive war
- First mention establishes: human kingdoms in conflict is the natural state of fallen political life; **Matthew 24:7** — "nation shall rise against nation, kingdom against kingdom" — echoes from the first war to the last; Genesis 14 is the prototype of all subsequent international conflict

### First Use of "Hebrew" (Ivri) Applied to a Person

**Genesis 14:13** — "Abram the Hebrew"
- First time the covenant-ethnic designation is applied to an individual in Scripture
- Spoken by outsiders, identifying Abram by his covenant distinctiveness
- First mention establishes: "Hebrew" = the crossing-over people; the covenant identity visible to the watching world; identified most sharply when the believer acts in crisis with covenant courage

### First Use of "Priest" (Kohen) in Scripture

**Genesis 14:18** — "He was the priest (kohen) of the most high God"
- First use of kohen (priest) in Scripture
- The first priest serves El Elyon (not a tribal deity), holds no genealogical credential, and is simultaneously a king
- First mention establishes the priesthood's archetype: not Aaronic but Melchizedek; not genealogical but divine appointment; not temporary but abiding; the first priest points to the last and greatest Priest (Christ) more directly than any Levitical priest does

### First Use of "El Elyon" (Most High God)

**Genesis 14:18, 19, 20, 22** — introduced four times in three verses by both Melchizedek and Abram
- First use of the most absolute divine supremacy title in Scripture
- Immediately adopted by Abram when invoking God in his oath to the king of Sodom
- First mention establishes: God's supremacy over all human kingdoms is named precisely in the context where a human king is trying to claim credit for Abram's wealth; El Elyon is the theological counter to every human claim of ultimate authority

### First Pre-Law Tithe

**Genesis 14:20** — "He gave him tithes of all"
- First act of tithing in Scripture
- Performed by Abram to Melchizedek, 430 years before the Mosaic Law
- First mention establishes: returning a portion to God through His appointed priest is a covenant instinct that precedes legal codification; the Levitical system institutionalized what was already covenant practice; Hebrews 7's argument depends entirely on this precedence

### First "I Have Lift Up Mine Hand" Oath in Scripture

**Genesis 14:22** — "I have lift up mine hand unto the LORD"
- First recorded formal oath-gesture in Scripture
- The raised hand toward heaven signified invoking God as witness and judge of the oath
- First mention establishes: God as the ultimate witness and binding authority of human commitments; **Matthew 5:33-37** — Christ's teaching on oaths builds on this foundation; Hebrews 6:16 — "men verily swear by the greater... an oath for confirmation is to them an end of all strife"

### First Instance of God as "Exceeding Great Reward"

**Genesis 15:1** — immediate response to Genesis 14's refusal — "I am thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward"
- Not in Genesis 14 itself but the immediate divine response to it
- After Abram refused the king of Sodom's wealth, God appeared with the self-declaration: "I AM thy exceeding great reward"
- The connection: what the world offered (goods, riches) and Abram refused, God replaced with Himself; this is the first time God declares Himself to be the reward of the believer's faith-sacrifice

## NAME MEANINGS

| Name | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Melchizedek (Malki-Tzedeq) | My King Is Righteousness / King of Righteousness | The king-priest whose name declares the character of his reign; righteousness before peace (Hebrews 7:2) |
| Salem (Shalem) | Peace, Wholeness | Abbreviated Jerusalem; the king of peace reigns in the city of peace; the eternal city of the new creation |
| El Elyon | God Most High | The first and supreme name for God as possessor of all; answers all human claims to ultimate ownership |
| Chedorlaomer | Servant of Lagamar (an Elamite deity) | His name reveals his allegiance — to a regional god; overmatched by El Elyon who delivers him into Abram's hand |
| Amraphel | Said to mean "Sayer of darkness" or possibly related to "Hammurabi" | King of Shinar (Babel territory); the Babel-system king in the coalition |
| Mamre | Fatness, Robustness, Vigor | Abram's Amorite ally; his name reflects the abundance of his territory; the terebinths of Mamre become the location of God's greatest visits to Abraham |
| Eshcol | Cluster (of grapes) | The valley of Eshcol would later be where the twelve spies found the great cluster of grapes (Numbers 13:23-24) |
| Aner | Boy, Youth / Waterfall | Abram's third Amorite ally |
