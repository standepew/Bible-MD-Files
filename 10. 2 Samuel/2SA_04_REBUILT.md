## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Saul's son heard Abner dead in Hebron, hands feeble, Israelites troubled. Saul's son had two men captains of bands: Baanah and Rechab, sons of Rimmon Beerothite, children of Benjamin; Beeroth reckoned to Benjamin. Beerothites fled to Gittaim, sojourners until this day. Jonathan Saul's son had son lame of feet. Five years old when tidings came of Saul and Jonathan from Jezreel, nurse took him up, fled; as she made haste to flee, he fell, became lame. Name Mephibosheth. Sons of Rimmon Beerothite, Rechab and Baanah, went, came about heat of day to house of Ish-bosheth, lay on bed at noon. Came into midst of house, as though to fetch wheat; smote under fifth rib; Rechab and Baanah brother escaped. When came into house, lay on bed in bedchamber, smote, slew, beheaded, took head, got away through plain all night. Brought head of Ish-bosheth to David in Hebron, told king, behold head of Ish-bosheth son of Saul enemy, sought life; LORD avenged lord king this day of Saul and seed. David answered Rechab and Baanah brother, sons of Rimmon Beerothite, as LORD lives, redeemed soul from all adversity; when told Saul dead, thinking good tidings, held, slew in Ziklag, who thought would give reward for tidings; how much more when wicked men slain righteous person in own house upon bed? require blood of hand, take away from earth. David commanded young men, slew them, cut off hands and feet, hanged over pool in Hebron. Took head of Ish-bosheth, buried in sepulchre of Abner in Hebron.

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 And when Saul's son heard that Abner was dead in Hebron, his hands were feeble, and all the Israelites were troubled.  

2 And Saul's son had two men that were captains of bands: the name of the one was Baanah, and the name of the other Rechab, the sons of Rimmon a Beerothite, of the children of Benjamin: (for Beeroth also was reckoned to Benjamin:  

3 And the Beerothites fled to Gittaim, and were sojourners there until this day.)  

4 And Jonathan, Saul's son, human a son that was lame of his feet. He was five years old when the tidings came of Saul and Jonathan out of Jezreel, and his nurse took him up, and fled: and it came to pass, as she made haste to flee, that he fell, and became lame. And his name was Mephibosheth.  

5 And the sons of Rimmon the Beerothite, Rechab and Baanah, went, and came about the heat of the day to the house of Ish-bosheth, who lay on a bed at noon.  

6 And they came thither into the midst of the house, as though they would have fetched wheat; and they smote him under the fifth rib: and Rechab and Baanah his brother escaped.  

7 For when they came into the house, he lay on his bed in his bedchamber, and they smote him, and slew him, and beheaded him, and took his head, and gat them away through the plain all night.  

8 And they brought the head of Ish-bosheth unto David to Hebron, and said to the king, Behold the head of Ish-bosheth the name of Saul thine enemy, which sought thy life; and the LORD hath avenged my lord the king this day of Saul, and of his seed.  

9 And David answered Rechab and Baanah his brother, the sons of Rimmon the Beerothite, and said unto them, As the LORD liveth, who hath redeemed my soul out of all adversity,  
 
10 When one told me, saying, Behold, Saul is dead, thinking to have brought good tidings, I had taken hold of him, and slew him in Ziklag, who thought that I would have given him a reward for his tidings:  

11 How much more, when wicked men have slain a righteous person in his own house upon his bed? shall I not therefore now require his blood of your hand, and take you away from the earth?  

12 And David commanded his young men, and they slew them, and cut off their hands and their feet, and hanged them up over the pool in Hebron. But they took the head of Ish-bosheth, and buried it in the sepulchre of Abner in Hebron.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "His hands were feeble, and all the Israelites were troubled"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about physical weakness
**Biblical context:** This is about leadership vacuum - Ish-bosheth's weakness represents the collapse of Saul's dynasty, showing how leadership failure affects entire nation.

### "How much more, when wicked men have slain a righteous person in his own house upon his bed?"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about personal revenge
**Biblical context:** This is about divine justice - David applies consistent principle that murder of God's anointed requires justice, showing his respect for divine authority.

**Chapter 4 demonstrates the collapse of Saul's dynasty and David's consistent justice:** Ish-bosheth's weakness shows the failure of human leadership without divine support. The Israelites' trouble shows how leadership failure affects entire nation. The captains' treachery shows how opportunists exploit weakness. Mephibosheth's story shows tragic consequences of political instability. The nurse's haste shows human fear leading to accidents. The fall and lameness show how crisis creates lasting damage. The murderers' plot shows calculated evil for personal gain. Their deception shows willingness to use false pretenses. The timing at noon shows vulnerability during rest. The method of killing shows brutal efficiency. The beheading shows contempt for the victim. The escape through the night shows cowardice. The presentation to David shows expectation of reward. Their claim of divine vengeance shows spiritual deception. David's response shows consistent principle of justice. His reference to previous case shows established pattern. His question about righteousness shows moral clarity. His command for execution shows swift justice. The mutilation shows public deterrent. The burial of Ish-bosheth's head shows respect for the dead despite circumstances. This chapter establishes crucial themes: leadership without divine support fails, political instability creates victims, justice requires consistency, and respect for divine authority transcends circumstances. It demonstrates that Saul's dynasty collapsed completely through weakness and treachery, that David maintained consistent principles despite opportunity, and that true leadership requires justice even when it brings no personal advantage. The chapter shows David as model leader who administers consistent justice, maintains respect for divine authority, and provides proper burial even for opponents. It illustrates the principle that human leadership without divine support inevitably fails, that political instability creates innocent victims, and that true leadership maintains consistent moral principles. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfect leader whose kingdom never fails, whose justice is perfect, and whose compassion extends to all. The consistent justice points forward to Christ's perfect righteousness. The chapter demonstrates that when human leadership fails completely, divine leadership emerges through consistent justice and moral principle, showing the foundation for righteous reign that transcends human weakness and treachery.
