## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### JEHU
- **Meaning:** "The LORD is He"
- **Context:** Prophet who pronounced judgment on Baasha
- **Significance:** Divine messenger of judgment
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual prophecy (Amos 3:7)

### ZIMRI
- **Meaning:** "Musical" or "Praiseworthy"
- **Context:** Assassin who reigned seven days
- **Significance:** Brief and violent reign
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual violence (James 4:1)

### OMRI
- **Meaning:** "Sheaf of grain"
- **Context:** Founder of Samaria dynasty
- **Significance:** Political founder
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual foundation (1 Corinthians 3:10)

### AHAB
- **Meaning:** "Father's brother"
- **Context:** Most wicked king of Israel
- **Significance:** Peak of Israelite idolatry
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual wickedness (Revelation 2:20)

1 Then the word of the LORD came to Jehu the son of Hanani against Baasha, saying,  

2 Forasmuch as I exalted thee out of the dust, and made thee prince over my people Israel; and thou hast walked in the way of Jeroboam, and hast made my people Israel to sin, to provoke me to anger with their sins;  

3 Behold, I will take away the posterity of Baasha, and the posterity of his house; and will make thy house like the house of Jeroboam the son of Nebat.

5 Now the rest of the acts of Baasha, and what he did, and his might, are they not written in the book of the chronicles of the kings of Israel?  

6 So Baasha slept with his fathers, and was buried in Tirzah: and Elah his son reigned in his stead.  

7 And also by the hand of the prophet Jehu the son of Hanani came the word of the LORD against Baasha, and against his house, even for all the evil that he did in the sight of the LORD, in provoking him to anger with the work of his hands, in being like the house of Jeroboam; and because he killed him.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Forasmuch as I exalted thee out of the dust, and made thee prince over my people Israel"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about personal merit
**Biblical context:** This is about divine grace - Baasha was elevated from nothing by God's grace, but he abused this elevation by leading Israel into sin.

### "He took to wife Jezebel the daughter of Ethbaal king of the Zidonians"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about political marriage
**Biblical context:** This is about spiritual compromise - Ahab's marriage to Jezebel brought Baal worship to Israel, showing how foreign alliances corrupt true worship.

**Chapter 16 demonstrates rapid succession and escalating idolatry in northern Israel:** Word LORD came to Jehu prophet against Baasha. God elevated Baasha from dust to prince, but Baasha walked in Jeroboam's sins, made Israel sin. Prophesied complete destruction of Baasha's house like Jeroboam's. Baasha died, Elah succeeded. Elah reigned two years, assassinated by Zimri while drunk. Zimri reigned seven days, slew all Baasha's house, fulfilling prophecy. People divided between Omri and Tibni; Omri prevailed. Omri reigned twelve years, bought Samaria hill, built capital. Omri did evil worse than predecessors, followed Jeroboam's sins. Omri died, Ahab succeeded. Ahab reigned twenty-two years, did evil above all before him. Married Jezebel, served Baal, built Baal temple, made grove. Provoked LORD more than all kings. Hiel rebuilt Jericho, losing sons in fulfillment of Joshua's curse. This chapter establishes crucial themes: sin brings judgment, prophecy is fulfilled, idolatry escalates, and disobedience has generational consequences. It demonstrates that northern Israel descended deeper into idolatry through successive kings, that divine prophecies were fulfilled precisely, and that foreign alliances brought spiritual corruption. The chapter shows Baasha as ungrateful king who forgot divine elevation. It shows Zimri as violent usurper who fulfilled prophecy. It shows Omri as political founder who established idolatrous capital. It shows Ahab as ultimate idolater who brought Baal worship to Israel. It illustrates the principle that sin escalates when unrepented, that divine prophecy is certain, and that foreign alliances corrupt spiritual purity. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfectly righteous king whose kingdom is pure, whose worship is true, whose foundations are eternal, and whose reign is blessed. The perfect righteousness points forward to Christ's perfect holiness. The prophecy fulfillment points forward to Christ's perfect fulfillment of all prophecy. The chapter demonstrates that when leaders persist in idolatry and disobedience, they bring escalating judgment and corruption, showing how sin compounds through generations and how divine judgment is certain for persistent disobedience.
