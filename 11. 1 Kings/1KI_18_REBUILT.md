## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### CARMEL
- **Meaning:** "Garden" or "Vineyard"
- **Context:** Mountain where contest occurred
- **Significance:** Place of divine victory
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual mountain (Matthew 17:1)

### KISHON
- **Meaning:** "Winding" or "Crooked"
- **Context:** Brook where prophets were slain
- **Significance:** Place of judgment
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual judgment (Revelation 20:12)

### OBADIAH
- **Meaning:** "Servant of the LORD"
- **Context:** Faithful official in Ahab's court
- **Significance:** Godly remnant
* **New Testament parallel:** Faithful servant (Matthew 25:21)

### BAAL
- **Meaning:** "Lord" or "Master"
- **Context:** Canaanite deity worshipped in Israel
- **Significance:** False god
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual idolatry (Colossians 3:5)

1 And it came to pass after many days, that the word of the LORD came to Elijah in the third year, saying, Go, shew thyself unto Ahab; and I will send rain upon the earth.  

2 And Elijah went to shew himself unto Ahab. And there was a sore famine in Samaria.  

3 And Ahab called Obadiah, which was the governor of his house. (Now Obadiah feared the LORD greatly:  

4 For it was so, when Jezebel cut off the prophets of the LORD, that Obadiah took an hundred prophets, and hid them by fifty in a cave, and fed them with bread and water.)  

5 And Ahab said unto Obadiah, Go into the land, unto all fountains of water, and unto all brooks: peradventure we may find grass to save the horses and mules alive, that we lose not all the beasts.  

6 So they divided the at the land between them to pass throughout it: Ahab went one way by himself, and Obadiah went another way by himself.  

7 And as Obadiah was in the way, behold, Elijah met him: and he knew him, and fell on his face, and said, Art thou that my lord Elijah?  

8 And he answered him, Ahab, go, tell thy lord, Behold, Elijah is here.  

9 And he said, What have I sinned, that thou wouldest deliver thy servant into the hand of Ahab, to slay me?  

10 As the LORD thy God liveth, there is no nation or kingdom, whither my lord hath not sent to seek thee: and when they said, He is not there; he took an oath of the kingdom and nation, that they found thee not.  

11 And now thou sayest, Go, tell thy lord, Behold, Elijah is here.  

12 And it shall come to pass, as soon as I am gone from thee, that the Spirit of the LORD shall carry thee whither I know not; and so when I come and tell Ahab, and he cannot find thee, they shall slay me: but I thy servant fear the LORD from my youth.  

13 Was it not told my lord what I did when Jezebel slew the prophets of the LORD, how I hid an hundred men of the LORD's prophets by fifty in a cave, and fed them with bread and water?

**Chapter 18 demonstrates Elijah's victory over Baal through divine power and prayer:** Word LORD came Elijah third year, show Ahab, send rain. Elijah met Ahab during famine. Obadiah, faithful governor, had hidden prophets from Jezebel. Elijah confronted Ahab, accused him of troubling Israel through Baal worship. Challenged Israel at Mount Carmel: choose between LORD and Baal. Proposed contest: two bulls, no fire, call on gods, answer by fire. Baal prophets chose bull, called Baal from morning to noon, no response. Elijah mocked Baal's impotence. Prophets cut themselves, no answer. Elijah repaired LORD's broken altar, used twelve stones for all tribes. Built trench, placed wood and bull, poured water three times until trench filled. Prayed for God's glory and people's recognition. Fire from LORD consumed sacrifice, wood, stones, dust, licked water. People fell faces, confessed LORD as God. Elijah executed Baal prophets at Kishon. Told Ahab rain coming, ate and drank. Elijah prayed on Carmel top, sent servant seven times to look for rain. Small cloud appeared like hand's shape; great rain came. Elijah ran before Ahab to Jezreel by divine power. This chapter establishes crucial themes: God's power defeats false gods, prayer brings divine intervention, true worship requires exclusive devotion, and divine judgment follows false worship. It demonstrates that Elijah's bold confrontation led to divine victory, that prayer brought rain after drought, and that God's power was unmistakably demonstrated. The chapter shows Elijah as bold prophet who challenged idolatry, who trusted God's power, who prayed persistently, and who demonstrated God's supremacy. It shows Israel as vacillating people who needed decisive demonstration of God's power. It shows Baal prophets as defeated opponents whose power was proven false. It illustrates the principle that God's power defeats all false gods, that prayer brings divine intervention, and that exclusive devotion to God brings blessing. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfectly victorious prophet whose power is absolute, whose prayer is perfect, whose victory is complete, and whose reign is eternal. The divine victory points forward to Christ's perfect triumph. The prayer power points forward to Christ's perfect intercession. The chapter demonstrates that when God's servants confront false gods boldly, trust His power completely, pray persistently, and demonstrate His supremacy, they bring divine victory that turns people back to true worship and brings God's blessing after spiritual drought.
