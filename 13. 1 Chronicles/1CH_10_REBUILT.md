## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### PHILISTINES
- **Meaning:** "Immigrants"
- **Context:** Israel's enemies who defeated Saul
- **Significance:** Divine judgment on disobedient king
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual opposition (1 Peter 5:8)

### GILBOA
- **Meaning:** "Fountain of weeping"
- **Context:** Mountain where Saul died
- **Significance:** Place of divine judgment
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual weeping (Matthew 5:4)

### JABESH-GILEAD
- **Meaning:** "Dry place of Gilead"
- **Context:** City that rescued Saul's body
- **Significance:** Model of loyalty and honor
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual loyalty (Revelation 2:10)

### DAGON
- **Meaning:** "Fish god"
- **Context:** Philistine deity
- **Significance:** False religion
* **Spiritual application:** False worship (1 Corinthians 10:20)

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 Now the Philistines fought against Israel; and the men of Israel fled from before the Philistines, and fell down slain in mount Gilboa.  

2 And the Philistines followed hard after Saul, and after his sons; and the Philistines slew Jonathan, and Abinadab, and Malchi-shua, the sons of Saul.  

3 And the battle went sore against Saul, and the archers hit him, and he was wounded of the archers.  

4 Then said Saul to his armourbearer, Draw thy sword, and thrust me through therewith; lest these uncircumcised come and abuse me. But his armourbearer would not; for he was sore afraid. So Saul took a sword, and fell upon it.  

5 And when his armourbearer saw that Saul was dead, he fell likewise on the sword, and died.  

6 So Saul died, and his three sons, and all his house died together.  

7 And when all the men of Israel that were in the valley saw that they fled, and that Saul and his sons were dead, then they forsook their cities, and fled: and the Philistines came and dwelt in them.  

8 And it came to pass on the morrow, when the Philistines came to strip the slain, that they found Saul and his sons fallen in mount Gilboa.  

9 And when they had stripped him, they took his head, and his armour, and sent into the land of the Philistines round about, to carry tidings unto their idols, and to the people.  

10 And they put his armour in the house of their gods, and fastened his head in the temple of Dagon.  

11 And when all Jabesh-gilead heard all that the Philistines had done to Saul,  

12 They arose, all the valiant men, and took away the body of Saul, and the bodies of his sons, and brought them to Jabesh, and buried their bones under the oak in Jabesh, and fasted seven days.  

13 So Saul died for his transgression which he committed against the LORD, even against the word of the LORD, which he kept not, and also for asking counsel of one that had a familiar spirit, to enquire of it;  

14 And enquired not of the LORD: therefore he slew him, and turned the kingdom unto David the son of Jesse.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Now the Philistines fought against Israel; and the men of Israel fled from before the Philistines"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about military defeat
**Biblical context:** This is about divine judgment - Israel's defeat came through Saul's disobedience, showing how sin brings divine judgment.

### "Then said Saul to his armourbearer, Draw thy sword, and thrust me through therewith"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about suicide
**Biblical context:** This is about divine judgment - Saul's suicide was the result of divine judgment for his disobedience, showing how sin leads to tragic ends.

This chapter establishes crucial themes: disobedience brings divine judgment, human kingship fails, loyalty honors even defeated leaders, and divine appointment establishes true authority. It demonstrates that Saul's disobedience brought defeat and death, that his human kingship ended in tragedy, that Jabesh-gilead honored him even in defeat, and that divine appointment transferred the kingdom to David. The chapter shows Saul as disobedient king whose reign ended in judgment. It shows Jonathan as loyal son whose death was tragic. It shows Jabesh-gilead as faithful city that honored defeated king. It shows David as divinely appointed king whose reign would be blessed. It illustrates the principle that disobedience brings divine judgment, that human kingship fails without divine blessing, that loyalty honors even defeated leaders, and that divine appointment establishes true authority. The chapter points forward to Christ as the perfectly obedient King whose reign is eternal, whose authority is divine, whose loyalty is perfect, and whose kingdom is unshakable. The divine judgment points forward to Christ's perfect justice. The kingdom transition points forward to Christ's perfect authority. The chapter demonstrates that when people disobey God, they experience divine judgment, when they remain loyal, they honor others, and when God appoints leaders, He establishes true authority, showing how divine sovereignty works through human events to accomplish His purposes of justice and grace.
