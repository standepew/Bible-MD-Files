## CHAPTER SUMMARY

The civil code continues. Theft laws: fivefold restitution for a stolen and slaughtered ox; fourfold for a sheep. A thief killed breaking in at night — no blood guilt; in daylight — blood guilt. Restitution is the core principle: if stolen goods are found alive, pay double; if you have nothing, be sold for your theft. Various property responsibility cases follow: straying animals, fire spreading to a neighbor's field, deposits that are stolen or damaged. Moral law: seducing an unbetrothed virgin — marry her or pay the dowry. Capital offenses: witchcraft, bestiality, sacrificing to other gods. Social covenant: do not oppress the stranger, the widow, or the fatherless — "I will hear their cry; my wrath shall wax hot." Prohibition on usury for the poor. Garment taken as pledge must be returned by nightfall. Do not revile God or curse the ruler of your people. Firstfruits and firstborn belong to God. Holiness of diet: do not eat what is torn.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Restitution — The Core Principle of Biblical Justice

**Exodus 22:1-4** — Fivefold for ox (most productive animal), fourfold for sheep, double for found goods

- **Luke 19:8** — Zacchaeus: "if I have taken any thing from any man by false accusation, I restore him fourfold" — Zacchaeus applies Exodus 22's restitution standard voluntarily; Jesus says: "This day is salvation come to this house"
- **Proverbs 6:30-31** — "Men do not despise a thief, if he steal to satisfy his soul when he is hungry; But if he be found, he shall restore sevenfold; he shall give all the substance of his house"
- **Numbers 5:7** — "He shall recompense his trespass with the principal thereof, and add unto it the fifth part thereof"
- *Interpretation:* Biblical justice is fundamentally about restitution, not merely punishment. The thief pays back more than was taken — the ox represents the productive economic capacity (hence fivefold, not just double). The goal is to make the victim whole plus, not merely to punish the offender. This is the alternative to both excessive punishment (the ancient Near Eastern norm) and mere incarceration (which does nothing for the victim). Zacchaeus's voluntary restitution (Luke 19:8) is presented as evidence of genuine salvation — the change in the thief's heart is measured by what he pays back.

### "Do Not Oppress the Stranger" — Israel's Empathy Law

**Exodus 22:21** — "Thou shalt neither vex a stranger, nor oppress him: for ye were strangers in the land of Egypt"

- **Exodus 23:9** — "Also thou shalt not oppress a stranger: for ye know the heart of a stranger, seeing ye were strangers in the land of Egypt"
- **Leviticus 19:33-34** — "the stranger that dwelleth with you shall be unto you as one born among you, and thou shalt love him as thyself; for ye were strangers in the land of Egypt"
- **Deuteronomy 10:18-19** — "He doth execute the judgment of the fatherless and widow, and loveth the stranger... Love ye therefore the stranger: for ye were strangers in the land of Egypt"
- **Matthew 25:35** — "I was a stranger, and ye took me in"
- *Interpretation:* The foundation of Israel's stranger-protection law is not humanitarian sentiment — it is covenantal memory. Israel was the stranger; they know what oppression of the stranger feels like from the inside. The Exodus creates an empathy mandate: because you were oppressed, you may not oppress. This is the strongest possible moral argument — grounded not in abstract principle but in lived experience within covenant. The New Testament equivalent: "Forgive as the Lord has forgiven you" (Colossians 3:13) — the same structure.

### God Hears the Widow and Fatherless — The Divine Advocate

**Exodus 22:22-24** — "Ye shall not afflict any widow, or fatherless child. If thou afflict them in any wise, and they cry at all unto me, I will surely hear their cry; And my wrath shall wax hot, and I will kill you with the sword"

- **Psalm 68:5** — "A father of the fatherless, and a judge of the widows, is God in his holy habitation"
- **Psalm 146:9** — "The LORD preserveth the strangers; he relieveth the fatherless and widow: but the way of the wicked he turneth upside down"
- **Isaiah 1:17, 23** — "Relieve the oppressed, judge the fatherless, plead for the widow... they judge not the fatherless, neither doth the cause of the widow come unto them"
- **James 1:27** — "Pure religion and undefiled before God and the Father is this, To visit the fatherless and widows in their affliction, and to keep himself unspotted from the world"
- *Interpretation:* The widow and fatherless have no male advocate in the ancient covenant community's legal structure — so God Himself steps in as their advocate. The cry of the oppressed widow goes directly to God; the consequence for their oppressor is that their own wives become widows and their own children become fatherless. This is the precision of divine justice — the punishment mirrors the crime exactly. James 1:27 calls care for the fatherless and widow the definition of pure religion — it is not peripheral social concern, it is the heart of covenant faithfulness.

### Usury Prohibited for the Poor

**Exodus 22:25** — "Thou shalt not be to him as an usurer, neither shalt thou lay upon him usury"

- **Leviticus 25:35-37** — Expanded: no interest at all for a poor Israelite brother
- **Deuteronomy 23:19-20** — No interest from a brother Israelite; interest permitted from a foreigner
- **Nehemiah 5:1-13** — Nehemiah rebukes the nobles for charging interest to their own brethren; quotes this law; they restore the fields and interest charged
- **Proverbs 28:8** — "He that by usury and unjust gain increaseth his substance, he shall gather it for him that will pity the poor"
- **Ezekiel 18:8, 13, 17** — Charging usury is listed among the abominations of the wicked man; not charging usury is listed among the marks of the righteous man
- **Luke 6:34-35** — "lend, hoping for nothing again; and your reward shall be great" — Jesus extends the principle: lend without expectation of return
- *Interpretation:* The prohibition on usury (neshek = interest, literally "a bite") for the poor is grounded in the nature of money in covenant community. When a poor brother needs to borrow, he needs help, not a transaction. Charging interest on poverty is predatory — "biting" the vulnerable. The law does not prohibit all lending with interest (Deuteronomy 23:20 permits it from foreigners in commercial contexts) but absolutely forbids it between covenant brothers in need. The theological basis: "your money shall not be working against your brother."

### "Thou Shalt Not Revile the Gods, Nor Curse the Ruler" — Authority and Speech

**Exodus 22:28** — "Thou shalt not revile the gods (Elohim), nor curse the ruler of thy people"

- Hebrew: Elohim here may mean "God" (the divine judge) or "judges/magistrates" (a secondary meaning — Psalm 82:6, John 10:34)
- **Acts 23:5** — Paul, when told he had spoken against the high priest: "I wist not, brethren, that he was the high priest: for it is written, Thou shalt not speak evil of the ruler of thy people" — Paul quotes Exodus 22:28 directly
- **Romans 13:1-2** — "Let every soul be subject unto the higher powers. For there is no power but of God: the powers that be are ordained of God"
- **1 Peter 2:17** — "Honour all men. Love the brotherhood. Fear God. Honour the king"
- *Interpretation:* Paul's citation of Exodus 22:28 in Acts 23:5 shows this law was still operative in New Testament practice. Cursing governing authority is not merely politically imprudent — it is a covenant violation. This does not mean governments are beyond rebuke (Elijah rebuked Ahab; John the Baptist rebuked Herod) — but there is a distinction between prophetic rebuke and cursing/reviling. The speech prohibition reflects the God-ordained nature of authority itself.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### USURY (Hebrew: neshek)
- **Exodus 22:25** — neshek = interest, literally "a bite" — the image of biting/gnawing at the borrower through compounding interest
- *Definition here:* Any charge of interest levied against a poor brother in need; the prohibition is specifically against exploiting vulnerability through financial predation

### WITCH (Hebrew: mekashshephah)
- **Exodus 22:18** — "Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live" — mekashshephah = one who practices sorcery, uses enchantments/drugs/potions for occult purposes
- **Deuteronomy 18:10-12** — Expands the list: divination, observer of times, enchanter, witch, charmer, consulter with familiar spirits, wizard, necromancer — all are abomination
- **Galatians 5:20** — witchcraft (pharmakeia) listed among works of the flesh
- *Definition here:* One who operates in supernatural power derived from sources other than the LORD; the capital penalty reflects the severity of introducing alternative spiritual power into the covenant community*

## THE ACTUAL VERSES — EXODUS 22 (KJV)

**1** If a man shall steal an ox, or a sheep, and kill it, or sell it; he shall restore five oxen for an ox, and four sheep for a sheep.
**2** If a thief be found breaking up, and be smitten that he die, there shall no blood be shed for him.
**3** If the sun be risen upon him, there shall be blood shed for him; for he should make full restitution; if he have nothing, then he shall be sold for his theft.
**4** If the theft be certainly found in his hand alive, whether it be ox, or ass, or sheep; he shall restore double.
**5** If a man shall cause a field or vineyard to be eaten, and shall put in his beast, and shall feed in another man's field; of the best of his own field, and of the best of his own vineyard, shall he make restitution.
**6** If fire break out, and catch in thorns, so that the stacks of corn, or the standing corn, or the field, be consumed therewith; he that kindled the fire shall surely make restitution.
**7** If a man shall deliver unto his neighbour money or stuff to keep, and it be stolen out of the man's house; if the thief be found, let him pay double.
**8** If the thief be not found, then the master of the house shall be brought unto the judges, to see whether he have put his hand unto his neighbour's goods.
**9** For all manner of trespass, whether it be for ox, for ass, for sheep, for raiment, or for any manner of lost thing, which another challengeth to be his, the cause of both parties shall come before the judges; and whom the judges shall condemn, he shall pay double unto his neighbour.
**10** If a man deliver unto his neighbour an ass, or an ox, or a sheep, or any beast, to keep; and it die, or be hurt, or driven away, no man seeing it:
**11** Then shall an oath of the LORD be between them both, that he hath not put his hand unto his neighbour's goods; and the owner of it shall accept thereof, and he shall not make it good.
**12** And if it be stolen from him, he shall make restitution unto the owner thereof.
**13** If it be torn in pieces, then let him bring it for witness, and he shall not make good that which was torn.
**14** And if a man borrow ought of his neighbour, and it be hurt, or die, the owner thereof being not with it, he shall surely make it good.
**15** But if the owner thereof be with it, he shall not make it good: if it be an hired thing, it came for his hire.
**16** And if a man entice a maid that is not betrothed, and lie with her, he shall surely endow her to be his wife.
**17** If her father utterly refuse to give her unto him, he shall pay money according to the dowry of virgins.
**18** Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live.
**19** Whosoever lieth with a beast shall surely be put to death.
**20** He that sacrificeth unto any god, save unto the LORD only, he shall be utterly destroyed.
**21** Thou shalt neither vex a stranger, nor oppress him: for ye were strangers in the land of Egypt.
**22** Ye shall not afflict any widow, or fatherless child.
**23** If thou afflict them in any wise, and they cry at all unto me, I will surely hear their cry;
**24** And my wrath shall wax hot, and I will kill you with the sword; and your wives shall be widows, and your children fatherless.
**25** If thou lend money to any of my people that is poor by thee, thou shalt not be to him as an usurer, neither shalt thou lay upon him usury.
**26** If thou at all take thy neighbour's raiment to pledge, thou shalt deliver it unto him by that the sun goeth down:
**27** For that is his covering only, it is his raiment for his skin: wherein shall he sleep? and it shall come to pass, when he crieth unto me, that I will hear; for I am gracious.
**28** Thou shalt not revile the gods, nor curse the ruler of thy people.
**29** Thou shalt not delay to offer the first of thy ripe fruits, and of thy liquors: the firstborn of thy sons shalt thou give unto me.
**30** Likewise shalt thou do with thine oxen, and with thy sheep: seven days it shall be with his dam; on the eighth day thou shalt give it me.
**31** And ye shall be holy men unto me: neither shall ye eat any flesh that is torn of beasts in the field; ye shall cast it to the dogs.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Garment as Pledge — The Most Intimate Security

- **Exodus 22:26-27** — The garment taken as pledge must be returned by sundown; it is his only covering; when he cries, God will hear
- **Deuteronomy 24:12-13** — The same law repeated; "it shall be righteousness unto thee before the LORD thy God"
- **Amos 2:8** — Israel is condemned for lying "down upon clothes laid to pledge by every altar" — violating this exact law
- **Job 22:6** — Eliphaz accuses Job of "taking a pledge from thy brother for nought"
- *Thread:* The garment is the most basic human dignity. To hold a man's garment overnight is to deprive him of warmth and covering — to strip him of his minimal dignity. This law is one of the most personal in the entire Torah: God specifically says "when he crieth unto me, that I will hear; for I am gracious." The garment-pledge law is where the covenant gets most intimate — God watches over the poor man's one coat.
