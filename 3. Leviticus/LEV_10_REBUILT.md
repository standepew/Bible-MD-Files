## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Immediately after the glory falls and the people shout and prostrate themselves, Nadab and Abihu — Aaron's two eldest sons, just consecrated — take their censers, put fire in them, add incense, and offer before the LORD "strange fire, which he commanded them not." The LORD's fire goes out from Him and devours them. They die before the LORD. Moses quotes God: "I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the people I will be glorified." Aaron holds his peace. Moses orders their cousins Mishael and Elzaphan to carry the bodies out of the sanctuary in their tunics. Aaron and his surviving sons Eleazar and Ithamar are forbidden to mourn, uncover their heads, or rend their clothes, lest they die and wrath come on all the people. Then God speaks directly to Aaron (one of only two times in Leviticus): no wine or strong drink before entering the Tabernacle, ever, throughout your generations — so you can distinguish holy from unholy and teach Israel all God's statutes. Moses then discovers the sin-offering goat has been burned rather than eaten; he confronts Eleazar and Ithamar. Aaron explains. Moses accepts the explanation.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Strange Fire — What God Did Not Command

**Leviticus 10:1** — "strange fire before the LORD, which he commanded them not"

- The precise nature of the offense: not that it was evil fire, but that it was fire "which he commanded them not" — the criterion is not natural evil but divine authorization
- **Deuteronomy 12:32** — "What thing soever I command you, observe to do it: thou shalt not add thereto, nor diminish from it" — the governing principle
- **1 Samuel 15:22-23** — "Behold, to obey is better than sacrifice, and to hearken than the fat of rams. For rebellion is as the sin of witchcraft, and stubbornness is as iniquity and idolatry" — Saul's unauthorized sacrifice judged; same category of offense
- **2 Samuel 6:6-7** — Uzzah touches the Ark to steady it; God strikes him dead — another unauthorized act done with good intent, judged immediately
- **Matthew 7:22-23** — "Many will say to me in that day, Lord, Lord, have we not prophesied in thy name?... And then will I profess unto them, I never knew you" — "which he commanded them not" is the final criterion at the judgment
- *Interpretation:* The death of Nadab and Abihu establishes a principle that runs through all of Scripture: God's holiness is not negotiated by the sincerity of the worshipper or the proximity of the worshipper to the divine fire. What God did not command is not acceptable regardless of enthusiasm, experience, or office. They were just consecrated; they had stood at the Tabernacle door for seven days; they had seen the glory fall. None of this authorized what they did. The criterion for acceptable worship is not passion but obedience.*

### "I Will Be Sanctified in Them That Come Nigh Me"

**Leviticus 10:3** — Moses' word to Aaron: "This is it that the LORD spake, saying, I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the people I will be glorified"

- This is the word Moses quotes — it appears to be a prior statement of God not recorded elsewhere; Moses knew it; Aaron knew it; Nadab and Abihu knew it
- The nearer you come to God, the higher the standard; proximity is privilege and danger
- **Hebrews 12:28-29** — "Wherefore we receiving a kingdom which cannot be moved, let us have grace, whereby we may serve God acceptably with reverence and godly fear: For our God is a consuming fire"
- **1 Peter 4:17** — "For the time is come that judgment must begin at the house of God"
- **Ezekiel 9:6** — "Begin at my sanctuary" — God's judgment begins at the place of closest access
- *Interpretation:* Leviticus 10:3 is the theological explanation God provides for the deaths of Nadab and Abihu: God is sanctified (set apart, shown to be holy, distinguished from all that is common) precisely in those who come nearest to Him. The closer the access, the clearer the distinction between holy and common must be — because any blurring of that distinction at the center distorts the entire understanding of God among the people. 1 Peter 4:17 extends this: New Covenant judgment begins at the house of God. Those who lead, who come nearest to the throne in ministry, bear the strictest accountability.*

### Aaron Held His Peace

**Leviticus 10:3** — "And Aaron held his peace"

- Aaron's two eldest sons have just been killed by divine fire on what should have been the greatest day of his life — the first day of his priestly ministry, the day the glory fell
- He does not speak, does not protest, does not question
- **Psalm 46:10** — "Be still, and know that I am God"
- **Job 1:20-22** — Job "fell down upon the ground, and worshipped... In all this Job sinned not, nor charged God foolishly"
- **Isaiah 53:7** — "He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth" — the Servant's silence under judgment
- **Habakkuk 2:20** — "the LORD is in his holy temple: let all the earth keep silence before him"
- *Interpretation:* Aaron's silence is one of the most profound moments in Leviticus. He has just watched his sons die. He is forbidden to mourn. He holds his peace. This is not numbness — it is a man who understands that what God does is right, even when it costs him everything. The same God who told him not to mourn was the God who took his sons. Aaron's silence is the deepest form of the fear of the LORD — the recognition that God's holiness is not negotiable, even at personal cost.*

### No Wine Before Entering — Clear Mind to Distinguish Holy from Unholy

**Leviticus 10:9-11** — "Do not drink wine nor strong drink, thou, nor thy sons with thee, when ye go into the tabernacle of the congregation, lest ye die... And that ye may put difference between holy and unholy, and between unclean and clean; And that ye may teach the children of Israel all the statutes"

- The wine prohibition is connected to a threefold responsibility: discernment, distinction, teaching
- **1 Peter 5:8** — "Be sober, be vigilant; because your adversary the devil, as a roaring lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour" — sobriety as a requirement for spiritual discernment
- **1 Timothy 3:2-3** — The overseer must be "sober, of good behaviour... not given to wine" — the Levitical principle applied to New Covenant leadership
- **Ephesians 5:18** — "And be not drunk with wine, wherein is excess; but be filled with the Spirit" — the contrast Paul draws; not wine but Spirit
- *Interpretation:* The prohibition on wine before priestly service is not temperance law — it is epistemological: the priest must be able to discern and distinguish and teach. A clouded mind cannot maintain the distinctions that the entire Levitical system depends upon. The New Covenant equivalent is not just sobriety from alcohol — it is the sobriety of being filled with the Spirit rather than dulled by anything that impairs discernment. Ephesians 5:18 is the direct parallel: not wine but Spirit.*

### Aaron's Explanation — Moses Content

**Leviticus 10:19-20** — Aaron explains that in light of what had befallen him (the deaths of his sons), if he had eaten the sin offering, "should it have been accepted in the sight of the LORD?" Moses, when he heard that, was content.

- Aaron's argument: the priest mediating atonement while in a state of profound grief over God's own judgment on his house — would that have been acceptable?
- Moses is satisfied — God's written instructions did not anticipate this exact circumstance; Aaron's reasoning about acceptability before God is sound
- **Hosea 6:6** — "For I desired mercy, and not sacrifice; and the knowledge of God more than burnt offerings" — the heart state of the priest matters, not merely mechanical compliance
- **Matthew 12:3-4** — Jesus cites David eating the showbread when necessary: "Have ye not read what David did, when he was an hungred... How he entered into the house of God, and did eat the shewbread, which was not lawful" — the principle that extreme circumstances may govern application without violating principle
- *Interpretation:* The closing episode of Leviticus 10 is a law-within-the-law moment: the written instruction said eat the sin offering; Aaron did not eat it; Moses was content with his explanation. This is the recognition that the spirit of the law — acceptability before God — governs its application. The priest whose heart is devastated by divine judgment, serving on that same day, may not be in a state to eat what is most holy in a manner worthy of it. Moses' contentment with Aaron's answer is God's endorsement through His servant that wisdom, not mere mechanical compliance, governs the application of His commands.*

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### STRANGE FIRE (Hebrew: esh zarah)
- **Leviticus 10:1** — esh = fire; zarah = foreign, unauthorized, strange; literally "foreign fire" — fire from a source other than the altar, or offered in a manner not commanded
- *Definition here:* Worship offered in a manner or with elements not prescribed by God; the category of religious action that is self-generated rather than God-commanded; the defining disqualifier: "which he commanded them not" — not that it was sinister, but that it was unauthorized*

### SANCTIFIED (Hebrew: qadash — Niphal: "I will be sanctified")
- **Leviticus 10:3** — "I will be sanctified (eqqadesh) in them that come nigh me" — Niphal = passive/reflexive: God will demonstrate His own holiness through those who approach Him
- *Definition here:* God's holiness vindicated and made visible in the judgment of those who approach Him improperly; the inverse of defilement — where defilement makes the holy common, sanctification makes God's holiness unmistakably distinct; the purpose of Leviticus 10:1-3 is not punishment as an end but the public, undeniable declaration of God's otherness*

## THE ACTUAL VERSES — LEVITICUS 10 (KJV)

**1** And Nadab and Abihu, the sons of Aaron, took either of them his censer, and put fire therein, and put incense thereon, and offered strange fire before the LORD, which he commanded them not.
**2** And there went out fire from the LORD, and devoured them, and they died before the LORD.
**3** Then Moses said unto Aaron, This is it that the LORD spake, saying, I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the people I will be glorified. And Aaron held his peace.
**4** And Moses called Mishael and Elzaphan, the sons of Uzziel the uncle of Aaron, and said unto them, Come near, carry your brethren from before the sanctuary out of the camp.
**5** So they went near, and carried them in their coats out of the camp; as Moses had said.
**6** And Moses said unto Aaron, and unto Eleazar and unto Ithamar, his sons, Uncover not your heads, neither rend your clothes; lest ye die, and lest wrath come upon all the people: but let your brethren, the whole house of Israel, bewail the burning which the LORD hath kindled.
**7** And ye shall not go out from the door of the tabernacle of the congregation, lest ye die: for the anointing oil of the LORD is upon you. And they did according to the word of Moses.
**8** And the LORD spake unto Aaron, saying,
**9** Do not drink wine nor strong drink, thou, nor thy sons with thee, when ye go into the tabernacle of the congregation, lest ye die: it shall be a statute for ever throughout your generations:
**10** And that ye may put difference between holy and unholy, and between unclean and clean;
**11** And that ye may teach the children of Israel all the statutes which the LORD hath spoken unto them by the hand of Moses.
**12** And Moses spake unto Aaron, and unto Eleazar and unto Ithamar, his sons that were left, Take the meat offering that remaineth of the offerings of the LORD made by fire, and eat it without leaven beside the altar: for it is most holy:
**13** And ye shall eat it in the holy place, because it is thy due, and thy sons' due, of the sacrifices of the LORD made by fire: for so I am commanded.
**14** And the wave breast and heave shoulder shall ye eat in a clean place; thou, and thy sons, and thy daughters with thee: for they be thy due, and thy sons' due, which are given out of the sacrifices of peace offerings of the children of Israel.
**15** The heave shoulder and the wave breast shall they bring with the offerings made by fire of the fat, to wave it for a wave offering before the LORD; and it shall be thine, and thy sons' with thee, by a statute for ever; as the LORD hath commanded.
**16** And Moses diligently sought the goat of the sin offering, and, behold, it was burnt: and he was angry with Eleazar and Ithamar, the sons of Aaron which were left alive, saying,
**17** Wherefore have ye not eaten the sin offering in the holy place, seeing it is most holy, and God hath given it you to bear the iniquity of the congregation, to make atonement for them before the LORD?
**18** Behold, the blood of it was not brought in within the holy place: ye should indeed have eaten it in the holy place, as I commanded.
**19** And Aaron said unto Moses, Behold, this day have they offered their sin offering and their burnt offering before the LORD; and such things have befallen me: and if I had eaten the sin offering to day, should it have been accepted in the sight of the LORD?
**20** And when Moses heard that, he was content.

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### The Two Sons of Aaron and the Two Sons of God's Household

Leviticus 10 immediately follows Leviticus 9. The glory falls; two sons of the high priest immediately abuse their access. This pattern repeats:

- **Numbers 16** — Korah, Dathan, and Abiram: claiming priestly access not authorized for them
- **1 Samuel 2:12-17** — Hophni and Phinehas, sons of Eli: taking what was not theirs from the sacrifice
- **Luke 15:11-32** — The prodigal and the elder brother: two sons, two responses to the father's house

*The warning of Leviticus 10 is embedded in the structure of God's redemptive story: being born near the fire does not mean you have the right to handle the fire. Privilege of access is not license for innovation. The sons of Aaron were consecrated — and still died for what they brought without authorization. The New Covenant is not more permissive about this principle; it is more serious (Hebrews 10:29 — "of how much sorer punishment... shall he be thought worthy, who hath trodden under foot the Son of God").*
