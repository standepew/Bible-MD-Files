## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Moses gives warfare regulations: when facing superior enemy forces with horses and chariots, don't fear because God who delivered from Egypt fights for you. The priest shall encourage the people not to faint, fear, tremble, or be terrified, for God goes with them to save them. Officers then grant military exemptions: those who built new houses but haven't dedicated them, planted vineyards but haven't eaten from them, or betrothed wives but haven't married them may return home, lest they die and another enjoy their blessings. The fearful and fainthearted may also leave lest they discourage others. After exemptions, captains lead the army. When approaching a city to fight, first proclaim peace—if they accept and open the gates, they become tributaries. If they refuse war, besiege them. When God delivers the city, kill all males but take women, children, cattle, and spoil as provision. This applies to distant cities; however, for Canaanite cities given as inheritance, save nothing alive—utterly destroy Hittites, Amorites, Canaanites, Perizzites, Hivites, and Jebusites to prevent learning their abominations. During long sieges, don't destroy fruit trees (they're man's life) but may eat from them; only destroy non-fruit trees for building bulwarks until the city falls.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Be not afraid of them" — Divine Courage in Battle

**Deuteronomy 20:1, 3-4** — "When thou goest out to battle against thine enemies, and seest horses, and chariots, and a people more than thou, be not afraid of them... let not your hearts faint, fear not, and do not tremble, neither be ye terrified because of them: For the LORD your God is he that goeth with you, to fight for you against your enemies, to save you."

- **Joshua 1:9** — "Have not I commanded thee? Be strong and of a good courage; be not afraid, neither be thou dismayed: for the LORD thy God is with thee whithersoever thou goest."
  - *Interpretation:* God gives Joshua the exact same promise when commissioning him to lead Israel into Canaan. This is the foundational promise for Israel's warfare.

- **2 Chronicles 20:15-17** — "And he said, Hearken ye, all Judah, and ye inhabitants of Jerusalem, and thou king Jehoshaphat, Thus saith the LORD unto you, Be not afraid nor dismayed by reason of this great multitude; for the battle is not yours, but God's... Ye shall not need to fight in this battle: stand ye still, and see the salvation of the LORD"
  - *Interpretation:* Jehoshaphat experiences this promise when facing overwhelming forces. God fights the battle without Israel's weapons.

- **Romans 8:31** — "What shall we then say to these things? If God be for us, who can be against us?"
  - *Interpretation:* Paul applies this principle to spiritual warfare. The same divine presence that gave Israel courage in physical battles gives believers courage in spiritual battles.

### "Built a new house, and hath not dedicated it" — Military Exemptions

**Deuteronomy 20:5-9** — "What man is there that hath built a new house, and hath not dedicated it? let him go and return to his house... What man is he that hath planted a vineyard, and hath not yet eaten of it?... What man is there that hath betrothed a wife, and hath not taken her?... What man is there that is fearful and fainthearted? let him go and return unto his house"

- **Deuteronomy 24:5** — "When a man hath taken a new wife, he shall not go out to war, neither shall he be charged with any business: he shall be free at home one year, and shall cheer up his wife which he hath taken."
  - *Interpretation:* Moses provides additional protection for newly married men—one year free from military service to establish the marriage.

- **1 Corinthians 7:32-34** — "But I would have you without carefulness. He that is unmarried careth for the things that belong to the Lord, how he may please the Lord: But he that is married careth for the things that are of the world, how he may please his wife"
  - *Interpretation:* Paul acknowledges the divided interests of married people, showing the wisdom of Moses' exemptions to prevent distraction in battle.

- **Revelation 7:9** — "After this I beheld, and, lo, a great multitude, which no man could number, of all nations, and kindreds, and people, and tongues, stood before the throne, and before the Lamb, clothed with white robes, and palms in their hands"
  - *Interpretation:* The palm branches symbolize victory and celebration, similar to the dedication celebrations the exemptions preserve.

### "Proclaim peace unto it" — Diplomatic Protocol

**Deuteronomy 20:10-12** — "When thou comest nigh unto a city to fight against it, then proclaim peace unto it. And it shall be, if it make thee answer of peace, and open unto thee, then it shall be, that all the people that is found therein shall be tributaries unto thee, and they shall serve thee"

- **Luke 14:31-32** — "Or what king, going to make war against another king, sitteth not down first, and consulteth whether he be able with ten thousand to meet him that cometh against him with twenty thousand? Or else, while the other is yet a great way off, he sendeth an ambassage, and desireth conditions of peace"
  - *Interpretation:* Jesus uses this as a picture of counting costs in discipleship, showing this was standard ancient Near Eastern practice.

- **Romans 12:18** — "If it be possible, as much as lieth in you, live peaceably with all men."
  - *Interpretation:* Paul applies this principle to Christian relationships—peace is always the first option, conflict only when necessary.

- **Hebrews 12:14** — "Follow peace with all men, and holiness, without which no man shall see the Lord"
  - *Interpretation:* The writer to the Hebrews makes peace-seeking a fundamental characteristic of the godly life, building on this Old Testament principle.

### "Save alive nothing that breatheth" — Canaanite Destruction

**Deuteronomy 20:16-18** — "But of the cities of these people, which the LORD thy God doth give thee for an inheritance, thou shalt save alive nothing that breatheth: But thou shalt utterly destroy them... That they teach you not to do after all their abominations, which they have done unto their gods"

- **Genesis 15:16** — "But in the fourth generation they shall come hither again: for the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet full."
  - *Interpretation:* God told Abraham that his descendants would return when Canaanite iniquity was complete. The destruction is divine judgment, not ethnic hatred.

- **Deuteronomy 9:4-5** — "Speak not thou in thine heart, after that the LORD thy God hath cast them out from before thee, saying, For my righteousness the LORD hath brought me in to possess this land... Not for thy righteousness, nor for the uprightness of thine heart, dost thou go to possess their land"
  - *Interpretation:* Moses emphasizes this destruction is due to Canaanite wickedness, not Israelite righteousness.

- **1 Corinthians 10:20** — "But I say, that the things which the Gentiles sacrifice, they sacrifice to devils, and not to God: and I would not that ye should have fellowship with devils."
  - *Interpretation:* Paul identifies pagan worship as demon worship, showing the spiritual danger behind Canaanite practices that justified their destruction.

### "The tree of the field is man's life" — Environmental Protection

**Deuteronomy 20:19-20** — "Thou shalt not destroy the trees thereof by forcing an axe against them: for thou mayest eat of them, and thou shalt not cut them down (for the tree of the field is man's life) to employ them in the siege: Only the trees which thou knowest that they be not trees for meat, thou shalt destroy and cut them down"

- **Leviticus 19:23-25** — "And when ye shall come into the land, and shall have planted all manner of trees for food, then ye shall count the fruit thereof as uncircumcised: three years shall it be as uncircumcised unto you: it shall not be eaten of. But in the fourth year all the fruit thereof shall be holy to praise the LORD withal. And in the fifth year shall ye eat of the fruit thereof"
  - *Interpretation:* Leviticus shows the long-term view of trees as valuable resources, not to be destroyed casually.

- **Psalm 1:3** — "And he shall be like a tree planted by the rivers of water, that bringeth forth his fruit in his season; his leaf also shall not wither; and whatsoever he doeth shall prosper."
  - *Interpretation:* The psalmist uses fruit trees as a picture of prosperity and blessing, reinforcing their value.

- **Revelation 22:2** — "In the midst of the street of it, and on either side of the river, was there the tree of life, which bare twelve manner of fruits, and yielded her fruit every month: and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations."
  - *Interpretation:* The New Jerusalem features the tree of life, showing the ultimate value of trees as life-giving resources in God's economy.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### FEARFUL AND FAINTHEARTED
- **Military context:** Soldiers whose fear would spread to others
- **Spiritual principle:** Fear is contagious and undermines collective courage
- **Modern application:** Remove negative influences from critical situations
- **Psychological insight:** Fear affects group dynamics and morale

### TRIBUTARIES
- **Political status:** Conquered people who pay taxes and provide labor
- **Not slavery:** Maintain local autonomy under foreign rule
- **Economic benefit:** Conqueror receives resources without administration costs
- **Alternative to destruction:** Mercy option for cooperative cities

### UTTERLY DESTROY (HEREM)
- **Hebrew concept:** Complete devotion to destruction
- **Divine judgment:** God's wrath against persistent wickedness
- **Spiritual protection:** Prevents contamination from idolatry
- **New Testament parallel:** Separation from worldly corruption (2 Corinthians 6:17)

### TREE OF THE FIELD IS MAN'S LIFE
- **Literal meaning:** Fruit trees provide essential sustenance
- **Economic value:** Long-term agricultural investment
- **Environmental principle:** Sustainable warfare practices
- **Spiritual application:** Preservation of life-giving resources

1 When thou goest out to battle against thine enemies, and seest horses, and chariots, and a people more than thou, be not afraid of them: for the LORD thy God is with thee, which brought thee up out of the land of Egypt. 

2 And it shall be, when ye are come nigh unto the battle, that the priest shall approach and speak unto the people, 

3 And shall say unto them, Hear, O Israel, ye approach this day unto battle against your enemies: let not your hearts faint, fear not, and do not tremble, neither be ye terrified because of them; 

4 For the LORD your God is he that goeth with you, to fight for you against your enemies, to save you. 

5 And the officers shall speak unto the people, saying, What man is there that hath built a new house, and hath not dedicated it? let him go and return to his house, lest he die in the battle, and another man dedicate it. 

6 And what man is he that hath planted a vineyard, and hath not yet eaten of it? let him also go and return unto his house, lest he die in the battle, and another man eat of it. 

7 And what man is there that hath betrothed a wife, and hath not taken her? let him go and return unto his house, lest he die in the battle, and another man take her. 

8 And the officers shall speak further unto the people, and they shall say, What man is there that is fearful and fainthearted? let him go and return unto his house, lest his brethren's heart faint as well as his heart. 

9 And it shall be, when the officers have made an end of speaking unto the people, that they shall make captains of the armies to lead the people. 

10 When thou comest nigh unto a city to fight against it, then proclaim peace unto it. 

11 And it shall be, if it make thee answer of peace, and open unto thee, then it shall be, that all the people that is found therein shall be tributaries unto thee, and they shall serve thee. 

12 And if it will make no peace with thee, but will make war against thee, then thou shalt besiege it: 

13 And when the LORD thy God hath delivered it into thine hands, thou shalt smite every male thereof with the edge of the sword: 

14 But the women, and the little ones, and the presence, and all that is in the city, even all the spoil thereof, shalt thou take unto thyself; and thou shalt eat the spoil of thine enemies, which the LORD thy God hath given thee. 

15 Thus shalt thou do unto all the cities which are very far off from thee, which are not of the cities of these nations. 

16 But of the cities of these people, which the LORD thy God doth give thee for an inheritance, thou shalt save alive nothing that breatheth: 

17 But thou shalt utterly destroy them; namely, the Hittites, and the Amorites, and the Canaanites, and the Perizzites, the Hivites, and the Jebusites; as the LORD thy God hath commanded thee: 

18 That they teach you not to do after all their abominations, which they have done unto their gods; so should ye sin against the LORD your God. 

19 When thou shalt besiege a city a long time, in making war against it to take it, thou shalt not destroy the trees thereof by forcing an axe against them: for thou mayest eat of them, and thou shalt not cut them down (for the tree of the field is man's life) to employ them in the siege: 

20 Only the trees which thou knowest that they be not trees for meat, thou shalt destroy and cut them down; and thou shalt build bulwarks against the city that maketh war with thee, until it be subdued.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Save alive nothing that breatheth"
**Common misinterpretation:** This promotes genocide and ethnic cleansing
**Biblical context:** This specifically applies to the Canaanite nations in their specific land because of their extreme wickedness and idolatry that threatened Israel's spiritual survival. It's not a general principle for warfare but specific divine judgment against particular cultures.

### "The tree of the field is man's life"
**Common misinterpretation:** This is about environmentalism generally
**Biblical context:* This specifically protects fruit trees during sieges because they provide long-term sustenance. It's a practical war regulation, not a general environmental principle, though it does show God's concern for sustainable resource use.

**Chapter 20 establishes just warfare principles:** Divine courage replaces fear, human exemptions protect life's blessings, peace is offered before war, and destruction is limited to what's necessary. The complete destruction of Canaanites protects Israel's spiritual purity, while environmental protection preserves future provision. This system shows that even in warfare, God values life, provides for human needs, and maintains justice tempered with mercy where possible.
