## CHAPTER SUMMARY

After Abimelech, Tola son of Puah (son of Dodo), an Issachar man, arises to defend Israel. He judges Israel twenty-three years, dwelling in Shamir in mount Ephraim until his death and burial there. After him, Jair the Gileadite judges Israel twenty-two years. He has thirty sons who ride thirty ass colts and control thirty cities called Havoth-jair in Gilead. Jair dies and is buried in Camon. Then Israel does evil again, serving Baalim, Ashtaroth, and the gods of Syria, Zidon, Moab, Ammon, and the Philistines while forsaking the LORD. God's anger burns, and He sells Israel into Philistine and Ammonite hands. That year they oppress Israel for eighteen years, particularly those east of Jordan in Amorite land (Gilead). The Ammonites also cross Jordan to fight Judah, Benjamin, and Ephraim, causing severe distress. Israel cries to the LORD, confessing they sinned by forsaking God and serving Baalim. God reminds them of His past deliverances from Egyptians, Amorites, Ammonites, and Philistines, and also from Zidonians, Amalekites, and Maonites. He declares that since they forsook Him for other gods, He will deliver them no more and tells them to cry to their chosen gods for deliverance in tribulation. Israel responds with complete repentance, acknowledging their sin and asking God to do whatever seems good to them, only requesting deliverance that day. They put away their foreign gods and serve the LORD again, whose soul is grieved by Israel's misery. The Ammonites gather and encamp in Gilead. Israel assembles and encamps at Mizpeh. The people and princes of Gilead ask each other who will begin fighting against Ammon, promising that person will be head over all Gilead inhabitants.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### HAVOTH-JAIR
- **Meaning:** "Villages of Jair"
- **Context:** Thirty cities controlled by Jair's sons
- **Significance:** Family prosperity and influence
* **Spiritual application:** Spiritual inheritance (1 Peter 1:4)

### BAALIM AND ASHTAROTH
- **Meaning:** Canaanite male and female deities
- **Context:** Multiple false gods Israel served
- **Significance:** Complete abandonment of true worship
* **New Testament parallel:** Idolatry (Colossians 3:5)

### STRANGE GODS
- **Meaning:** Foreign or unfamiliar deities
- **Context:** Gods of surrounding nations
- **Significance:** Cultural compromise leads to spiritual corruption
* **Spiritual application:** Worldly influences (James 4:4)

### MISERY
- **Meaning:** Great suffering and distress
- **Context:** Israel's condition under oppression
- **Significance:** Consequences of sin but also moves God to compassion
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual suffering (Romans 8:18)

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Go and cry unto the gods which ye have chosen"
**Common misinterpretation:** This shows God's permission to worship other gods
**Biblical context:** This is divine sarcasm and rebuke. God is telling Israel to experience the futility of idolatry by seeking help from gods that cannot deliver them.

### "Do thou unto us whatsoever seemeth good unto thee"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about accepting any punishment
**Biblical context:** This is complete surrender to God's will, showing genuine repentance. Israel accepts God's judgment while still requesting deliverance, demonstrating trust in divine mercy.

**Chapter 10 demonstrates the cycle of apostasy, oppression, repentance, and divine mercy:** The brief accounts of Tola and Jair show periods of relative peace and faithful leadership, with Jair's prosperity (thirty sons and thirty cities) indicating God's blessing during obedience. However, Israel returns to idolatry on an unprecedented scale, serving seven different nations' gods while completely forsaking the LORD. This widespread apostasy brings double oppression from both Philistines and Ammonites, with Ammonites even crossing Jordan to attack western tribes. The eighteen-year oppression shows the severity of divine judgment. Israel's initial repentance acknowledges sin but lacks complete surrender. God's refusal to deliver and sarcastic command to seek help from their chosen gods demonstrates divine frustration with repeated cycles of shallow repentance. Only when Israel completely surrenders - acknowledging sin, accepting whatever judgment God deems good, and removing foreign gods - does God's soul grieve with compassion for their misery. This shows that genuine repentance moves God's heart. The gathering for battle at Mizpeh and the search for a leader show that repentance must be followed by action. This chapter establishes important principles: minor judges can bring periods of peace, widespread idolatry brings severe judgment, shallow repentance is insufficient, complete surrender moves God's compassion, and genuine repentance leads to divine readiness to act. It demonstrates that God's patience has limits but His mercy responds to complete contrition. The chapter shows that spiritual restoration requires both internal repentance and external action against the causes of sin. The search for a leader to begin the fight shows that repentance must be accompanied by commitment to confront the sources of oppression.
