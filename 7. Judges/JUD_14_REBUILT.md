## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Samson goes to Timnath and sees a Philistine woman. He tells his parents to get her as his wife. They object, asking why he wants a wife from the uncircumcised Philistines instead of his own people. Samson insists, saying she pleases him well. His parents don't realize this is from the LORD, who seeks an occasion against the Philistines who dominate Israel. Samson and his parents go to Timnath's vineyards where a young lion roars at Samson. The Spirit of the LORD comes mightily upon him, and he tears the lion apart as if it were a kid, though he has nothing in his hand. He doesn't tell his parents what happened. He talks with the woman, who pleases him well. Later he returns to marry her and checks the lion's carcass, finding a swarm of bees and honey inside. He takes honey in his hands, eats it, and gives some to his parents without telling them where it came from. His father goes to arrange the marriage, and Samson makes a feast as young men do. The Philistines bring thirty companions. Samson proposes a riddle: if they can solve it within seven days, he'll give them thirty sheets and thirty garments; if not, they give him the same. They agree. His riddle: "Out of the eater came forth meat, and out of the strong came forth sweetness." For three days they cannot solve it. On the seventh day, they threaten Samson's wife with burning her and her father's house unless she gets the answer. She weeps before Samson, accusing him of hating her for not telling her the riddle. He says he hasn't even told his parents. She weeps for seven days until he tells her. She immediately tells her people. On the seventh day before sunset, they solve it: "What is sweeter than honey? and what is stronger than a lion?" Samson replies they wouldn't have solved it without "plowing with his heifer." The Spirit of the LORD comes upon him, and he goes to Ashkelon, kills thirty men, takes their spoil, and gives the garments to those who solved the riddle. His anger burns, and he goes to his father's house. His wife is given to his companion who had been his friend.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### "Get her for me to wife... of the uncircumcised Philistines" — Interfaith Marriage

**Judges 14:2-3** — "And he came up, and told his father and his mother, and said, I have seen a woman in Timnath of the daughters of the Philistines: now therefore get her for me to wife... Is there never a woman among the daughters of thy brethren, or among all my people, that thou goest to take a wife of the uncircumcised Philistines?"

- **Deuteronomy 7:3-4** — "Neither shalt thou make marriages with them; thy daughter thou shalt not give unto his son, nor his daughter shalt thou take unto thy son. For they will turn away thy son from following me, that they may serve other gods: so the anger of the LORD be kindled against you, and destroy thee suddenly."
  - *Interpretation:* Deuteronomy specifically forbids marriage with Canaanites (including Philistines), showing Samson's choice violates clear divine command.

- **2 Corinthians 6:14** - "Be ye not unequally yoked together with unbelievers: for what fellowship hath righteousness with unrighteousness? and what communion hath light with darkness?"
  -Interpretation:* Paul's principle against unequal yoking applies to Samson's choice of a Philistine wife.

### "The Spirit of the LORD came mightily upon him, and he rent him as he would have rent a kid" — Divine Strength

**Judges 14:6** — "And the Spirit of the LORD came mightily upon him, and he rent him as he would have rent a kid, and he had nothing in his hand: but he told not his father or his mother what he had done"

- **Judges 15:14** — "And when he came unto Lehi, the Philistines shouted against him: and the Spirit of the LORD came mightily upon him, and the cords that were upon his arms became as flax that was burnt with fire, and his bands loosed from off his hands."
  - *Interpretation:* The Spirit's mighty coming upon Samson is a repeated pattern, showing divine enablement for supernatural strength.

- **Zechariah 4:6** - "Not by might, nor by power, but by my spirit, saith the LORD of hosts."
  -Interpretation:* Zechariah teaches that divine strength comes through God's Spirit, which Samson demonstrates.

### "Out of the eater came forth meat, and out of the strong came forth sweetness" — Divine Paradox

**Judges 14:14** — "And he said unto them, Out of the eater came forth meat, and out of the strong came forth sweetness. And they could not in three days expound the riddle"

- **1 Corinthians 1:27** — "But God hath chosen the foolish things of the world to confound the wise; and God hath chosen the weak things of the world to confound the things which are mighty"
  - *Interpretation:* Paul teaches that God uses weak things to shame the strong, which Samson's riddle demonstrates - sweetness from the strong (lion), meat from the eater (honey).

- **John 6:55** - "For my flesh is meat indeed, and my blood is drink indeed."
  -Interpretation:* Jesus speaks of coming from death to life, which parallels the life-giving honey coming from the dead lion.

### "If ye had not plowed with my heifer, ye had not found out my riddle" — Marital Metaphor

**Judges 14:18** — "And he said unto them, What is sweeter than honey? and what is stronger than a lion? And he said unto them, If ye had not plowed with my heifer, ye had not found out my riddle"

- **Proverbs 30:18-19** — "There be three things which are too wonderful for me, yea, four which I know not: The way of an eagle in the air; the way of a serpent upon a rock; the way of a ship in the midst of the sea; and the way of a man with a maid."
  - *Interpretation:* Proverbs speaks of the mysterious way of a man with a maid, which relates to Samson's reference to his wife as his "heifer."

- **Jeremiah 31:16** - "Thus saith the LORD; Refrain thy voice from weeping, and thine eyes from tears: for thy work shall be rewarded, saith the LORD; and they shall come again from the land of the enemy."
  -Interpretation:* Jeremiah speaks of weeping ending and reward coming, which relates to the wife's weeping and the Philistines' "reward" of solving the riddle.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "Get her for me to wife... of the uncircumcised Philistines"
**Common misinterpretation:** This shows cultural tolerance
**Biblical context:** This violates clear biblical commands against intermarriage with pagans. The narrator notes Samson's parents don't realize this is from God for an occasion against the Philistines, but this doesn't make the choice itself right.

### "Out of the eater came forth meat, and out of the strong came forth sweetness"
**Common misinterpretation:* This is about natural processes
**Biblical context:** This is a supernatural paradox - sweetness (honey) coming from death (lion carcass) and meat (honey) coming from what should eat, not produce food.

**Chapter 14 demonstrates how God uses human weakness and even poor choices to accomplish divine purposes:** Samson's desire for a Philistine wife violates clear biblical commands, but God uses this as "an occasion against the Philistines." This shows that God can work through imperfect human decisions to accomplish His will. The lion encounter demonstrates Samson's supernatural strength through the Spirit, though he keeps it secret from his parents. The honey in the lion's carcass represents God's ability to bring sweetness (blessing) out of death and danger, a divine paradox that becomes the basis for his riddle. The wedding feast with thirty companions shows cultural assimilation with the enemy. The riddle itself contains profound truth about God's power to bring life from death and sweetness from strength. The wife's betrayal through persistent weeping shows how human relationships can compromise divine purposes. Samson's violent revenge in killing thirty men and taking their garments shows his impulsive nature and tendency to solve problems through violence. The loss of his wife to his companion demonstrates the consequences of poor choices and compromised relationships. This chapter establishes crucial patterns: God works through flawed human choices, divine strength enables supernatural feats, God brings blessing from dangerous situations, human relationships can compromise divine purposes, and impulsive violence creates more problems. It demonstrates that Samson's calling is genuine, but his character flaws repeatedly undermine his effectiveness. The chapter shows that divine purpose can be accomplished through imperfect vessels, but human weakness still has consequences. The honey from the lion's carcass serves as a powerful metaphor for how God can bring good out of even dangerous and deadly situations.
