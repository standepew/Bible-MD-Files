## CHAPTER SUMMARY

In days without a king, a Levite sojourning in mount Ephraim takes a concubine from Bethlehem-judah. She plays the whore against him and returns to her father's house for four months. Her husband follows with servant and asses to speak friendly and bring her back. Her father rejoices to meet him and retains him three days for eating and drinking. On the fourth day, the father urges him to comfort his heart with bread before departing. They eat and drink together, and the father encourages him to stay the night with merry heart. The man rises to depart but his father-in-law urges him to stay. On the fifth day, the father again urges him to stay and comfort his heart; they stay until afternoon eating. When the man rises to depart with his concubine, servant, and father-in-law, the father urges him to stay the night as evening approaches, promising early departure the next day. The man refuses, departing and reaching Jebus (Jerusalem) at sunset with two saddled asses and his concubine. The servant suggests lodging in Jebus, but the master refuses to enter a city of strangers not of Israel, preferring Gibeah. They arrive at Gibeah in Benjamin at sunset and turn aside to lodge, but no one receives them. An old man from mount Ephraim, also sojourning in Gibeah among Benjamites, returns from field work and sees the wayfaring man in the street. He asks where they're going, and the Levite explains they're passing from Bethlehem-judah to mount Ephraim, having gone to Bethlehem-judah but now going to the LORD's house, with no one receiving them. He mentions they have provisions for asses and food for themselves and servants. The old man offers peace and hospitality, telling him not to lodge in the street. He brings them home, feeds the asses, washes their feet, and they eat and drink. While making merry, sons of Belial from the city surround the house, beat the door, and demand the man to know him. The master goes out and begs them not to do wickedly since the man is in his house. He offers his daughter and the concubine to humble and do as they please, but not to do such a vile thing to the man. The men refuse, so the man brings out his concubine. They abuse her all night until morning, then let her go. At dawn, the woman falls at the door where her lord is until daylight. Her lord rises, opens the doors, and finds his concubine fallen at the door with hands on the threshold. He tells her to rise and go, but she doesn't answer. He puts her on an ass, rises, and goes to his place. At home, he takes a knife, divides her with her bones into twelve pieces, and sends her throughout all Israel's coasts. All who see it declare no such deed has been done or seen since Israel came from Egypt, urging consideration, advice, and speaking minds.

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### CONCUBINE
- **Meaning:** Secondary wife or mistress
- **Context:** Levite's unfaithful partner
- **Significance:** Marital relationship without full covenant protection
* **New Testament parallel:** Spiritual adultery (James 4:4)

### SONS OF BELIAL
- **Meaning:** "Worthless men," wicked or lawless men
- **Context:** Gibeah's criminal element
- **Significance:** Complete moral breakdown in society
* **New Testament parallel:** Men of corrupt mind (2 Timothy 3:13)

### BELIAL
- **Meaning:** "Worthlessness" or "wickedness"
- **Context:** Characterization of Gibeah's criminals
- **Significance:** Moral and spiritual worthlessness
* **New Testament parallel:** Works of darkness (Ephesians 5:11)

### TWELVE PIECES
- **Meaning:** Division into tribal portions
- **Context:** Method of national communication and horror
- **Significance:** Unity through shared outrage
* **Spiritual application:** Body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12)

## THE ACTUAL VERSES (KJV)

1 And it came to pass in those days, when there was no king in Israel, that there was a certain Levite sojourning on the side of mount Ephraim, who took to him a concubine out of Beth-lehem-judah. 

2 And his concubine played the whore against him, and went away from him unto her father's house to Beth-lehem-judah, and was there four whole months. 

3 And her husband arose, and went after her, to speak friendly unto her, and to bring her again, having his servant with him, and a couple of asses: and she brought him into her father's house: and when the father of the damsel saw him, he rejoiced to meet him. 

4 And his father in law, the damsel's father, retained him; and he abode with him three days: so they did eat and drink, and lodged there. 

5 And it came to pass on the fourth day, when they arose early in the morning, that he rose up to depart: and the damsel's father said unto his son in law, Comfort thine heart with a morsel of bread, and afterward go your way. 

6 And they sat down, and did eat and drink both of them together: for the damsel's father had said unto the man, Be content, I pray thee, and tarry all night, and let thine heart be merry. 

7 And when the man rose up to depart, his father in law urged him: therefore he lodged there again. 

8 And he arose early in the morning on the fifth day to depart: and the damsel's father said, Comfort thine heart, I pray thee. And they tarried until afternoon, and they did eat both of them. 

9 And when the man rose up to depart, he, and his concubine, and his servant, his father in law, the damsel's father, said unto him, Behold, now the day draweth toward evening, I pray you tarry all night: behold, the day groweth to an end, lodge here, that thine heart may be merry; and to morrow get you early on your way, that thou mayest go home. 

10 But the man would not tarry that night, but he rose up and departed, and came over against Jebus, which is Jerusalem; and there were with him two asses saddled, his concubine also was with him. 

11 And when they were by Jebus, the day was far spent; and the servant said unto his master, Come, I pray thee, and let us turn in into this city of the Jebusites, and lodge in it. 

12 And his master said unto him, We will not turn aside hither into the city of a stranger, that is not of the children of Israel; we will pass over to Gibeah. 

13 And he said unto his servant, Come, and let us draw near to one of these places to lodge all night, in Gibeah, or in Ramah. 

14 And they passed on and went their way; and the sun went down upon them when they were by Gibeah, which belongeth to Benjamin. 

15 And they turned aside thither, to go in and to lodge in Gibeah: and when he went in, he sat him down in a street of the city: for there was no man that took them into his house to lodging. 

16 And, behold, there came an old man from his work out of the field at even, which was also of mount Ephraim; and he sojourned in Gibeah: but the men of the place were Benjamites. 

17 And when he had lifted up his eyes, he saw a wayfaring man in the street of the city: and the old man said, Whither goest thou? and whence comest thou? 

18 And he said unto him, We are passing from Beth-lehem-judah toward the side of mount Ephraim; from thence am I: and I went to Beth-lehem-judah, but I am now going to the house of the LORD; and there is no man that receiveth me to house. 

19 Yet there is both straw and provender for our asses; and there is bread and wine also for me, and for thy handmaid, and for the young man which is with thy servants: there is no want of any thing. 

20 And the old man said, Peace be with thee; howsoever let all thy wants lie upon me; only lodge not in the street. 

21 So he brought him into his house, and gave provender unto the asses: and they washed their feet, and did eat and drink. 

22 Now as they were making their hearts merry, behold, the men of the city, certain sons of Belial, beset the house round about, and beat at the door, and spake to the master of the house, the old man, saying, Bring forth the man that came into thine house, that we may know him. 

23 And the man, the master of the house, went out unto them, and said unto them, Nay, my brethren, nay, I pray you, do not so wickedly; seeing that this man is come into mine house, do not this folly. 

24 Behold, here is my daughter a maiden, and his concubine; them I will bring out now, and humble ye them, and do with them what seemeth good unto you: but unto this man do not so vile a thing. 

25 But the men would not hearken to him: so the man took his concubine, and brought her forth unto them; and they knew her, and abused her all the night until the morning: and when the day began to spring, they let her go. 

26 Then came the woman in the dawning of the day, and fell down at the door of the man's house where her lord was, till it was light. 

27 And her lord rose up in the morning, and opened the doors of the house, and went out to go his way: and, behold, the woman his concubine was fallen down at the door of the house, and her hands were upon the consideration. 

28 And he said unto her, Up, and let us be going. But none answered. Then the man took her up upon an ass, and the man rose up, and gat him unto his place. 

29 And when he was come into his house, he took a knife, and laid hold on his concubine, and divided her, together with her bones, into twelve pieces, and sent her into all the coasts of Israel. 

30 And it was so, that all that saw it said, There was no such deed done nor seen from the day that the children of Israel came up out of the land of Egypt unto this day: consider of it, take advice, and speak your minds.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED PASSAGES

### "We will not turn aside hither into the city of a stranger, that is not of the children of Israel"
**Common misinterpretation:** This shows proper separation
**Biblical context:** This shows tribal prejudice that leads to disaster. By avoiding Jebus (Jerusalem), they go to Gibeah where the crime occurs, showing that separation based on prejudice can be dangerous.

### "There was no such deed done nor seen from the day that the children of Israel came up out of the land of Egypt unto this day"
**Common misinterpretation:* This describes the worst crime ever
**Biblical context:** This is hyperbole emphasizing the horror, but other crimes (like the golden calf) were also terrible. It shows the complete breakdown of social order.

**Chapter 19 demonstrates the complete breakdown of social order and hospitality in Israel:** The opening statement about no king in Israel sets the context for societal chaos. The Levite's concubine playing the whore shows marital infidelity and the breakdown of proper family relationships. The Levite's journey to retrieve her shows proper initiative in reconciliation. The extended hospitality in Bethlehem shows cultural customs of family honor and proper reception. The repeated delays show the father's desire to maintain family connection. The Levite's refusal to lodge in Jebus (Jerusalem) shows tribal prejudice that leads to disaster - had they stayed there, the crime wouldn't have occurred. The lack of hospitality in Gibeah shows the complete breakdown of social norms in a Benjaminite city. The old man's hospitality shows that some still maintain proper customs. The sons of Belial represent the complete moral collapse - wicked men who commit atrocities without shame. The old man's attempt to protect his guests shows proper values, but his offer of his daughter and concubine shows compromise under pressure. The concubine's abuse shows the horrific violence that occurs when wicked men are unrestrained. Her death at the threshold symbolizes the death of hospitality and social order. The Levite's horrific response - dividing her body and sending it throughout Israel - shows both his own moral collapse and his method of rallying national outrage. This act, while brutal, succeeds in uniting all Israel against Gibeah's wickedness. The chapter serves as a powerful warning about the consequences of social breakdown, the importance of hospitality, the danger of moral relativism ("every man did what was right in his own eyes"), and the violence that emerges when proper social structures fail. It demonstrates that when there's no proper leadership, social norms collapse, and horrific crimes occur with impunity. The chapter shows that even religious leaders (Levites) can become morally compromised when society breaks down. It illustrates that hospitality is a sacred duty whose violation brings divine judgment. The horrific crime at Gibeah becomes the catalyst for Israel's civil war, showing how individual sins can have national consequences. This chapter sets the stage for the next chapters showing Israel's response to this atrocity and the resulting near-destruction of Benjamin.</think>
