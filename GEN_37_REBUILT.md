## CHAPTER SUMMARY

Genesis 37 opened with a single sentence that grounded everything that followed: "Jacob dwelt in the land wherein his father was a stranger, in the land of Canaan." The covenant land. The land of the promise. Jacob was settled. And then the text delivered the toledot of Jacob — not through the stories of eleven sons but through the story of one: "Joseph, being seventeen years old."

The chapter established the conditions for everything that would follow in the Joseph narrative: a father whose love was preferential and visible, brothers whose hatred was comprehensive and long-standing, a young man who told the truth about his brothers and received dreams from God that told the truth about his future. The coat was not the cause of the brothers' hatred — it was the visible sign of what they had already seen: "when his brethren saw that their father loved him more than all his brethren, they hated him." The coat was Jacob's announcement in fabric of what his heart had already declared.

The two dreams escalated the hatred precisely because they were true. The sheaves bowing (the first dream) spoke of material provision and governance. The sun, moon, and stars bowing (the second dream) spoke of family and cosmic authority. The brothers asked, "Shalt thou indeed reign over us?" Jacob rebuked but "observed the saying" — he knew the dreams were significant. He could not dismiss them. He kept them.

Jacob sent Joseph from Hebron to check on the brothers at Shechem — the same Shechem where the massacre of Genesis 34 had occurred, where the covenant field had been purchased in Genesis 33. A man found Joseph wandering and directed him to Dothan. The brothers saw him coming "afar off." The seeing from a distance was deliberate — they had time to plan before he arrived. "Behold, this dreamer cometh." The mockery of the title was the mockery of the dream; the mockery of the dream was the mockery of the God who gave it; the conspiracy against the dreamer was the conspiracy against the prophetic word itself.

Reuben intervened — not from righteousness but from the guilt of Genesis 35:22; he intended to rescue Joseph and restore him to his father. He persuaded them to use the pit rather than the sword. They stripped the coat — the first stripping of Joseph's garments, the first of four garment-moments in the Joseph narrative — and threw him into a dry pit (bor — the word for a cistern, a dungeon, a figure of Sheol throughout the Psalms). Then they sat down to eat bread. The detail was the narrator's silent moral indictment: their brother was in the pit and they sat down to eat.

The Ishmaelite/Midianite caravan from Gilead was the providential instrument God used — carrying spicery, balm, and myrrh to Egypt. Judah, whose name was the Hebrew form of the same name the New Testament would render as Judas (Yehudah/Ioudas), proposed the sale. "What profit is it if we slay our brother and conceal his blood?... he is our brother and our flesh." The argument was simultaneously an acknowledgment of brotherhood and a willingness to monetize it. Joseph was sold for twenty pieces of silver — precisely the Levitical valuation of a young male slave (Leviticus 27:5). The price that the Law would later attach to the young male human life was the price they accepted.

Reuben returned to find the pit empty, tore his clothes, and said: "The child is not; and I, whither shall I go?" The question was unanswerable. They took the coat, killed a kid, dipped the coat in the blood, and sent it to their father. Jacob recognized it: "It is my son's coat; an evil beast hath devoured him." The man who had deceived his own father with a kid, false garments, and a false voice (Genesis 27) was now deceived by his sons with a kid, a false garment, and a false report. The method of Jacob's original sin was the method of his punishment. He mourned, refused to be comforted, said he would go down to the grave mourning, and wept.

Meanwhile Joseph arrived in Egypt and was sold to Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh and captain of the guard. The chapter ended on that word: Egypt. The place of exile and bondage into which God was sending the instrument of the covenant's preservation.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Joseph as a Type of Christ — The Structure the Bible Itself Built

**The parallel was not constructed by commentators — it was embedded in the text by God through repeated verbal and structural correspondences**

- **The beloved son, sent by the father:**
  - Genesis 37:3 — "Now Israel loved Joseph more than all his children" — the father's singular love for the son
  - Genesis 37:13-14 — "Come, and I will send thee unto them... And he sent him out of the vale of Hebron" — the father sends the son to the brothers
  - Matthew 3:17 — "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased" — the Father's declaration of singular love at the Son's presentation
  - John 3:16-17 — "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only begotten Son... For God sent not his Son into the world to condemn the world; but that the world through him might be saved" — the giving and the sending; the beloved son dispatched to those who would reject and kill him
  - John 17:3 — "and Jesus Christ, whom thou hast sent" — the sent Son; the same structure as Jacob sending Joseph
  - The parallel was structural at its deepest level: a father's love, a son's willingness ("Here am I" — Genesis 37:13), a journey to those who would not receive him

- **Rejected by his brethren:**
  - Genesis 37:4 — "they hated him, and could not speak peaceably unto him" — the comprehensive hatred; not an incident but a settled state
  - Genesis 37:8 — "they hated him yet the more for his dreams, and for his words" — the dreams made the hatred worse; the more clearly the son's future glory was declared, the more intense the rejection
  - John 1:11 — "He came unto his own, and his own received him not" — the same structure: came to those who should have received him; was not received
  - Isaiah 53:3 — "He is despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief: and we hid as it were our faces from him; he was despised, and we esteemed him not" — the prophetic portrait of the rejected servant; the brothers hid their faces from the pit and sat down to eat; they esteemed the dreams nothing
  - Acts 7:9 — Stephen in his sermon: "And the patriarchs, moved with envy, sold Joseph into Egypt: but God was with him" — the Holy Spirit through Stephen explicitly identified the Joseph story as the pattern the brothers repeated with Christ: Acts 7:51-52 — "Ye stiffnecked and uncircumcised in heart and ears, ye do always resist the Holy Ghost: as your fathers did, so do ye also. Which of the prophets have not your fathers persecuted? and they have slain them which shewed before of the coming of the Just One; of whom ye have been now the betrayers and murderers" — Stephen connected the patriarchs' envy-driven sale of Joseph directly to Israel's rejection of Christ; the Scripture interpreted the type explicitly

- **The conspiracy to kill:**
  - Genesis 37:18-20 — "they conspired against him to slay him. And they said one to another, Behold, this dreamer cometh. Come now therefore, and let us slay him, and cast him into some pit" — the conspiracy from a distance; they planned before he arrived; the decision to kill was reached while he was still coming toward them
  - Matthew 26:3-4 — "Then assembled together the chief priests, and the scribes, and the elders of the people, unto the palace of the high priest, who was called Caiaphas, And consulted that they might take Jesus by subtlety, and kill him" — the same structure: assembled, conspired, decided to kill; planned while the object of the conspiracy continued his approach
  - John 11:53 — "Then from that day forth they took counsel together for to put him to death" — the formal conspiracy decision; the same pattern as "let us slay him"

- **Betrayed by one named Judah/Judas for silver:**
  - Genesis 37:26-27 — "And Judah said unto his brethren, What profit is it if we slay our brother... Come, and let us sell him to the Ishmaelites" — Judah (Yehudah) proposed the sale
  - Genesis 37:28 — "sold Joseph to the Ishmaelites for twenty pieces of silver"
  - Matthew 26:14-15 — "Then one of the twelve, called Judas Iscariot, went unto the chief priests, And said unto them, What will ye give me, and I will deliver him unto you? And they covenanted with him for thirty pieces of silver" — Judas (Ioudas — the Greek form of Yehudah) betrayed Christ for silver; the same name, the same act, the same currency
  - Zechariah 11:12-13 — the thirty pieces of silver prophesied; the "goodly price" at which the shepherd was valued; the silver thrown to the potter in the house of the LORD
  - The progression: twenty pieces for the type (Joseph — the Levitical price of a young male; Leviticus 27:5); thirty pieces for the antitype (Christ — the Mosaic price of a slave gored by an ox; Exodus 21:32); the price increased from type to antitype, and yet both prices were insults to the dignity of the one being sold; the mathematics of betrayal embedded in the Law

- **Stripped of the garment:**
  - Genesis 37:23 — "they stript Joseph out of his coat, his coat of many colours that was on him" — the first stripping; at the point of betrayal and descent
  - Matthew 27:28 — "And they stripped him, and put on him a scarlet robe" — at the point of condemnation and mockery
  - John 19:23-24 — "Then the soldiers, when they had crucified Jesus, took his garments, and made four parts, to every soldier a part; and also his coat: now the coat was without seam, woven from the top throughout... That the scripture might be fulfilled, which saith, They parted my raiment among them, and for my vesture they did cast lots" — Psalm 22:18 fulfilled; the garment divided; the stripping of honor at the moment of deepest humiliation

### The Pit — Death, Sheol, and the Resurrection Pattern

**Genesis 37:24** — "And the pit was empty, there was no water in it"

- **Bor — the cistern/pit as figure of death and Sheol:**
  - The Hebrew bor was the word for a cistern, a well-shaft, a dungeon — any deep pit in the ground; its use throughout the Psalms as an image of death, the grave, and Sheol was consistent and deliberate; when Joseph was thrown into a dry bor, the text was using the full weight of that imagery
  - Psalm 28:1 — "be not silent to me: lest, if thou be silent to me, I become like them that go down into the pit (bor)" — the pit as the place of divine silence, of death
  - Psalm 40:2 — "He brought me up also out of an horrible pit (bor), out of the miry clay, and set my feet upon a rock" — the pit as the starting point of the rescue; being brought up from the pit as the image of salvation; Joseph would be brought up from this pit to Potiphar's house, from Potiphar's dungeon to Pharaoh's throne; the Psalm captured the pattern of his entire narrative
  - Psalm 88:3-4 — "For my soul is full of troubles: and my life draweth nigh unto the grave. I am counted with them that go down into the pit (bor): I am as a man that hath no strength" — the language of death-in-the-pit; the psalmist used it for spiritual desolation; Joseph in the dry pit, stripped, having eaten nothing, while his brothers ate bread above him — was in exactly this condition
  - Zechariah 9:11 — "As for thee also, by the blood of thy covenant I have sent forth thy prisoners out of the pit wherein is no water" — the same phrase as Genesis 37:24: "a pit wherein is no water"; God promising to release prisoners from "the pit wherein is no water" through the blood of the covenant; the Genesis 37 pit explicitly echoed and made the basis of a covenant promise; God would deliver prisoners from pits like Joseph's through the blood of the covenant — ultimately through the blood of Christ

- **Joseph's entrance into and exit from the pit as death-and-resurrection:**
  - Descent into the pit: stripped, helpless, his brothers eating above him — the condition of death; "cast into the pit" was cast into the grave
  - Exit from the pit: lifted up by the Midianites; but this was not the final exit; the full resurrection trajectory ran from the pit (death) through Potiphar's house (partial elevation) through Potiphar's dungeon (second pit — Genesis 39:20) through Pharaoh's court (full resurrection and exaltation — Genesis 41:41-42)
  - Hosea 6:2 — "After two days will he revive us: in the third day he will raise us up, and we shall live in his sight" — the death-third-day-resurrection pattern; Joseph was in the pit, then in Potiphar's service, then in the dungeon, then raised on the "third" great movement to Pharaoh's throne; the typological death-and-resurrection of Joseph across the whole narrative
  - Romans 10:7 — Paul, quoting the Moses passage about "who shall descend into the deep (abyss)?... that is, to bring up Christ again from the dead" — the deep/abyss as the place of death from which Christ was raised; the pit without water of Genesis 37 was in the same theological category; the one thrown into the pit came out as lord

### "This Dreamer Cometh" — The Mockery of God's Prophetic Vessel

**Genesis 37:19** — "And they said one to another, Behold, this dreamer cometh"

- **Baal chalomoth — master of dreams:** The brothers used the phrase as mockery; "the dream-lord" — said contemptuously; yet the title was accurate and prophetic; Joseph WAS the master of dreams — not because he invented them but because God gave them through him and God alone could interpret them (Genesis 40:8 — "Do not interpretations belong to God?")
- **Dreams as the established channel of divine communication:**
  - Numbers 12:6 — God's own declaration: "If there be a prophet among you, I the LORD will make myself known unto him in a vision, and will speak unto him in a dream" — the divine definition of prophetic communication; dreaming was not imagination or psychology but the chosen method of the LORD's self-disclosure to prophets; to mock Joseph's dreams was to mock the LORD's chosen channel
  - Joel 2:28 — "And it shall come to pass afterward, that I will pour out my spirit upon all flesh; and your sons and your daughters shall prophesy, your old men shall dream dreams, your young men shall see visions" — the Pentecost promise; dream and vision as the Spirit's instruments in the last days; Acts 2:17 — Peter confirmed this was being fulfilled at Pentecost; the channel the brothers mocked in Joseph was the channel the Spirit would pour out on all flesh
  - Matthew 1:20; 2:12-13, 19, 22 — in the New Testament, God communicated through dreams in the Joseph narrative of Jesus's birth: Joseph the carpenter was warned in dreams four times; the angel appeared in dreams; the name "Joseph" and the dream-communication connected the two Josephs across the testaments; both were instruments of divine guidance through dreams in the crucial moments of the covenant's advance
- **"We shall see what will become of his dreams" (v.20):** The brothers' challenge was ironic: their act of throwing Joseph into the pit was the very act that began the fulfillment of the dreams; had they not sold him, Joseph would not have gone to Egypt; had he not gone to Egypt, he would not have risen to power; had he not risen to power, the famine would not have been managed; had it not been managed, the brothers would not have come to Egypt; and had they not come to Egypt, they would not have bowed before Joseph — exactly as the dreams declared; the attempt to destroy the dream was the first step in its fulfillment; Acts 4:27-28 — "For of a truth against thy holy child Jesus, both Herod, and Pontius Pilate, with the Gentiles, and the people of Israel, were gathered together, For to do whatsoever thy hand and thy counsel determined before to be done" — the conspirators against God's anointed enacted His predetermined plan

### Reuben and Judah — Two Partial Responses and Their Later Redemption

**Genesis 37:21-22** — Reuben: "Shed no blood... that he might rid him out of their hands, to deliver him to his father again"
**Genesis 37:26-27** — Judah: "What profit is it if we slay our brother... let us sell him"

- **Reuben — the firstborn trying to recover lost standing:** Genesis 35:22 — Reuben had defiled his father's bed; his standing in the family was already compromised; his desire to "deliver him to his father again" (v.22) was the impulse of a man who needed to restore himself in Jacob's eyes; the motivation was mixed — genuine concern for Joseph and self-interested restoration; he failed regardless; when he returned to find the pit empty (v.29), his grief was genuine: "The child is not; and I, whither shall I go?" — the unanswerable question of a man who knew his failure was complete
- **Judah — the proposer of the sale who would become the offerer of himself:**
  - Genesis 37:26-27 — Judah framed the sale in terms of profit and the avoidance of direct bloodshed; "What profit is it" — the mercantile language; "he is our brother and our flesh" — the acknowledgment of relationship even while arranging the sale; it was the most calculating of the responses; he made the sin manageable and commercially justified
  - Genesis 44:33 — decades later, Judah stood before Joseph (not yet recognized) and said: "Now therefore, I pray thee, let thy servant abide instead of the lad a bondman to my lord; and let the lad go up with his father" — the same Judah who had arranged Joseph's sale now offered himself as a slave in Joseph's stead; the transformation from "let us sell him" to "let me be the slave instead" was the trajectory of genuine repentance; Judah's speech in Genesis 44:18-34 was one of the most eloquent transformations in all of Scripture; it was the speech that broke Joseph and caused him to weep
  - Genesis 49:8-10 — Jacob's deathbed blessing on Judah: "Judah, thou art he whom thy brethren shall praise: thy hand shall be in the neck of thine enemies; thy father's children shall bow down before thee... The sceptre shall not depart from Judah, nor a lawgiver from between his feet, until Shiloh come" — the tribe of the man who had sold Joseph for silver received the scepter promise; the Messianic line ran through the repentant brother, not through the one who had tried to restore (Reuben)
- **The typological significance of both:** Reuben attempted to save through a plan of his own devising that failed. Judah's intervention initially made things worse but was later transformed by genuine repentance into the highest self-offering in the Joseph narrative. The tribe of Reuben scattered; the tribe of Judah produced the kings and the Messiah. The Bible's internal reward structure for genuine repentance was visible in the diverging trajectories of the two brothers from Genesis 37 onward.

### The Kid's Blood on the Coat — Deception Turned on the Deceiver

**Genesis 37:31-33** — "they took Joseph's coat, and killed a kid of the goats, and dipped the coat in the blood... And he knew it, and said, It is my son's coat; an evil beast hath devoured him"

- **The exact inversion of Jacob's own deception:**
  - Genesis 27:9, 16 — Jacob's deception of his father Isaac: Rebekah said "fetch me from thence two good kids of the goats; and I will make them savoury meat for thy father... And she put the skins of the kids of the goats upon his hands, and upon the smooth of his neck" — Jacob used a kid to deceive his blind father; the skin of the kid was part of the deception; the false garments were part of the deception; the false report ("I am Esau thy firstborn") completed it
  - Genesis 37:31-33 — Jacob's sons used a kid to deceive their father; the blood of the kid on the false garment was the deception; the false report ("know now whether it be thy son's coat or no") completed it; Jacob, who had deceived his father with a kid and false garments, was now deceived by his sons with a kid and a false garment
  - Galatians 6:7 — "Be not deceived; God is not mocked: for whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap" — the principle that was operating; the method of Jacob's original sin was the method of his punishment; the kid-and-garment deception returned to him; the Bible's own moral arithmetic was exact
  - The generational pattern: Jacob deceived his father Isaac with false garments; Jacob's sons deceived him with false garments; the sin pattern transmitted through the generation; the method was inherited even if the specific act was different; the family pattern of mirmah (deception — the word of Genesis 27:35 and Genesis 34:13) continued
- **Jacob's identification of the coat — "he knew it":** The Hebrew wayyakireha = and he recognized it; the same root (nakar) as in Genesis 42:7-8 — "And Joseph saw his brothers, and he knew them (wayyakrem), but made himself strange unto them, and spake roughly unto them" — the same vocabulary of recognition; Jacob recognized the coat that condemned his son to death; Joseph recognized the brothers who had condemned him; the word of recognition echoed between the two scenes across the years; Jacob's recognition was the beginning of his twenty years of mourning; Joseph's recognition was the beginning of the redemption arc
- **"I will go down into the grave (Sheol) to my son mourning" (v.35):** Jacob's grief was comprehensive; he refused consolation; he said he would descend to Sheol weeping; the word he used was Sheol — the place of the dead; he did not say "I will grieve a long time"; he said "I will take this grief to the grave"; his expectation was that Joseph was in Sheol and he would join him there still mourning; he would mourn until he died; Genesis 46:30 — when Jacob finally saw Joseph alive in Egypt: "And Israel said unto Joseph, Now let me die, since I have seen thy face, because thou art yet alive" — the grief that had been promised to last to the grave dissolved at the sight of the living son; what Jacob had spoken in the darkest hour was answered by a resurrection he had not dared to hope for

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### KETONET PASSIM — COAT OF MANY COLORS / ORNAMENTED TUNIC (Hebrew: כְּתֹנֶת פַּסִּים)

**Genesis 37:3** — "he made him a coat of many colours (ketonet passim)"

- **Root:** Ketonet = a tunic, a long garment worn as an undergarment or primary robe; the same word as the garments God made for Adam and Eve (Genesis 3:21 — "coats of skins"); passim = the disputed modifier; three main interpretations: (1) passim from pas (the palm of the hand/sole of the foot) — a garment with long sleeves and a long hem, reaching to the extremities — a garment of the non-laboring class; (2) passim as "multi-colored, decorated" — a coat of varied colors or ornamentation; (3) the LXX (Greek Septuagint) translated it as poikilos = variegated/many-colored, which gave the English "coat of many colors"
- **The 2 Samuel 13 identification:** 2 Samuel 13:18-19 — "And she had a garment of divers colours (ketonet passim) upon her: for with such robes were the king's daughters that were virgins apparelled. Then Tamar put ashes on her head, and rent her garment of divers colours that was on her, and laid her hand on her head, and went on crying" — Tamar, the king's daughter, wore a ketonet passim; the garment was the distinctive dress of a virgin royal daughter; Jacob's gift of this garment to Joseph was the gift of royal/distinguished status; it was not merely decorative but status-marking; it said: this son has a different standing in this household; the brothers read the message correctly — they just responded to it with hatred rather than honor
- **The garment thread through the Joseph narrative:**
  - Genesis 37:3 — coat given by the father: the covenant of honor and election
  - Genesis 37:23 — coat stripped by the brothers: the first humiliation
  - Genesis 39:12-13 — garment left in Potiphar's wife's hand: the false accusation
  - Genesis 41:42 — Pharaoh "arrayed him in vestures of fine linen" and put a gold chain around his neck: the resurrection clothing; the exaltation garment
  - The theological trajectory: the garment of honor given by the father → stripped at betrayal → used for false accusation → replaced by the garment of royal authority; the same pattern as Christ: the glory of the Father's presence → the stripping and mockery at the cross → the vindication and exaltation at the resurrection; Philippians 2:5-11 — "Who, being in the form of God... took upon him the form of a servant... became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross. Wherefore God also hath highly exalted him"

### BOR — PIT / CISTERN / DUNGEON / SHEOL (Hebrew: בּוֹר)

**Genesis 37:20** — "let us slay him, and cast him into some pit (bor)"
**Genesis 37:24** — "the pit was empty, there was no water in it"

- **Root:** Bur = to bore, to dig; bor = that which is dug — a pit, a cistern, a dungeon, a grave; the word covered the entire range from a practical water-storage pit to the symbolic grave to the underworld; in every use, it was a deep place below the surface, a place of confinement or death
- **The bor in the Psalms — consistently the figure of death/Sheol:**
  - Psalm 28:1 — "lest, if thou be silent to me, I become like them that go down into the pit (bor)"
  - Psalm 40:2 — "He brought me up also out of an horrible pit (bor shaon — the pit of tumult/noise), out of the miry clay" — the rescue from the pit as a paradigm of salvation
  - Psalm 88:3-6 — "my life draweth nigh unto the grave. I am counted with them that go down into the pit (bor)... like the slain that lie in the grave, whom thou rememberest no more: and they are cut off from thy hand" — the pit as the place of divine abandonment; the psalmist in the pit felt cut off from God's remembrance
  - Psalm 143:7 — "Deliver me, O LORD... lest I be like unto them that go down into the pit (bor)" — the pit as the ultimate place to be delivered from
- **Zechariah 9:11 — the covenant deliverance from the pit without water:** "As for thee also, by the blood of thy covenant I have sent forth thy prisoners out of the pit wherein is no water (bor ein bo mayim)" — this was the exact phrase of Genesis 37:24 ("a pit, and the pit was empty, there was no water in it — bor ein bo mayim"); the prophet invoked the same image; the blood of the covenant would release prisoners from pits like Joseph's; the language bridge between Genesis 37 and Zechariah 9 was built into the Hebrew; the prisoners released from the waterless pit by covenant blood were released by what the cross accomplished — Christ's blood was the blood of the covenant (Matthew 26:28 — "this is my blood of the new covenant") that opened the pit and released the captives
- **The two bors of Joseph's narrative:** The pit at Dothan (Genesis 37:24) and the dungeon in Egypt (Genesis 39:20 — "And Joseph's master took him, and put him into the prison, a place where the king's prisoners were bound: and he was there in the prison") — two descents into the pit; two episodes of unjust confinement; two experiences of the grave; each followed by a greater elevation; the pattern of death-and-resurrection doubled within the Joseph narrative before the final exaltation

### BAAL CHALOMOTH — MASTER OF DREAMS (Hebrew: בַּעַל חֲלֹמוֹת)

**Genesis 37:19** — "Behold, this dreamer (baal chalomoth) cometh"

- **Root:** Baal = lord, master, owner, husband; chalomoth = dreams (plural of chalom = a dream); baal chalomoth = "the lord/master of dreams"; the construction was the Hebrew idiom for a person characterized by or identified with a thing; a "baal" of something was the person who owned or was owned by it; the brothers used it contemptuously — "the dream-lord coming at us" — but the title was descriptively accurate
- **Chalom (dream) as the instrument of divine revelation:** Genesis recorded multiple divinely given dreams, all prophetically accurate:
  - Genesis 20:3 — God appeared to Abimelech in a dream: "thou art but a dead man"
  - Genesis 28:12 — Jacob's ladder: "a dream... behold a ladder set up on the earth"
  - Genesis 31:10-11 — the angel of God appeared to Jacob in a dream showing the spotted cattle
  - Genesis 37:5-10 — Joseph's two dreams; the sheaves and the stars
  - Genesis 40:5-22 — the butler's and baker's dreams; both accurately fulfilled
  - Genesis 41:1-36 — Pharaoh's two dreams; both interpreted by Joseph, both fulfilled
  - Numbers 12:6 — God's own statement: "I the LORD will make myself known unto him in a vision, and will speak unto him in a dream" — the divine endorsement of the dream as a prophetic channel
  - The pattern in Genesis: every dream was from God; every dream came true; no dream in Genesis was idle fancy; to be a "master of dreams" in Genesis was to be the instrument of the most reliable communication in the narrative
- **The mockers became the fulfillers:** Genesis 42:6 — "And Joseph was the governor over the land, and he it was that sold to all the people of the land: and Joseph's brethren came, and bowed down themselves before him with their faces to the earth" — the first fulfillment of the first dream; Genesis 43:26 — the brothers bowed again; Genesis 44:14 — "Judah and his brethren came to Joseph's house; for he was yet there: and they fell before him on the ground" — the brothers who said "we shall see what will become of his dreams" (Genesis 37:20) became the people who bowed before him; Isaiah 55:11 — "So shall my word be that goeth forth out of my mouth: it shall not return unto me void, but it shall accomplish that which I please, and it shall prosper in the thing whereto I sent it" — the dreams that the brothers tried to destroy accomplished exactly what they were sent to accomplish

### EVILREPORT — DIBBAH (Hebrew: דִּבָּה)

**Genesis 37:2** — "Joseph brought unto his father their evil report (dibbah)"

- **Root:** Dabar = to speak; dibbah = an evil report, a defamatory report, a report that damaged someone's reputation; it was not merely "news" or "information" — it was specifically a negative, damaging report; the same word was used in Numbers 13:32 — "And they brought up an evil report (dibbah) of the land which they had searched unto the children of Israel" — the ten spies who brought back the discouraging report about Canaan brought a dibbah; the word carried the implication that the report was harmful, demoralizing, or possibly exaggerated
- **The ambiguity the text preserved:** The text did not clarify whether Joseph's dibbah was accurate or embellished; he brought a report of his brothers to his father; whatever it contained, Jacob received it and believed it enough to make Joseph his trusted messenger (Genesis 37:13-14 — "come, and I will send thee unto them"); but the brothers knew about the report and it added to their hatred; the text was silent about whether their hatred was proportionate; what it recorded was the hatred and the report together, without editorial judgment on the content of the report
- **The theological significance:** Joseph who brought a dibbah about his brothers was later falsely reported about by Potiphar's wife (Genesis 39:17-18 — she made a dibbah of him); the reporter of bad news about his brothers experienced false reporting against himself; the one who had spoken a damaging word was spoken of damagingly; the narrative's moral architecture was exact without being pedantic; the text showed rather than told

### VAYISHTEHU — AND THEY BOWED DOWN (Hebrew: וַיִּשְׁתַּחֲווּ)

**Genesis 37:7, 9** — the sheaves/stars made obeisance (vayishtachavu)

- **Root:** Shachah = to bow down, to prostrate oneself, to worship; the same word used for worship of God throughout the Old Testament; when the sheaves bowed to Joseph's sheaf in the dream, the word used was the word for worship and full prostration; when Jacob understood the dream (v.10 — "shall I and thy mother and thy brethren indeed come to bow down ourselves to thee"), he used the same word
- **The dream's fulfillment in the vayishtehu of Egypt:**
  - Genesis 42:6 — "Joseph's brethren came, and bowed down themselves before him with their faces to the earth" — the word vayishtachavu; the fulfillment of the dream's word
  - Genesis 43:26 — they bowed (vayishtachavu) before him with the present
  - Genesis 43:28 — "they bowed down their heads, and made obeisance (vayishtachavu)"
  - Genesis 44:14 — "fell before him on the ground (vayipelu lefanav artza)" — full prostration
  - The dream used the worship-word deliberately; Joseph would not merely be served by his brothers — they would bow before him in the posture of worship; the full prostration of Genesis 44:14 was the most complete fulfillment; it was also the moment Judah offered himself as a slave in Joseph's place — the worship posture and the self-offering coming together
- **The theological trajectory to worship of Christ:** Philippians 2:10-11 — "That at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, of things in heaven, and things in earth, and things under the earth; And that every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father" — the vayishtachavu of Joseph's dreams was the anticipation of the universal bowing at Christ's name; what the eleven stars did to Joseph in the dream, every knee would do to Christ in the final fulfillment; the dream-word for worship became the apostle's word for the universal confession

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### Joseph's Dreams Were Self-Promoting Pride — He Should Not Have Told Them

**The Misuse:** Genesis 37:5-10 — Joseph told his brothers and father both dreams, producing intensified hatred; the telling of the dreams is read as Joseph's pride or social naivety — that a spiritually mature person would have kept the dreams private rather than producing conflict.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Every God-given prophetic word in Genesis was meant to be declared:** Genesis 20:7 — God told Abimelech to restore Abraham's wife and said "he is a prophet"; the prophetic role was always connected to declaration; Genesis 28:13-15 — God gave Jacob the dream at Bethel with explicit content; Jacob did not hide it; he set up a pillar and vowed; the pattern of God-given revelation was reception followed by response and acknowledgment, not concealment
- **The dreams were not Joseph's construction — they were God's revelation:** The text never said Joseph invented the dreams or embellished them; Numbers 12:6 — "I the LORD will make myself known unto him in a vision, and will speak unto him in a dream" — the dream was the LORD's speaking; the content of the dreams was not about Joseph's pride but about God's ordained plan; the question was not whether to tell what God had spoken but how; Joseph was seventeen, and his method of telling — going to his brothers who already hated him — was perhaps unwise; but the content of the dreams was true and the declaration of them was the declaration of God's word
- **Jacob's response distinguished the messenger from the message:** Genesis 37:10-11 — "And his father rebuked him... And his brethren envied him; but his father observed the saying" — Jacob rebuked the manner or the apparent impiety of the statement but "observed the saying" — he kept it in his mind; Jacob knew the dreams were significant; his rebuke was the natural response of a father tempering a son's manner, not a theological condemnation of the content
- **Isaiah 55:11** — "So shall my word be that goeth forth out of my mouth: it shall not return unto me void, but it shall accomplish that which I please" — the dreams accomplished exactly what God sent them to accomplish; the telling of them was part of the process; had they not been told, the brothers' envy would not have been fully expressed, the conspiracy would not have formed, and the providential chain leading to Joseph's exaltation and the family's salvation would not have begun; the word was not wasted
- *Correction:* Joseph's telling of the dreams was the declaration of what God had revealed. The method — telling brothers who already hated him — may have been unwise for a seventeen-year-old. But the content was God's word, and Jacob's "observing of the saying" showed that the message was recognized as significant. God-given revelation was meant to be declared, not suppressed. The dreams' content was true; their telling set in motion the chain of events that fulfilled them.

---

### "He Is Our Brother and Our Flesh" — Judah's Argument Showed Genuine Compassion

**The Misuse:** Genesis 37:27 — Judah's argument "he is our brother and our flesh" is read as evidence of genuine compassion and moral concern for Joseph; that Judah was the merciful voice who saved Joseph from death; that the sale was an act of mercy compared to murder.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Judah's argument was framed entirely in terms of profit and self-interest:** v.26 — "What profit is it if we slay our brother, and conceal his blood?" — the first word was "profit"; the argument was economic; the question was not "this is wrong" but "what do we gain?"; the acknowledgment of brotherhood in v.27 was deployed in the service of the commercial alternative, not as a genuine moral stand
- **Selling Joseph was not mercy — it was a different form of disposal:** Selling a brother into slavery was not mercy; it was trafficking in human beings; the alternative to murder was not "we should not do this" but "we should profit from him instead"; the "mercy" was the mercy of the man who chose a more profitable sin over a less profitable one; the sin remained; the brother was sold; the father was destroyed with grief; the family was broken for twenty years
- **Acts 7:9 identified the motivation accurately:** "And the patriarchs, moved with envy, sold Joseph into Egypt" — the Holy Spirit through Stephen identified the primary motivation as envy; not compassion; not brotherly concern; but the envy that had produced the hatred of Genesis 37:4-8; Judah's articulation of brotherhood was the rationalizing language of envy-driven sin making itself sound reasonable
- **The genuine compassion came later:** Genesis 44:33 — this was Judah's genuine compassion; this was his real "he is our brother"; twenty-plus years later, standing before Joseph unrecognized, offering himself as a slave in Benjamin's place — "for how shall I go up to my father, and the lad be not with me?" (Genesis 44:34) — that was the compassion for a brother and a father that Genesis 37:27 had failed to be
- *Correction:* Judah's argument in Genesis 37:27 was a profit-motivated commercial alternative to murder, not genuine compassion. The "he is our brother and our flesh" was the acknowledging of brotherhood in the service of trafficking, not in the service of protection. The genuine compassion of Judah came twenty years later in Genesis 44:33. The Bible's internal narrative tracked the difference.

---

### Jacob's Grief Was Excessive — He Should Have Trusted God

**The Misuse:** Genesis 37:34-35 — Jacob mourned for many days, refused to be comforted, and declared he would go to the grave in grief — this is sometimes used to argue that Jacob's grief was a failure of faith; that a trusting person would not have mourned so deeply; that "excessive" grief indicates spiritual weakness.

**What Scripture says:**

- **Jacob was deceived — he had no basis for trust because he did not know the truth:** The text was clear: Jacob believed Joseph was dead; he had received what appeared to be incontrovertible evidence (the identified coat, the blood); he was not grieving a loss he could have trusted God through — he was grieving a death he had no reason to doubt; his grief was proportionate to the information he had; the excessive grief was not a failure of trust but the natural consequence of a father who had loved a son and believed him dead
- **Refusing to be comforted was a prophetic statement, not a spiritual failure:** The reason Jacob could not be comforted was that the object of grief was not actually dead — and at some level Jacob knew it; Matthew Henry and many traditional expositors noted that comfort for the death of a living person was impossible because death had not actually occurred; Jacob's refusal of comfort was the heart's instinct that what he was being asked to accept was not true; he could not receive comfort for Joseph's death because Joseph was not dead
- **Jeremiah 31:15 — Rachel's grief used as the biblical model of genuine mourning:** "Rachel weeping for her children refused to be comforted for her children, because they were not" — the same phrase "refused to be comforted" was used for Rachel's grief over the exiled children; this grief was recognized by God as genuine covenant grief; it was not rebuked but honored; the refusing of comfort was the insistence that the loss was real and total, not to be softened by platitudes
- **John 11:35 — Jesus wept at Lazarus's tomb, knowing the resurrection was imminent:** The model of faith-filled grief was not the absence of grief but grief in the presence of hope; Jesus wept and then raised Lazarus; Jacob wept and then saw Joseph alive in Egypt (Genesis 46:29-30 — "and he fell on his neck, and wept on his neck a good while"); the weeping was not opposed to the reunion — it was part of the journey toward it
- *Correction:* Jacob's grief was the natural response of a father who believed his beloved son was dead, based on what appeared to be compelling evidence. His refusing of comfort was the heart's prophetic resistance to accepting a lie as true. The Bible never rebuked Jacob's mourning. The model of grief in Scripture was not the absence of tears but the presence of hope alongside them — and Jacob's grief was transformed into the reunion of Genesis 46:29-30.

- The theological constant: Egypt in Scripture was the place that preserved the covenant people in their most vulnerable hour, the place of exile and testing, and the place from which God called His people out in greater strength than they entered; the Joseph story and the Exodus story and the infancy narrative of Jesus were all instances of the same pattern

- Genesis 42:6, 9 — "Joseph's brethren came, and bowed down themselves before him with their faces to the earth. And Joseph remembered the dreams which he dreamed of them" — the dream fulfilled; Joseph recognized the fulfillment in the moment it happened
- Isaiah 55:10-11 — "For as the rain cometh down, and the snow from heaven, and returneth not thither, but watereth the earth, and maketh it bring forth and bud... So shall my word be that goeth forth out of my mouth: it shall not return unto me void" — the theological principle that the Joseph narrative demonstrated; the dream that the brothers tried to destroy by selling the dreamer came true through the very act of selling him; the attempt to void the word was the instrument of its fulfillment

### The Deception That Returned — Jacob's Method Turned on Himself

**Genesis 27:9-16 / Genesis 37:31-33 — the kid-and-garment deceptions**

**Thread:**
- Genesis 27:9 — Rebekah: "fetch me from thence two good kids of the goats; and I will make them savoury meat for thy father" — the kid used as the instrument of deception
- Genesis 27:16 — "she put the skins of the kids of the goats upon his hands, and upon the smooth of his neck" — the skin of the kid on the body
- Genesis 27:22 — Isaac: "The voice is Jacob's voice, but the hands are the hands of Esau" — the deception worked; the blind father could not see through the false garment and the false report
- Genesis 37:31-33 — "they killed a kid of the goats, and dipped the coat in the blood... And Jacob said, It is my son's coat" — the same instruments: a kid, a garment, a false report; the father deceived again; but this time Jacob was the father, not the son; the method of his original sin was the instrument of his punishment
- Galatians 6:7 — "whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap" — the principle operating; the seed sown in Genesis 27 was harvested in Genesis 37; the harvest was not immediate — it came through a generation; but it came with exact correspondence in method
- The wider thread: Jacob had deceived with kid and garment and false voice; Laban had deceived Jacob with false brides (Genesis 29:23-25); Jacob's sons deceived Jacob with kid and garment and false evidence; the deception was sown, grew through a generation, and was harvested with exact method; the Bible's moral arithmetic was precise and unhurried

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time a Prophetic Dream Was Given to a Member of the Covenant Family That Was Explicitly About Their Future Governance and the Family's Submission

**Genesis 37:5-9** — Joseph's two dreams
- The first explicitly personal prophetic dreams about the future governance of the dreamer over his family within the covenant narrative (distinct from the national/covenant dreams like Genesis 15 or 28)
- First mention establishes: prophetic dreams about future governance were to be received and held, not suppressed; the response to such dreams was the telling of them (which Jacob "observed") and the living faithfully until they were fulfilled; the immediate response of the hearers was rejection and envy, not acceptance — yet the dreams came true; the first mention established that God-given prophetic words about future position were not subject to the veto of those who rejected them

### First Use of the Phrase "He Could Not Speak Peaceably" as a Description of Settled Hatred

**Genesis 37:4** — "they hated him, and could not speak peaceably unto him"
- The first instance of this phrase in Scripture; the inability to speak peaceably as the evidence of complete, settled hatred
- First mention establishes: the category of hatred that had gone beyond an incident or a grievance into a settled condition of the heart; the brothers had not merely been offended — they had moved to a state where peace in ordinary conversation was impossible; this was the settled enmity that the psalm writers described (Psalm 55:21) and that James addressed (James 4:1-2); the first mention of this phrase in the Joseph narrative established it as the condition from which the entire twenty-year story of betrayal, exile, and eventual reconciliation needed to be healed

### First Time Potiphar Was Named — The Covenant Person Placed Under a Pagan Authority

**Genesis 37:36** — "Potiphar, an officer of Pharaoh's, and captain of the guard"
- The first mention of Potiphar; the first establishment of Joseph's Egyptian context
- First mention establishes: the covenant person's placement under pagan authority was not coincidental but providential; Potiphar was the captain of the guard, the most security-conscious officer in Pharaoh's household; Joseph's placement there was the preparation for the dungeon (Potiphar's dungeon held Pharaoh's prisoners — Genesis 39:20 — 40:3), and the dungeon was the preparation for Pharaoh's court; God placed Joseph under the authority of the man whose household would eventually be the door to the throne

## NAME MEANINGS

| Name/Term | Meaning | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| Joseph (Yosef) | He will add / May he add | From yasaf (to add, to increase); Rachel's original naming (Genesis 30:24 — "The LORD shall add to me another son"); the name carried both the looking-back (God has taken away my reproach) and the looking-forward (may He add another); the name of the covenant's great preserving instrument |
| Benjamin (Binyamin) | Son of the right hand | The younger brother of Joseph; born in Genesis 35:18; the most-beloved of Jacob's remaining sons after Joseph was presumed dead; his place in the Joseph narrative (Genesis 42-44) as the test of the brothers' repentance was central |
| Judah (Yehudah) | Praise / I will praise the LORD | Leah's declaration at his birth (Genesis 29:35); the same root as todah (thanksgiving) and yadah (to praise); the one named Praise who arranged the sale of his brother; who later offered himself as a slave in his brother's place; who received the scepter promise (Genesis 49:10); from whose line came David and Christ |
| Potiphar | He whom Ra (the sun god) gave | Egyptian name: pa-di-pa-Ra = he who was given by Ra; an officer of Pharaoh in the household of the sun-worshipping empire of Egypt; the pagan name of the man God used to position Joseph for the throne |
| Dothan | Two wells / Two cisterns | The place where Joseph found his brothers and was thrown into the pit; the name of the place meant wells/cisterns — the very pit the brothers threw Joseph into was at a place named for pits; and 2 Kings 6:13 — the place where the Syrian army surrounded Elisha; Dothan as the recurring location of covenant persons surrounded by those who sought to destroy them, and whom God preserved |
| Ishmaelites / Midianites | Descendants of Ishmael / Midian | The caravan was identified by both names; consistent with ancient caravans that included members of both groups, or with the overlap between the two peoples in that region; the instruments God used to bring Joseph to Egypt were the descendants of Abraham's non-covenant sons — Ishmael and Midian (also a son of Abraham by Keturah — Genesis 25:2) |
