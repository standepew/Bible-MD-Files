## CHAPTER SUMMARY

The famine was severe. Jacob's household had eaten through all the grain brought from Egypt. Jacob said: "Go again, buy us a little food." Judah reminded him directly: the man had said "Ye shall not see my face, except your brother be with you." There was no second trip without Benjamin. Jacob's response was a grief-laden accusation: "Wherefore dealt ye so ill with me, as to tell the man whether ye had yet a brother?" — as if the disclosure had been strategically avoidable. The brothers answered that the man had asked directly about their family; they could not have known he would say "Bring your brother."

Judah stepped forward with a different register than Reuben had used in Genesis 42:37. Reuben had offered his two sons as surety — an extravagant but practically hollow offer (killing Jacob's grandsons would not restore Benjamin). Judah offered himself: "I will be surety for him; of my hand shalt thou require him: if I bring him not unto thee, and set him before thee, then let me bear the blame for ever." This was the surety-language of covenant guarantee — the staking of one's own standing, permanently, for the safe return of another. Jacob yielded. He told them to take a present — balm, honey, spices, myrrh, nuts, and almonds — and double money, and to take Benjamin.

The brothers arrived in Egypt. Joseph saw Benjamin and commanded his steward to bring the men to his house and prepare a feast. The brothers were afraid: they thought they were being brought in because of the money found in their sacks — that they would be seized as thieves. They addressed the steward preemptively, explaining about the returned money. The steward's response was extraordinary: "Peace be to you, fear not: your God, and the God of your father, hath given you treasure in your sacks: I had your money." — the steward of an Egyptian official speaking the name of the God of Israel and attributing the unexplained treasure to divine grace; the steward's word functioned as a preview of the revelation to come: the gift was from God, not from a trap.

Simeon was brought out. The brothers were given water to wash their feet and provender for their animals. They prepared the present for Joseph's arrival at noon. When Joseph arrived they bowed down to him to the earth — all eleven, with Benjamin present for the first time; the second dream of Genesis 37:9 (sun/moon/eleven stars bowing) was approaching its fulfillment. Joseph asked: "Is your father well, the old man of whom ye spake? Is he yet alive?" They answered, "Thy servant our father is in good health, he is yet alive. And they bowed down their heads, and made obeisance." Joseph's eyes moved to Benjamin: "Is this your younger brother, of whom ye spake unto me? And he said, God be gracious unto thee, my son." Then Joseph made haste — his bowels yearned upon his brother — and sought where to weep; he entered his chamber and wept there; then he washed his face and returned and restrained himself.

The feast was served with the three groups at three separate tables: the Egyptians, Joseph by himself, and the eleven brothers. The Egyptians would not eat with Hebrews — "for that is an abomination unto the Egyptians." The brothers' portions were served before them; Benjamin's portion was five times the portion of any of his brothers. Joseph watched to see their response. They drank and were merry with him.

## CROSS-REFERENCES (Scripture Interpreting Scripture)

### Judah's Surety — The Guarantee of Personal Accountability for Another

**Genesis 43:9** — "I will be surety for him; of my hand shalt thou require him: if I bring him not unto thee, and set him before thee, then let me bear the blame for ever"

- **Root of the surety concept:** Arav = to pledge, to give security, to be surety; the surety was the person who stood in the place of the one for whom the guarantee was given; if the principal could not fulfill, the surety bore the consequence; Judah was pledging his permanent honor — "let me bear the blame for ever (chata'ti lecha kol-ha-yamim — I will have sinned against you all the days)" — an irrevocable personal commitment
- **Proverbs 22:26-27 — the warning about surety-pledging:** "Be not thou one of them that strike hands, or of them that are sureties for debts: If thou hast nothing to pay, why should he take away thy bed from under thee?" — the general wisdom against casual surety-pledging; Judah's surety was not casual but deliberate; the wise man's warning was about the weight of what Judah was undertaking; Judah understood the weight and accepted it
- **Hebrews 7:22 — Jesus the surety of a better covenant:** "By so much was Jesus made a surety of a better testament" — the direct New Testament application of the surety concept to Christ; Christ stood as the guarantee of the new covenant; His life, His righteousness, His death, His resurrection were the pledge that God's covenant promises would be honored for all who were in Him; Judah's "I will be surety for Benjamin" was the type; Christ's covenant-surety was the antitype; the one who pledged himself for the safe delivery of the younger brother was the type of the One who pledged Himself for the safe delivery of all who believed
- **Romans 5:6-8 — the surety who acted when we could not:** "For when we were yet without strength, in due time Christ died for the ungodly... But God commendeth his love toward us, in that, while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us" — Benjamin could not secure his own safe passage; he was dependent on Judah's pledge; the sinner cannot secure his own safe passage to God; the believer is dependent on Christ's pledge; Judah's surety-speech was the most direct type of the atonement-surety language in the patriarchal narratives

### The Steward's Answer — "Your God Hath Given You Treasure"

**Genesis 43:23** — "Peace be to you, fear not: your God, and the God of your father, hath given you treasure in your sacks: I had your money"

- The steward was an Egyptian servant of an Egyptian official; the theology he spoke was the theology of the covenant God of Israel; he named "your God and the God of your father" — the covenant formula; he attributed the unexplained treasure to divine grace; the steward's speech was Joseph's teaching in the steward's mouth — Joseph had instructed him to return the money (Genesis 42:25); the steward was the instrument; but the attribution to God was either Joseph's instruction or the steward's own Spirit-prompted speech
- Philippians 4:19 — "But my God shall supply all your need according to his riches in glory by Christ Jesus" — the divine supply of what the recipients had not earned; the money in the sacks was the brothers' payment returned plus the grace of the provider; God's supply exceeded the brothers' expectation or payment; the same pattern of grace-supply ran from the steward's announcement to Paul's Philippian declaration
- Romans 8:32 — "He that spared not his own Son, but delivered him up for us all, how shall he not with him also freely give us all things?" — the God who gave the treasure in the sacks when they had already paid was the God who gave His Son and promised to freely give all things with Him; the steward's theology was the beginning of the answer to the brothers' terrified question of Genesis 42:28 — "What is this that God hath done unto us?" — God had given them treasure; He was the giver, not the accuser

### Joseph Sees Benjamin — "God Be Gracious Unto Thee, My Son"

**Genesis 43:29** — "And he lifted up his eyes, and saw his brother Benjamin, his mother's wife's son, and said, Is this your younger brother, of whom ye spake unto me? And he said, God be gracious unto thee, my son"

- Joseph's first words to his full brother were a blessing — "God be gracious unto thee (Elohim yachancha) my son" — the language of priestly blessing; the verb chanan (to be gracious) was the same root as chen (grace, favor); the one who had wept in his chamber then returned and spoke grace over the one he had not seen for years without being able to name their relationship; the blessing was as close to "I love you" as the hidden identity permitted
- Numbers 6:25 — the Aaronic blessing: "The LORD make his face shine upon thee, and be gracious unto thee (wichuneka)" — the same verbal root; the priestly blessing of the covenant people; Joseph spoke over Benjamin the language of the covenant blessing in the form of the priestly office; the concealed brother blessed his brother in the grammar of the sanctuary
- The phrase "my son" (beni) was the term of a father for a child, a teacher for a student, or a superior for a beloved inferior; Joseph could not say "my brother" without revealing himself; he said "my son" — expressing the depth of covenant affection in the closest substitute for the word he could not yet speak

### Benjamin's Five-Fold Portion — The Grace That Distinguished

**Genesis 43:34** — "And he took and sent messes unto them from before him: but Benjamin's mess was five times so much as any of theirs"

- Joseph sent five portions to Benjamin and one to each of the others; he watched to see the brothers' response; would they resent the favored treatment of another? would the old jealousy arise? their acceptance of the five-fold portion without resentment was part of the evidence Joseph gathered about their changed character
- Genesis 37:3-4 — "Israel loved Joseph more than all his children, because he was the son of his old age: and he made him a coat of many colors. And when his brethren saw that their father loved him more than all his brethren, they hated him" — the original jealousy was triggered by preferential treatment; Joseph's five-fold portion to Benjamin was a deliberate echo of that original trigger; he was testing whether the pattern of Genesis 37 would repeat; the brothers drank and were merry — they did not repeat it
- Matthew 20:15 — "Is it not lawful for me to do what I will with mine own? Is thine eye evil, because I am good?" — the parable of the workers in the vineyard; the master's generosity to the last-hired was resented by the first-hired; the eye of envy versus the eye of generosity; the brothers had once been unable to speak peaceably to the favored brother (Genesis 37:4); now they drank with the favored one; the test passed

## KEY TERMS WITH CONTEXTUAL DEFINITIONS

### ARAV — SURETY / PLEDGE / GUARANTEE (Hebrew: עָרַב)

**Genesis 43:9** — "I will be surety for him (ve-anochi e'ervennu)"

- **Root:** Arav = to weave together, to intermix, to pledge, to be surety; the metaphor was of lives woven together — the surety's fate was woven into the principal's fate; if the principal failed, the surety bore the consequence; the surety could not walk away from the pledge
- **Proverbs 11:15 — the cost of surety:** "He that is surety for a stranger shall smart for it: and he that hateth suretiship is sure" — the wisdom literature's consistent acknowledgment that surety carried real cost; Judah was not unaware of this; the irrevocability of his pledge ("let me bear the blame for ever") showed he understood the cost and accepted it
- **Hebrews 7:22 — Christ as the egguos (Greek: guarantor) of the better covenant:** "By so much was Jesus made a surety (egguos) of a better testament" — the Greek egguos was the LXX/New Testament equivalent of the Hebrew arav; the one who stood as the personal guarantee of the covenant's promises; Christ's suretyship meant that every covenant promise was backed by His personal standing; the new covenant's certainty rested on the indestructible life of the guarantor (Hebrews 7:16) — the surety who could not fail

### MINCHAH — PRESENT / GIFT / TRIBUTE-OFFERING (Hebrew: מִנְחָה)

**Genesis 43:11** — "take of the best fruits in the land in your vessels, and carry down the man a present"

- **Root:** Minchah = a gift given to a superior, a grain offering, a tribute brought to honor one of higher rank; in the sacrificial system, the minchah was the grain offering brought to God (Leviticus 2); in the diplomatic/relational context, it was the gift that acknowledged the recipient's superior standing and sought their favor
- **The components of the minchah in Genesis 43:11:** Balm, honey, spices, myrrh, pistachio nuts, and almonds — the same trade goods the Ishmaelite merchants were carrying when they purchased Joseph in Genesis 37:25 (balm, spices, myrrh); the irony was precise: the goods used to conduct the sale of Joseph were now the goods being carried as a gift to the unknown governor; the instruments of the sin were now the instruments of the approach to reconciliation
- **The minchah as the acknowledgment of dependence:** Jacob sent the minchah not because it would impress an Egyptian official but because it expressed the family's supplication; they had nothing to offer but the products of their diminished land; the gift was the acknowledgment: we come to you as dependents, as those who need your favor

### SHALOM — PEACE / WHOLENESS / THE GREETING OF RESTORATION

**Genesis 43:23** — "Peace be to you, fear not" (shalom lachem al-tireu)
**Genesis 43:27** — "And he asked them of their welfare (shalom), and said, Is your father well?"

- **Root:** Shalom = wholeness, completeness, the absence of brokenness, the presence of everything needed; not merely the absence of conflict but the positive presence of well-being in every dimension
- **The steward's "shalom lachem" as the first peace-declaration in the chapter:** The brothers came in fear (Genesis 43:18); the steward spoke peace (v.23); the fear that drove them to preemptive confession was answered with peace before the governor had even entered; the peace preceded the audience and prepared the way for the feast; the same pattern as Christ who gave peace before the disciples understood what He had accomplished (John 14:27 — "Peace I leave with you, my peace I give unto you")
- **Joseph's shalom inquiry (v.27):** Three times he asked about shalom: Is your father well (shalom)? Is he yet alive? — the shalom of Jacob was Joseph's first concern when he could finally speak personally; the wholeness of the family, the life of the father — this was the question behind all the testing

## PROPHETIC/TYPOLOGICAL CONNECTIONS

### Judah as Surety — The Tribe-of-Promise Guaranteeing the Beloved Son

**Genesis 43:9 / Genesis 49:10 / Hebrews 7:22 / Romans 5:6-8**

Judah stood as surety for Benjamin. From Judah's tribe would come the Lion of Judah (Genesis 49:10). The surety-role of Genesis 43:9 prefigured the ultimate surety-role of Judah's descendant — the one who stood as the guarantee of the new covenant (Hebrews 7:22). The pattern: the one through whom the promise flowed (Judah/Christ) was the one who pledged himself for the one who could not secure his own safe passage (Benjamin/the believer). The surety's willingness to "bear the blame for ever" was the shadow of the One who bore the blame on the tree.

---

### The Three Tables — The Division Before the Reconciliation

**Genesis 43:32 / Galatians 3:28 / Ephesians 2:14-16**

Three tables: the Egyptians, Joseph by himself, and the eleven brothers. The division was the maximum separation — the covenant person (Joseph) isolated from both the Gentile Egyptians and his own brothers by the barriers of identity, office, and the concealed relationship. Ephesians 2:14-16 — "For he is our peace, who hath made both one, and hath broken down the middle wall of partition between us... to make in himself of twain one new man." The three tables of Genesis 43 were the pre-reconciliation state; after Joseph revealed himself, there would be one table. The middle wall broken down by the revelation was the type of the middle wall broken down by Christ's cross.

---

### Joseph's Hidden Grief — The Man of Sorrows Restrained

**Genesis 43:30-31 / Isaiah 53:3 / John 11:35 / Hebrews 4:15**

Joseph who yearned for his brother, sought where to weep, wept in his chamber, washed his face, and returned — "refrained himself" — was the consistent image of the mediator whose grief was deeper than the circumstances permitted him to express. Isaiah 53:3 — "He is despised and rejected of men; a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief." The hidden weeping of Genesis 43 was the type of the grief of the One who bore the sorrows of those He came to save while the appointed time of full revelation had not yet come.

## COMMONLY MISQUOTED / MISAPPLIED PASSAGES

### "If I Be Bereaved of My Children, I Am Bereaved" — Jacob's Fatalism as Faith

**The Misuse:** Genesis 43:14 is read as the statement of a broken man surrendering hope — a fatalistic resignation that has nothing to do with faith, and therefore not a model for the believer.

**What Scripture says:**

- The phrase "if I be bereaved, I am bereaved" was the statement of a man who had exhausted all his attempts to control the outcome and was releasing the result to God; it was not nihilism but resignation in the theological sense — the surrender of the uncontrollable to God; the prayer immediately preceding it ("God Almighty give you mercy before the man") was the appeal to the divine; Jacob prayed and then released
- Job 1:21 — "The LORD gave, and the LORD hath taken away; blessed be the name of the LORD" — the same structure: acknowledgment of loss combined with trust in the sovereign God; not cheerfulness but submission; not the denial of grief but the release of control
- Philippians 4:11 — "I have learned, in whatsoever state I am, therewith to be content" — Paul's "I have learned" indicated that this was not natural but acquired through experience; Jacob's "if I be bereaved" was the same acquired acceptance — not the absence of grief but the presence of submission alongside it
- *Correction:* Genesis 43:14 was Jacob's prayer-preceded release of the uncontrollable; he prayed for mercy and then released the outcome; the sentence was not fatalism but the courage to act when all other options had been exhausted and the result was in God's hands alone.

## SYMBOLIC THREADS

### The Minchah to the Unknown Governor — The Gift That Was Also a Confession

**Genesis 43:11 / Genesis 37:25 / Romans 12:20 / Proverbs 16:14**

- Genesis 43:11 — balm, honey, spices, myrrh, nuts, almonds — the same commodities listed in Genesis 37:25 as the Ishmaelites' trade goods, the trade in which Joseph was sold
- The brothers carrying the same goods they had traded for Joseph's life as a gift to the one before whom they would soon confess that crime was the Bible's own built-in irony; the text preserved the detail; the reader who had read Genesis 37:25 would recognize the items
- Romans 12:20 — "if thine enemy hunger, feed him; if he thirst, give him drink: for in so doing thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head" — the gift given to the one who was an enemy (from the brothers' perspective, the threatening Egyptian governor) was the instrument of transformation; the gift returned the relationship; the minchah was not merely diplomatic protocol but the approach of dependent supplicants to the one who held their lives

### The Seating by Birth Order — "The Men Marvelled One at Another"

**Genesis 43:33 / Psalm 139:1-4**

Joseph's steward seated eleven brothers in their precise birth order — from firstborn to youngest. They had not told Joseph their birth order. They marvelled. The inexplicable precision — seated in the exact sequence of their birth — was the sign that the one before them knew them completely, knew them from before the arrangement of the feast, knew them as they were. Psalm 139:1-4 — "O LORD, thou hast searched me, and known me. Thou knowest my downsitting and mine uprising, thou understandest my thought afar off... and art acquainted with all my ways. For there is not a word in my tongue, but, lo, O LORD, thou knowest it altogether." The brothers marvelled because someone knew them more completely than they could explain. The one they did not recognize knew them entirely — in the correct order of their lives.

## FIRST MENTION PRINCIPLES

### First Time a Non-Israelite Named "Your God and the God of Your Father" as the Source of Grace

**Genesis 43:23** — "your God, and the God of your father, hath given you treasure in your sacks"
- An Egyptian steward spoke the covenant formula — the God of the patriarchs identified by name-of-relationship; first time a Gentile servant attributed an unexplained gift specifically to the covenant God
- Establishes: the God of Israel was working through the Gentile administration to communicate grace to the covenant people; even the steward of an Egyptian official was an instrument of the covenant God's communication; Acts 17:28 — "in him we live, and move, and have our being" — the God of Israel was sovereign over Egyptian households; the steward's declaration was the preview of the Gentiles acknowledging the God of Israel

### First Time Judah Spoke in a Role of Covenantal Leadership and Substitution

**Genesis 43:8-9** — Judah's surety-speech
- Earlier in the narrative Judah had spoken twice: Genesis 37:26 (proposing the sale of Joseph — self-interest) and Genesis 38:26 (acknowledging Tamar's righteousness — awakening conscience); here he spoke as the guarantor of another's life
- The progression: betrayal (Genesis 37:26) → conviction (Genesis 38:26) → covenant substitution (Genesis 43:8-9) → ultimate substitution (Genesis 44:33 — offering himself in Benjamin's place); the first surety-speech of Genesis 43:9 was the formation of the character that Genesis 44:33 would express at its fullest
